In Bali, motorcycles are a common mode of transportation for locals, especially in places like Ubud, where you'll often see them weaving through rice fields. Many tourists are tempted by the idea that motorcycles are both economical and convenient for freely exploring Bali.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_ff2d79a6384a7682bb6d83c4de623131.jpg)

## Is Riding a Motorcycle in Bali Safe?

Short answer: It depends. If you've never ridden a motorcycle before and are only here for a short vacation, we don't recommend it. But if you're an experienced rider and feel confident, give it a try!

Riding a motorcycle in Bali offers a unique experience—cruising through rice paddies, feeling the breeze, and soaking in the surrounding beauty is unparalleled. However, don't forget that Bali's traffic conditions are completely different from other places, especially in busy urban areas. Locals and vacationing foreigners often drive by intuition, leading to risky situations. You might also encounter traffic jams or sudden downpours. Be mentally prepared!

## How Much Does It Cost to Rent a Motorcycle?

Motorcycle rental prices in Bali vary depending on the model, age, and location.

**Short-term Rental Costs**

- Scoopy or Vario 110cc: 70,000–110,000 IDR (approx. $6) per day
- Vario 125cc: 100,000–150,000 IDR (approx. $7) per day
- NMax: 100,000–200,000 IDR (approx. $8) per day

**Long-term Rental Costs** If planning an extended stay in Bali, opting for long-term rentals is usually more cost-effective.

- Scoopy or Vario 110cc: 1,100,000 IDR (approx. $70) per month
- Vario 125cc: 1,500,000 IDR (approx. $95) per month
- NMax: 2,000,000 IDR (approx. $125) per month

**Fuel** Street vendors in Bali often sell gasoline in old vodka bottles, priced at around 10,000 IDR (approx. $0.6) per liter.

## Reasons Not to Recommend Riding a Scooter in Bali 🚫🏍️

However, for foreign tourists, riding a scooter in Bali carries significant risks. Here are several reasons why we don't recommend choosing scooters as the primary mode of transportation:

### 1. Heavy Traffic and Complex Rules 🚦

Navigating Bali's traffic environment is challenging, especially in popular areas like Kuta, Jimbaran, and Seminyak, where severe congestion is common. Additionally, Bali follows right-hand driving, which differs from Taiwan or China. For tourists accustomed to left-hand driving, adapting to the local traffic flow can be quite difficult. Factors such as constant vehicle movement, rare traffic lights, and drivers frequently switching lanes during traffic jams add to the higher risks of riding a scooter.

### 2. Unstable Driving by Foreign Tourists 🌍

Bali's roads are filled not only with locals but also many foreign tourists, some of whom may have never ridden scooters in their home countries. Their lack of experience and unstable driving often leads to reckless behavior on the roads, posing significant safety hazards. Even if you're a skilled rider, encountering these unpredictable drivers can still put you at risk.

### 3. International Driver's License and Safety Gear Requirements 📄

Riding a scooter in Bali requires an international driver's license; otherwise, you may be stopped by local police and fined. Additionally, wearing a helmet is mandatory when riding a scooter in Bali. However, many tourists overlook this rule due to unfamiliarity, which is not only unsafe but could also result in fines.

### 4. High Medical Costs 💰

According to the owner of [Mr. Lobster](/blog/2024-08-08-66a3943afd89780001cde8eb/), who operates a restaurant in Bali, he often encounters tourists injured in scooter accidents. Due to limited mobility, they are unable to participate in other activities and end up resting and dining at his restaurant. It’s important to note that medical expenses in Bali are extremely high. If you’re unfortunate enough to get injured, the medical bills could significantly increase your travel budget, putting a damper on your trip. (Personal experience: A friend once fractured their ankle, and a simple cast/bandage cost 12,000,000 IDR, approximately USD$800.)

### 5. Road Conditions and Natural Factors 🌧️

Road conditions in Bali can be unpredictable, especially in areas like Ubud and rural regions, where roads are narrow and muddy. Weather changes can quickly worsen the situation. During the rainy season, sudden downpours can make roads slippery, and Bali’s mountainous terrain adds to the challenge. For tourists unfamiliar with the local landscape, slipping or losing balance is a real risk. Bali’s weather is highly variable, so even on seemingly sunny days, be prepared for sudden rainstorms or strong winds.

### 6. Affordable Alternatives 🚖

Bali offers many affordable transportation options, such as private car hires, ride-hailing scooters, or ride-hailing cars. Compared to renting a scooter, these options are safer and more convenient, making them a more reliable choice for tourists unfamiliar with local traffic.

## Key Safety Tips for Scooter Rentals

If you still insist on using a scooter as your mode of transportation, here are some essential reminders to keep in mind:

- **Always wear a helmet!** No matter how uncomfortable or uncool it may seem, wearing a helmet is a must! Many accidents occur due to overconfidence or driving under the influence.
- **Drive on the left side:** Remember, Bali follows right-hand drive/left-side traffic rules!
- **Avoid hanging your handbag on your body:** Bag snatching incidents are common in the area.
- **Obtain an international driver's license:** While many rental shops don't check, you could be fined if stopped by the police.
- **Purchase health insurance:** Medical costs are high locally, and insurance provides security in case of accidents.
- **Only provide a copy of your passport:** Never hand over your original passport to the rental shop.

## **Inspect the Scooter's Condition**

### Devil's in the Details (Pre-Rental Inspection)

Don't just take the keys and ride off! Before starting the engine or leaving the shop, take out your phone, start recording, and carefully inspect the following points while documenting everything.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_de43e176127f414dfd12cd2155b28955.png)

**1. Exterior Condition (Are You the Scratch King?)**

- **Walk around the scooter:** Examine the front, side panels, rear, and mirrors for any scratches, paint chips, dents, or cracks.
- **Pay special attention:** Check if the screws on the body panels have been tampered with or are loose, as this could indicate a previous accident.
- **Recording tip:** As you record, point out each flaw with your finger. It’s best to have the owner or staff member in the frame giving a thumbs-up to confirm these are pre-existing damages.

**2. Basic Functionality Test**

- **Mirrors:** Are they securely fastened? Is the view clear? Many local scooters have loose mirrors, which wouldn’t pass inspection in Taiwan but are common here. Tighten them or consider switching scooters.
- **Lighting system:** Have a friend stand in front and behind the scooter to test the **headlights (high/low beam), turn signals, and brake lights**. In Bali, driving without lights at night is dangerous, and non-functional turn signals can lead to rear-end collisions.
- **Horn:** Give it a press. In Bali, honking is usually a "reminder" rather than a "provocation," so the horn must work.

**3. Mechanical and Tire Condition (Safety Essentials)**

- **Brakes:** Test both the front and rear brakes. Are they too loose or too tight? Any unusual noises?
- **Tires:** Check the tread depth. If they're worn flat, you'll definitely skid on wet road markings! Insist on a different bike.
- **Dashboard:** After starting the engine, check if the speedometer and fuel gauge are functioning properly.

**4. Bike Compartment and Accessories**

- **Helmet:** Usually, a half-shell helmet is provided. Check if the straps are intact and if the inner lining is clean. If hygiene is a concern, consider bringing a lightweight helmet liner from Taiwan or buying an inexpensive shower cap at a local convenience store to use as a liner.
- **Ignition:** Does it start smoothly? Can the steering lock be engaged?

## Essential Documents (Getting These Wrong Means Facing Extortion)

In Bali, police checks are common. If you can't produce the correct documents, they'll often directly ask for a 'fine' (commonly referred to as coffee money), ranging from IDR 50,000 to IDR 500,000.

**1. International Driving Permit (IDP) — Most Important!**

- **Taiwan Driver's License:** You must first obtain an **International Driving Permit** (available at the Motor Vehicles Office, usually issued within a day for trips under 100 km). Note that the small pink booklet issued by the Motor Vehicles Office must be used alongside your original Taiwan car or motorcycle license.
- **Important:** A mere translation of your Taiwan driver's license **is not valid** here! Make sure you have your 'International Driving Permit' ready.
- **Category A:** Ensure that Category A (motorcycle) is checked on your International Driving Permit.

**2. Passport and Visa**

- Carry a photocopy of your passport (it's advisable to keep the original locked in your hotel safe and carry the photocopy). If the police insist on seeing the original, you can retrieve it later.

**3. Rental Agreement**

- Keep the small receipt from the rental shop with the license plate number and rental period, either behind your phone case or in your wallet for verification if needed.

## Insurance Verification Steps (So You Won't Cry if Something Happens)

Many people think the insurance included with car rentals is all-encompassing, but that's not the case! Bali car rentals typically fall into three categories:

**1. Understand the English on the Contract**

- **Loss Damage Waiver (LDW) / Theft Insurance:** How much would you have to pay if the car is stolen?
- **Collision Damage Waiver (CDW) / Collision Insurance:** Would you have to pay a deductible if the car is damaged in a collision?
- **TPL (Third Party Liability) / Third-Party Liability Insurance:** This is the most important! If you hit someone or damage someone else's property, the insurance company will cover the costs. Make sure this is included in the contract.

**2. What is a 'Deductible'?**

- Typically, rental car insurance has a deductible (Excess/Deposit). For example, if the policy states a deductible of 2 million Indonesian Rupiah, and the repair costs 3 million, you only pay 2 million, and the insurance company covers the remaining 1 million.
- **Ask the Rental Shop:** Always ask, "If I crash, how much do I pay?" If the amount is outrageously high, consider paying extra for full coverage with zero deductible.

**3. Take a Photo of the Insurance Sticker with Your Phone**

- In Bali, legally rented scooters usually have a mandatory insurance sticker on the windshield, as required by the government. **Take a photo of it!** It contains the policy number and validity period. If something happens, this number can help you verify basic coverage at the hospital or police station.

## Conclusion

While renting a scooter is indeed the most economical and convenient way to explore Bali, safety should always be the top priority when traveling abroad. Especially in a foreign land, if an unfortunate accident occurs, not only will you be unfamiliar with the surroundings, but you may also face exorbitant medical costs, making it a lose-lose situation. We recommend considering safer transportation options when exploring this beautiful island, so you can truly enjoy Bali's scenery and culture without worries.
