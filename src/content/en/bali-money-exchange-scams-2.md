The incident happened on the streets of Ubud. The group originally planned to exchange money at "BMC," recommended by their hotel. However, they passed by an exchange booth advertising a rate of "16675," significantly higher than the day's standard rate of around 16325. Tempted by the seemingly great deal, one member decided to exchange money there. Everything seemed normal at first, but when the rest of the group followed suit, the nightmare began...

## **The Magic Trick Revealed: How Our Fellow Travelers' Money Was Stolen!**

This shady exchange booth's technique was incredibly clever, and the group only realized what happened afterward:

- One member who exchanged USD$500 had **2 million** IDR (approximately USD$130) stolen
- Another member who exchanged USD$1000 lost **4 million** IDR (approximately USD$260)

How did they do it? Here's a step-by-step breakdown:

1. The staff counts the money in front of you
2. You personally count and confirm the amount
3. While you're focused on counting, the staff "helpfully" stacks the bills neatly
4. In that brief moment of stacking, the bills are magically skimmed!
5. The entire process takes less than three seconds — it's like a magic trick

## **Bali Money Exchange Safety Guidelines: Protect Your Hard-Earned Money**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_790c4aaf4dab053d3e6bae094881f2f9.jpg)

Based on suggestions from fellow travelers, here are some safety guidelines:

### **Choose Reputable Money Changers**

1. Many recommend "BMC" or "Central Kuta" money changers. While the exchange rate might not be the best, honesty and safety are the top priorities (look for stores with the green **"PVA Authorized Money Changer"** sign).
2. Some people always go to "MPB" for exchanges, where staff might even offer a better rate than advertised (e.g., 16,325 instead of 16,500).
3. This kind of scam isn’t new, so don’t be tempted by attractive rates. Always count all the money yourself, and don’t let the staff touch it again after you’ve counted. Stick to large, reputable chains!

### **Observe the Store Environment**

1. Always choose a physical store with a glass window.
2. Avoid street vendors without a proper storefront or shops in alleys offering suspiciously good rates.
3. Compare several places to understand the normal exchange rate range for the day.
4. Stores with average rates along the way are relatively safer.
5. Make sure it’s "no commission" and there are no hidden fees.

### **Insist on "Eyes on the Money, Hands on the Counter"**

1. No matter how "helpful" the staff tries to be, firmly refuse any assistance in organizing the bills.
2. After counting the money yourself, recount it before putting it in your wallet.
3. Don’t give up your principles out of politeness.

### **How to Handle Suspicious Situations**

1. If something bad happens, remember: keep your cool!
2. Shout loudly to attract attention from passersby
3. In Indonesia, if you make enough noise to gather a crowd, people will naturally help resolve the situation
4. Call the Tourist Police for assistance: `(+62) 361 224 111` or `(+62) 361 759 687`
5. If you get scammed while exchanging money in **Bali**, **you can call the police**, and the Tourist Police take such incidents that damage tourism very seriously. Based on current cases, although these scams (sleight of hand, shortchanging) usually involve small amounts, reporting or threatening to report often has the following effects:

- **Recovering losses:** Many scammers operate on the assumption that tourists won't bother with the hassle. When you call the police or threaten to do so, most illegal money changers will **refund your money to avoid trouble**.
- **Blacklisting:** Bali authorities have been cracking down hard on illegal money changers in recent years. Your report can get the shady establishment blacklisted or even shut down.

6. **Recommended SOP after being scammed**  
   If you notice that money is missing, follow these steps:

- **Stay calm and don't leave the scene:** Once you leave, it becomes difficult to prove that they shortchanged you.
- **Record evidence:** If possible, start recording during the argument, capturing the store's sign and the staff's face.
- **Protest firmly:** Point out directly that the amount is incorrect. If they refuse to make up the difference, call the police immediately and tell the staff: **"I am calling the Tourist Police now."**
