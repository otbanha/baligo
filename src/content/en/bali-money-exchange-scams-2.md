The incident happened on the streets of Ubud. They were originally planning to exchange money at 'BMC,' recommended by their hotel, but along the way, they passed by an exchange booth advertising a rate of '16675,' significantly higher than the normal rate of around 16325 that day. One of the group members thought it was a rare opportunity and decided to exchange there. Everything seemed fine at first, but when other members went in to exchange, the nightmare began...

## **Magic Trick Revealed: This Is How the Scammers Stole the Money!**

The scam was quite sophisticated, and the group members only realized what had happened afterward:

- One member who exchanged $500 lost 2 million IDR (approximately USD$65)
- Another member who exchanged $1000 lost even more, 4 million IDR (approximately USD$130)

How did they do it? Here's how the scam unfolded:

1. The staff counted the money in front of you
2. You then counted it yourself to confirm
3. While you were focused on counting, the staff 'helpfully' stacked the bills neatly
4. In that moment of stacking, the bills were secretly removed!
5. The whole process took less than three seconds, as fast as a magic trick

## **Bali Money Exchange Safety Tips: Protect Your Hard-Earned Cash**

![Bali Money Exchange Safety Tips: Protect Your Hard-Earned Ca](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_790c4aaf4dab053d3e6bae094881f2f9.jpg)

Based on suggestions from fellow travelers, here are some safety guidelines:

### **Choose Reputable Money Changers**

1. Many recommend "BMC" or "Central Kuta" money changers. While the rates may not be the best, trust and safety are most important (look for stores with the **"PVA Authorized Money Changer"** green sign).
2. Some prefer "MPB" for exchanges, where staff might even offer better rates than advertised (e.g., 16325 listed but giving 16500).
3. This isn’t a new issue—don’t chase the best rates. Either count all the money at once or don’t let the staff touch it again after you’ve counted. Stick to big chain stores!

### **Observe the Store Environment**

1. Always choose a physical store with glass windows.
2. Avoid street vendors without proper storefronts or shops in alleys offering suspiciously good rates.
3. Compare a few places to understand the normal rate range for the day.
4. Stores with average rates along the way are relatively safer.
5. Make sure it’s "no commission" and there are no hidden fees.

### **Insist on 'Eyes on the Money, Hands on the Counter'**

1. No matter how "helpful" the staff tries to be in organizing the bills, firmly refuse.
2. After counting yourself, recount before putting the money in your wallet.
3. Don’t give up your insistence out of politeness.

### **How to Handle Situations Like This**

1. If something bad happens, remember: keep your cool!
2. Shout loudly to attract attention from passersby
3. In Indonesia, if you make enough noise to gather a crowd, people will naturally help resolve the situation
4. Contact the Tourist Police for assistance: `(+62) 361 224 111` or `(+62) 361 759 687`
5. If you get scammed while exchanging money in **Bali**, **you can call the police**, and the Tourist Police take such incidents that damage tourism very seriously. Based on current cases, although these scams (sleight of hand, shortchanging) usually involve small amounts, reporting or threatening to report often has the following effects:

- **Recovering losses:** Many scammers operate on the assumption that tourists won't bother making a fuss. When you call the police or threaten to, most illegal money changers will immediately **return the money to avoid trouble**.
- **Blacklisting:** Bali authorities have been cracking down hard on illegal money changers in recent years. Your report can get the shady establishment blacklisted or even shut down.

6. **Recommended SOP after being scammed**
   If you notice missing money, follow these steps:

- **Stay calm and don't leave the scene:** Once you leave, it's hard to prove the other party shortchanged you.
- **Record evidence:** If possible, start recording during the argument, capturing the store sign and the staff's face.
- **Protest firmly:** Directly point out the incorrect amount. If they refuse to make up the difference, call immediately and tell the staff: **"I am calling the Tourist Police now."**
