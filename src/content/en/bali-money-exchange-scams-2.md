The incident happened on the streets of Ubud. They were originally planning to exchange money at 'BMC', recommended by their hotel, but on the way, they passed a money changer advertising a rate of '16675', which was significantly higher than the normal rate of around 16325 that day. One of the group members thought it was a rare opportunity and decided to exchange money there. Everything seemed fine at first, but when the rest of the group went in to exchange, the nightmare began...

## **The Magic Trick Revealed: How Our Friends' Money Was Stolen!**

The scam was quite sophisticated, and the group only realized what had happened after the fact:

- One member who exchanged USD$500 had 2 million IDR (approximately USD$130) stolen
- Another member who exchanged USD$1000 lost 4 million IDR (approximately USD$260)

How did they do it? Here's how the process unfolded:

1. The staff counts the money in front of you
2. You count it yourself to confirm
3. While you're focused on counting, the staff 'helpfully' stacks the money neatly
4. In that moment of stacking, the bills are secretly removed!
5. The whole process takes less than three seconds, as fast as a magic trick

## **Bali Money Exchange Safety Tips: Protect Your Hard-Earned Money**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_790c4aaf4dab053d3e6bae094881f2f9.jpg)

Based on suggestions from fellow travelers, here are some safety guidelines:

### **Choose Reputable Money Changers**

1. Many recommend "BMC" or "Central Kuta" money changers. While the exchange rate might not be the best, trust and safety are paramount (look for shops with the green **"PVA Authorized Money Changer"** sign).
2. Some prefer "MPB" for exchanges, where staff might even offer better rates than advertised (e.g., 16,325 instead of 16,500).
3. This isn’t new—don’t be tempted by overly attractive rates. Always count the money yourself, and don’t let the staff touch it again after you’ve counted. Stick to large, reputable chains!

### **Observe the Shop Environment**

1. Always choose a physical shop with a glass window.
2. Avoid street vendors or small alley shops with suspiciously good rates.
3. Compare a few shops to understand the normal exchange rate range for the day.
4. Shops with average rates along the way are generally safer.
5. Ensure it’s "no commission" and that there are no hidden fees.

### **Insist on "Eyes on the Money, Hands on the Counter"**

1. No matter how "helpful" the staff seems, firmly refuse their assistance in handling your money.
2. After counting yourself, recount before putting the money in your wallet.
3. Don’t give up your insistence out of politeness.

### **How to Handle Issues**

1. If something bad happens, remember: don't lose face!
2. Shout loudly to attract attention from passersby
3. In Indonesia, if you make enough noise to gather a crowd, locals will naturally help resolve the situation
4. Call the Tourist Police for assistance: `(+62) 361 224 111` or `(+62) 361 759 687`
5. If you get scammed while exchanging money in **Bali**, **you can call the police**, and the Tourist Police take such incidents that damage tourism very seriously. Based on current cases, although these scams (sleight of hand, shortchanging) usually involve small amounts, reporting or threatening to report often has the following effects:

- **Recover losses:** Many scammers operate on the mindset that 'tourists won't bother making a fuss.' When you call or threaten to call the police, most illegal money changers will immediately **return the money to avoid trouble** to prevent being shut down or jailed.
- **Blacklist:** Bali authorities have been cracking down hard on illegal money changers in recent years. Your report can get these shady businesses blacklisted or even shut down.

6. **Recommended SOP after being scammed**  
   If you find that money is missing, follow these steps:

- **Stay calm and don't leave the scene:** Once you leave, it becomes difficult to prove that they shortchanged you.
- **Record evidence:** If possible, start recording during the argument, capturing the store sign and the staff's face.
- **Protest firmly:** Directly point out the incorrect amount. If they refuse to make up the difference, call immediately and tell the staff: **"I am calling the Tourist Police now."**
