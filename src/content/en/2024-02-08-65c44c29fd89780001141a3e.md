Bali is one of Indonesia’s most popular tourist destinations, attracting millions of visitors from around the world every year. With the development of tourism, English has gradually become a common language in Bali.

![峇里島有來自各個國家、不同人種/母語的外國人](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_af3cffb9f72b6ef11a499c8c3fa47d3f.jpg)

Bali is home to foreigners from various countries and different ethnicities/languages.

Why has English become a common language in Bali? Here are some reasons:

- Many hotels, restaurants, and shops in Bali cater to foreign tourists, so their staff often speak English.
- The Balinese government is also promoting English education to enhance the island’s international competitiveness.
- Many Balinese believe that learning English can help them secure better job opportunities.

Therefore, when traveling in Bali, even if you don’t speak Indonesian, you can usually communicate in English.

## What to Do If You’re Not Good at English?

For travelers from non-English-speaking countries, visiting Bali might pose some language challenges, but don’t worry! In this article, I’ll share some methods to help you overcome language barriers and make your trip to Bali smoother and more enjoyable.

### Using Translation Apps 🌍 Must-Have Translation Apps for 2026 Travel

### 1. Google Translate: The All-Rounder Essential

This app is practically a staple on everyone's phone. Its strongest feature is the 'instant camera translation'—just point your camera at a sign or menu, and the Chinese translation appears directly on the screen.

- **Why We Recommend It:** Supports the widest range of languages, and with offline maps paired with offline translation packs, you can survive without internet.
- **Must-Use Feature:** Real-time text replacement by pointing your camera—no need to type a single word.

### 2. VoiceTra (Woyitong): Your Japan Travel Buddy

If you're planning a trip to Japan, this app developed by a Japanese government research institute is a must-download. Its voice recognition is extremely accurate and optimized for travel scenarios, producing very natural Japanese translations.

- **Why We Recommend It:** Super simple interface—just one microphone button.
- **Highlight:** Shows 'reverse translation' to confirm if the app misunderstood you, avoiding miscommunication.
- (A little shoutout to VoiceTra—it's really handy! Even translating your speech into Indonesian is quite accurate.)

### 3. Naver Papago: The Go-To for K-Culture Enthusiasts

When traveling to South Korea, Google Translate can sometimes feel a bit stiff. That's where Papago comes to the rescue. Developed by Korea's national search engine NAVER, it handles Korean tones and honorifics flawlessly.

- **Why We Recommend It:** The most localized for Korea, with beautifully formatted photo translations.
- **Thoughtful Touch:** Built-in common travel conversation categories (like shopping, hotels, transportation)—no need to even open your mouth.

### 4. DeepL: The Most Natural AI Translation

If you need to translate lengthy articles or formal conversations (such as discussing business or long-term rental contracts in Bali), DeepL's semantic logic is widely recognized as the most 'human-like,' avoiding awkward machine-like tones.

- **Why We Recommend It:** Extremely high semantic accuracy, producing very fluent sentences.
- **Note:** More suitable for long texts or complex needs, with a professional-oriented interface.

## 💡 Pro Tips: How to Use It More Effectively?

- **Download Offline Packs Before Traveling:** Prevent disconnection in airports, mountainous areas, or places with poor signal.
- **Combine with Voice Input:** When communicating with locals, hold the microphone to speak directly, then play the translated audio for quick understanding.

We hope these tools make your journey smoother!

### Familiarize Yourself with Common Travel Scenarios

Before heading to Bali, familiarizing yourself with common travel scenarios and useful phrases will help you navigate various situations, such as seeking assistance, ordering food, and shopping.

For example:

**Seeking Help:**

1. Excuse me, do you speak English?
2. I'm lost, can you help me?
3. I want to go to XXX, can you tell me how to get there?

**Ordering Food:**

1. I'd like to order a XXX.
2. Can you make it not spicy?

**Shopping:**

1. How much is this?
2. Can you give me a better price?
3. I'd like to purchase this.
