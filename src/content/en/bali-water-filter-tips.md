Recently, someone in our Facebook group [asked](https://www.facebook.com/share/p/1CZKDGoT7N/): '**Do I need to bring my own shower head for Bali? Should I use bottled or drinking water for brushing my teeth and rinsing?**' The original poster noted that online info is all over the place and wanted to hear from those who’ve actually been. The comments section was incredibly active, with experiences ranging across different body types, accommodation types, and tales of both 'getting hit' and 'being fine.'

We’ve compiled over 50 real experiences from travelers who’ve stayed at various hotels and villas in Bali, and found a fascinating pattern: **On the same island, water quality varies wildly from one hotel to another.** Some saw their filter turn alarmingly yellow after three nights in an Ubud villa, while others found their filter barely changed at a five-star hotel. So I decided to organize these insights into a 'Hotel Guide' to help you know what to expect before booking.

## High-End Hotels and Resorts: Usually No Issues, But There Are Exceptions

**Most travelers who stayed at five-star hotels and well-known resorts reported the same thing**: the filter or shower head they brought stayed clean, with little to no discoloration over three to seven days. In popular resort areas like Seminyak and Kuta, most guests used the hotel’s water for showering and brushing without any noticeable discomfort.

One person shared, 'We’ve stayed at several hotels without any problems—used the hotel shower head for bathing and even for brushing and rinsing.' Another said, 'Choose your restaurants carefully; decent hotels generally have fine water. I always boil tap water for tea and coffee.'

**But here’s a key counterintuitive finding**: Star rating ≠ guaranteed water quality. One traveler checked into a five-star hotel in Kuta and found black particles in the bathtub, forcing them to use bottled water for brushing the entire stay. So while some saw their filter yellow at a five-star, others found it spotless at a branded hotel. **The key isn’t necessarily the hotel’s star rating, but whether they properly maintain their water filtration system.**

## Ubud Villas and Homestays: The Area Where Filters Show Their True Colors Most

Villas and guesthouses in the Ubud area are the most likely to turn your filter yellow.

One guest shared a photo saying, 'The filter after three nights in Ubud' — the filter was clearly yellowed with visible sediment. Another traveler said, 'I changed the showerhead at the hotel, but the filter didn't get dirty. The filter got super dirty after two nights at a guesthouse.' This reflects that many guesthouses in Ubud have relatively simple water filtration systems, and some don't even have extra filter devices.

Someone said bluntly, 'It's not that bad, unless you stay at a guesthouse. Hotels are fine.' Another suggested, 'It depends on your own constitution, where you stay, and how much you pay for accommodation.' So when choosing in Ubud, you really need to be more cautious.

## Breakdown by Hotel Area

Based on the experiences of over 50 guests, here's a simple breakdown:

**Areas with relatively stable water quality**

- Well-known resorts and five-star hotels in Seminyak, Nusa Dua, and Kuta
- Boutique hotels and high-end villas in Ubud
- Beachfront hotels in Sanur
- Bottled water is usually provided in hotel rooms

**Areas that require extra caution**

- Guesthouses and small villas in Ubud (especially those without automatic water filtration systems)
- Guesthouses in inland rural areas
- Newly opened hotels with incomplete infrastructure
- Some so-called five-star hotels with aging plumbing

## Do you need to bring your own showerhead?

**Most people staying in hotels say it's unnecessary.** "No need to replace the showerhead—just shower directly, no problem." "I swapped the showerhead at the hotel, but the filter cartridge never got dirty." "I always stay in hotels, and the filter cartridge never changed color."

**But for those staying in guesthouses or the Ubud area, bringing a filter cartridge as insurance brings more peace of mind.** "The water quality in Ubud is indeed a bit dirtier, so you can bring one along—it doesn't take up much weight." "If you're staying in a Ubud guesthouse, bring a filtered showerhead as a safety net."

Some people have a practical approach: "I brought a filtered showerhead mainly because I don't want to develop skin issues or allergies." Others bluntly say, "If you need to bring a showerhead, just skip this place and go to Japan, Europe, or the US instead," but that's clearly a minority view.

**The practical advice is:**

- **Staying at a five-star hotel**: Not necessary to bring one, but it doesn't hurt—peace of mind matters most.
- **Staying in Ubud or a guesthouse**: Bring a filtered showerhead to gauge water quality by how quickly the filter changes color.
- **Staying at a branded hotel**: Mostly unnecessary, but you can bring one as a backup.

## Brushing Teeth and Rinsing: Hotel Water Quality Is Key

**There are three approaches:**

**Bottled water camp:** "I use bottled water for rinsing and brushing." "I brush with bottled water, then just rinse with a bit more bottled water." "I use bottled water to brush my teeth." "There's plenty of water, and you can always ask the hotel for more."

**Tap water camp:** "I just use the tap to brush—no discomfort." "I use regular tap water to brush." "I always stay in hotels and just turn on the tap to brush and rinse." Most people with strong stomachs say that as long as the hotel isn't too low-end, using tap water to rinse is fine.

**The most honest feedback:** One guest shared, "At the same hotel, my friend used bottled water to brush but still got sick from the water, while I used tap water and was fine." "Some people eat street food in India without issues, while others get an upset stomach even in Taiwan." "I lived in India for a year—I had diarrhea at first, but it never happened again."

**A Realistic Take**:

- **First time in Bali, traveling with kids or elderly, or have a sensitive stomach**: Stick to bottled water for rinsing your mouth. It's not worth the risk to save a few bucks.
- **Iron stomach, frequent visitor, or staying at a nice hotel**: Tap water is usually fine.
- **Staying at a Ubud homestay or an unknown hotel**: Use bottled water as much as possible—prevention is better than cure.

## Ice, Drinks, and Street Food—The Real Culprits

**Ice**: Many say they drink ice in restaurant beverages without issue, but others warn, "Avoiding ice in drinks is basic self-protection." One traveler shared a painful experience: "On the last day before heading home, my husband let his guard down and ordered an iced Americano from a roadside stall. After returning to Taiwan, he came down with Bali Belly." Someone who had visited multiple times without problems ended up defeated by a single cup of coffee.

**Street Food**: Widely considered the biggest risk. "Some people eat grilled corn or noodles from street stalls and are fine, but high-risk groups should skip them." "Avoid roadside stalls, including their drinks." "Don't add ice to drinks, and stick to sealed, branded bottled drinks—just don't open the can."

**Restaurant Drinks**: These are relatively safer. Guests share, "We had drinks with ice at restaurants and had no issues at all." "We drank iced beverages at restaurants without a second thought." The difference is that formal restaurants have much better water treatment systems and ice hygiene management than roadside stalls.

**Practical Tips**:

- ✅ Ice in restaurant drinks is usually fine.
- ✅ Canned drinks and bottled water from supermarkets are the safest bet.
- ❌ Avoid cold drinks and ice from street stalls.
- ❌ Skip unknown drink shops.

## The Reality of Hotel Water Filtration Systems

**Many hotels have no idea about their own water quality.** One traveler shared that when they asked about the filtration system at a certain hotel, the owner couldn't even confirm if there were extra filters installed. So a "higher star rating" or "higher price" doesn't necessarily mean their water treatment system is regularly maintained.

Someone made an interesting observation: "It varies by area and water supply system—every place is different. The water comes out slightly yellow."

**Practical Pre-Booking Checklist**:

1. 📧 Message the hotel to ask if they have filtered showerheads or can install one
2. 📧 Ask if the bathtub has an additional water purification system
3. ⭐ Check guest reviews—if multiple people mention "water discomfort" or "need to use bottled water," that's a red flag
4. 📊 Look for photos or experiences shared in reviews about filter discoloration

## Practical On-Site Prevention Checklist

**Before You Go**

- ✅ Bring a filtered showerhead (especially for Ubud or homestays)
- ✅ Bring probiotics (take on an empty stomach every morning—prevention is better than cure)
- ✅ Get stomach medication from a clinic as backup
    - Headache/fever relief, stomach meds, anti-diarrhea, anti-nausea, allergy meds, etc.

**After Arriving at the Hotel**

- ☑ Check the filter color (if available) to see how long it's been since it was changed
- ☑ Test the clarity of the shower water

**Daily Prevention**

- ✅ Brushing teeth and rinsing mouth: Use bottled water or hotel-provied water (especially at homestays)
- ✅ Drinks: Ice cubes at restaurants are fine, but skip street-side stalls
- ✅ Canned drinks are the safest bet
- ✅ Keep stomach meds handy just in case
- ✅ **If you do get sick**: [**Check out this quick self-help guide**](/blog/bali-belly-medicine-guide/)

**The most practical tip**: Some travelers share, 'Before the trip and during breakfast on an empty stomach, take probiotics. On the first day, mix a bottle of Taiwanese mineral water with local mineral water (a way to adapt to the local water during travel), then you can drink local mineral water directly afterward.' This gradual adaptation method gives your digestive system time to adjust.

## An Honest Conclusion

**Choosing a hotel is also choosing water filtration facilities.** Bali isn't a place where a 3-star or 2-star rating automatically means poor water quality, but if you end up at an older hotel where the owner doesn't prioritize maintenance, that's a different story.

One traveler concluded, 'Bali isn't India—there's no need to be overly anxious, but prevention is better than cure. Don't let Bali Belly ruin your entire trip.'

Others bluntly said, 'It's better to be over-prepared' and 'Pack some stomach medicine, then just enjoy Bali.'

**The reality is**: Some people still feel the need to drink mineral water even at a 5-star hotel, while others staying at a villa can drink straight from the pool without issues. But since most people don't visit Bali often, rather than gambling on that slim chance, it's better to prepare a little more—it doesn't take up much weight or cost much, but it gives you peace of mind to enjoy the entire trip. That's the best deal. 🌴
