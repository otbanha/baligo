Recently, in our Facebook group, [someone asked](https://www.facebook.com/share/p/1CZKDGoT7N/): "**Do I need to bring my own showerhead for bathing in Bali? Should I use bottled water for brushing my teeth?**"
The original poster mentioned that online information is polarized and wanted to hear from those who have actually been there. The comment section was very active, with experiences from various body types, accommodation types, and both "affected" and "unaffected" cases.

I’ve summarized nearly 40 comments into three key points to give you peace of mind (or a heads-up).

### 🔹 Conclusion 1: **Showerhead—Most Say ‘No Need,’ but Filters Turn Yellow in Ubud/Homestays**

- **Most comments**: No need to replace the showerhead; it’s fine to use as is.
- **But some experiences**: Bringing a filtered showerhead to **Ubud or homestays**, the filter noticeably turned yellow with impurities. Staying in **hotels**, the filter remained clean.
- **Netizen’s test**: Someone posted a photo of a filter after three nights in Ubud—it was indeed very yellow.
- **Conclusion**: If you’re staying in high-end hotels or resorts, the water quality is usually fine. If staying in **Ubud villas, homestays, or if you have sensitive skin**, bring a filtered showerhead as insurance—it’s lightweight.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775969361098-shower_head.jpg)

### 🔹 Conclusion 2: **Brushing Teeth—Half Bottled Water, Half Tap Water, It Depends on ‘Your Constitution’**

- **Bottled water group**: Rinse with bottled water after brushing for peace of mind.
- **Tap water group**: Brush directly with tap water, no discomfort.
- **Harsh reality**: One netizen shared that **even using bottled water for brushing, their friend still got sick**; another mentioned eating street food in India without issues but getting diarrhea in Taiwan. **It really depends on the person.**
- **Recommendation**:
    - Sensitive stomach, first-time traveler, traveling with elderly or children → **Stick to bottled water for brushing**.
    - Strong stomach, frequent traveler, staying in good hotels → Most say it’s fine.

### 🔹 Conclusion 3: **Ice, Street Food, Restaurant Drinks—High-Risk Items**

- **Ice**: Many say they’ve had restaurant ice without issues, but others advise: "Avoid ice in drinks" as a basic precaution.
- **Street food**: Universally considered the biggest risk. Someone fell ill from a roadside iced coffee, with symptoms appearing only after returning home. Grilled corn and noodle stalls were fine for some, but high-risk groups should skip.
- **Five-star hotel water**: One netizen shared that the bathtub water in a five-star hotel had black particles, prompting them to use bottled water for brushing throughout their stay. **Star rating doesn’t guarantee water filtration quality**.

### 🧳 Essential Prevention Kit (Crowdsourced Edition)

- ✅ **Before & During Trip**: Take **probiotics** on an empty stomach every morning
- ✅ **Brushing Teeth**: Stick to bottled water for safety
- ✅ **Showering**: Bring a filtered showerhead (especially if staying in Ubud/guesthouses)
- ✅ **Drinks**: Ice in restaurants is fine, skip it at roadside stalls; canned drinks are safest
- ✅ **Stomach Medicine**: Bring from home (e.g., Zhang Guozhou Stomach Powder, anti-diarrheal meds)
- ✅ **If It Happens**: [**Check this quick self-help guide**](/blog/bali-belly-medicine-guide/)

### ✍️ My Takeaway

**There’s no definitive answer to Bali’s water quality—it’s all about what your stomach can handle.**
If you’re prone to worry or traveling with family, **bring an extra filter, use bottled water for brushing, and avoid street food** for peace of mind.
If you have an iron stomach and a carefree attitude, relax and enjoy—most people don’t have issues.
**The key is: pack stomach meds, then savor Bali to the fullest!** 🌴
