Recently, someone asked in our Facebook group: 'Do I need to bring my own showerhead for bathing in Bali? Should I use bottled water for brushing my teeth?' The original poster mentioned that online information is polarized and wanted to hear from those who have actually been there. The comment section was very active, with various experiences shared by people with different constitutions and types of accommodations, ranging from 'got sick' to 'had no issues.'

I've summarized nearly 40 comments into three key points to give you peace of mind (or a heads-up).

### 🔹 Conclusion 1: **Showerhead—Most Say 'No Need,' but Filters Turn Yellow in Ubud/Homestays**

- **Majority of comments**: No need to replace the showerhead; it's fine to use as is.
- **However, some experiences**: Bringing a filtered showerhead to **Ubud or homestays**, the filter noticeably turned yellow with impurities. Staying at **hotels**, the filter remained clean.
- **User test**: Someone posted a photo of a filter after three nights in Ubud, and it was indeed very yellow.
- **Conclusion**: If you're staying at high-end hotels or resorts, the water quality is usually fine. If you're staying in **Ubud villas, homestays, or have sensitive skin**, bringing a filtered showerhead as a precaution doesn't add much weight.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775969361098-shower_head.jpg)

### 🔹 Conclusion 2: **Brushing Teeth—Half Bottled Water, Half Tap Water, It Depends on 'Constitution'**

- **Bottled water camp**: Rinse with bottled water after brushing for peace of mind.
- **Tap water camp**: Brush directly with tap water, no discomfort.
- **Harsh reality**: A user shared that **even at the same hotel, a friend who used bottled water still got sick**; another mentioned eating street food in India without issues but getting diarrhea in Taiwan. **It really varies by person.**
- **Advice**:
    - Sensitive stomach, first-time visitors, traveling with elderly or children → **Stick to bottled water for brushing**.
    - Strong stomach, frequent visitors, staying at good hotels → Most say it's fine.

### 🔹 Conclusion 3: **Ice, Street Food, Restaurant Drinks—High-Risk Items**

- **Ice**: Many say they’ve had no issues with restaurant ice, but others warn: 'Avoid ice in drinks' as a basic precaution.
- **Street food**: Universally considered the biggest gamble. Someone fell ill from a roadside iced coffee that didn’t hit until returning home. Grilled corn and noodle stalls were fine for some, but high-risk groups should skip.
- **Five-star hotel water**: A user shared that a five-star hotel’s bathtub water had black particles, prompting them to use bottled water for brushing throughout their stay. **Star rating doesn’t guarantee water filtration quality**.

### 🧳 Essential Prevention Kit (Crowdsourced Edition)

- ✅ **Before & During Trip**: Take **probiotics** on an empty stomach every morning
- ✅ **Brushing Teeth**: Bottled water is safest
- ✅ **Showering**: Bring a filtered showerhead (especially for Ubud/stay in guesthouses)
- ✅ **Drinks**: Ice in restaurants is fine, skip roadside stalls; canned drinks are safest
- ✅ **Stomach Medicine**: Bring from Taiwan (e.g., Zhang Guozhou Stomach Powder, anti-diarrheal meds)
- ✅ **If You Get Sick**: [**Check Quick Self-Help Tips Here**](/blog/2026-01-02-69523427fd89780001caa652/)

### ✍️ My Takeaway

**There’s no definitive answer to Bali’s water quality—only your stomach can decide.**
If you’re prone to anxiety or traveling with family, **bring an extra filter, use bottled water for brushing, and avoid street food** for peace of mind.
If you have an iron stomach and are easygoing, relax and enjoy—most people don’t have issues.
**The key is: Pack stomach meds, then fully embrace Bali!** 🌴
