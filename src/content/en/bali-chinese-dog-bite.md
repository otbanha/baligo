To be honest, every time I visit Bali, the dogs on the streets make me feel both fond and wary. A recent incident in Bangli serves as a stark reminder that you really shouldn't get too close to stray dogs on the streets.

## Here's What Happened

Around 4 PM on July 13, a 30-year-old Chinese tourist named Li Qiujun was bitten on her right thigh by a white dog after shopping at a supermarket in Kelurahan Kawan, Bangli, just as she was about to meet her guide to return to Ubud. She had simply finished shopping at the supermarket across from Bangli Hospital and was waiting for a ride back to Ubud, never expecting such an ordeal.

What's even more alarming is that before biting her, the same dog had already attacked a local resident named Ni Ketut Suciasih near her home north of the Bangli Detention Center. In other words, this dog bit two people in a single day.

## Treatment Hit a Snag After the Bite

Li Qiujun sought medical attention about three hours after being bitten. Bangli Hospital performed emergency wound debridement and applied a pressure bandage. However, the problem was that the hospital itself did not have the rabies vaccine (VAR) in stock and could only advise her to go to a clinic or health center on her own to get vaccinated. She was admitted around 7 PM and discharged less than half an hour later, meaning the hospital could only provide basic wound care.

Fortunately, Li Qiujun later received the vaccine at a clinic in Ubud, while the other victim, Suciasih, also got vaccinated at a local health center.

## What Happened to That Dog

Local livestock and animal health authorities reported that the aggressive dog had already been killed by residents. Its brain sample has been sent for testing, with results expected in two weeks—only then can it be confirmed whether the two victims actually contracted rabies. What's puzzling is that officials said the dog showed no abnormalities the day before it bit someone; it was only the following day that it suddenly changed temperament and began attacking.

## A heads-up for those heading to Bali

To be honest, Bali has quite a large number of stray dogs, especially in rural areas or around markets. Once rabies develops, it's almost untreatable, and the incubation period can vary widely, so it's not something to take lightly. If you're traveling to Bali, it's best to keep your distance and avoid petting dogs on the street. If you do get bitten or scratched, immediately rinse the wound and seek medical attention as soon as possible. Be proactive in asking where you can get a rabies vaccine—because, as this incident shows, not every hospital has it in stock. Knowing in advance which nearby clinic or health center has the vaccine can save you a lot of time in a critical moment.
