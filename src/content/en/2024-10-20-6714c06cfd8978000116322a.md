## Trip Planning Tips

### 1. Overpacking Your Itinerary

**Mistake**: Many tourists underestimate the size of Bali and try to cover all the attractions in a short time, resulting in a rushed itinerary that doesn't allow them to fully enjoy each location.

Although Bali is an island, it is actually quite large and diverse, offering unique charms from the beaches in the south to the mountains in the north. When planning your itinerary, choose areas based on your stay duration:

- **One week**: Choose 1-2 main accommodation bases to avoid too much moving around.
- **Two weeks**: Can cover 3-4 accommodation bases, with moderate travel time.
- **Three weeks or more**: Up to 5-6 locations, but be careful not to make the itinerary too heavy.

Try to reduce frequent accommodation changes to avoid wasting time packing and moving, which can leave you exhausted.

### 3. Scheduling Island Travel at the End

**Mistake**: Many tourists schedule trips to the Nusa Islands (Nusa Lembongan, Nusa Ceningan, Nusa Penida) or the Gili Islands towards the end of their journey, but bad weather leading to boat cancellations can disrupt your return plans.

To avoid delays in your return due to weather-related ferry cancellations, it's advisable to schedule these island trips in the middle or early part of your journey. This way, even if delays occur, they won't affect your overall travel plans.

### 6. Risks of Renting a Car/Motorcycle for Self-Driving

**Mistake**: Renting a scooter is convenient and cost-effective.

Bali’s roads are narrow and winding, primarily designed for motorcycles. For tourists unfamiliar with local traffic, driving a car can be risky. ### 10. Monkey Safety Guidelines

https://youtube.com/shorts/tpPWsvsH-Eo

>   **Mistake**: Underestimating the potential danger of monkeys and carrying valuables or food while interacting with them.

In certain areas of Bali, such as Uluwatu (Uluwatu Temple) and the Monkey Forest in Ubud, monkeys are common "residents." While they may appear cute, they can be aggressive, especially when interested in valuables.

- **Avoid carrying valuables or food**: Monkeys are particularly interested in food and shiny objects, so avoid bringing these items with you.
- **Do not make eye contact**: Making eye contact with monkeys can be dangerous, as some may perceive it as a challenge.
- **Protect glasses and phones**: Monkeys often snatch items like glasses and phones from tourists, so keep them secure in monkey-populated areas.
- **Maintain distance**: While monkeys may seem friendly, remember they are wild animals and keep a safe distance.

## Cultural Etiquette

### 11. Respect Local Culture

>   **Mistake**: Ignoring local religious and cultural etiquette, leading to disrespectful behavior.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_2051bf25b8c56908609a59e3c8151f2a.jpg)

Bali is a cultural hub of Hinduism, where locals place great importance on religious rituals and traditions. Respecting their customs is crucial for visitors. Here are some key cultural etiquettes:

- **Temple Dress Code**: When entering temples, dress modestly and wear a sarong (temples usually provide these for free).
- **Avoid Stepping on Offerings**: On the streets of Bali, you'll often see small basket-like offerings. These are daily offerings made by locals, so be careful not to step on them.

Further Reading: [Understanding Bali's Offering Rituals: Canang Sari](/blog/2024-07-16-66964aa7fd89780001c7051c/)

- **Taboo Behaviors**: Do not touch someone's head, as it is considered disrespectful in local culture. Additionally, handing items with your left hand is seen as impolite.
- **Remove Shoes Before Entering**: When entering a local's home or certain shops, always remove your shoes as a sign of respect.
- **Learn Basic Greetings**: Learning a few simple phrases in the local language, such as "thank you," can help you build rapport with locals.
  - Indonesian "Thank you": **Terima kasih**
  - Balinese "Thank you": **Suksma**

### 12. Ethical Animal Tourism

>   **Mistake**: Participating in unethical tourist activities that harm animals.

Bali offers many animal-related tourist activities, but many involve inhumane treatment of animals, sparking significant controversy. To protect wildlife, it's recommended to avoid the following activities:

- **Zoos and Marine Parks**: Many animals in zoos and marine parks are kept in confined spaces and cannot move freely.
- **Elephant Parks**: Some parks offer elephant rides, but these elephants are often mistreated and in poor health. Bali has banned elephant riding across the island starting in 2026.
- **Wild Animal Performances**: Wild animals in performances are usually subjected to cruel training to perform unnatural behaviors.

Support activities that respect animals' natural habitats, such as:

- **Lovina Dolphin Watching**: The dolphins here swim freely in open waters, not confined to aquariums.
- **Turtle Watching in Natural Habitats**: Join organizations or activities that support wildlife conservation to observe wild turtles without interfering with their natural lives.


## **Practical Tips**

When traveling in Bali, being well-prepared and understanding local conditions can make your trip more enjoyable and help avoid potential issues. Here are some practical tips for traveling in Bali to help you have a smoother vacation.

### 13. Currency Exchange

>   **Mistake**: Exchanging cash before departure or at the airport.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_e33228060f6e7d878900e7d041abfc80.png)
  
- **Exchange Money After Arrival**: Refer to "[Bali Currency Exchange Guide](/blog/2024-01-28-65b5c7e2fd89780001e96fac/)" to learn how and where to exchange money.
- **Avoid Carrying Large Amounts of Cash**: Carrying too much cash increases risk. Using a debit card or electronic payment is safer.

### 14. Bargaining Tips

>   **Mistake**: Accepting the first price offered at markets or street vendors without understanding local bargaining culture.

- **Negotiable Prices for Non-Fixed Items**: In many open-air markets and small stalls in Bali, prices are not fixed, and you can negotiate appropriately.
- **Avoid Accepting the First Offer**: Sellers often quote higher prices. Try to negotiate a lower price and purchase only when both parties are satisfied.
- **Support the Local Economy**: While bargaining is common, avoid driving prices too low. Respect local vendors to support their livelihoods.

### 15. Weather Awareness

>   **Mistake**: Ignoring Bali's weather changes and not checking the weather forecast in advance.

- **Follow BMKG (Bali Meteorology, Climatology, and Geophysical Agency)** on their [official Instagram account](https://www.instagram.com/infobmkg/): They post daily English travel weather forecasts.
- **Use the Accuweather App**: Often provides more accurate predictions than Google.
- **Check weather on Windy.com**: Ideal for viewing Bali's weather forecasts, especially for wave and wind speed information.
- **Morning activities are preferable**: Bali's weather is usually more stable in the morning, making it a great time for outdoor activities.
- **Afternoons are prone to rain**: Especially during the rainy season, short showers often occur in the afternoon, so it's recommended to schedule indoor activities or rest during this time.

### 16. Packing Tips

>   **Mistake**: Packing too many unnecessary clothes or overlooking the local weather characteristics.

Based on Bali's climate and activity needs, here are the essentials:

- **Lightweight and breathable clothing**: Due to Bali's hot and humid climate, it's advisable to bring thin, breathable outfits to stay comfortable in the heat.
- **Swimwear**: Whether you plan to stay near the beach or not, swimwear is essential, as Bali boasts countless beautiful beaches and pools to enjoy.
- **Simple flats or sandals**: Most activities in Bali, such as visiting markets, strolling on the beach, or exploring temples, can be comfortably done in flats or sandals.
- **Pack a light jacket for mountainous areas**: If you plan to visit Ubud or Munduk, evenings can be cooler, so bringing a light jacket will keep you comfortable.

### 17. Motorcycle Safety

>   **Mistake**: Neglecting motorcycle safety measures.

- **Always wear a helmet**: Whether you're driving or riding a motorcycle, a helmet is mandatory—it's not only the law but also a crucial safety measure.
- **Request a spare helmet when booking rides**: When using Gojek or Grab, if you're riding a motorcycle, make sure to ask the driver for a helmet.
- **Follow traffic rules**: Bali's road conditions and traffic rules may differ from your home country, so drive cautiously and adhere to local regulations.

### 18. Ignoring Local Food Safety

>   **Mistake**: Ignoring food hygiene and eating at unclean street stalls or places.

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

- **Choose clean restaurants or well-reviewed street stalls**: Bali offers many delicious local foods and restaurants, but not all places meet hygiene standards. Opt for restaurants with good cleanliness or recommended vendors.
- **Be cautious with drinking water**: Avoid drinking tap water directly; choose bottled water or ensure the restaurant provides safe drinking water.
- **Avoid raw or undercooked food**: Especially in remote areas or unfamiliar restaurants, raw or undercooked food may pose a risk of stomach discomfort. Prioritize cooked meals.
- **Carry stomach medicine**: If you have a sensitive stomach, bring common medications like anti-diarrheal pills or probiotics for emergencies.
