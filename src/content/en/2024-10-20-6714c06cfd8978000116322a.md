## Itinerary Planning

### 1. Overpacking Your Schedule

**Mistake**: Many tourists underestimate the size of Bali and try to visit all the attractions in a short time, resulting in a rushed itinerary that doesn't allow them to fully enjoy each location.

Although Bali is an island, it's actually quite large and diverse, offering everything from southern beaches to northern mountains, each with its unique charm. When planning your itinerary, consider choosing suitable areas based on your stay duration:

- **One week**: Select 1-2 main accommodation bases to avoid excessive moving around.
- **Two weeks**: Cover 3-4 accommodation bases, with moderate travel time between them.
- **Three weeks or more**: Arrange up to 5-6 locations, but be mindful not to overload your schedule.

Try to minimize frequent changes in accommodation to avoid wasting time packing and moving, which can leave you exhausted.

### 3. Scheduling Island Trips at the End

> **Mistake**: Many travelers plan their trips to the Nusa Islands (Nusa Lembongan, Nusa Ceningan, Nusa Penida) or the Gili Islands at the end of their journey. However, if bad weather causes ferry cancellations, it could disrupt your return plans.

To avoid delays in your return due to weather-related ferry cancellations, it's advisable to schedule these island trips in the middle or early part of your journey. This way, even if delays occur, they won't affect your overall travel plans.

### 7. Bank Card Security

> **Mistake**: Using credit cards for large purchases without considering the potential risk of fraud.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0648b8fa6386a0758be5f8b1fa666c66.jpg)

Some areas in Bali may pose a risk of credit card fraud, so it's recommended to protect your funds using the following methods:

- **Use a debit card with limited funds**: Keep the balance on your debit card below USD$300 to minimize losses in case of fraud.
- **Online bank transfers**: When making large payments or purchases, transfer additional funds via online banking to prevent having too much money on the card.
- **Use e-payments**: Whenever possible, use e-wallets like Gojek to reduce the use of physical cards.

### 8. Mosquito Protection

> **Mistake**: Ignoring the threat of mosquitoes

Dengue fever is not uncommon in Bali, so mosquito protection is crucial. Here are some essential items and tips:

- **Insect repellent**: Carry insect repellent with you and use it frequently, especially during outdoor activities.
- **Wear long sleeves and pants**: Mosquitoes are most active from dusk to night, so it's advisable to wear protective clothing.
- **Secure doors and windows**: Ensure your accommodation's doors and windows are tightly closed, or use mosquito nets to prevent mosquitoes from entering.
- **Be aware of dengue risk**: If you feel unwell, such as experiencing fever or muscle pain, seek medical attention immediately to check for dengue fever.

### 10. Monkey Safety Guidelines

https://youtube.com/shorts/tpPWsvsH-Eo

> **Mistake**: Underestimating the potential danger of monkeys and carrying valuables or food while interacting with them.

In certain areas of Bali, such as Uluwatu (Uluwatu Temple) and the Monkey Forest in Ubud, monkeys are common "residents." While they may appear cute, they can be aggressive, especially when interested in valuables.

- **Do not carry valuables or food**: Monkeys are particularly interested in food and shiny objects, so avoid carrying food or valuables with you.
- **Avoid eye contact with monkeys**: Making eye contact with monkeys can be extremely dangerous, as some may perceive it as a challenge.
- **Protect glasses and phones**: Monkeys often snatch items like glasses and phones from tourists, so it's advisable to keep them secure in monkey areas.
- **Maintain distance**: While monkeys are often seen as friendly, remember they are wild animals and it's important to keep a safe distance.

## Cultural Etiquette

### 11. Respect Local Culture

>   **Mistake**: Ignoring local religious and cultural etiquette, leading to disrespectful behavior.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_2051bf25b8c56908609a59e3c8151f2a.jpg)

Bali is a cultural hub of Hinduism, and locals place great importance on religious ceremonies and traditions. Respecting their customs is crucial for visitors. Here are some key cultural etiquettes:

- **Temple dress code**: When entering temples, dress modestly and wear a sarong (usually provided for free by the temple).
- **Avoid stepping on offerings**: On the streets of Bali, you'll often see small basket-like offerings. These are daily offerings made by locals, so be careful not to step on them.

Further reading: [Understanding Bali's Offering Rituals: Canang Sari](/blog/2024-07-16-66964aa7fd89780001c7051c/)

- **Taboo behaviors**: Do not touch someone's head, as it is considered disrespectful in local culture. Additionally, handing items with your left hand is also seen as impolite.
- **Remove shoes indoors**: When entering locals' homes or certain shops, it's respectful to remove your shoes first.
- **Learn basic greetings**: Learning a few simple phrases in the local language, such as "thank you," can help you build rapport with locals.
- - Indonesian "Thank you": **Terima kasih**
    - Balinese "Thank you": **Suksma**

### 12. Ethical Animal Tourism

>   **Mistake**: Participating in unethical animal tourism activities that harm animals.

Bali offers many animal-related tourism activities, but a significant number of them involve inhumane treatment of animals, sparking considerable controversy. To protect wildlife, it's advisable to avoid the following tourist activities:

- **Zoos and Marine Parks**: Many animals in zoos and marine parks are confined to small spaces, unable to move freely.
- **Elephant Parks**: Some parks offer elephant rides, but these elephants are often mistreated and in poor health. Starting from 2026, Bali has banned elephant riding across the island.
- **Wildlife Performances**: Wild animals in performances are often subjected to cruel training to perform unnatural behaviors.

Support activities that respect animals' natural habitats, such as:

- **Lovina Dolphin Watching**: Dolphins here are free in open waters, not confined to aquariums.
- **Sea Turtle Watching in Natural Habitats**: Participate in organizations or activities that support wildlife conservation, observing sea turtles in their natural environment without interfering.

### 14. Bargaining Tips

> **Mistake**: Accepting the first price offered at markets or street vendors without understanding local bargaining culture.

- **Negotiable Prices**: In many open-air markets and small stalls in Bali, prices are not fixed, and you can negotiate appropriately.
- **Avoid Accepting the First Offer**: Sellers often quote higher prices initially. Try to lower the price reasonably and purchase only when both parties are satisfied.
- **Support the Local Economy**: While bargaining is a common practice, avoid excessive haggling. Respect local vendors to support their livelihoods.

### 15. Weather Awareness

> **Mistake**: Ignoring Bali's weather changes and failing to check the forecast in advance.

- **Follow BMKG (Bali Meteorology, Climatology, and Geophysical Agency)** on their [official Instagram account](https://www.instagram.com/infobmkg/): They post daily English weather forecasts for travelers.
- **Use the Accuweather App**: Often provides more accurate predictions than Google.
- **Check Weather on Windy.com**: Ideal for viewing Bali's weather forecasts, especially wave and wind speed information.
- **Morning Activities Are Best**: Bali's weather is usually more stable in the morning, making it a great time for outdoor activities.
- **Afternoon Rain is Common**: Especially during the rainy season, brief showers often occur in the afternoon, so plan indoor activities or rest for this time.

### 16. Packing Tips

> **Mistake**: Bringing too many unnecessary clothes or ignoring the local weather characteristics.

Based on Bali's climate and activity needs, here are essential items to pack:

- **Lightweight and Breathable Clothing**: Due to Bali's hot and humid climate, pack light and airy outfits to stay comfortable in the heat.
- **Swimwear**: Whether you plan to stay near the beach or not, swimwear is a must. Bali has countless beautiful beaches and pools to enjoy.
- **Simple Flat Shoes or Sandals**: Most activities in Bali, like exploring markets, beach walks, or visiting temples, can be comfortably done in flat shoes or sandals.
- **Light Jacket for Mountain Areas**: If you plan to visit mountainous regions like Ubud or Munduk, evenings can be cooler. A light jacket will keep you comfortable.

### 17. Motorcycle Safety

https://youtu.be/lIV-T2jYhVk

> **Mistake**: Ignoring motorcycle safety measures.

- **Always wear a helmet**: Whether you're driving or riding as a passenger, a helmet is essential. This is not only a legal requirement but also a crucial safety measure.
- **Request a spare helmet when booking a ride**: When using ride-hailing services like Gojek or Grab for motorcycles, always ask the driver for a helmet.
- **Follow traffic rules**: The road conditions and traffic regulations in Bali may differ from those in your home country. Drive cautiously and adhere to local traffic laws.

### 18. Ignoring Local Food Safety

> **Mistake**: Not paying attention to food hygiene, casually eating at street stalls or unclean places.

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

- **Choose clean restaurants or well-reviewed street vendors**: Bali offers many delicious local snacks and restaurants, but not all places meet hygiene standards. It's recommended to select restaurants with good cleanliness or vendors that come highly recommended.
- **Be cautious with drinking water**: Never drink tap water directly. Opt for bottled water or ensure that the restaurant provides safe drinking water.
- **Avoid raw or undercooked food**: Especially in remote areas or unfamiliar restaurants, raw and undercooked food may pose a risk of stomach discomfort. It's advisable to prioritize cooked meals.
- **Carry stomach medicine**: If you have a sensitive stomach, carry common stomach medications such as anti-diarrheal pills or probiotics for emergencies.
