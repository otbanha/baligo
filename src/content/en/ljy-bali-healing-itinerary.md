## Day 1｜From Taoyuan to Bali, first stop: straight to the beach for sunset 🌅

This time, I departed from Taoyuan Airport on China Airlines flight CI771 in economy class. Originally booked in standard economy, I later paid an extra USD$84 to upgrade to premium economy.

***

### 🛂 Immigration was smoother than expected

This time, I handled almost everything online in advance, so the immigration process was surprisingly fast.

#### 1️⃣ Electronic Visa (B1)

I applied for the electronic visa before departure, which cost around USD$32. After landing, I went straight to the e-gate, and the actual clearance took only about 1 minute—no waiting in line at all.

If you're visiting Bali during peak season, I really recommend getting the e-visa in advance. Queuing for a VOA on-site can sometimes make you question your life choices 😆

#### 2️⃣ Electronic Customs Declaration

I also recommend filling this out in advance. You can submit it online up to three days before departure. Just save the QR code screenshot on your phone, and scan it on-site when you arrive—done.

#### 3️⃣ Tourist Levy

I also paid the tourist levy online in advance, around USD$10.
Many processes in Bali are gradually going digital now, and handling things early really saves a lot of time.

## Day 2｜A day of slowing down in Sanur: morning beach walk, Beach Club, charity visit, and more 🌊

The rhythm of the second day was exactly my ideal Bali mode. No crammed-in attractions, no rushing from morning to night—just a seamless flow between the beach, coffee bars, community spaces, and Sanur's laid-back pace.

***

### 🌞 Seminyak Beach at dawn, even more enchanting than during the day

Around 6 a.m., I walked straight from the back door of the hotel onto the sand.

This is exactly why I love staying at beachfront hotels. No need to plan a special itinerary—just waking up in the morning and stepping onto the sand for a stroll is already worth it.

The morning Seminyak Beach is a completely different world from the night. No loud music, no crowds of tourists—just the sound of waves, morning joggers, and a group of foreigners already heading into the water to surf.

And honestly, the "surfer hottie density" in Seminyak in the morning is seriously high 🤣. Many people hit the water before dawn, and the beach vibe is super chill. Watching everyone walk into the sea with their surfboards gives you that feeling: 'This is the real Bali daily life.'

***

### 🍸 Atlas Beach Club｜The So-Called World's Largest Beach Club

During the day, I visited Atlas Beach Club, which has been a hot topic lately.

![Atlas Beach Club｜傳說中的全球最大 Beach Club](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-06/1781014745875-atlas2.jpg)

Photo provided by Lai Jiaying at Atlas Beach Club

It's always been known as the "world's largest beach club," and seeing it in person, the scale is truly overwhelming. The entire space is massive—from the pool, DJ area, restaurant, to the lounge zone—it almost feels like a small resort.

This time, thanks to a collaboration with TUL BLUE BERAWA, guests staying in suites or above get free entry, plus front-row sofa seating and a welcome drink. The front-row area makes a huge difference—you face the ocean directly, with waves, music, people, and the sunset all around, creating a powerful atmosphere.

That said, I personally feel Atlas leans more toward a "lively party-style beach club." If you love music, energy, and a strong photo-worthy vibe, it's perfect. But if you prefer a quiet, relaxing spot, you might enjoy other beach clubs more.

### 💚 Bali Life Foundation｜The most profound stop of this trip

The most memorable part of the afternoon wasn't a tourist spot, but a visit to the Bali Life Foundation.

https://youtube.com/shorts/ljVgfCUTOyg

Provided by Lai Jiaying, Bali Life Foundation

The foundation opens for预约 visits every Friday from 4 to 7 PM. The campus is actually quite small, currently housing about 50 children.

When we arrived, we were about to unload the rice and cooking oil we brought for donation. A few young boys immediately came over to help on their own.

What shocked me the most was that those kids, probably only five or six years old, each carried a 10-kilogram bag of rice all by themselves.

That scene was a bit jarring. It suddenly hits you that some children grow up in environments vastly different from the world we're used to.

But at the same time, they weren't gloomy. Instead, they kept laughing, chatting, and even took the initiative to welcome everyone.

![Bali Life Foundation｜這趟旅程最深刻的一站](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-06/1781014986137-bali_life_foundation2.jpg)

Provided by Lai Jiaying

The foundation has dinner together at 6 PM, and visitors are welcome to stay and join. More than just a 'tour,' it felt like briefly stepping into their lives.

They also run a kindergarten offering half-day to full-day childcare. Many foreign parents who come to Bali for surfing or work send their kids here, and this income helps support the foundation's operations.

I personally really enjoy this model. It’s not entirely reliant on donations, but instead focuses on building a cycle that can sustain itself long-term.

### 🌅 Morning Stroll on Sanur Beach｜The Gentle Sea Breeze at Dawn

I headed straight to Sanur Beach for a morning walk.

The sea here feels completely different from Bali’s west coast. The waves are much calmer, and in the early morning, the entire surface looks almost like a mirror.

There’s no music blasting from beach clubs, no crowds. Just people walking, cycling, or sitting by the shore, lost in thought.

I love the morning air in Sanur. The sea breeze isn’t strong—it’s soft and soothing, making you slow down without even realizing it.

Sometimes the most healing moments in travel aren’t about how many sights you see, but about doing nothing at all—just sitting by the sea, staring at the horizon.

And when you visit, you'll find the overall atmosphere is warm and pressure-free. It feels more like a simple act of support.

## Day 4｜Riding a scooter through mountain roads, from Tirta Empul Temple all the way to Ubud and Sanur 🛵

Day four kicked off in scooter mode. I set off from Sanur at 7 AM sharp, heading straight into central Bali.

Honestly, riding a scooter in Bali gives you a unique sense of freedom. Along the way, you pass through small villages, rice terraces, temples, and mountain roads. Sometimes, just stopping at a red light and seeing roadside offerings, breakfast stalls, or students riding to school makes you think, 'Ah, this is the real Bali everyday life.'

But a heads-up: if you're not used to riding a scooter or aren't familiar with Southeast Asian traffic rhythms, hiring a driver will make things much easier 😂

### 🙏 Tirta Empul Temple｜Coming early in the morning makes a huge difference

The first stop was straight to Tirta Empul Temple.

![聖泉寺｜一大早來真的差很多](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-06/1781015518073-tirta_empul_3.jpg)

Tirta Empul Temple — Photo by Lai Jiaying

We arrived early, so the crowds were still manageable. I had seen many online photos showing long queues, but visiting in the morning makes a huge difference — both for photos and for the purification ritual experience.

The temple is most famous for its holy water purification. Many locals and tourists line up in order to cleanse themselves under the different spouts.

The atmosphere on-site is actually quite serene. Even with a fair number of tourists, you can still feel a very peaceful energy.

But I have to give you a serious heads-up:

⭐️ No hair dryers. ⭐️ No hair dryers. ⭐️ No hair dryers.

Your hair will have to air dry naturally after getting wet 😅 Luckily, I brought my own towel — otherwise, riding a scooter with wet hair would have been a nightmare.

Continuing north on the bike, I arrived at Penglipuran Village.

Penglipuran Village — Photo by Lai Jiaying

This village has long been known as one of the cleanest villages in the world. The entire environment is impeccably maintained, and it preserves many traditional Balinese architectural styles.

Unlike typical tourist attractions, this is still a living village where real people reside. Many homes have small shops out front selling drinks, snacks, and little souvenirs, giving it a wonderfully down-to-earth feel.

I really enjoyed the pace here. There's no need to do anything special—just walk slowly and take it all in.

A few shops also sell local snacks, making them perfect spots to sit and rest if you're tired from riding.

### 💦 Tegenungan Waterfall: Thrilling Down, Exhausting Up 🤣

After eating, we headed southward and made a detour to Tegenungan Waterfall along the way.

This waterfall is one of the more accessible ones in Bali. You don't need to hike for ages through mountain trails, but you still have to descend all the way into the valley.

As you approach the waterfall, the mist hitting you is truly breathtaking. The roar of the falls is so loud that you can barely hear anyone standing nearby.

What's special about Bali's waterfalls is that many aren't the 'thin and tall' type — instead, they have a thick, powerful flow.

The only problem is...

The climb up is really tiring 😆
Especially after eating dirty duck rice and then climbing stairs—it feels like life is testing you.

### 🧘‍♀️ Taman Beji Griya Waterfall: The Five-Stage Holy Water Purification Ceremony

Our first stop is the increasingly popular purification sanctuary—Taman Beji Griya Waterfall.

![‍ Taman Beji Griya Waterfall｜五階段聖水淨化儀式](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-06/1781016977848-beji.jpg)

Taman Beji Griya Waterfall (Photo by Lai Jiaying)

It's best known for the 'Five-Stage Holy Water Purification Ritual (Melukat).'

Unlike typical waterfalls you visit on your own, the entire process is guided, from prayers and cleansing to the holy water blessings at each stage, offering a complete ritualistic experience.

Even if you're not religious, you can still feel the deep Balinese cultural emphasis on purification and blessings.

For women joining the ritual, it's recommended to wear a swimsuit underneath for convenience.

Beyond the Melukat, the site offers various other Balinese healing experiences, such as blessing ceremonies and energy treatments, though most require an additional fee.

When we arrived, I realized this was an experience that tourists could never easily access.

The driver showed me around a traditional Balinese house layout, introducing the family shrine, the courtyard, and the living spaces for family members.

It's a completely different Bali from what you see in hotels and tourist spots.

This is real life.
