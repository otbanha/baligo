### 🛂 Immigration was smoother than expected

This time, I handled almost everything online in advance, so the entry process was surprisingly fast.

#### 1️⃣ Electronic Visa (B1)

I applied for the electronic visa before departure, costing around USD$32. After landing, I went straight to the e-gate lane and cleared customs in about 1 minute—no queue at all.

If you're visiting Bali during peak season, I highly recommend getting the e-visa in advance. Waiting in line for a VOA on-site can sometimes make you question your life choices 😆

#### 2️⃣ Electronic Customs Declaration

I also recommend filling this out in advance. You can submit it online up to three days before departure. Just save the QR code screenshot on your phone and scan it on arrival—done.

#### 3️⃣ Tourist Levy

I paid the tourist levy online in advance, around USD$10.
Many processes in Bali are gradually going digital now, and handling things early really saves a lot of time.

## Day 2｜A day of slowing down in Sanur with a morning beach walk, Beach Club, and a charity visit 🌊

The rhythm of the second day was exactly my ideal Bali mode. No packed sightseeing, no rushing from morning to night—just a seamless flow between the beach, coffee bars, a charity space, and Sanur's laid-back pace.

***

### 🌞 Seminyak Beach in the early morning is even more enchanting than during the day

Around 6 a.m., I walked straight out the back door of the hotel onto the sand.

This is exactly why I love staying at beachfront hotels. No need to plan any special itinerary—just waking up and stepping onto the sand for a morning stroll is already worth it.

The morning Seminyak beach is a completely different world from the night. No thumping music, no crowds of tourists—just the sound of waves, morning joggers, and a handful of foreigners already paddling out to surf.

And honestly, the 'surfer hottie density' in Seminyak in the morning is seriously high 🤣. Many people hit the water before dawn, and the beach vibe is super chill. Watching everyone carry their boards into the sea, you get that feeling: 'This is the real Bali everyday life.'

### 🍸 Atlas Beach Club｜The Legendary World's Largest Beach Club

During the day, I visited the much-talked-about Atlas Beach Club.

![Atlas Beach Club｜傳說中的全球最大 Beach Club](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-06/1781014745875-atlas2.jpg)

Photo provided by Lai Jiaying at Atlas Beach Club

It's known as the 'world's largest beach club,' and seeing it in person, the scale is truly overwhelming. The space is enormous, from the pool and DJ area to the restaurant and lounge zones—it almost feels like a small resort.

This time, thanks to a collaboration with TUL BLUE BERAWA, guests staying in suites or above get free entry, plus a front-row sofa area and a welcome drink. The front-row spot makes a huge difference—you face the ocean directly, with waves, music, crowds, and sunset all around, creating an incredibly immersive vibe.

That said, I personally feel Atlas leans more toward a 'lively party-style beach club.' If you love music, energy, and strong photo ops, it's a perfect fit. If you prefer a quiet, relaxing escape, you might enjoy other beach clubs more.

### 💚 Bali Life Foundation｜The most profound stop of this trip

The most memorable part of the afternoon wasn't a tourist attraction, but a visit to the Bali Life Foundation.

https://youtube.com/shorts/ljVgfCUTOyg

Provided by Lai Jiaying, Bali Life Foundation

The foundation is open for预约 visits every Friday from 4 PM to 7 PM. The campus is actually not very large, currently housing about 50 children.

When we arrived, we were about to unload the rice and cooking oil we brought for donation. A few young boys immediately came over to help on their own.

What shocked me the most was that these kids, probably only five or six years old, each carried a 10-kilogram bag of rice by themselves.

That scene was quite striking. It suddenly makes you realize how different the growing environment of some children is from the world we're used to.

But at the same time, they weren't gloomy. Instead, they kept laughing, chatting, and even proactively helped welcome everyone.

![Bali Life Foundation｜這趟旅程最深刻的一站](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-06/1781014986137-bali_life_foundation2.jpg)

Provided by Lai Jiaying

The foundation has a communal dinner at 6 PM, and visitors are welcome to stay and join. Rather than just a 'tour,' it felt more like briefly stepping into their lives.

They also run a kindergarten offering half-day to full-day childcare. Many foreign parents who come to Bali for surfing or work send their kids there, and this income helps support the foundation's operations.

I personally really like this model. It doesn't rely entirely on donations but instead focuses on building a cycle that can sustain itself long-term.

## Day 4｜Riding a scooter through mountain roads, from Tirta Empul Temple all the way to Ubud and Sanur 🛵

Day four was all about scooter travel mode. We set off from Sanur sharp at 7 AM, heading straight into central Bali.

Honestly, riding a scooter in Bali gives you a unique sense of freedom. Along the way, you pass through small villages, rice terraces, temples, and mountain roads. Sometimes, just stopping at a red light and seeing roadside offerings, breakfast stalls, or students riding to school makes you think: 'Ah, this is the real Bali everyday life.'

But a heads-up: if you're not used to riding a scooter or unfamiliar with Southeast Asian traffic rhythms, hiring a driver will make things much easier 🤣

### 🙏 Tirta Empul Temple｜Coming early really makes a difference

Our first stop was straight to Tirta Empul Temple.

![聖泉寺｜一大早來真的差很多](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-06/1781015518073-tirta_empul_3.jpg)

Tirta Empul Temple — Photo by Lai Jiaying

We arrived early, so the crowds were still manageable. I had seen many online photos of long queues, but coming in the morning made a huge difference — both for taking photos and for experiencing the purification ritual.

The temple is most famous for its holy water purification. Many locals and tourists line up at different spouts to perform the cleansing ritual.

The atmosphere on-site is actually quite serene. Even with a fair number of visitors, you can still feel a very peaceful energy.

But I have to give you a serious heads-up:

⭐️ There are no hair dryers. ⭐️ No hair dryers. ⭐️ No hair dryers.

Your hair will have to air-dry naturally after getting wet 🤣 Luckily, I brought my own towel — otherwise, riding a scooter with wet hair would have been a nightmare.

I really enjoyed the pace here. There's no need to do anything special—just take a slow stroll and soak it all in.

A few shops also sell local snacks, making them a perfect spot to sit and rest if you're tired from riding.

### 💦 Tegenungan Waterfall｜Thrilling to go down, exhausting to climb back up 🤣

This waterfall is one of the more accessible ones in Bali. You don't need to hike for ages through the mountains, but you still have to descend all the way into the valley.

As you get close to the waterfall, the mist hitting you is truly breathtaking. The roar of the water is so loud that you can barely hear someone standing right next to you.

What's special about Bali's waterfalls is that many aren't the 'thin and tall' type — instead, they have a thick, powerful flow.

The only problem is…

The climb up is really tiring 😆

### 🐬 Dolphins at Dawn｜A dreamy sunrise over Lovina Beach

We arrived at Lovina Beach before the sky had even begun to lighten.

https://youtu.be/XRR7ddLAiV4

Photo by Lai Jiaying

Then we hopped onto a traditional local outrigger boat (Jukung) and headed out to sea in search of dolphins.

To be honest, waking up in the middle of the night was absolutely brutal. Especially riding all the way to North Bali in the dark—I was basically in a zombie state the whole time.

But not long after the boat set off, dolphins suddenly started appearing.

And not just one or two. Whole pods kept leaping out of the water right next to the boat.

Pretty much everyone on board started screaming and cheering at the same time 🤣 That sight will jolt you awake instantly.

Add to that the sunrise slowly rising over the horizon, with the sky shifting from deep blue to orange-red—the whole atmosphere felt surreal.

But here's a must-know tip:

🌊 Bring a change of clothes and pants. The waves really do crash right into the boat.

Especially if you're sitting in the front—your chances of getting soaked are super high 🤣

### 🐒 Feeding Monkeys on the Mountain Trail｜The monkeys here are much gentler

On the way back, we stopped by a roadside monkey feeding spot along the mountain.

There are monkeys all over Bali, but the ones here are noticeably gentler. Unlike the monkeys at Uluwatu, some of which will straight-up snatch your glasses or phone 🤣

They charge a small fee at the spot (though I honestly can't remember how much 😆), and you can buy food to feed them.

Some monkeys even sit quietly waiting for food—totally different from the 'gangster monkey vibe' at Uluwatu.

### 🧘‍♀️ Taman Beji Griya Waterfall: The Five-Stage Holy Water Purification Ceremony

Our first stop is the increasingly popular purification sanctuary—Taman Beji Griya Waterfall.

![‍ Taman Beji Griya Waterfall｜五階段聖水淨化儀式](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-06/1781016977848-beji.jpg)

Taman Beji Griya Waterfall (Photo by Lai Jiaying)

It's best known for the "Five-Stage Holy Water Purification Ritual (Melukat)."

Unlike typical waterfalls you visit on your own, the entire process is guided, from prayers and cleansing to the different stages of holy water blessings, offering a complete ritualistic experience.

Even if you're not religious, you can still feel the importance Balinese culture places on purification and blessings.

If you're a woman planning to join the ritual, it's more convenient to wear a swimsuit underneath.

Beyond the Melukat, the site also offers various other Balinese healing experiences, such as blessing ceremonies and energy healing sessions, though most require an additional fee.

> [Taman Beji Griya # Soul Retreat & Self-Healing Experience](https://affiliate.klook.com/redirect?aid=116349&aff_adid=1299850&k_site=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.klook.com%2Fzh-TW%2Factivity%2F75759-retreat-self-healing-purification-trip-bali-aga-taman-beji-griya%2F%3Fspm%3DActivity.SearchSuggest_LIST%26clickId%3Df36411cfec)

### 🏠 Unexpected Twist｜Invited to the Driver's Home

What came next was arguably one of the most unique experiences of the entire trip.

The driver suddenly warmly invited me to his home.

To be honest, if it were my first time in Bali, I probably wouldn't have dared to accept.

But since we had already gotten quite familiar over the past few days, I decided to go along and see.

When we arrived, I realized this was an experience that tourists could never easily access.

The driver showed me around a traditional Balinese house layout, introducing the family shrine, the courtyard, and the living spaces for family members.

Completely different from the Bali you see in hotels and tourist spots.

This is real life.

The driver's mother runs a small shop.

As soon as she saw a guest arrive, she immediately brought out drinks and snacks.

Even though we couldn't really communicate with words, you could still feel their overflowing warmth.

What surprised me even more was that they kept insisting I stay for dinner.

That kind of unreserved hospitality is truly touching.

Sometimes the most precious travel memories aren't the sights, but the people.
