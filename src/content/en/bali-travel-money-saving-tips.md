## 1. Avoid Peak Travel Seasons

First, let's start with choosing your travel time! Avoid visiting Bali during peak seasons, when flight, accommodation, and activity prices tend to skyrocket. Opt for the off-season or less busy periods to save on flights and lodging while enjoying a more peaceful travel experience.

So, when is Bali's peak travel season? Generally, the dry season with lower rainfall is the best time to visit Bali, roughly from April to October, which is the peak season. What are the downsides of avoiding the peak season? Check out [this link](/blog/bali-best-time-to-visit/) to learn about Bali's rainy season.

Beyond seasonal distinctions, here are five specific times when crowds flood into Bali, ranked by the level of people and traffic:

1. Indonesia's Eid al-Fitr holiday is a key moment. A huge number of domestic Indonesian tourists flock to this paradise island during this roughly ten-day break. This is the busiest peak season in Bali, ranking first!
2. The Christmas-New Year holiday period typically attracts global travelers, as it's a long break for most Western countries, and Indonesians also find ways to take time off. So, the Christmas-New Year holiday ranks second for crowds and traffic. Here's a short video showing [the highway to the airport on 2023/12/29](https://www.facebook.com/groups/indonesia.travelguide/permalink/3579975912271532/).
3. Around the Chinese Lunar New Year is another special time. Besides China and Taiwan, countries like Hong Kong, Malaysia, Singapore, South Korea, and Vietnam also have two to five days off; the Philippines, Thailand, and Indonesia have a single day off on New Year's Day. The Lunar New Year holiday ranks third for attracting large crowds to tourist spots.
4. From July 1 to September 5, Europe's long holidays and the Northern Hemisphere's summer break often create a travel surge, with many families and students heading to Bali for vacations. Flight and hotel prices are never cheap during summer!
5. Australian school holidays and Indonesian international school holidays (one week between September 20 and October 10) also bring in many Australian tourists and families from Indonesian international schools, who embark on trips during this time.

During these periods, tourist numbers surge, and accommodation and attraction prices may rise due to high demand. So if you're planning a trip to Bali during peak season, it's best to book early and prepare thoroughly to ensure a smooth, worry-free journey.

## 6. Choose the Right Mode of Transport

For travelers, Bali offers three convenient, **direct** transport options: renting a scooter, taking a taxi, or hiring a private driver. We won't discuss the bus system, which stops at every station.

Renting a scooter in Bali is ideal for travelers who want to explore freely; taxis are great for short trips or those who prefer not to drive; and hiring a private driver suits those seeking a deeper experience and customized itinerary—especially if you split the cost with travel companions, it's quite reasonable. ## 9. Pay with Cash

In Bali, cash is the most widely accepted payment method. Be sure to carry enough cash to avoid extra fees from using credit cards.

Using credit cards abroad has several drawbacks:

1. You may have to pay additional fees, such as foreign transaction fees, withdrawal fees, and exchange rate markups.
2. You might be charged a surcharge by retailers if you don't pay in the local currency.
3. You could overspend if you don't keep track of your daily expenses.
4. You may face fraud risks if your credit card lacks a security chip or gets skimmed.

So, be cautious when using credit cards overseas.

But carrying millions of Indonesian rupiah (with an exchange rate of about 1:500 to TWD) can be cumbersome. If you'd rather not carry that much cash, consider using the e-wallet in the Gojek app mentioned in the previous post.

## 16. Tipping Appropriately

Tipping appropriately is a cultural courtesy during travel and a way to express gratitude for service. When traveling in Bali, giving the right tip can help you build friendly relationships with locals while respecting local customs. Here are Xiao Jie's tips on [tipping occasions and things to keep in mind](https://youtu.be/B_oT905RkQw) to help you make proper choices on your journey.

Additionally, a common question often pops up in our Bali community: "Can I tip in US dollars?" Let's share some thoughts on this. In Indonesia or Bali, the only currency in circulation is the Indonesian Rupiah (IDR). So, if you want to give a tip, always use the local currency—Indonesian Rupiah. Avoid using other currencies or foreign money for tipping. This not only shows respect for the locals but also conveys your sincere appreciation to the service provider.

Think about it from another angle: imagine if a foreign friend handed you foreign currency in Taiwan—wouldn't you find it a bit inconvenient? Similarly, Bali residents might face the same difficulty, potentially causing unnecessary hassle.

So, to ensure your tip accurately conveys your gratitude, **always use Indonesian Rupiah for tipping**. Doing so not only respects the locals but also better expresses your appreciation for the service.
