## 1. Avoid Peak Travel Seasons

First, let's start with choosing your travel time! Avoid visiting Bali during peak seasons as flight tickets, accommodations, and activity prices usually skyrocket. Opting for off-peak or less busy periods can save you money on flights and accommodations while offering a more peaceful travel experience.

So when is Bali's peak travel season? Generally, the dry season with lower rainfall probability is the best time to visit Bali, typically from April to October, which is also the peak travel season. What are the drawbacks of avoiding peak season? Check out [here](/blog/bali-best-time-to-visit/) to learn about Bali's rainy season.

Besides distinguishing peak and off-peak seasons by weather, here are five periods when Bali sees a surge in visitors, ranked by crowd/traffic volume:

1. Indonesia's Eid al-Fitr holiday is a crucial time. A large number of local Indonesian tourists flock to this paradise island during this approximately ten-day holiday. This is Bali's busiest peak season!
2. Christmas-New Year holidays typically attract global tourists, as it's a long holiday for most Western countries, and Indonesians also try to take time off. Thus, the Christmas-New Year holiday ranks second in terms of crowd and traffic. Here's a short video showing [the situation on the airport highway on December 29, 2023](https://www.facebook.com/groups/indonesia.travelguide/permalink/3579975912271532/).
3. Around Chinese New Year is a special time, with holidays ranging from two to five days in countries like China, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Malaysia, Singapore, South Korea, and Vietnam; the Philippines, Thailand, and Indonesia have a one-day holiday on the first day of the lunar new year. The Chinese New Year holiday ranks third in attracting large numbers of tourists, causing crowded tourist spots.
4. The period from July 1 to September 5 coincides with Europe's long holidays and the summer vacation for students in the Northern Hemisphere, leading to a travel trend where many families and students take this time to vacation in Bali. Summer flight tickets and accommodation prices are never cheap!
5. Australian school holidays and Indonesian international school holidays (one week between September 20 and October 10) also bring a significant number of Australian tourists and families from Indonesian international schools, who embark on trips during this period.

During these peak periods, the number of tourists surges dramatically, and prices for accommodations and attractions may rise due to high demand. Therefore, if you plan to visit Bali during these busy seasons, it's best to book in advance and prepare thoroughly to ensure a pleasant and worry-free trip.

For travelers in Bali, there are three convenient and **direct** transportation options: renting a scooter, taking a taxi, or hiring a private car. We won't discuss public buses that stop at every station.

## 9. Use Cash for Payments

In Bali, cash is the most widely accepted form of payment. Remember to carry enough cash to avoid additional fees from card transactions.

There are several drawbacks to using credit cards abroad:

1. You may incur additional fees, such as foreign transaction fees, ATM withdrawal fees, and unfavorable exchange rates.
2. Retailers may charge you a fee if you don't pay in the local currency.
3. You might overspend if you don't keep track of your daily expenses.
4. You could face fraud risks if your credit card lacks a security chip or is stolen.

Therefore, be cautious when using credit cards abroad.

However, carrying Indonesian rupiah often means dealing with amounts in the millions (approximately 1:500 for USD to IDR). If you prefer not to carry so much cash, consider using the electronic wallet feature in the Gojek app mentioned earlier.

## 16. Tipping Appropriately

Tipping appropriately is a cultural etiquette during travel and a way to express gratitude for services. When traveling in Bali, tipping appropriately can help build friendly relations with locals and is also a way to respect local customs. Here, Jay shares some [occasions and considerations for tipping](https://youtu.be/B_oT905RkQw), allowing you to make appropriate choices during your journey.

Additionally, a common question often raised in our Bali community is: 'Can I tip in US dollars?' Here, we'd like to share some thoughts on this topic. In Indonesia or Bali, the only circulating currency is the Indonesian Rupiah (Rupia/IDR). Therefore, if we wish to tip, it's essential to use the local currency, the Indonesian Rupiah. We should avoid using other currencies or foreign money as a means of tipping. This not only shows respect for the local culture but also sincerely expresses our gratitude to the service providers.

Let's think from another perspective: imagine if in Taiwan, a foreign friend handed you foreign currency, wouldn't you find it a bit troublesome? Similarly, residents of Bali might feel the same inconvenience, possibly even causing them discomfort.

Therefore, to ensure our tips accurately convey our gratitude, we should **always use Indonesian Rupiah for tipping**. Doing so not only respects the locals but also better expresses our appreciation and thanks for the services received.
