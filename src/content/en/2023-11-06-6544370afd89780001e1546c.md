For many Taiwanese families, Bali is the first destination to take their children abroad—airfare is relatively affordable, the climate is warm, and hotels have pools, which sounds perfect. But when you actually start packing, you'll find problems popping up one after another: Can you buy formula in Bali? What cold medicines should you bring? Does the brand of diapers matter?

This article compiles our years of experience taking kids to Bali, along with practical information used by local families, into a comprehensive checklist to ensure you're well-prepared.

***

### Pre-Trip Planning: Don't Underestimate the Importance of Itinerary Planning

The biggest pitfall when traveling with toddlers is 'thinking you can figure it out when you get there.' Although Bali is rich in tourism resources, many things toddlers need are not readily available or require special effort to find. Before departure, it's recommended to:

- Confirm flight options (the impact of long direct flights vs. layovers on toddlers can be significant)
- Confirm if accommodations provide cribs and have children's play facilities
- Plan daily itinerary pace in advance, not too rushed, as toddlers need nap time

Budget planning is also crucial. ### The Flight Challenge: Preparing Luggage for Toddlers

The flight is often the most stressful part of traveling with young children, but proper preparation can significantly reduce the pressure.

Flight aids: For children aged 1-3, consider bringing an inflatable footrest to allow them to lie flat, or a ride-on suitcase that doubles as a bed (like JetKids). However, note that not all airlines permit these, so it's best to check beforehand. For infants under one year, you can request a bassinet seat, but this needs to be arranged weeks in advance.

In-flight hydration: Cabin air is very dry, so remember to bring baby lotion and saline nasal spray to keep your baby's nasal passages moist. During takeoff and landing, ear pressure changes are noticeable—breastfeeding or giving a bottle is the most effective way to relieve discomfort, while older children can be given candy.

Travel bag tip: Prepare a small multi-pocket bag filled with snacks, small toys, stickers, and mini books for your child to explore—the process of discovery itself can keep them entertained for quite some time.

Baby carrier: A good-quality, natural-material carrier is highly practical for moving around airports and Bali. Many places in Bali aren't stroller-friendly, so a carrier offers much more flexibility.

### Baby food and formula: How much to bring and can you buy it locally?

This is a major concern for many moms. Honestly, Bali isn't completely lacking in supplies, but there are definitely some pitfalls to avoid.

**Formula**: If your baby requires special formulas (hydrolyzed protein, hypoallergenic), strongly recommend bringing enough supply as options on the island are very limited. Regular formula can be found in supermarkets and pharmacies, with common brands including Nutrilon, Lactogen (Nestlé), and Enfamil, which also offers a lactose-free version. Australian organic brands Bellamy's (cow milk) and Karihome (goat milk) are available but at higher prices.

**Baby food jars**: It's advisable to bring enough meat purees, especially turkey, which is hard to find in Bali. Imported supermarkets like Pepito occasionally stock Heinz or Bellamy's fruit purees, but they're mostly vegetable and fruit flavors and cost about twice as much as in Taiwan. If your baby is still in the baby food stage, buying a blender to prepare local ingredients might be a more practical approach.

**Dairy products**: The overall quality of dairy products in Bali isn't ideal, often high in sugar or expensive. If your child is used to yogurt or kefir, consider bringing cultures to make your own. Plant-based milk options are plentiful, and lactose-free milk brands like Milk life are available.

**Health food stores**: Bali Buda is a chain of organic health food stores across the island, offering various grains (including buckwheat, which is rare in Taiwan), gluten-free, and dairy-free products, making it a great supply stop for families with young children.

### Diapers and hygiene products

**Diapers**: The brand selection differs from Taiwan. Better options include Korean and Japanese brands like Genki, Makuku, and Merries, with some places also offering biodegradable Ecoboom. Note that Merries sold on the island are mostly locally produced in Indonesia, with varying quality. Authentic Japanese-made white packaging is available only at select outlets (like Clandy's or Bali Hypermart) or can be ordered online via Shopee/Tokopedia. Swim diapers are mostly available online, so it's advisable to bring enough from Taiwan.

**Size reminder**: Many diaper brands run narrow, so consider sizing up.

**Other baby essentials**: For offline shopping, check out Balonku, Genyc, and Mothercare for baby products. For nursery furniture, visit Informa. For tableware and storage solutions, IKEA is your go-to (available in-store or via their app, with home delivery options).

**Specialty skincare**: If your child requires specific shampoos or creams for skin conditions (e.g., Mustela), it's best to bring them from Taiwan as options on the island are limited.

**Hats**: Sun protection hats for toddlers are crucial. Bring your trusted hat from home—while Bali markets offer cheap options, many are made with thick materials and have poorly designed straps that won't stay put on your child's head.

### Medications: Take this list seriously

Most pharmacies in Bali sell generic drugs. Brand-name medications are available but cost two to three times more, and many require prescriptions. If your child is on long-term medication, be sure to bring enough.

Other recommended medications to pack:

- **Digestive**: Simethicone (e.g., Gas-X) for gas relief, probiotics, intestinal adsorbents, digestive enzymes
- **Wound care**: Antiseptic spray, healing ointment, band-aids, liquid skin adhesive
- **Throat & cold**: Throat anti-inflammatory spray, throat lozenges, saline nasal rinse, fever reducers/pain relievers
- **Eyes**: Artificial tears or anti-inflammatory eye drops
- **Nose**: Saline nasal spray, nasal decongestants
- **Allergies & insect bites**: Antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine/Zyrtec), anti-itch gel
- **Baby-specific**: Teething gel, nasal aspirator
- **Miscellaneous**: COVID/flu rapid tests, thermometer

Most importantly: **Remember to purchase travel medical insurance before departure**. While private hospitals in Bali are decent, they're very expensive. Without insurance, unexpected medical expenses could hit your wallet hard.

## **Additionally, Here Are Some Extra Tips:**

- Bring your child's favorite toys and activities on the plane to help keep them entertained.
- In hot weather, make sure your child drinks plenty of water and apply sunscreen.
- Before visiting temples or other religious sites, teach your child to respect local culture.
- Learn some basic Indonesian phrases, such as "thank you." Check out Xiao Jie's [video tutorial on how to pronounce it](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VuG8s-u_I0Q&t=106s).

Bali is truly a great destination for traveling with young children—as long as you're well-prepared. Nowadays, most basic necessities can be found in Bali, though brand options are limited, some items need to be ordered online, and some are more expensive than in Taiwan. Bring essentials like pureed food, special formula, regular medications, favorite hats, and a few familiar toys from home—everything else can be managed locally.

Having familiar items around helps children adapt more quickly; when adults are well-prepared, they can truly relax and enjoy the trip.
