Taking elderly parents on an overseas trip may seem like a warm and filial gesture on the surface, but for many, it can turn into a thankless and exhausting experience. According to surveys, over half of families have felt stressed during such trips, even leaving behind "painful" memories. This is because traveling with elders often involves more than just sightseeing—it comes with physical, psychological, and even familial challenges.

## Why Is Traveling with Elders So Complicated?

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_e3af29c3d44c3341f926bc65755d9b29.png)

### **1. The Challenge of Physical Differences**

Most elders are not as physically fit as younger people, and what you consider a relaxing itinerary might be a huge physical burden for them. For example, if the schedule involves a lot of walking or uphill climbs, it could leave elders exhausted or even unwell. One extreme example shared online involved a family trip to Jimbaran Beach in Bali to watch the sunset. The elders struggled to walk just a few meters on the sandy terrain and ended up waiting in the parking lot, leaving everyone feeling helpless.

### **2. Differences in Eating Habits**

Many elders are conservative with their food preferences and may struggle to adapt to foreign cuisines. One family took their elders to Thailand, but the elders couldn’t handle the spicy and sour flavors. They spent the entire trip searching for Chinese restaurants and even brought along instant noodles and preserved snacks, completely missing out on the local culinary experience and dampening everyone’s enthusiasm.

### **3. Elders’ "Sightseeing Expectations" Differ from Younger Generations**

Elders’ travel preferences often lean toward comfort and tradition, such as visiting temples, gardens, or historical sites, while younger travelers might prioritize photo ops or adventurous activities. A friend once shared that they arranged a helicopter tour over Bali’s Bukit Peninsula, but their parents opted out due to fear of heights, turning what was meant to be a trip highlight into a point of generational conflict.

### **4. The Burden of Care**

Traveling with elderly parents often feels less like relaxation and more like a "double responsibility" trip. Someone once shared that they stayed at a resort in Seminyak, Bali, hoping their parents could enjoy a relaxing atmosphere. However, due to their father's high blood pressure, they had to urgently search for a pharmacy in the middle of the night and even involve a local doctor, turning what was supposed to be a romantic vacation into a stressful ordeal.

### **5. The Impact of Elders' "Psychological Gap"**

Some elderly parents may feel anxious in unfamiliar foreign environments, becoming overly sensitive to minor issues. A reader once shared that while traveling in Japan with their parents, they arranged to take the subway. However, the mother felt "neglected" because the reader was busy discussing the route with others, and the crowded subway made her uncomfortable. She became upset, feeling that her child wasn't considerate enough, leading to constant emotional reassurance throughout the trip. As a result, the planned city subway sightseeing spots were almost impossible to visit.

## Painful Experiences: Cases of Elders' Uncooperativeness

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_c0d8fb61ba227fd4d75c613228e45179.png)

When traveling with elderly parents, differences in lifestyle or psychological gaps often lead to uncooperative behavior, causing the entire trip to hit a deadlock. Here are a few real-life stories that many readers might relate to:

### **1. "Insisting on Waking Up Early, but Everyone Else is Exhausted"**

One family went on vacation to Bali, planning to slow down and enjoy the island's relaxed vibe. However, the elderly parent insisted on waking up at 5 AM every morning, demanding that the whole family join them to watch the sunrise. On the first day, everyone reluctantly complied, but by the second day, everyone felt physically drained, and the trip's joy was replaced by a "military-style schedule." Eventually, the children and the parent argued over whether to wake up early, creating an awkward atmosphere.

### **2. "Mother Refused to Use the Outdoor Pool Because It Was Dirty"**

A pair of siblings took their mother to a famous infinity pool hotel in Bali, but the mother deemed the pool "outdoor and dirty," refusing to swim and opting to stay in the room drinking tea instead. In the end, the family had to split up, turning the trip into a fragmented "awkward memory."

### **3. "If the Attraction Isn't Their Cup of Tea, They Won't Bother"**

A couple took their elderly parents to Ubud, Bali, to visit the famous rice terraces, thinking the natural beauty would leave a lasting impression. However, upon arrival, the parents refused to get out of the car, complaining that the terraces were "too sunny and not photogenic." They stayed inside scrolling on their phones and questioned why they weren’t taken to "an air-conditioned mall" instead. The children’s well-planned itinerary was instantly dampened.

### **4. "Stubbornness Leads to Conflict with the Guide"**

A family once hired a private driver for a Bali tour. The driver followed the agreed-upon itinerary, but the elderly parents suddenly demanded to visit a different location. When the driver explained that the detour would be inconvenient and incur extra costs, the parents argued, accusing him of "scamming" them. The children had to step in to mediate, leaving everyone feeling awkward and frustrated.

### **5. "Insisting on Carry-On Luggage They Can’t Handle"**

A family planned a relaxing trip to Seminyak, Bali, and suggested using one large suitcase for everyone’s belongings. However, the elderly parents insisted on bringing their own carry-on luggage, claiming they "didn’t want to share with the kids." At the airport, they struggled to carry their bags and ended up asking the children for help, blaming the luggage design for their inconvenience.

### **6. "Rigid Eating Habits Ruin the Travel Mood"**

One traveler shared that their parents insisted on eating rice every day during their Bali trip and couldn’t tolerate the local spiced cuisine. As a result, the family spent mealtimes searching for Chinese restaurants, disrupting their culinary exploration. The parents would complain, "Why isn’t there normal food here?" making the entire trip stressful.

### **7. "Too Tired to Take Photos, Leaving Family Memories Incomplete"**

A family arranged a group photo session at Sanur Beach in Bali to capture cherished memories. However, on the day of the shoot, the elderly parents refused to participate, citing the hot weather and wind-ruined hair. Despite the family’s pleas, they remained stubborn, leaving the group photo incomplete and the children deeply disappointed.

### **8. "Demanding Immediate Attention Without Considering the Situation"**

During a family water activity in Bali, the parents suddenly asked for water. However, the children, who had just changed into swimsuits and were about to enter the water, couldn’t help immediately and asked them to wait a few minutes. This led to the parents becoming upset, even questioning whether their children still had "filial piety." In the end, everyone’s mood was ruined by this small incident, completely affecting the atmosphere of the entire activity.

### **9. "Disliking the Itinerary and Showing Displeasure Throughout"**

One family arranged for their parents to attend a cultural dance performance in Bali, thinking it would allow them to experience local traditions. However, the parents found the performance too long and boring, yawning multiple times during the show and even quietly complaining in front of others, which drew attention. The children felt extremely embarrassed, and the entire performance became unbearable.

***

These "painful experiences" may seem minor, but when they accumulate during a trip, they can leave the children feeling exhausted and even defeated. However, understanding and communication are key. Although the journey may not be perfect, with the right mindset, it can still become an important memory for family bonding.

## How to Reduce "Effort Without Reward" Situations?

### **1. Avoid Overpacking the Itinerary: Don’t Turn the Trip into a "Marathon"**

**Painful Experience**:

A family traveled to Bali, planning to visit Kuta, Seminyak, and Ubud in a single day. However, the elderly members of the family became exhausted from the constant getting in and out of the car due to the tight schedule, and one even felt dizzy and unwell midway. Ultimately, the family had to cut the trip short and return to the hotel to rest. What was supposed to be an eagerly anticipated journey turned into a chaotic experience.

**Recommendation**: It’s best to plan only 2-3 attractions per day, especially since Bali’s traffic conditions can be complex. Avoid long-distance travel as much as possible.

### **2. Avoid High Floors or Hotels Without Elevators: Save Energy and Reduce Risks**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family booked a hotel in Ubud and chose a room on a higher floor for the great view. However, the elderly members struggled with the stairs due to mobility issues, which even affected their knee health, making the entire trip inconvenient.

**Recommendation**: Choose hotels with elevators or stay on lower floors to minimize the need for elderly family members to climb stairs.

### **3. Don’t Make Elderly Family Members Wait, Especially in Hot Weather**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family traveling in Bali thought it would be convenient and cost-effective to use ride-hailing services (Gojek/Grab) within the city. However, due to traffic, the family had to wait under the scorching sun for over 20 minutes. An elderly member suffered from heatstroke, which dampened their mood and made them lose interest in the rest of the trip.

**Recommendation**: When traveling with elderly family members, it’s best to opt for private car services to avoid making them wait.

### **4. Avoid Attractions with Stairs**

**Painful Lesson**:

A couple took their elderly parents to visit Besakih Mother Temple in Bali, only to discover upon arrival that reaching the main structure required climbing numerous stairs. The elderly parents grew tired midway, with one nearly collapsing from exhaustion, forcing them to abandon the visit.

**Advice**: Before heading out, research whether attractions involve long walks or stairs, such as rice terraces or waterfalls, and assess whether elderly family members have the stamina. Refer to "[Complete Guide to Bali Travel Destinations Suitable for Seniors](/blog/2024-11-27-6746742bfd897800018d4906/)"

### **5. Consider Dietary Preferences and Restrictions**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family tried the local specialty Babi Guling (roast suckling pig) in Bali, but the elderly parents couldn’t adjust to the unfamiliar flavors. They ended up watching others eat while going hungry, creating an awkward situation for everyone.

**Advice**: Check restaurant menus in advance and choose places with diverse options or dishes that cater to elderly dietary needs.

### **6. Carry Essential Medications for Elderly Family Members**

**Painful Lesson**:

During one trip, an elderly family member suddenly experienced stomach pain, but we had forgotten to bring stomach medicine. Unable to quickly purchase suitable medication at local pharmacies, the entire itinerary was delayed, leaving everyone feeling helpless.

**Advice**: Always prepare essential medications for elderly family members before traveling, such as blood pressure medication, stomach medicine, pain relievers, and mosquito repellent.

### **7. Avoid Arranging Stimulating or Adventurous Activities**

**Painful Experience**:

One family took elderly members on a Bali river tubing activity, thinking it would be a fun experience. However, they didn’t anticipate having to climb down a long flight of stairs, and the elderly member refused to continue! They were left unsure what to do with the already paid-for activity.

**Advice**: When traveling with elderly family members, prioritize relaxed sightseeing activities.

### **8. Elderly Members Should Avoid Getting Wet in Rain or Damp Conditions**

**Painful Experience**:

One family traveled to Bali during the rainy season. Due to an overly rushed itinerary, the elderly member got soaked in the rain multiple times, caught a cold upon returning to the hotel, and disrupted the entire travel plan.

**Advice**: Always carry rain gear and ensure ample time in the itinerary during the rainy season to prevent elderly family members from becoming overly fatigued or getting wet.

### **9. Avoid Noisy Areas for Peace and Comfort**

**Painful Experience**:

A family stayed at a hotel in the bustling Kuta area of Bali. The constant music from nearby bars and nightclubs kept the elderly family members awake at night, leading to several sleepless nights and a decline in their mood.

**Recommendation**: Choose hotels away from busy areas, such as Sanur or Jimbaran, where the environment is relatively quiet and comfortable.

### **10. Plan Breaks for Long Drives**

**Painful Experience**:

A family traveled from Nusa Dua in the south to Lovina in the north to watch dolphins, a journey that took 3-4 hours one way. The elderly family members experienced back pain from sitting too long and began to question whether the trip was worth it.

**Recommendation**: For drives longer than 2 hours, plan for stops along the way to allow the elderly to stretch or rest.

### **11. Ensure Convenient Access to Restrooms**

**Painful Experience**:

A family visiting the rice terraces in Bali found themselves in a difficult situation due to the limited number of restrooms in the area. An elderly family member had to hold their bladder for an extended period, eventually leading to discomfort and complaints about the entire trip.

**Advice**: Remind elderly family members to use the restroom before heading out. When hiring a private car, communicate with the driver to arrange rest stops along the way. Additionally, always confirm the location of restrooms upon arriving at a destination.

### **12. Avoid Overcrowded Places**

**Painful Experience**:

A family took their elderly relatives to Uluwatu in Bali to watch the sunset, only to find the area overly crowded. The elderly couldn't find a place to rest and were nearly pushed over in the crowd, making the entire experience unpleasant.

**Advice**: Popular spots in Bali often attract large crowds. When traveling with elderly family members, consider visiting during off-peak hours or opting for quieter locations, such as enjoying the sunset at Jimbaran Beach instead of the crowded Uluwatu.

**Painful Experience**:

A couple, fearing their elderly family member's displeasure, frequently asked for their opinion on decisions like where to eat or what to do next. This constant questioning created significant pressure, leading the elderly family member to respond with 'whatever you decide,' though their mood had already been affected.

一對夫妻在旅行中因為怕老的不高興，頻繁詢問長輩意見，例如去哪裡吃飯或下一個行程如何安排，導致長輩感到壓力大，最後直接表示「隨便你們」，但情緒已經受到影響。

**Advice**: Don't leave all decisions to the elders. Plan your options in advance and simply ask for their preferences occasionally to reduce the pressure they might feel about 'needing to make decisions.'

### **14. Prepare Sun Protection Items Like Hats and Sunscreen**

**Painful Lesson**:

One family took their elders to a photo spot with little shade. Due to the hot weather and forgetting to bring sunscreen, the elders suffered from sunburn, causing skin irritation, and they later refused to participate in outdoor activities.

**Advice**: It's best to schedule elders' activities early in the morning, considering both their energy levels and Bali's hot weather, especially between noon and 2 PM. Hats, sunscreen, and sunglasses are essential for elders during outdoor activities. Reapply sunscreen every 2-3 hours to prevent sunburn.

### **15. Avoid Setting the Air Conditioner Too Cold or Too Hot**

**Painful Lesson**:

One family stayed at a hotel in Bali and set the air conditioner too low for comfort. As a result, the elders caught a cold at night, which even disrupted their subsequent travel plans.

**Advice**: Adjust the hotel air conditioner to a moderate temperature, ideally between 24-26°C, to avoid it being too cold or too hot. Similarly, adjust the car's air conditioning according to the elders' comfort.

### **16. Reserve Time for Naps or Rest**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family planned a full-day itinerary from 6 AM to 9 PM, but the elderly members were visibly exhausted by midday. With the tight schedule leaving no room for rest, they had to cut the trip short and return to the hotel early.

**Recommendation**: Always allocate 1-2 hours of rest time in your daily itinerary, allowing elderly members to nap or relax and avoid fatigue from prolonged activities. In Bali's Sanur or Ubud areas, many cafes and hotels offer comfortable resting spaces, making them excellent choices.

### **17. Provide Comfortable Shoes and Clothing**

**Painful Lesson**:

During a visit to the Monkey Forest, an elderly family member developed blisters from wearing uncomfortable shoes during extended walking, forcing them to return to the hotel early.

**Recommendation**: Prepare comfortable shoes that fit the elderly's feet well, avoiding new or hard-soled shoes. Additionally, opt for lightweight, breathable clothing and adjust based on the itinerary—consider bringing raincoats, warm jackets, or swimwear as needed.

### **18. Pay Attention to Elderly Members' Emotions, Avoid Neglect**

**Painful Lesson**:

While the family was busy taking influencer-style photos at Seminyak Beach in Bali, they neglected an elderly member who was left waiting alone. Feeling overlooked, the elderly member became upset, affecting the overall mood of the trip.

**Recommendation**: During the trip, interact more with elderly family members to help them feel included in the itinerary, avoiding long periods where they might feel "left out." You can arrange family photo sessions in certain settings or share your travel experiences with them, making them feel valued.

### **19. Prepare Emergency Contact Information in Advance**

**Painful Lesson**:

During a family trip, an elderly member suddenly got separated from the group. Since they didn’t have a phone, it took two hours to locate them, filled with anxiety and worry.

**Recommendation**: Record important contact details in advance, such as the hotel address, local driver’s phone number, and emergency contacts. Alternatively, prepare a small card that says, "I'm lost! Help me call my family," along with the phone number. Provide this information to the elderly family member to carry in their waist bag, avoiding panic in case of emergencies.

### **20. Maintain Patience and Understanding, Adjust Your Mindset When Needed**

The most important thing when traveling with elderly family members is patience. They might complain due to discomfort or anxiety during the trip. In such situations, it’s essential to stay calm, empathize, and focus on creating a pleasant atmosphere rather than confronting their emotions. For example, if they feel the itinerary is too rushed, you can adjust the order of activities or save certain attractions for the next trip.

## Final Reminder: Every Moment on the Journey is a Memory

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_696668bc21571e4b94503611a5a3bc51.png)

Although there are many details to consider when traveling abroad with elderly family members, these shared moments will likely become the most cherished memories in the future. Through advance preparation, patient communication, and moderate compromise, the journey can become smoother and more meaningful. Try to relax and view this trip as a "bonding and relationship-maintenance journey." No matter how the process unfolds, the memories will surely be sweet! 😊
