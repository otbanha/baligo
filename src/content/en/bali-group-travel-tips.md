## Challenges and Solutions for Group Travel in Bali with 10+ People

Bali is one of the most popular group travel destinations in Asia, but planning a trip for 10 or more people involves navigating numerous challenges, from itinerary planning to managing unexpected situations. Here are five common challenges when organizing group trips for 10+ people and how professional travel agencies can help resolve these issues to create a perfect journey.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_f185d21cca0ea1179fff3e7a15c7aad9.png)

## 5 Major Challenges for Group Travel with 10+ People

**1. Complex Itinerary Planning, Difficult to Balance Needs**

With group members aged 10 and above having diverse interests, it’s essential to cater to cultural experiences, adventure activities, family-friendly needs, and relaxation. This often leads to the dilemma of "pleasing everyone." For example, some members may want to wake up early for yoga classes, while others prefer sleeping in and enjoying breakfast leisurely; some may be eager to explore local markets, while others prefer relaxing by the beach. Designing an itinerary that satisfies such diverse needs is a significant challenge.

**2. Resource Integration and Booking Difficulties**

Popular Bali attractions like Ubud’s rice terraces and Tanah Lot Temple attract large crowds during peak seasons. For groups of 10 or more, it’s crucial to book transportation, guides, restaurants, and unique venues (such as private beach dinners or traditional dance performances) months in advance. Additionally, the quality of local services varies, and self-screening can be time-consuming and risky. Another important point is that while you might find a restaurant recommended online, without on-site verification, it may not accommodate large groups or meet expectations in terms of food quality.

**3. Budget Control and Hidden Costs**

When group size increases, while unit costs for transportation and accommodation may decrease, additional needs (such as special diets, medical support, insurance) and hidden expenses (such as costs arising from delays) are often overlooked, leading to overall budget overruns. For example, adding a vegetarian at the last minute may require re-coordinating restaurant menus and even incurring additional fees.

**4. Insufficient Emergency Response Capability**

From sudden weather changes affecting outdoor activities to member health issues or visa delays, the ripple effects of emergencies are more pronounced in groups of 10 or more, requiring immediate support from a team with local networks. For instance, if a member gets injured while rafting, how to quickly contact local hospitals and adjust subsequent itineraries will directly impact the overall experience.

**5. Main Planner Falls Ill Suddenly, Affecting Itinerary Progress**

Large group tours often rely on the main planner for itinerary coordination and on-the-spot decision-making. If the main planner falls ill suddenly or experiences severe discomfort in Bali, it can halt itinerary progress, leaving other members unsure how to proceed. Another example is when the planner cannot join due to unforeseen circumstances just before departure, leaving members lacking detailed itinerary knowledge and emergency handling experience potentially unable to manage last-minute changes or urgent issues.
