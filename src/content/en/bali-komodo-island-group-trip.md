> This post is adapted from a travelogue shared by group member [Sandra Yun](https://www.facebook.com/share/p/R9sqkkpWfnrB6Bzr/):

This time, the six of us—all girls—traveled to Bali and Komodo Island in September 2023. We set off with high hopes for a fulfilling vacation, but the trip turned out to be full of surprises and challenges. Though it was just 6 days and 5 nights, and we didn't manage to complete our entire itinerary, everyone had a blast. Unfortunately, on Day 4, all of us came down with gastroenteritis, which seriously dampened our energy—by around 8:00 PM, we could only rest in bed. Even so, the journey was incredibly rewarding. Here are some of our real experiences, hoping they'll help future travelers!

## 1. Recommended Private Driver: Gede Arimbawa

What touched us most on this trip, aside from the stunning scenery, was our private driver, Gede Arimbawa! He was not only thoughtful but also incredibly kind and helpful. He respected our itinerary and offered practical advice at just the right moments, making our days in Bali go smoothly. Whether reminding us about travel times or helping book restaurants and activities, his service gave us immense peace of mind. Highly recommend this wonderful driver! 🥰

## 2. Airport Arrival: EVOA Fast Track

The entry process was quite smooth—we used the EVOA fast track and encountered no issues at all; the customs officers didn't ask any questions. However, waiting for our luggage took forever—nearly an hour!

> Editor's note: For the latest Bali entry procedures, please refer to [2025/08 Bali Entry Using the 'All Indonesia' Arrival Card: Understand the Latest Process in 1 Minute—From Landing to Customs to Baggage Claim](/blog/bali-all-indonesia-arrival-guide/)

## 3. Airport Wi-Fi Is Terribly Weak!

As soon as we arrived at Bali Airport, we noticed the Wi-Fi was incredibly weak, probably because too many people were connected at the time. Luckily, a friend in our group had Chunghwa Telecom's roaming service; otherwise, we wouldn't have known how to contact our driver. If you plan to buy a local Telkomsel SIM card, it costs around USD$10 to USD$16, including 14GB of data. However, the signal is still weak in some remote areas (like the Labuan Bajo island-hopping region).

Further reading: [【Essential for Bali Travel】How to Get Bali Internet/SIM Card?](/blog/bali-sim-card-esim-roaming-guide/)

## 4. Traffic Conditions in Bali

During this trip, we found that traffic jams in busy areas are really severe! Especially between 1 PM and 7:30 PM, the congestion in Uluwatu, Ubud, and Jimbaran is even more extreme. Although Google Maps estimates short travel times, the actual journey often takes 2.5 to 3 times longer. We recommend avoiding long-distance travel during these hours.

> Editor's note: [Don't Trust Google Maps in Bali!](/blog/bali-google-maps-warning/)

## 5. Tips for Booking Spas

If you plan to enjoy a spa in Bali, it's best to book in advance via WhatsApp or ask your private driver to help you book. Especially for highly-rated spas, walking in without a reservation often means no availability. If you ask your driver to book, they may need to pay a deposit first, so think carefully before canceling—otherwise, remember to return the deposit to the driver.

> Further reading: [【Essential Collection】Top-Rated Bali Spa Recommendations by Area for Easy Reference](/blog/spa/)

## 6. Getting Around the Island

Since our trip also included island hopping, we only had three days in Bali. Yet we chose to explore three different areas, and ended up spending every day stuck in traffic. If you want to visit more places, it's best to stay 1–2 days in the same area so you don't waste precious time on the road.

## 8. Weather and Queues at Rock Bar

If you want to visit Rock Bar, keep an eye on the weather! The day we went was a bit overcast, so while we missed the sunset, we barely had to queue. We arrived at 6 PM and found plenty of empty seats, letting us quietly enjoy the ocean view. Even without the sunset, the beautiful seascape was still mesmerizing.

## 9. Observations on Tipping

In Bali, we noticed that many restaurants would return the first tip we gave, only accepting it when we offered a second time. But if you give a small tip to housekeeping, bellboys, or activity instructors, they’ll be absolutely delighted!

> Read more: [Should You Tip in Bali? How to Tip? Tipping Guide for Various Occasions in Bali](/blog/bali-tipping-guide-driver-spa/)

## 10. Souvenirs: Bali Banana

If you’re looking for souvenirs, Bali Banana is a great choice. There are several shops in the city. Note that while the airport has three branches, they’re located at the domestic arrivals/departures and the international departures area outside the terminal. If you plan to buy them at the airport to take home, you’ll need to walk to those spots. You can also pick them up on Kuta Street on your last day before boarding.

## 12. The Importance of Bringing Stomach Medicine

This bout of gastroenteritis taught us the hard way: always pack stomach medicine when traveling! The first three days went smoothly, but on day four, whether from an infection or something we ate, everyone ended up vomiting and having diarrhea. Luckily, we had medicine on hand, and by the next day, we were all back to normal.

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

> Editor's note: These six ladies are professional healthcare workers who brought their own prescription meds. For most people, the over-the-counter medicine shown in the video above is sufficient.

## 13. Beware of the Motorcycle Scam!

In Ubud, we encountered some motorcycle taxi drivers who haggled with us. We booked two rides, but they took us to the wrong places and charged us again when we finally reached the correct destinations. This is likely the white-plate motorcycle scam that many travelers have mentioned. Be sure to confirm the route and price in advance.
