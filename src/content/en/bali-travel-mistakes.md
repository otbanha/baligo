## "Avoiding Tap Water and Ice" Is Just the First Step in Doing Your Homework

Not drinking untreated water is probably basic knowledge for anyone traveling to Southeast Asia.

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

But the nuances of traveling in Bali go far beyond this.

Tap water and ice are merely the baseline for "avoiding health hazards." What truly impacts your travel experience are things you might not even know to research—how to choose a massage parlor, which areas are walkable, and where to shop without getting ripped off.

This information isn't hard to find, but you need to know where to look.

***

## Bad massage therapist? You just haven't found the right place

Bali massages are one of the **best value-for-money experiences in all of Asia** — if you know where to go.

Ubud, Seminyak, and Jimbaran all have many highly-rated massage parlors with transparent pricing and well-trained therapists. However, the quality of small roadside shops can be hit or miss.

Before you go, check out our curated list of "🔗[**Bali Massage Recommendations**](/go/spa)" based on user reviews. You can choose by area, and the difference in experience can be night and day.

***

## Difficult roads? It's because you haven't figured out Bali's transportation

Bali's road conditions are notoriously "complicated."

Lack of proper sidewalks, reckless motorbikes, and pothole-ridden roads — these are no secret, but it doesn't mean you have to suffer by walking.

https://youtu.be/ePutHL_Ob1Q

Bali is **not a "walking city"** to begin with.

Here, getting around mainly relies on three methods: **private drivers, Grab/Gojek (ride-hailing apps), and renting a motorbike**.

If you have kids or prefer not to ride a bike yourself, hiring a private driver for a half-day or full-day is the most hassle-free option, and it's much cheaper than taking a taxi in Taiwan. Grab/Gojek is more suitable for short distances and on-demand rides.

{{block:包車}}

If you don't clarify this before your trip, no matter how mentally prepared you are, walking on the streets of Bali can still be quite overwhelming.

## The 'shocking' prices of clothes are because you're shopping in the wrong places

I completely understand her shock.

Some clothing stores in tourist areas do indeed have prices that make you feel like you're buying luxury items—but those prices are designed for tourists who don't know how to bargain.

Here are a few tips for shopping in Bali:

- **In tourist areas, start by halving the price and then negotiate**—it's normal to begin by asking for a 50% discount
- **Ubud Market and the alleys around Seminyak** offer many stylish yet affordable options
- **Knowing the market rate is crucial**: A reasonable price for a cotton-linen top is between IDR 50,000 to 100,000 (approximately USD$3 to $6)

If you buy without knowing the market rate, of course, you'll feel 'shocked'.
