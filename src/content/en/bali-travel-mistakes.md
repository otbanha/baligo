## 'Avoiding tap water and ice cubes' is just the first step of doing your homework

Not drinking raw water is pretty much common knowledge for anyone traveling to Southeast Asia.

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

But the nuances of traveling in Bali go way beyond that.

Tap water and ice are just the baseline for avoiding health pitfalls. What truly shapes your travel experience are the things you probably never thought to look up—how to pick a massage place, which areas are walkable, and where to shop without being treated like a cash cow.

This info isn't hard to find, but you have to **know to look for it**.

***

## Bad massage technique? You just haven't found the right spot

Massages in Bali are **one of the best value experiences in all of Asia**—if you know where to go.

Ubud, Seminyak, and Jimbaran all have plenty of well-reviewed massage spots with transparent pricing and skilled therapists. But the quality at random roadside shops can be wildly inconsistent.

Before you go, check out our roundup of user reviews in the 🔗[**Bali Massage Guide**](/go/spa)—you can browse by area, and the difference in experience can be night and day.

## The roads are tough because you haven't figured out how to get around Bali

Bali's road conditions are famously 'complicated.'

No proper sidewalks, scooters weaving everywhere, pothole-riddled streets—it's no secret, but that doesn't mean you have to suffer on foot.

https://youtu.be/ePutHL_Ob1Q

Bali was never meant to be a **walkable city**.

Getting around here mainly relies on three options: **private drivers, Grab/Gojek (ride-hailing apps), and renting a scooter**.

If you have kids or don’t want to drive yourself, hiring a private driver for a half-day or full-day trip is the most hassle-free choice, and the cost is much cheaper than taxis in Taiwan. Grab/Gojek is better for short distances when you need a ride on demand.

If you don’t figure this out before you go, no matter how mentally prepared you are, walking around Bali will still drive you crazy.

## The 'jaw-dropping' prices on clothes? That’s because you’re shopping in the wrong places

I totally get her shock.

Some clothing stores in tourist areas have price tags that make you feel like you’re buying luxury goods—but those prices are designed for tourists who don’t know how to bargain.

Here are a few shopping tips for Bali:

- **Start by offering at least half the marked price in tourist areas**—opening with a 50% discount is a normal starting point
- **Ubud Market and the small lanes around Seminyak** offer plenty of stylish yet affordable options
- **Knowing the market matters**: a decent cotton-linen top should cost between 50,000 and 100,000 IDR (roughly USD$2 to $3)

If you buy without knowing the market, of course you’ll be shocked by the prices.
