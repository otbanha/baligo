## 'Avoiding Tap Water and Ice' Is Just the First Step in Your Homework

Not drinking untreated water is probably common knowledge for anyone traveling to Southeast Asia.

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

But there's much more to traveling in Bali than meets the eye.

Tap water and ice cubes are merely the baseline for avoiding health hazards. What truly impacts your travel experience are the many things you might not even know to look into—how to choose a massage parlor, which areas are walkable, where to shop without getting ripped off.

This information isn't hard to find, but you need to **know to look for it**.

***

## Bad massage technique? You just haven't found the right place

Massages in Bali are **one of the best value-for-money experiences in all of Asia**—if you know where to go.

Ubud, Seminyak, and Jimbaran have plenty of massage parlors with consistently good reviews, transparent pricing, and well-trained therapists. But the quality at random roadside shops can be very hit or miss.

## Hard to get around? You just haven't figured out Bali's transportation

Bali's road conditions are notoriously 'complex'.

With incomplete sidewalks, motorcycles darting everywhere, and some roads full of potholes — it's no secret, but it doesn't mean you have to suffer by walking.

https://youtu.be/ePutHL_Ob1Q

Bali was never meant to be a 'walking city'.

Getting around here primarily relies on three methods: **private drivers, Grab/Gojek (ride-hailing apps), and renting scooters**.

If you have kids or prefer not to ride a scooter yourself, hiring a private driver for half or full day is the most hassle-free option, and it's significantly cheaper than taxis in Taiwan. Grab/Gojek is ideal for short-distance trips on demand.

If you don't understand this before arriving, even with the best mental preparation, navigating Bali's roads can be overwhelming.

## The 'shocking' prices of clothes are because you're shopping in the wrong places

I completely understand her surprise.

Some clothing stores in tourist areas really have prices that make you think you're buying designer goods — but those prices are designed for tourists who don't know how to bargain.

Here are some shopping tips for Bali:

- **Start by halving the price in tourist areas**, opening with a 50% discount is a normal starting point
- **Ubud Market and the alleys around Seminyak** offer many good quality and affordable options
- **Knowing the market price is crucial**: A reasonable price for a cotton-linen top is between 60,000 to 100,000 IDR (about USD$2 to $3)

If you buy without knowing the market price, of course you'll feel 'shocked by the price'.
