A woman wrote about her 4-day, 3-night trip to Bali, saying she "completely avoided tap water and ice"—which theoretically means she did her homework—but her final verdict was: **terrible experience, wouldn't want to visit again**.

She mentioned: massages felt like foot rubs, the roads were a nightmare to navigate, and the prices for clothes were shockingly high.

After reading this, I can't say she's wrong. Bali does have these issues. But what I really want to say is—**these pitfalls can actually be avoided.**

***

## "Avoiding Tap Water and Ice" Is Just the First Step in Doing Your Homework

Not drinking untreated water is probably common knowledge for anyone traveling to Southeast Asia.

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

But there's much more to know about traveling in Bali than just this.

Tap water and ice are merely the baseline for avoiding health hazards. What truly impacts your travel experience are things you might not even know to check—like how to choose a massage parlor, which areas are walkable, and where to shop without getting ripped off.

This information isn't hard to find, but you need to **know to look for it**.

***

## Bad massage? You just haven't found the right place

Massages in Bali are **one of the best value-for-money experiences in all of Asia**—if you know where to go.

Ubud, Seminyak, and Jimbaran all have plenty of consistently well-reviewed massage parlors with transparent pricing and well-trained therapists. But the quality at random roadside shops can be hit or miss.

Before your trip, check out our curated list of 🔗[**recommended Bali massage spots**](https://gobaligo.id/go/spa) sorted by area—the difference in experience can be night and day.

***

## Struggling with transportation? You just haven't figured out how to get around Bali

Bali's road conditions are notoriously 'complex'.

With incomplete sidewalks, motorcycles weaving recklessly, and some stretches riddled with potholes — it's no secret, but that doesn't mean you have to suffer by walking.

https://youtu.be/ePutHL_Ob1Q

Bali was never meant to be a 'walking city'.

Getting around here primarily relies on three methods: **private drivers, Grab/Gojek (ride-hailing apps), and renting scooters**.

If you have kids or prefer not to ride yourself, hiring a driver for half or full day is the most hassle-free option, costing significantly less than taxis in Taiwan. Grab/Gojek works best for short, on-demand trips.

If you don't understand this before arriving, no matter how mentally prepared you are, navigating Bali's roads can still be utterly frustrating.

## The 'shocking' clothing prices are because you're shopping in the wrong places

I completely understand her astonishment.

Some clothing stores in tourist areas really have prices that make you think you're buying designer goods — but those prices are designed for tourists who don't know how to bargain.

Here are some shopping tips for Bali:

- **Start by halving the price in tourist areas**, opening with a 50% discount is a normal starting point
- **Ubud Market and the alleys around Seminyak** offer many stylish yet affordable options
- **Knowing the market rate is crucial**: A reasonable price for a cotton-linen top is between IDR 50,000 to 100,000 (about USD$1.50 to $3)

Buying without knowing the market rate will naturally make you feel 'shocked'.
