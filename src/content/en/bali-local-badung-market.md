When visiting Bali, buying souvenirs can be overwhelming. Wood carvings, batik fabrics, scented candles, silver jewelry... walking through each artisan market, you’ll find so many things that you won’t know what to take home. But honestly, locals themselves never shop at these touristy souvenir stalls.

So where do locals go shopping? The answer is surprisingly simple—**the wet market**.

In recent years, more and more tourists have discovered this secret. Compared to souvenirs made for tourists, items bought from traditional markets are more authentic, cheaper, and more practical. Among the most popular are **Indonesian spices**.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-05/1779287700843-artikel_201905120521_badungtraditionalmarket.jpg)

### Even Ubud Hides a Morning Market You Can’t See

Here’s an interesting phenomenon: even in tourist-heavy areas like Ubud, the traditional market is bustling in the early morning. The only difference is—it **closes by 8 AM**.

This means that by the time most tourists wake up and head out for the day, the market has already vanished without a trace, as if it never existed. No wonder many people spend a whole week in Bali without realizing there’s a “parallel world” hidden near their hotel.

This is part of daily life in almost every neighborhood in Bali. Local families typically visit the morning market every two or three days for fresh fruits and vegetables, while bulkier items like rice, noodles, eggs, and cooking oil are purchased in larger quantities once a month or so. The morning market is an integral part of Balinese life, not a performance for anyone.

### Why Is Everyone Obsessed with Spices?

Fresh fruits and vegetables can't be brought on planes, which I believe everyone knows. But **dried spices can**, and Indonesia's spices are world-renowned.

Speaking of Indonesia, even Europeans fought over these spices for centuries. That aroma that hits you in the morning markets — turmeric, cloves, nutmeg, pandan leaves — is the same scent that drove Western explorers crazy over a hundred years ago.

What resonates most are the tourists who've taken cooking classes. After learning to make Babi Guling or Ayam Betutu in Bali, they try to recreate the flavors at home, only to find that the spices from supermarkets are completely different. **The only solution is to return to the source and buy the spices directly**.

Denpasar's **Badung Market** (Pasar Badung) is currently the hottest spot. According to local vendors, foreign visitors mainly come for spices, especially powdered vanilla and chili. Market data shows that nearly 17,000 international tourists visited in 2025, and this year's numbers are expected to surpass that.

### Hidden Ways to Explore Badung Market

Badung Market is Denpasar's largest traditional market, theoretically open 24 hours, but most crowds gather in the early morning. Right next door is the **Kumbasari Art Market**, so many people shop for spices first and then head over to pick up souvenirs, efficiently covering both needs in one trip.

Here's my recommended way to explore:

- **Morning**: Shop for spices at Badung Market and enjoy a local breakfast
- **Noon**: Cross the bridge to Kumbasari to check out batik, wood carvings, and other crafts
- **Afternoon**: Stroll along the historic **Jalan Gajah Mada** (Gajah Mada Street), Denpasar's old town area, which is packed with cafes

Denpasar actually has some of Bali's best coffee shops, like NADHI Heritage, The Ruum Coffee, and Rumah Momo. When you're tired, just sit down at one of these spots — it's the perfect rhythm for the day. If you have extra time, the **Bajra Sandhi Monument** and **Werdhi Buddha Art Center** are also nearby.

### Want to eat but afraid to venture out alone?

Honestly, traditional markets aren't for everyone. Most vendors only speak Indonesian, menus aren't in English, and figuring out how to order and pay can feel like a mini adventure. The variety of spices is overwhelming too — even chili powder comes in several different heat levels.

If you're interested in exploring local markets but worried about language barriers, **Kreneng Night Market** is a more accessible option. We previously wrote about [a food tour of Kreneng Night Market with a Chinese-speaking guide](https://gobaligo.id/blog/kreneng-night-market-food-tour/). It's where Denpasar locals really eat — from roast suckling pig to satay and Soto Ayam, you'll hardly see any tourists. Having a guide makes a huge difference, ensuring you don't miss those hidden stalls in the alleys.

### Final Thoughts

After spending time in Bali, you'll realize that the most memorable moments aren't the SPAs or beach sunsets, but these unplanned little things — the scent of spices in the morning market, vendors gesturing with you in Indonesian, and finally boarding your flight home with a bag of cloves and nutmeg.

Next time you're in Bali, if you've already checked off all the popular spots, dedicate half a day to the local market. Shift some of your souvenir budget from gift shops to spice stalls. Every time you cook at home, you'll be reminded of this trip. **Now that's a souvenir with real flavor.**
