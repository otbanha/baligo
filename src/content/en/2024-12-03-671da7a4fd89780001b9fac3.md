> Editor's Note: Siobak: A Balinese dish from Singaraja

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_5c5fd5848222adbc7cbc5b7ebac95bdf.jpg)

Siobak is an authentic dish originating from Singaraja, Bali. Made with pork, its name derives from Chinese culture (char siu), perfectly blending local and Chinese flavors. As Hinduism predominates in Bali where beef is considered sacred, pork has become a popular alternative.

This dish is seasoned with spices, with the pork first boiled at low temperature to remove any gaminess, then mixed with cloves, shallots, garlic and other spices to create its unique flavor.

You can find this dish in Singaraja and some specific restaurants in Denpasar. If you visit Bali, don't miss the chance to try Siobak's distinctive flavor!

Early in the morning, the staff at Ubud Element prepared a birthday cake and softly sang 'Happy Birthday,' giving us an incredibly heartwarming surprise in the Bali dawn. Next, we headed to Bali's famous sea temple, **Tanah Lot**. This sacred temple sits on a rocky outcrop in the sea and can only be approached during low tide. As we walked closer with the crowd, the sea breeze gently brushed past us, and the waves lapped against the rocks, splashing water around. The majestic and sacred scene left us breathless. ## **September 10: GWK Cultural Park & Kecak Fire Dance Tour**

![（張家偉提供）](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_1033d331528078fc690724949582df98.jpg)

As our trip neared its end, the surprises continued. We started the day with a visit to **GWK Cultural Park** (Garuda Wisnu Kencana), famous for its colossal Garuda statue and rich Balinese traditional dance performances. The park hosts different dance shows almost every hour, showcasing the beauty of Balinese culture. We watched two or three performances, and before each show, we used ChatGPT to translate the descriptions, which helped us understand the mythological stories behind the performances, adding to the enjoyment.

For lunch, we followed our driver's recommendation and dined at **Waroeng De Dusun**, which served delicious and affordable Indonesian-Chinese fusion cuisine. The flavors were refreshing, and we were thoroughly satisfied.

In the afternoon, we headed to **Uluwatu Temple**, but beware of the monkeys along the way! These clever and mischievous creatures are known to snatch (or swiftly steal) visitors' sunglasses, hats, and even hand sanitizers! We witnessed monkeys "unlocking" human belongings and chewing on them with gusto, only to have the items "ransomed" back by the park staff using food. So, it's best to leave valuables in the car to avoid trouble.

![（張家偉提供）](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_430945d058d78bbef4733cf6ac83c202.jpg)

As evening fell, we found our seats and prepared to witness the renowned **Kecak Fire Dance**. This performance, blending Balinese mythology with traditional dance, was staged on a semicircular, terraced platform, creating a visually stunning effect as the audience was surrounded by the fire dance. The entire performance had no instrumental accompaniment, only the deep, powerful rhythm of 'Kecak' chanted by dozens of dancers, creating a captivating background sound. This unique performance style completely immersed us. As the show neared its end, a spectator proposed to their beloved amidst this scene, and with the firelight illuminating the moment, this surprise proposal became one with the classic performance, adding a delightful anecdote to our journey.

## **Important PS: Arriving in Bali: From Smooth Customs to Luggage Troubles**

One thing worth mentioning is that when we arrived in Bali, everything seemed to go smoothly at first. We had already completed our visa application online and paid the tourism tax, and the customs process was surprisingly quick and seamless. Apart from a slight delay of 3 to 5 minutes during the new health declaration step, the overall procedure was quite straightforward and reassuring. However, the first hiccup of our journey quietly unfolded at the baggage carousel.

At that time, like other passengers, we anxiously waited for our luggage. Nearly an hour later, we finally saw our suitcase slowly sliding out on the conveyor belt. When I spotted my luggage, I quickly grabbed it. On the surface, everything appeared normal, so I left the airport directly. Little did I know, upon arriving at the hotel and opening the suitcase, I discovered that the zipper had been pried open, and even the TSA lock was damaged. Some small change and a golden trinket (though not real gold) were missing. Fortunately, important documents and credit cards were still intact, which gave me some relief, but I learned a valuable lesson from this experience: always keep valuables with you!
