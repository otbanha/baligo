> Editor's Note: Siobak: A Balinese delicacy from Singaraja

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_5c5fd5848222adbc7cbc5b7ebac95bdf.jpg)

Siobak is a traditional dish from Singaraja, Bali, made with pork. Its name originates from Chinese culture (char siu), perfectly blending local and Chinese flavors. Since beef is considered sacred in Hinduism, pork has become a popular choice.

The dish is seasoned with spices, and the pork is boiled at a low temperature to remove any gaminess, then mixed with cloves, shallots, garlic, and other spices, creating a unique flavor.

You can find this dish in Singaraja or at specific restaurants in Denpasar. If you have the chance to visit Bali, don't miss out on trying Siobak's distinctive flavor!

We followed the crowd, feeling the sea breeze and hearing the waves gently lap against the rocks, splashing water everywhere. The majestic and sacred sight left us breathless. ## ****September 10: GWK Cultural Park & Kecak Fire Dance Tour****

![（張家偉提供）](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_1033d331528078fc690724949582df98.jpg)

As our trip neared its end, the surprises continued. We started the day with a visit to the ****GWK Cultural Park**** (Garuda Wisnu Kencana), famous for its massive Garuda statue and rich displays of traditional Balinese dance performances. The park hosts different dance shows almost every hour, showcasing the beauty of Balinese culture. We watched two or three performances, and before each one, we used ChatGPT to translate the descriptions of the performances, which helped us better understand the mythological stories behind them and added to our enjoyment.

For lunch, we followed our driver's recommendation and dined at ****Waroeng De Dusun****. This restaurant served delicious and affordable Indonesian-style Chinese cuisine, and it certainly didn't disappoint. The flavors were light and satisfying.

In the afternoon, we headed to ****Uluwatu Temple****. Along the way, we had to be cautious of the monkeys! These clever and mischievous creatures are known for stealing (or swiftly snatching) visitors' sunglasses, hats, and even hand sanitizer! We witnessed monkeys 'unlocking' people's belongings and happily chewing on them, only to have the items 'redeemed' by park staff with food. So, it's best to leave valuables in the car to avoid any trouble.

![（張家偉提供）](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_430945d058d78bbef4733cf6ac83c202.jpg)

As evening fell, we found our seats and prepared to watch the renowned **Kecak Fire Dance**. This performance blends Balinese mythology with traditional dance, presented on a semi-circular tiered stage that immerses the audience in a visually stunning experience surrounded by fire. The entire show is performed without musical instruments, relying instead on the deep, rhythmic chanting of 'cak cak cak' by dozens of dancers, creating a mesmerizing backdrop of sound. This unique performance style completely captivated us. As the show neared its end, a surprise proposal took place amidst the flames, blending the romantic moment with the classic performance and adding a delightful twist to our journey.

## **[Important PS] Arriving in Bali: From Smooth Customs to Luggage Troubles**

One thing worth mentioning is that when we arrived in Bali, everything seemed to go smoothly at first. We had already obtained our visas online and paid the tourism tax, and the customs process was surprisingly quick and seamless. Except for a slight delay of 3 to 5 minutes during the new health declaration procedure, the overall steps were quite simple and reassuring. However, the first hiccup of our journey quietly unfolded at the baggage carousel.

At that time, like other travelers, we anxiously waited for our luggage. Nearly an hour later, we finally saw our suitcase slowly sliding out on the conveyor belt. When I spotted my luggage, I quickly grabbed it. On the surface, everything seemed fine, so I left the airport directly. Little did I know, upon arriving at the hotel and opening my suitcase, I discovered that the zipper had been pried open, and even the TSA lock was broken. Some small change and a small golden trinket (though not real gold) were missing. Fortunately, important documents and credit cards were still there, which gave me some relief. But I definitely learned a lesson from this experience: it's best to carry valuables with you!
