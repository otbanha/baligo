> Adapted from fellow traveler [Cheng Yi Lin's travelogue](https://www.facebook.com/share/p/1cFj662JSR/)

The moment I arrived in Uluwatu, I truly understood what is meant by 'the temperament of the Indian Ocean.'

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350324393-671176730_122284511336018343_7573830652949289882_n.jpg)

For this Bali trip, we chose to hire a car and drive south from the bustling Seminyak, through narrow country lanes, all the way to the southern tip of the peninsula. The driver slowed down on the final stretch and pointed out the window, 'Here comes Uluwatu.' Before I could react, a two-meter-high wave crashed against the reef—not a gentle lapping wave, but a forceful punch against the limestone, exploding into a spray of water. The power was raw and rugged, as if the ocean was asserting its dominance.

A few black dots were skillfully cutting through the wave walls—real pros surfing. They crouched, accelerated, and slipped into the wave tubes, their movements fluid and seamless. I stood at the cliff edge watching for a while, feeling a strange reverence—not fear, but a clear understanding that this place doesn't belong to you; you're just an observer.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350376308-672095457_122284511564018343_2046526594648930330_n.jpg)

## The Seven-Floor Challenge

The hotel we stayed in was quite audacious, built directly on the cliff. Staying here felt more like a physical test than a vacation.

The car parked at the top road, and I looked down with my luggage—stone steps winding down, roughly the height of at least seven floors. We knew to contact the hotel beforehand, but we arrived fifteen minutes early and had to carry our luggage down ourselves. As I was panting and dragging my suitcase down, two staff members came running up from below, smiling as they took over the heavy luggage and descended another six floors. Even following them empty-handed made my legs weak, yet they carried nearly twenty kilograms with steps as steady as if they were walking on flat ground.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350404778-672211150_122284511414018343_6399489268464153983_n.jpg)

After check-in, I tipped them USD$1, sincerely believing it was the most worthwhile expense of the entire trip. Honestly, this place isn't suitable for the elderly or children—the staircase alone could halt the trip. But if you're young or an adventurer with some stamina, this place will be where you dream of returning.

## The Golden Illusion

When I walked into the room, I stood frozen at the doorway for a good ten seconds.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350442358-mixcollage-16-apr-2026-09-15-pm-7389.jpg)

The floor-to-ceiling windows faced the Indian Ocean without any obstructions, flooding the entire room with the blue of the sea. There was no balcony, no railing to interrupt the view—it felt like sitting right on the edge of the ocean. I had booked the room a month and a half in advance, and only the smallest room was left; the ones with terraces were long gone. But honestly, it was enough. This window alone was worth every penny.

In the evening, just before sunset, I was lying on the bed daydreaming when I suddenly noticed the room changing colors. The setting sun cast a slanting beam of warm yellow light from the horizon, gradually turning orange-red, until the entire room seemed drenched in gold powder—the walls, the bedsheets, even the air seemed sprinkled with golden flecks. It was surreal, like a dream sequence you’d see in slow motion in a movie.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350485086-mixcollage-16-apr-2026-09-13-pm-578.jpg)

I practically leapt out of bed, slipped on my sandals, and dashed toward the beach.

## Healing Moments in the Intertidal Zone

After the tide receded, the rocky platform emerged, and the tidal pools reflected the sunset like shattered mirrors. I carefully walked across the slippery rocks, the seawater warm, the breeze carrying the scent of salt and seaweed. In the distance, a few surfers were packing up their boards, walking barefoot across the sand with their shortboards slung over their shoulders, their silhouettes stretched long by the setting sun.

I sat there on the rocks watching the sunset for nearly an hour. No photos, no scrolling on my phone—just watching the sun sink bit by bit into the sea, its color shifting from golden yellow to blood orange, until only a purple afterglow lingered on the horizon. It was pure therapy.

## The Lullaby of Waves in a Bamboo House

It wasn’t until I returned to the room at night that I truly realized—the entire structure was made of bamboo.

The ceiling is woven from bamboo, the walls are pieced together with bamboo strips, and when the wind blows, the entire house emits subtle creaks. The soundproofing isn't great, but the sound of the waves in Uluwatu is so loud that you don't need it—it's not background noise, it's the main character. The low-frequency rumble of waves crashing against the rocks, mixed with the delicate sound of splashing spray, creates a kind of ancient, repetitive rhythm.

The hotel thoughtfully placed earplugs by the bedside, with a small card that read: 'Ocean sound can be the perfect lullaby.' I initially thought this was a bit pretentious, but that night I didn't even scroll through my phone. I lay down and soon fell deeply asleep. It was a strange feeling—not just falling asleep, but more like being 'taken away' by the sound of the waves—something like sound therapy, with the body relaxing, consciousness slowly dissolving, and then it was gone.

Dreamless night, or rather, the whole night was filled with dreams of the sea.

## Monkey Steals Fries and the Absurd Theater of Nature

Breakfast was decent, with fresh tropical fruits and freshly made Indonesian fried noodles, but the pizza for lunch was just average—soft crust, ordinary toppings. But none of that mattered because the highlight of lunchtime was—the monkeys.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350618459-672430407_122284511756018343_2605697048675478600_n.jpg)

The monkeys in Uluwatu are notorious, something I knew before arriving, but seeing it firsthand was absurdly funny. We ordered a plate of fries and left it on the table while we went to get drinks. When we returned, a female monkey was sitting firmly in my chair, holding a handful of fries in each hand, with three more stuffed in her mouth. She looked at me as I approached, didn't run away, just kept chewing while staring straight at me, as if to say, 'So what?'

Even more entertaining was the scene by the sea. I saw a monkey swimming in the ocean—yes, swimming. It paddled with a very standard doggy paddle, head held high, looking quite at ease. But upon closer inspection, there were three or four monkeys on the shore roaring, seemingly forbidding it from coming ashore. The poor swimmer was forced to swim back and forth not far from the shore, chased away whenever it tried to land. Eventually, it swam further out and disappeared into the waves.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350532473-mixcollage-16-apr-2026-09-11-pm-7409.jpg)

I don't know if it ever made it back to shore. This was probably the most absurd yet authentic theater of nature in Uluwatu.

## Surf Camp: A Pro's Playground

The area around Uluwatu is mostly filled with white tourists, rarely seeing group tours, creating a very chill atmosphere, like a hidden small community. Where we stayed was itself a surf camp, with the surf spot right downstairs. Classes were held promptly at 6:30 AM and 9:30 AM every day, with one-on-one sessions lasting two hours for about IDR 410,000 (roughly USD$27), which is quite reasonable given the quality of the surf spots.

But I have to say this seriously: **Beginners really shouldn't come here.**

The waves here aren't the kind you can stand and play in. They're powerful, rhythmic, and temperamental. I watched a lesson from the shore - as the instructor led students paddling out, just past the breakers, a nearly two-meter wave came crashing down, flipping the student underwater several times and sending the board flying three meters away. The instructor calmly retrieved both person and board, but that student spent the next half hour just treading water and catching their breath, with no energy left to chase waves.

I'd recommend being able to catch green waves (unbroken wave faces) on your own before coming here, otherwise the ocean will truly 'teach you a lesson.' This isn't a practice ground - it's an exam hall.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350803625-673713602_122284511372018343_9115007488135551525_n.jpg)

## Epilogue

On the morning I left Uluwatu, I stood at the cliff edge watching the waves one last time. Just as fierce as when I arrived, two-meter reef breaks still came crashing up with thunderous force. But now I'd grown accustomed to that sound, even found it somewhat comforting.

I took one last look at the bamboo room, its floor-to-ceiling windows reflecting the morning light, the sea still that same deep blue. I remembered the words on that little card - the sound of the sea truly is the best lullaby. More than just helping you sleep, it reminds you that in this vast world, you can temporarily surrender yourself to an ocean, to a little room on a cliff, to seven flights of stairs and a troop of swimming monkeys.

Uluwatu isn't for everyone. But if you happen to be someone who doesn't mind trouble, who isn't afraid of waves, who doesn't care if monkeys steal your fries - this will be the most worthwhile dream you've ever had.
