> Adapted from fellow traveler [Cheng Yi Lin's travelogue](https://www.facebook.com/share/p/1cFj662JSR/)

The moment I arrived in Uluwatu, I truly understood what is meant by 'the temper of the Indian Ocean.'

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350324393-671176730_122284511336018343_7573830652949289882_n.jpg)

For this Bali free-and-easy trip, we chose to drive southward by chartered car, passing through the bustling streets of Seminyak and the narrow country lanes, swaying and bumping towards the southern tip of the peninsula. The driver slowed down on the last stretch, pointing out the window and saying, 'Here comes Uluwatu.' Before I could react, a two-meter-high reef wave crashed into view—not the gentle lapping of the shore, but a solid punch against the limestone, exploding into a spray of water. The force was primitive, rugged, like the sea asserting its sovereignty.

Several black dots were skillfully cutting through the wave walls on the sea, real pro-level surfers. They crouched, accelerated, and dived into the wave tubes, their movements fluid and seamless. I stood at the cliff edge watching for a while, feeling a strange awe—not fear, but a clear understanding that this place does not belong to you, you are merely an observer.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350376308-672095457_122284511564018343_2046526594648930330_n.jpg)

## The Seven-Floor Challenge

The hotel we stayed in this time was quite capricious, built directly on the cliff. To say 'stay' is more like a physical test.

The car stopped at the topmost roadside, and I looked down with my luggage—the stone steps winding down, roughly estimating at least seven floors high. I knew before departure that we should contact the hotel first, but we arrived fifteen minutes early and had to carry our luggage down ourselves. Just as I was panting and dragging my suitcase down the stairs, two staff members came running up from below, smilingly took over the heavy luggage, and turned to walk down another six floors. Following them empty-handed made my legs weak, yet they carried nearly twenty kilograms of luggage as steadily as if walking on flat ground.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350404778-672211150_122284511414018343_6399489268464153983_n.jpg)

After check-in, I tipped them USD$1, sincerely believing it was the most worthwhile expense of the entire trip. Honestly, this place is not suitable for the elderly or children; just that staircase could halt the trip. But if you're young or an adventurer with some stamina, this place will be where you dream with a smile.

## The Golden Illusion Outside the Window

When I walked into the room, I stood frozen at the doorway for about ten seconds.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350442358-mixcollage-16-apr-2026-09-15-pm-7389.jpg)

The floor-to-ceiling windows faced the Indian Ocean unobstructed, flooding the entire room with the blue of the sea. There was no balcony, no railing to interfere with the view—it felt like sitting right on the edge of the ocean. I had booked the room a month and a half in advance, and only the smallest room type was left; the ones with terraces had already been snatched up. But honestly, it was enough. This window alone was worth every penny.

In the evening, just before sunset, I was lying on the bed daydreaming when I suddenly noticed the room changing colors. The setting sun cast a slanting beam from the horizon, first a warm yellow streak, then slowly turning orange-red, until the entire room seemed drenched in gold—the walls, the bedsheets, even the air felt like it was sprinkled with golden dust. The scene was so surreal, like a dreamlike moment that would be captured in slow motion in a movie.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350485086-mixcollage-16-apr-2026-09-13-pm-578.jpg)

I practically sprang out of bed, slipped on my sandals, and rushed toward the beach.

## Healing Moments in the Intertidal Zone

The rocky platform emerged after the tide receded, and the tidal pools reflected the sunset like shattered mirrors. I carefully walked across the slippery rocks, the seawater warm, the breeze carrying the scent of salt and seaweed. In the distance, a few surfers were packing up their boards, carrying their shortboards barefoot across the sand, their silhouettes stretched long by the setting sun.

I sat there on the rocks watching the sunset for nearly an hour. No photos, no scrolling on my phone—just watching the sun slowly sink into the sea, its color shifting from golden yellow to blood orange, until only a purple afterglow lingered on the horizon. It was incredibly healing.

## The Lullaby of Waves in the Bamboo House

It wasn’t until I returned to the room at night that I truly realized—the entire house was made of bamboo.

The ceiling is woven from bamboo, the walls pieced together with bamboo strips, and when the wind blows, the entire house emits a faint creaking sound. The soundproofing isn't great, but the waves at Uluwatu are so loud that you don't need it—they're not background noise, they're the main event. The low-frequency rumble of waves crashing against the rocks, mixed with the delicate sound of spray, creates a kind of ancient, repetitive rhythm.

The hotel thoughtfully placed earplugs by the bedside, accompanied by a small card that read: 'Ocean sound can be the perfect lullaby.' I initially found the sentiment a bit pretentious, but that night, I didn't even scroll through my phone. I lay down and soon drifted into sleep. It was a strange sensation—not just falling asleep, but being 'carried away' by the sound of the waves. It felt a bit like sound therapy, with my body relaxing and my consciousness slowly dissolving until it was gone.

I dreamt nothing that night, or rather, the entire night was a dream of the sea.

## Monkeys Stealing Fries and the Absurd Theater of Nature

Breakfast was decent, with fresh tropical fruits and freshly made Indonesian fried noodles, but the pizza for lunch was just average—soft crust, mediocre toppings. However, none of that mattered because the main event at lunchtime was the monkeys.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350618459-672430407_122284511756018343_2605697048675478600_n.jpg)

The monkeys at Uluwatu are notorious, something I knew before arriving, but seeing it firsthand was absurdly funny. We ordered a plate of fries and left it on the table while we went to grab drinks. When we returned, a female monkey was comfortably seated in my chair, clutching fries in both hands and stuffing three more into her mouth. She didn't run when I approached; instead, she chewed while staring straight at me, as if saying, 'What?'

The real spectacle was by the sea. I saw a monkey swimming—yes, swimming. It paddled with a perfect doggy paddle, its head held high, looking completely at ease. But upon closer inspection, three or four monkeys on the shore were roaring, seemingly forbidding it from coming ashore. The poor swimmer was forced to paddle back and forth near the shore, chased away whenever it tried to land. Eventually, it swam further out and disappeared into the waves.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350532473-mixcollage-16-apr-2026-09-11-pm-7409.jpg)

I don't know if it ever made it back to shore. It was perhaps the most absurd yet authentic theater of nature at Uluwatu.

## Surf Camp: A Pro's Playground

The area around Uluwatu is mostly filled with white tourists, with few group travelers, creating a chill, secluded community vibe. The place we stayed at was a surf camp, with a surf spot right downstairs. Classes were held promptly at 6:30 AM and 9:30 AM every day, with one-on-one sessions lasting two hours for about IDR 410,000 (around USD$27). Given the quality of the surf spots, the price was quite reasonable.

But let me be honest: **beginners really shouldn't come here.**

The waves here aren't the kind you can casually stand and play in. They're powerful, rhythmic, and temperamental. I watched a lesson from the shore—the coach led the students out, and just as they passed the breaking waves, a nearly two-meter wave crashed down, flipping the student underwater several times and sending the board flying three meters away. The coach calmly retrieved both the person and the board, but the student spent the next half-hour treading water and catching their breath, completely exhausted and unable to chase any waves.

I recommend coming only if you can already catch green waves (unbroken wave walls) on your own. Otherwise, the ocean will truly 'teach you a lesson.' This isn't a practice field—it's an exam hall.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776350803625-673713602_122284511372018343_9115007488135551525_n.jpg)

## Epilogue

On the morning I left Uluwatu, I stood at the cliff edge and watched the waves one last time. They were just as fierce as when I arrived, with two-meter-high reef waves crashing thunderously. But by then, I'd grown accustomed to the sound—it even felt somewhat comforting.

I glanced back at the bamboo room, its floor-to-ceiling windows reflecting the morning light, the sea still that same deep blue. I remembered the words on that little card—the sound of the ocean truly is the best lullaby. It doesn't just help you sleep; it reminds you that in this vast world, you can temporarily surrender yourself to a stretch of sea, to a little room on a cliff, to seven flights of stairs, and to a troop of swimming monkeys.

Uluwatu isn't for everyone. But if you happen to be someone who doesn't mind a bit of trouble, isn't afraid of waves, and doesn't mind monkeys stealing your fries, this place will be the most worthwhile dream you've ever had.
