## Exploring Bali's Two Major Rice Terraces

Bali is renowned not only for its enchanting beaches, vibrant cultural activities, and rich natural landscapes but also for its iconic rice terraces. The unique rice terraces offer stunning visual delights while showcasing local agricultural culture and social structures. **Bali's two major rice terrace areas—Tegalalang Rice Terraces** and **Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**—are considered world wonders, allowing visitors to deeply understand Balinese rural life and experience the serene beauty of nature.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_681188d17bd939f1995e2b811dd9ea8d.jpg)

## **What are Bali Rice Terraces?**

Taiwan has rice terraces too! So why travel all the way to Bali to see them? Behind Bali's rice terraces lies an ancient and intricate irrigation system—the **Subak System**. Originating in the 11th century, the Subak System has been designated as a **UNESCO World Cultural Heritage**. It is not only the cornerstone of local agriculture but also an integral part of Balinese culture and social structure.

Below, we’ll take a closer look at the features of these two major rice terrace areas and the best ways to explore them.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_4e4eb6216ccf69fd8413b958ab5010ec.png)

## **Tegalalang Rice Terrace**

Located north of Ubud, the Tegalalang Rice Terraces are one of Bali’s most iconic tourist attractions. Their stunning and picturesque landscapes, along with a variety of surrounding activities, draw countless visitors, making it a must-visit spot for photography enthusiasts and nature lovers.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_04c9efaa4e64a1161cd73552f0fa0eaf.jpg)

### **Features of Tegalalang Rice Terrace**

The most striking feature of Tegalalang Rice Terrace is its **spectacular scenery and unique geographical location**. The terraces vary in height and are mostly situated on hillsides. The Subak irrigation system allows water to flow smoothly through each layer of the paddies, creating beautiful reflections, especially during sunrise and sunset, making the scene dreamlike and captivating. Additionally, the surroundings of Tegalalang's terraces are filled with diverse tropical plants, immersing visitors in a lush green paradise.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_4b230e91b2e502eebc023b2f37cd16aa.jpg)

### **Tips for Visiting Tegalalang Rice Terrace**

- **Visit in the Morning or Evening**: It's recommended to visit during early morning or late afternoon when the light is soft, offering the best visual effects. The cooler temperatures during these times also help avoid the intense midday sun.
- **Opening Hours**: Daily from 6:00 AM to 7:00 PM.
- **Join a Local Tour**: Consider joining a local tour to learn about the history and workings of the Subak System and gain deeper insights into the local rice farming culture.
- **Try the Swing**: The "Bali Giant Swing" near Tegalalang Rice Terrace has become a网红打卡 spot. Experience the thrill of swinging while enjoying the surrounding majestic terrace views.
- **Be Cautious on the Paths**: The trails at Tegalalang Rice Terrace are quite steep, so wear comfortable shoes and watch your step for safety.

### **Tegalalang Rice Terrace Tickets and Transportation**

Currently, the ticket price for Tegalalang Rice Terrace ranges from IDR 10,000 to IDR 50,000 (depending on different areas with separate ticket booths). If you're departing from Ubud city center, it's very convenient to reach by car or motorbike, taking approximately 20 to 30 minutes.

## **Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_03240ac24d35267825628f03033462da.jpg)

Located in central Bali, the Jatiluwih Rice Terraces are a vast and well-preserved area of rice paddies. Compared to Tegallalang, Jatiluwih offers a more expansive and uninterrupted view of terraced fields, allowing visitors to immerse themselves in a sea of green. The scenery here is rustic and perfect for travelers who seek tranquility and natural beauty.

### **Features of Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_489574c7808e044a63eb6e36357de053.jpg)

Jatiluwih Rice Terraces are listed as a **UNESCO World Cultural Heritage Site**, showcasing the essence of the Subak irrigation system through their natural and harmonious layout. The terraces are vast, surrounded by mountains, and offer a tranquil atmosphere. The rice fields display a variety of color changes throughout the seasons, from lush green to golden yellow, ensuring a beautiful and majestic natural experience no matter when you visit.

### **Tips for Visiting Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

- **Circular Trail Hiking**: Jatiluwih Rice Terraces feature several trails of varying lengths, perfect for hiking. You can choose any trail to stroll through the rice fields, enjoying the fresh air and stunning natural scenery.
- **Bike Rentals**: Local bike rental services are available, allowing you to cycle through the terraces, combining exercise with scenic views—a favorite among foreign tourists.
- **Taste Local Cuisine**: Nearby traditional restaurants offer Balinese dishes, where you can enjoy meals while taking in the terrace views and experiencing local hospitality.

### **Jatiluwih Rice Terrace Tickets and Transportation**

The entrance fee for Jatiluwih Rice Terraces is approximately IDR 40,000. From major tourist areas like Seminyak or Kuta, it takes about 2 hours by car. It's recommended to hire a private car or join a local tour to save on transportation hassles.

## **Comparison of Tegallalang and Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

Tegallalang and Jatiluwih Rice Terraces each have their unique features. Tegallalang is closer to Ubud, making it suitable for short visits, while Jatiluwih is more expansive, ideal for those who enjoy in-depth exploration and tranquil nature. Whichever terrace you choose, you'll experience Bali's unique agricultural landscape and rich cultural heritage.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_ac65861b32e9c3e850c3f44aff108470.jpg)

## **Best Seasons to Visit and Recommendations**

Bali's rice terraces are beautiful year-round, with each season offering a unique view. Generally, the dry season (May to September) is the best time to visit, as the weather is pleasant and sunny, making the terraces look their most spectacular.

If considering the growth of rice seedlings, **February to April** is when the seedlings are flourishing, covering the terraces with a light green-yellow hue, resembling a beautiful green carpet.

During **June to July** (locally known as Sasih Sada), farmers begin harvesting, and you’ll witness them busily working in the fields, enjoying the sight of abundant rice harvests. This is the perfect time to experience local agricultural life.

However, these timings aren't fixed. Local farmers adjust their schedules based on weather conditions and the growth of the rice.

## **Photography Tips: How to Capture the Beauty of the Rice Terraces**

- **Choose the Golden Hour**: The warm, soft light during sunrise or sunset adds depth to your photos. The period from 8 to 10 AM is ideal for shooting, as the sunlight is moderate, creating gentle shadows and avoiding overexposure. The golden hour around 4 to 5 PM bathes the terraces in the glow of the setting sun, turning the rice fields into a warm golden hue, creating a dreamy atmosphere.
- **Use a Wide-Angle Lens**: The expansive landscape of the terraces is best captured with a wide-angle lens, allowing you to fully capture the grandeur of the layered fields. If using a smartphone, switch to panorama mode to capture the full breadth of the terraces.
- **Incorporate Foreground and Composition**: Include foreground elements like rice stalks or local villagers to add storytelling and depth to your photos. If you encounter farmers at work, seize the moment to capture their labor, adding a rich sense of life to your shots. You can also photograph small temples, Subak irrigation channels, or friends dressed in traditional Balinese attire to enhance the cultural atmosphere.
- **Utilize Layering**: The terraces’ stepped structure allows you to use foreground, midground, and background to create a sense of depth in your photos.
- **Leverage Contrast**: The vibrant green rice fields against the blue sky and white clouds create a striking contrast, making for a compelling composition.
- **Experiment with Low Angles**: Shooting from a low angle or crouching down to capture rice stalks adds dimension and highlights the intricate details of the fields.
- **Respect the Locals**: Always ask for permission before photographing farmers or villagers (a simple way to ask is: "Foto, foto?").

## Additional Information: What is the Subak System?

The Subak system mentioned above is Bali’s unique traditional irrigation method for rice terraces, with a history spanning over a thousand years. Since the 11th century, Subak has been at the heart of local agriculture and was designated as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**. It’s not just a technical irrigation system but also a cultural symbol that integrates religious beliefs, social structures, and environmental conservation. Rooted in Buddhist and water deity worship, the Subak system emphasizes harmony between humans and nature, fostering a spirit of cooperation and sharing among farming communities.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0ca73e6ea28937fc21a3beff6cc1905f.jpg)

The Subak system is collectively managed by local farmers, ensuring fair distribution of water resources to each terrace area. Farmers organize meetings through Subak associations to coordinate water allocation and hold ceremonies each planting season to thank the water and mountain deities for their blessings. The Subak system also effectively prevents water disputes and strengthens solidarity among farming communities.

![有名的Kanto Lampo瀑布並不是一個自然形成的瀑布，是因為蘇巴克灌溉系統的水溢出而形成](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_9ce193a07d5b914c7677aa9920e7b859.jpg)

The famous Kanto Lampo Waterfall is not a naturally formed waterfall but was created by the overflow of water from the Subak irrigation system.

### The Water Source of Subak

The water for the Subak system primarily comes from Bali's volcanic mountains, especially Mount Bratan and Mount Batur/Mount Agung. These volcanic regions receive abundant rainfall, providing a steady water supply to local streams and rivers. The Subak system ingeniously utilizes these natural water flows, channeling them to distribute water across terraces, ensuring stable rice cultivation.

### The Water Distribution Process

1. **Mountain Water Sources**: Rainwater collects in lakes within volcanic mountain ranges and flows downstream through rivers.
2. **Main Irrigation Channels**: The Subak system uses rivers and streams to construct main irrigation channels that divert water into the terraces.
3. **Distribution Channels**: The main water flow is distributed to each terrace area, with distribution channels ensuring even water supply to every level.
4. **Two Major Lakes Supplying the Terraces**: Tegallalang’s water primarily comes from Lake Batur (Danau Batur), while Jatiluwih’s water is sourced from Lake Beratan (Danau Beratan) near the water temple.

![水神廟所在的布拉坦湖供應著賈提魯維梯田區的灌溉用水](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_cb2b1f1de756cb57dc9c603d2552ddba.jpg)

Lake Bratan, where the water temple is located, supplies irrigation water to the Jatiluwih Terrace area.

### The Sacred Water Ritual

Subak is not just a technical irrigation system; it is deeply intertwined with local religious beliefs. Farmers pray to the water deity at the start of the planting season, holding ceremonies to express gratitude for the natural water sources and to pray for a bountiful harvest. These rituals symbolize the farmers' reverence and gratitude towards nature, adding a mystical aura to the terraced landscape.
