## Exploring Bali's Two Major Rice Terraces

Bali is famous for its beautiful beaches, lively cultural events, and rich natural scenery, but its rice terraces are among its most iconic attractions. These unique rice terraces offer breathtaking views and showcase the local agricultural culture and social structure. Bali's two major rice terrace areas—the Tegalalang Rice Terraces and the Jatiluwih Rice Terraces—are world wonders that allow visitors to delve into rural Balinese life and experience the serene beauty of nature.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_681188d17bd939f1995e2b811dd9ea8d.jpg)

## What Are Bali's Rice Terraces?

Bali has rice terraces, and so does Taiwan! So why travel to Bali to see them? Behind Bali's rice terraces lies an ancient and intricate irrigation system known as the Subak system. Dating back to the 11th century, the Subak system has been recognized by UNESCO as a World Cultural Heritage site. It is not only the foundation of local agriculture but also an integral part of Bali's culture and social structure.

Below, we’ll take a closer look at the features of these two major rice terrace areas and the best ways to explore them.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_4e4eb6216ccf69fd8413b958ab5010ec.png)

## Tegalalang Rice Terraces

Located north of Ubud, the Tegalalang Rice Terraces are one of Bali’s most iconic tourist attractions. Their stunning and picturesque landscapes, along with a variety of surrounding activities, draw countless visitors, making it a must-visit spot for photography enthusiasts and nature lovers.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_04c9efaa4e64a1161cd73552f0fa0eaf.jpg)

### Features of the Tegalalang Rice Terraces

The Tegallalang Rice Terraces are renowned for their **stunning landscapes and unique geographical location**. The terraces vary in height and are mostly situated on hillsides, with the Subak irrigation system ensuring water flows smoothly through each layer of paddy fields, creating beautiful reflections. This effect is particularly magical during sunrise and sunset. Additionally, the surrounding area is lush with diverse tropical plants, making it feel like a green paradise.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_4b230e91b2e502eebc023b2f37cd16aa.jpg)

### **Tips for Visiting Tegallalang Rice Terraces**

- **Visit in the Morning or Evening**: It’s best to visit during the early morning or late afternoon when the light is soft and the views are at their most picturesque. The cooler temperatures during these times also make the experience more comfortable compared to the midday heat.
- **Opening Hours**: Daily from 6:00 AM to 7:00 PM.
- **Join a Local Tour**: Consider joining a local guided tour to learn about the history and workings of the Subak system and gain deeper insights into the local rice farming culture.
- **Try the Giant Swing**: The Bali Swing near the Tegallalang Rice Terraces has become a popular Instagram spot. Experience the thrill of swinging while enjoying the breathtaking views of the terraces.
- **Be Cautious on the Paths**: The trails in Tegallalang can be steep, so wear comfortable shoes and watch your step.

### **Ticket Prices and Transportation to Tegallalang Rice Terraces**

Currently, the entrance fee for Tegallalang Rice Terraces ranges from 10,000 to 50,000 IDR (depending on the specific area and ticket booth). If traveling from Ubud, it’s convenient to reach by car or motorbike, taking approximately 20 to 30 minutes.

## **Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_03240ac24d35267825628f03033462da.jpg)

Located in central Bali, the Jatiluwih Rice Terraces are a vast and well-preserved area of rice paddies. Compared to Tegallalang, Jatiluwih offers a more expansive and uninterrupted view of terraced fields, allowing visitors to immerse themselves in a sea of green. The scenery here is rustic and perfect for travelers who seek tranquility and natural beauty.

### **Highlights of Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_489574c7808e044a63eb6e36357de053.jpg)

The Jatiluwih Rice Terraces are listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, showcasing the essence of the Subak irrigation system through their naturally harmonious layout. Spread across a vast area, these terraces are surrounded by mountains, offering a serene and peaceful atmosphere. The rice fields display a variety of color changes throughout the seasons, from lush green to golden yellow, ensuring a beautiful and majestic natural experience no matter when you visit.

### **Tips for Visiting Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

- **Circular Trail Hiking**: Jatiluwih Rice Terraces feature several trails of varying lengths, perfect for hiking. You can choose any trail and stroll through the rice fields, enjoying the fresh air and stunning natural scenery.
- **Bike Rentals**: Local bike rental services are available, allowing you to cycle through the terraces while exercising and taking in the views. This method is particularly popular among foreign tourists.
- **Savor Local Cuisine**: Nearby traditional restaurants offer Balinese dishes. You can enjoy your meal while admiring the terrace views and experiencing local hospitality.

### **Ticket Prices and Transportation for Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

The entrance fee for Jatiluwih Rice Terraces is approximately IDR 40,000. From major tourist areas like Seminyak or Kuta, it takes about 2 hours by car. It's recommended to hire a private car or join a local tour to save on transportation hassles.

## **Comparison Between Tegallalang and Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

Tegallalang and Jatiluwih Rice Terraces each have their unique features. Tegallalang is closer to Ubud, making it suitable for short visits, while Jatiluwih is more expansive, ideal for those who enjoy in-depth exploration and tranquil nature. Whichever terrace you choose, you'll experience Bali's unique agricultural landscape and rich cultural heritage.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_ac65861b32e9c3e850c3f44aff108470.jpg)

## **Best Seasons to Visit and Recommendations**

Bali's rice terraces are beautiful year-round, with each season offering a unique view. Generally, the dry season (May to September) is the best time to visit, as the weather is pleasant and sunny, making the terraces look their most spectacular.

If you're interested in the growth cycle of rice, February to April is when the young rice plants cover the terraces in a soft green-yellow hue, resembling a beautiful green carpet.

From June to July (known locally as Sasih Sada), farmers begin their harvest. You'll see them busy in the fields, enjoying the fruits of their labor. This is the perfect time to experience local agricultural life.

However, these timings aren't fixed. Local farmers adjust their schedules based on weather conditions and the growth of the rice.

## Photography Tips: Capturing the Beauty of Rice Terraces

- **Choose the Golden Hour**: The warm, soft light during sunrise or sunset adds depth to your photos. The best time for photography is between 8 AM to 10 AM when the sunlight is just right, creating soft shadows and avoiding overexposure. Another golden hour is around 4 PM to 5 PM, when the setting sun casts a golden glow over the terraces, creating a dreamy atmosphere.

## Additional Information: What is the Subak System?

The Subak system mentioned above is a traditional rice terrace irrigation method unique to Bali, with a history spanning over a thousand years. Since the 11th century, Subak has become the core of local agriculture and has been designated as a **World Cultural Heritage** site by UNESCO. It is not merely a technical irrigation system but also a unique cultural symbol that integrates religious beliefs, social structures, and environmental protection. Based on Buddhist and water deity beliefs, the Subak system emphasizes harmony between humans and nature and fosters a spirit of cooperation and sharing within farming communities.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0ca73e6ea28937fc21a3beff6cc1905f.jpg)

The Subak system is collectively managed by local farmers, ensuring fair distribution of water resources to each terrace area. Farmers organize meetings through Subak associations to coordinate water allocation and hold ceremonies each planting season to thank the water and mountain deities for their blessings. The Subak system also effectively prevents water disputes and strengthens solidarity among farming communities.

![有名的Kanto Lampo瀑布並不是一個自然形成的瀑布，是因為蘇巴克灌溉系統的水溢出而形成](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_9ce193a07d5b914c7677aa9920e7b859.jpg)

The famous Kanto Lampo Waterfall is not a naturally formed waterfall but was created by the overflow of water from the Subak irrigation system.

This spot is a must-visit for capturing stunning photos! Check out Jay's [Kanto Lampo Waterfall Guide](https://youtu.be/-0KOzA9nPGY) for more tips.

### The Water Source of Subak

The water for the Subak system primarily comes from Bali's volcanic mountains, especially Mount Bratan and Mount Batur/Mount Agung. These volcanic regions receive abundant rainfall, providing a steady water supply to local streams and rivers. The Subak system ingeniously utilizes these natural water flows, channeling them to distribute water across terraces, ensuring stable rice cultivation.

### The Water Distribution Process

1. **Mountain Water Sources**: Rainwater collects in lakes within the volcanic mountains and flows downstream through rivers.
2. **Main Irrigation Channels**: The Subak system uses rivers and streams to construct main irrigation channels that direct water to the terraces.
3. **Distribution Channels**: The main water flow is allocated to each terrace area, with distribution channels ensuring even water distribution across all levels.
4. **Two Major Lakes Supplying Terraces**: The Tegallalang Terraces are primarily supplied by Lake Batur (Danau Batur), while the Jatiluwih Terraces are mainly supplied by Lake Bratan (Danau Beratan) near the water temple.

![水神廟所在的布拉坦湖供應著賈提魯維梯田區的灌溉用水](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_cb2b1f1de756cb57dc9c603d2552ddba.jpg)

Lake Bratan, where the water temple is located, supplies irrigation water to the Jatiluwih Terrace area.

### The Sacred Water Ritual

Subak is not just a technical irrigation system; it is deeply intertwined with local religious beliefs. Farmers pray to the water deity at the start of the planting season, holding ceremonies to express gratitude for the natural water sources and to pray for a bountiful harvest. These rituals symbolize the farmers' reverence and gratitude towards nature, adding a mystical aura to the terraced landscape.
