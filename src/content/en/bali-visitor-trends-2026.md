## Bali Tourism Landscape Shakeup: Chinese Tourists Win Awards, Malaysia Ousts Australia for Top Spot!

To be honest, this news didn't surprise me at all - but seeing the numbers still gave me a shock.

Early 2026 saw a quiet 'earthquake' in Bali's tourist demographics. Not only are Chinese tourists making a strong comeback, but Australia, which had dominated Bali tourism for over a decade, has been knocked off its top spot.

### Chinese Tourists: Quality Transformation Seen Through Awards

First, the good news. Bali recently won an award at the 19th China Travel Awards held in Shanghai, being selected by Travel+Leisure China as one of the top travel destinations. Also on the list were Australia, Maldives, South Africa, Thailand, and Turkey - being ranked alongside these destinations is significant recognition for Bali.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-05/1778030614176-785728949570308120.png)

The media that won this award is targeting middle-to-high-end tourists with spending power, not the mass market of group tours. This indicates that Bali's image in the eyes of Chinese tourists has upgraded to a boutique travel destination worth spending time and money to experience - not just for beach photos, but for truly quality travel.

### Chinese Tourists Return, Numbers Speak

The numbers are equally astonishing. In 2025, Chinese tourists visiting Indonesia reached 1.34 million, with over one-third choosing Bali, totaling about 530,000 visitors.

As we enter 2026, this trend has accelerated significantly. Just in February of this year, Indonesia welcomed nearly 150,000 Chinese tourists, a 69% increase compared to the same period last year, with more than half entering through Bali's Ngurah Rai International Airport in Nusa Dua. In that same month, nearly 79,000 Chinese tourists landed in Bali alone, making it the only major source market to show clear growth against the trend—Australia, India, South Korea, and Russia all saw declines that month.

### Australian Tourists Lose Their Top Spot

This brings us to another surprising development.

For over a decade, Australian tourists have been Bali's largest source market, with 'Australians love Bali' almost becoming a tourism industry truism. For many Australians, Bali isn't just a travel destination—it's a true second home, with multiple visits a year being common.

However, according to the latest data from Indonesia's Central Statistics Agency (BPS), in March 2026, Indonesia welcomed 1.09 million international tourists, a growth of over 10% compared to the same period last year. In this wave of growth, Malaysian tourists took the top spot with 186,700 arrivals and a 17.14% market share, while Australia came in second with 130,700 arrivals, and Singapore ranked third.

During the same period, Indian tourists also emerged as a notable trend—thanks to newly launched direct flights, more and more Indian tourists are flocking to Bali, with nearly 80% entering through Ngurah Rai Airport.

### What Does This Tourism Map Reshuffle Mean for Us?

In the first quarter of 2026, Indonesia welcomed a cumulative total of 3.44 million international tourists, an 8.6% increase compared to the same period last year. The average spending per foreign tourist reached $1,345, with an average stay of nearly 11 nights—a fairly healthy figure.

I believe that the 'yielding' of Australian tourists isn't so much a waning of enthusiasm as it is an expansion of travel horizons. In recent months, direct flights from Australia to other Indonesian islands (such as Kalimantan and Pontianak) have opened up, and some Australian tourists have started using Bali as a springboard to explore less-trodden destinations. Multi-stop trips that 'start in Bali, loop around, and return to Bali' may become a new trend.

For Bali as a whole, the diversification of tourist sources is actually a positive development. The rapid growth of Malaysian and Chinese markets, the accelerated development of the Indian market, and the consumption upgrades of mid-to-high-end tourists have made the tourism industry more resilient than ever before.

***

Honestly, as someone who has lived in Bali long-term, seeing these changing numbers really resonates with me. The tourism market structure is constantly evolving, and no single demographic maintains its advantage forever. But for those of us who love Bali, no matter how the tourist demographics shift, it doesn't diminish the island's charm—it's just a matter of who's coming.

If you're planning a trip to Bali, now is definitely a great time—the market is growing, flights are increasing, but the island remains the same magical place.
