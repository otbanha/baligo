## Transportation

Transportation was one of my top concerns when traveling to Bali with my mother. Since many warned that taxi drivers in Bali often try to scam tourists, I decided to use Grab for rides. My previous experience with Grab in Thailand was excellent, and Bali was no exception. However, the airport transportation scene was chaotic—as soon as we stepped out, we were swarmed by taxi drivers aggressively offering rides, even claiming to be Grab drivers and proposing cheaper rates. This was particularly stressful when traveling with an elderly parent.

My advice is to head left immediately after exiting the airport, where you'll find a small parking lot where Grab drivers usually wait. This helps avoid the hassle of dealing with other taxi drivers and makes it easier to locate your ride. During the wait, some drivers may still approach you, but simply tell them firmly that you've already booked a ride.

During our stay in Bali, we relied almost entirely on Grab. Its biggest advantage is the abundance of drivers—you can usually get a ride within three minutes, whether at attractions or restaurants. Most drivers speak decent English and are friendly, and fares are strictly based on the app, so there's no risk of being overcharged. The only minor issue is the scarcity of small change in Bali. Sometimes, when we didn't have exact change, we'd tell the driver to keep the difference, which was usually just a few dollars, and they were always appreciative.

That said, Bali's traffic is a headache 🤣. There's no clear distinction between fast and slow lanes, and motorcycles weave through traffic unpredictably. Watching them dart between cars can make you feel like a collision is imminent. If you're brave, you might try Grab's motorcycle service, especially during peak hours when car traffic can be unbearable. Once, we traveled just 20 kilometers between two locations, but it took a full two hours—truly a mix of laughter and frustration.

Overall, Grab is a reliable transportation option in Bali, especially for those traveling with elderly family members—it's both convenient and safe. Just remember that Bali's traffic can be chaotic, so stay alert while driving and be extra cautious when crossing streets, keeping an eye on motorcycles and cars (unlike in Taiwan).

## Private Car Hire

During our trip to Bali, hiring a private car was one of the most hassle-free and convenient transportation options we chose. Our driver was recommended through a community forum with excellent reviews, and after experiencing his service, we can confidently say it was worth it. Below are the detailed insights from our private car hire experience for your reference.

### Booking and Communication:

We contacted the driver via Line, and the communication was smooth and efficient. First, we informed him about our desired itinerary and the duration of the car hire. Based on our needs, he provided suggestions, listing the total cost and the sequence of activities in detail. After confirming everything, he reached out the day before to ensure there were no changes to the plan. The best part was that payment was made only after the day’s activities were completed, eliminating any concerns about prepayment.

### Itinerary and Costs:

The main purpose of hiring the car was to explore the Ubud area, and we also asked the driver to drop us off at our next hotel in a different region. The total car hire duration was 10 hours, costing approximately USD$39, which we found very reasonable. Additionally, we asked the driver to arrange activities like ATV rides, river tubing, and swings. He included the costs of these activities in the total price, which was confirmed upfront. All payments were made in one go at the end of the day, making it incredibly convenient.

## Civet Coffee

During our Bali trip, the private car driver took us to a civet coffee plantation, one of the "classic" experiences many tourists encounter in Bali. Although this experience felt a bit commercialized, I wanted to share my thoughts to provide another reference for planning your itinerary.

### The Driver's Recommendation:

During our private car tour, the driver casually asked if we wanted to try Kopi Luwak, emphasizing that it's a specialty of Bali and only costs USD$3 per cup, making it well worth a try. Intrigued by the affordable price and driven by curiosity, we agreed. The driver then took us to a dedicated Kopi Luwak plantation.

### The Plantation Tour and Tasting:

As soon as we entered the plantation, the staff enthusiastically explained the process of making Kopi Luwak, highlighting how natural and organic their coffee is, and how it's 'naturally processed' by civet cats. They guided us through the plantation, showing the relationship between the civet cats (actually palm civets) and the coffee beans, before leading us to the tasting area.

The tasting experience was quite interesting. In addition to Kopi Luwak, they prepared a variety of other coffees and floral teas for us to sample. Each drink was accompanied by a detailed explanation, such as the flavor profile of the coffee or the benefits of the floral tea. After tasting everything, it did feel a bit awkward not to buy something.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_b47855e8d20e3f0b3936512c44f670a2.jpg)

### The Controversy Surrounding Kopi Luwak:

While tasting, I couldn't help but recall the controversy surrounding Kopi Luwak that I'd read about online. Many reports indicate that this coffee isn't entirely naturally produced, as many civet cats are kept in cages and force-fed coffee beans, which is far from animal-friendly. Additionally, Kopi Luwak is extremely expensive—while a tasting cup only costs USD$3, the actual purchase price might make you think twice.

### The Pressure to Buy and the Actual Experience:

During the tasting, the staff enthusiastically promoted their products right in front of us, which created a bit of pressure. In the end, we bought a small pack of mangosteen tea for around USD$13, which wasn't too expensive but also not exactly cheap. As for the Kopi Luwak, it did have a subtle sweetness and a smooth texture, but it didn't strike me as a 'must-buy.' If you're not particularly passionate about coffee or uninterested in this kind of commercialized activity, you can simply tell the driver to skip this part of the tour, saving you both time and money.

### My Experience Summary:

The civet coffee experience is considered a signature activity in Bali, but the hidden animal welfare issues and commercialized approach can be off-putting. If you're not particularly interested in coffee or don't want to spend too much time on shopping-oriented activities, it's recommended to communicate with your driver beforehand and skip this part. After all, Bali has many more worthwhile attractions and activities to explore. Time is precious, so why not dedicate it to creating even more beautiful memories!

## Food

Food was definitely one of the highlights that left a deep impression on me during this Bali trip! Bali's culinary culture is incredibly diverse, especially the local specialties, which are truly unforgettable. Below is my detailed sharing of this dining experience, hoping to provide some references for everyone.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_d7966ba428f0a4c7761ad02df03437bc.jpg)

### Dirty Duck Rice and Pork Ribs:

First and foremost, I highly recommend Bali's 'Dirty Duck Rice' and 'Pork Ribs'. Dirty Duck Rice is a traditional Balinese dish where the duck is marinated and cooked with spices for a long time, resulting in tender meat paired with fragrant local rice, offering a rich and layered flavor that keeps you coming back for more. As for the pork ribs, they are crispy on the outside and tender on the inside, with a sweet and savory sauce that's not greasy at all, perfect for those who love hearty meat dishes.

Interestingly, our driver told us that although the majority of Indonesia's population is Muslim and doesn't eat pork, over 85% of Bali's population practices Hinduism, so you can find many pork dishes here, which is quite rare in other parts of Indonesia. Additionally, Bali's lifestyle is relatively free, without the many dress or dietary restrictions found in Islamic regions, making our journey more relaxed and comfortable. The driver also mentioned that schools in Bali finish at noon, which made me feel that the pace of life here is really leisurely, sounds super chill 🤣.

### Traditional Indonesian Cuisine:

While Dirty Duck Rice and Pork Ribs amazed us, traditional Indonesian dishes didn't quite hit the mark for our taste buds. We even signed up for a cooking class, spending a morning learning to make six or seven Indonesian dishes. There were also a few Korean tourists in the class, and after tasting, everyone found the dishes too salty and oily, only taking a bite or two before stopping. However, the process of participating in the cooking class was still very fun, as it's a unique travel experience to personally engage in the making of local cuisine.

## Currency Exchange

When traveling in Bali, currency exchange is a crucial aspect to pay attention to, especially since the local currency amounts are very large, and it's easy to get confused. Below are my currency exchange tips from this trip, hoping to help everyone handle money matters more smoothly.

### Bringing USD is Most Convenient:

It's recommended to **exchange USD in Taiwan first, then convert to Indonesian Rupiah in Bali**. This is because the exchange rate for USD in Bali is relatively stable, and all currency exchange offices accept USD. For this trip, we brought USD to Bali and used it to pay for the visa on arrival at the airport, letting the staff give change in Indonesian Rupiah. This way, we had some local currency right after landing.

### Airport vs. City Currency Exchange:

Although there are currency exchange offices at the airport, the rates are usually not as favorable as those in the city. Therefore, we only exchanged a small amount of Rupiah for transportation from the airport to the city, and waited to exchange the rest at reputable exchange offices in the city.

### Amount Notation and Conversion Tips:

The denominations of Indonesian Rupiah are very large, often in the hundreds of thousands or even millions, which might be confusing for first-time visitors. Locals commonly denote the last three zeros with a "K", for example, 50,000 Rupiah is written as 50K. The simplest way to convert between Rupiah and USD is to multiply the number before "K" by 2, then add a little more for an approximate USD amount. For example, 50K Rupiah is roughly a bit over USD$3. This method makes calculations very convenient and helps avoid mistakes when shopping or making payments.

### Important Notes:

When exchanging money, always choose reputable currency exchange offices to avoid encountering counterfeit bills or unfair exchange rates at roadside stalls. Additionally, it's best to count the money on the spot and confirm the amount before leaving. Some exchange offices may charge service fees, so it's advisable to inquire about this before proceeding with the transaction.

## Accommodation

This Bali accommodation experience deeply impressed upon me the importance of choosing the right location and surrounding environment. Below are some insights from my stay that I hope will help you make more suitable choices when planning your trip.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_a547a653ea0227a0f9908d938d74da36.jpg)

### Mosquito Problem:

First and foremost, be aware that Bali has a lot of mosquitoes, and they are particularly large! This is likely due to the humid climate and the fact that hotels and villas often have lush gardens filled with tropical plants, which unfortunately also serve as breeding grounds for mosquitoes. Even in higher-end villas, mosquitoes can find their way inside. Therefore, **insect repellent is an absolute must**. I recommend carrying it with you at all times, especially during dusk and dawn when mosquitoes are most active.

[What Essential Medications Should You Bring for a Trip to Bali, Indonesia? How to Buy Insect Repellent/Sunscreen?](/blog/2024-03-22-65fcd4a5fd89780001513c9a/)

### Breakfast Service:

Although the mosquito issue was somewhat bothersome, the hotels and guesthouses we stayed at excelled in their breakfast offerings. The breakfast options were diverse, ranging from local specialties to Western-style dishes, all of high quality. I found breakfast to be particularly important during our stay in Bali because there were hardly any convenience stores or food stalls nearby. If we didn't eat enough at breakfast, we would have to wait until lunchtime to find something to eat. Therefore, I suggest paying special attention to whether your accommodation includes breakfast when making your choice, as it can make your journey much more comfortable.

### Choosing the Right Accommodation Location:

The biggest mistake we made during our stay was choosing a location that was quite far from the bustling areas. There were hardly any shops or restaurants nearby, and we had to call for a taxi every time we wanted to go out, which was very inconvenient. Bali's attractions are spread out, and transportation isn't particularly convenient. If your accommodation isn't in a busy area, the daily transportation costs can add up significantly. Each time we called for a taxi, it cost us around USD$39, and once we left, it was impossible to return to our accommodation to fetch something or rest, which somewhat limited our itinerary planning.

Therefore, **it's recommended to consider the surrounding convenience when choosing accommodations**. If you enjoy a lively atmosphere, you can stay in bustling areas like Kuta, Seminyak, or Ubud, where there are plenty of restaurants, shops, and evening entertainment options. If you opt for a more remote villa or resort, be prepared to rely on Grab food delivery or private car services for daily needs.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0391757a9b3ffecaea7406bc888c42e0.jpg)

### Accommodation Insights:

While Bali's accommodations are beautiful, mosquito issues and location selection are important factors to consider. Make sure to bring enough mosquito repellent and think about the surrounding convenience when choosing where to stay. Staying in less central areas may increase transportation costs and inconvenience, so keep this in mind when planning your itinerary. We hope these tips help you find the perfect accommodation in Bali, making your trip even more enjoyable!
