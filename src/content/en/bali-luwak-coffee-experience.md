**Sharing My Experience Traveling to Bali with My 70+ Mother from 1/13 to 1/17**

Traveling to Bali with my mother in her 70s was a journey filled with warmth and challenges. From transportation and accommodation to food, every experience deepened my appreciation for Bali's unique charm while teaching me valuable lessons. Through this travelogue, I hope to share insights that will help others planning trips with elderly family members to Bali.

***

## Transportation

Transportation was one of my top concerns for this trip. Many people warned me that taxi drivers in Bali often try to scam tourists, so I decided to use Grab for rides. My previous experience with Grab in Thailand was excellent, and Bali was no exception. However, the situation at Bali's airport was chaotic. As soon as we stepped out, we were swarmed by taxi drivers aggressively offering their services, even claiming to be Grab drivers and promising cheaper rates. This was particularly frustrating when traveling with an elderly parent.

My advice is to head left immediately after exiting the airport, where you'll find a small parking lot where Grab drivers usually wait. This helps avoid the hassle of being approached by other taxi drivers and speeds up finding your ride. Even so, some drivers may still approach you—just firmly tell them you’ve already booked a car.

During our stay in Bali, we relied almost entirely on Grab. The app’s advantage is its abundance of drivers; we could usually get a ride within three minutes, whether at attractions or restaurants. Most drivers spoke decent English and were friendly, and fares were strictly based on the app’s pricing, eliminating worries about being overcharged. The only minor issue was the scarcity of small change in Bali. Sometimes, we didn’t have exact change and simply told the driver to keep the difference, which was usually just a few dollars. The drivers were always appreciative.

That said, Bali’s traffic can be a headache 🤣. There’s no clear distinction between fast and slow lanes, and motorcycles weave through traffic unpredictably. Watching bikes squeeze through narrow gaps often felt like an accident was imminent. If you’re brave, you might try Grab’s motorcycle service, especially during peak hours when car traffic can be unbearable. Once, we traveled 20 kilometers between two locations, and it took a full two hours—both frustrating and amusing.

Overall, Grab is a reliable transportation option in Bali, especially for travelers with elderly companions. It’s convenient and safe, but be prepared for chaotic traffic conditions. Always stay alert while driving, and be extra cautious when crossing streets, as motorcycles and cars move differently than in Taiwan.

## Private Car Hire

During our Bali trip, hiring a private car was one of the most hassle-free and convenient transportation options we chose. Our driver came highly recommended from a community forum, and after experiencing his service firsthand, I can confidently say he lived up to the praise. Below, I’ll share detailed insights from our private car hire experience for your reference.

### Booking and Communication:

We communicated with the driver via Line, and the process was seamless. First, we shared our desired itinerary and the duration of the car hire. Based on our needs, the driver provided suggestions, listed the total cost, and outlined the itinerary in detail. Once confirmed, he reached out the day before to ensure there were no changes to the plan. The best part? Payment was made only after the day’s activities were completed, so there was no need to worry about prepayment.

### Itinerary and Costs:

Our main goal for hiring the car was to explore the Ubud area, with the driver dropping us off at our next hotel afterward. The total car hire duration was 10 hours, costing approximately USD$39, which I found very reasonable. Additionally, we asked the driver to arrange activities like ATV riding, river tubing, and swing experiences. He included these costs in the total price, which we confirmed beforehand. Payment for everything was made in one go at the end of the day, making it incredibly convenient.

## Luwak Coffee

During our Bali trip, the private car driver took us to a luwak coffee plantation, which is one of the "classic" experiences many tourists encounter in Bali. Although this experience felt a bit commercialized, I still want to share my thoughts with you, so you can have another reference when planning your itinerary.

### Driver's Recommendation:

During our private car tour, the driver casually asked if we wanted to try Kopi Luwak, emphasizing that it's a specialty of Bali and only costs USD$3 per cup, making it well worth a try. Intrigued by the low price and driven by curiosity, we agreed. The driver then took us to a plantation dedicated to Kopi Luwak.

### Plantation Tour and Tasting:

As soon as we entered the plantation, the staff enthusiastically explained the process of making Kopi Luwak, highlighting how natural and organic their coffee is, and how it undergoes 'natural processing' by the civet cats. They guided us through the plantation, showing us the relationship between the civet cats (actually palm civets) and the coffee beans, before leading us to the tasting area.

The tasting experience was quite interesting. In addition to Kopi Luwak, they also prepared a variety of other coffees and herbal teas for us to sample. Each drink was accompanied by a detailed explanation, such as the flavor profile of the coffee or the benefits of the herbal tea. After finishing the tasting session, it did feel a bit awkward not to buy something.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_b47855e8d20e3f0b3936512c44f670a2.jpg)

### The Controversy Surrounding Kopi Luwak:

However, while tasting, I couldn't help but recall the controversies surrounding Kopi Luwak that I'd read about online. Many reports suggest that these coffee beans aren't entirely naturally sourced, with many civet cats being kept in cages and force-fed coffee beans, which is not animal-friendly. Additionally, Kopi Luwak is extremely expensive; although the tasting cup only cost USD$3, the actual purchase price might be prohibitive.

https://youtu.be/NEnIZjZnrTk

### Purchase Pressure and Actual Experience:

During the tasting, the staff enthusiastically promoted their products right in front of us, which created a bit of pressure. In the end, we bought a small pack of mangosteen tea for around USD$13, which wasn't too expensive but also not exactly cheap. As for the Kopi Luwak, it did have a subtle sweetness and a smooth texture, but it didn't strike me as a 'must-buy.' If you're not particularly passionate about coffee or uninterested in such commercialized tours, you could simply tell the driver to skip this stop, saving both time and money.

### Experience Review:

The civet coffee experience is considered a signature activity in Bali, but the underlying animal welfare issues and commercialized approach make some people hesitant. If you're not particularly interested in coffee or don't want to spend too much time on this kind of shopping-oriented activity, it's recommended to communicate with your driver in advance to skip this part. After all, Bali has many more worthwhile attractions and activities to explore. Time is precious, so it's better to dedicate it to creating more beautiful memories!

## Food

During this Bali trip, food was definitely one of the highlights that left a deep impression on me! Bali's culinary culture is incredibly diverse, especially the local specialties, which are truly unforgettable. Below is my detailed sharing of this dining experience, hoping to provide some references for everyone.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_d7966ba428f0a4c7761ad02df03437bc.jpg)

### Bebek Bengil (Dirty Duck Rice) and Pork Ribs:

First, I must highly recommend Bali's 'Bebek Bengil' (Dirty Duck Rice) and pork ribs. Bebek Bengil is a traditional Balinese dish where the duck meat is marinated and cooked with spices for a long time, resulting in a very tender texture. Paired with the local spiced rice, the flavors are rich and layered, making it hard to stop eating. As for the pork ribs, they are crispy on the outside and tender on the inside, with a sweet and savory sauce that's not greasy at all, perfect for meat lovers.

Interestingly, our private driver told us that although the majority of Indonesia's population is Muslim and doesn't eat pork, over 85% of Bali's population practices Hinduism, so you can find many pork dishes here, which is quite rare in other parts of Indonesia. Additionally, Bali's lifestyle is relatively free, without many of the clothing or dietary restrictions found in Islamic regions, making our trip more relaxed and comfortable. The driver also mentioned that schools in Bali finish at noon, which made me feel that the pace of life here is really leisurely—sounds awesome 🤣.

### Traditional Indonesian Cuisine:

While Bebek Bengil and pork ribs amazed us, traditional Indonesian cuisine didn't quite suit our taste buds. We even signed up for a cooking class, spending a morning learning to make six or seven Indonesian dishes. There were also a few Korean tourists in the class, and after tasting, everyone found the dishes too salty and oily, ending up only taking a bite or two before leaving them untouched. However, the process of participating in the cooking class was still fun, as being able to personally experience the making of local dishes is a unique travel experience.

## Snacks

Beyond the main meals, Bali's snacks were a delightful surprise! Especially the 'coconut chips,' which quickly became one of my favorite treats from this trip. Made from fresh coconuts that are dried, these chips offer a crispy texture with a natural coconut sweetness, and they're completely non-greasy, making them refreshingly light. We bought several packs at a local supermarket to bring home, but I ended up finishing them all before we even returned to Taiwan 🤣. If you're a fan of coconut-flavored snacks, you definitely shouldn't miss out on these!

## Currency Exchange

When traveling in Bali, currency exchange is a crucial aspect to pay attention to, especially since the local currency amounts can be quite large, and it's easy to get confused. Here are some insights from my trip to help you manage your money more smoothly.

### Bringing USD is Most Convenient:

It's advisable to **exchange for USD in Taiwan first, then convert to Indonesian Rupiah in Bali**. This is because the exchange rate for USD in Bali is relatively stable, and all currency exchange places accept USD. On this trip, we brought USD to Bali and used it to pay for the visa fee upon arrival at the airport, receiving change in Indonesian Rupiah from the staff. This way, we had some local currency right after landing.

### Airport vs. City Currency Exchange:

Although there are currency exchange services at the airport, the rates are usually not as favorable as those in the city. Therefore, we only exchanged a small amount of Indonesian Rupiah at the airport for transportation to the city, and exchanged the rest at reputable currency exchange places in the city.

### Amount Representation and Conversion Tips:

The denominations of Indonesian Rupiah are very large, often in the hundreds of thousands or even millions, which can be confusing for first-time visitors. Locals commonly represent the last three zeros with a 'K,' for example, 50,000 Indonesian Rupiah is written as 50K. The simplest way to convert between Indonesian Rupiah and Taiwan Dollars is to multiply the number before 'K' by 2 and add a little more, giving you an approximate amount in Taiwan Dollars. For instance, 50K Indonesian Rupiah is roughly a bit over 100 Taiwan Dollars, making it very convenient for calculations and helping avoid mistakes when shopping or paying.

### Important Notes:

When exchanging money, always choose reputable currency exchange offices and avoid small roadside stalls to prevent encountering counterfeit bills or unfair exchange rates. Additionally, it's best to count the money on the spot and confirm the amount before leaving. Some exchange offices may charge a service fee, so it's advisable to ask about this before proceeding with the transaction.

## Accommodation

This accommodation experience in Bali has deeply impressed upon me the importance of choosing the right location and surrounding environment. Below are some insights from my stay, which I hope will help you make more suitable choices when planning your trip.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_a547a653ea0227a0f9908d938d74da36.jpg)

### Mosquito Issue:

First, I must remind everyone that Bali has a lot of mosquitoes, and they are particularly large! This might be due to the humid climate and the fact that hotels and villas often plant a lot of flowers and trees to create a tropical ambiance, which also becomes a breeding ground for mosquitoes. Even though we stayed in a relatively high-end villa, mosquitoes still managed to get inside the room. Therefore, **mosquito repellent is an absolute must-have item**. I recommend carrying it with you at all times, especially during dusk and dawn when mosquitoes are most active.

**Quick Reference:**

[What essential medicines should you bring for a trip to Bali? How to buy mosquito repellent and sunscreen?](/blog/bali-travel-medicine-checklist/)

### Breakfast Service:

Although the mosquito issue was somewhat bothersome, the hotels and guesthouses we stayed at performed exceptionally well in terms of breakfast. The breakfast options were diverse, ranging from local specialties to Western-style breakfasts, and the quality was consistently high. I believe breakfast is very important in Bali accommodations because there are hardly any convenience stores or food stalls around where we stayed. If you don't eat enough for breakfast, you might have to wait until lunchtime to find something to eat. Therefore, I suggest paying special attention to whether breakfast is provided when choosing accommodations, as it will make your journey much more comfortable.

### Choosing the Right Accommodation Location:

The biggest mistake in this trip was that our accommodation was quite far from the bustling areas, with almost no shops or restaurants nearby. Every time we went out, we had to call for a ride, which was very inconvenient. Bali's attractions are scattered, and transportation isn't very convenient. If your accommodation isn't in a bustling area, the daily transportation costs can add up significantly. Each time we called for a ride, it cost us around USD$40, and once we were out, it was impossible to come back to fetch something or rest, which somewhat limited our itinerary planning.

Therefore, **it’s highly recommended to consider the convenience of the surrounding area when choosing accommodation**. If you enjoy a lively atmosphere, you might want to stay in bustling areas like Kuta, Seminyak, or Ubud, where there are plenty of restaurants, shops, and evening entertainment options. However, if you opt for a more remote villa or resort, be prepared to rely on Grab delivery or private car services for your daily needs.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0391757a9b3ffecaea7406bc888c42e0.jpg)

### Accommodation Tips:

While Bali’s accommodations are beautiful, mosquito issues and location choices require special attention. Be sure to pack plenty of insect repellent and consider the convenience of the area when selecting your stay. Staying outside of busy areas may increase transportation costs and inconvenience, so keep this in mind when planning your itinerary. We hope these insights help you find the perfect accommodation in Bali, making your trip even more enjoyable!
