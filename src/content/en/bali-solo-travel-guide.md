Are you considering traveling solo to Bali? Let me tell you first: **This is definitely a great idea.**

Bali is one of the most suitable Southeast Asian destinations for solo travel in my opinion. It's safe, tourist-friendly, reasonably priced, and being "alone" here is never awkward. Whether you want to quietly unwind or meet new friends, Bali can satisfy you.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775350592267-mixcollage-05-apr-2026-07-55-am-578.jpg)

Here are practical tips I've compiled for solo travelers based on my personal experiences:

***

## 1. Where to Stay? Choosing the Right Area is Crucial

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775351493354-mixcollage-05-apr-2026-08-11-am-1312.jpg)

Bali is vast, and different areas have completely different vibes:

- **Ubud**: The top choice for solo travel. Yoga, meditation, healthy eating, rice field views—it's very comfortable walking alone on the streets, and everywhere you go, you'll find fellow solo travelers. Plenty of cafes and a thriving hostel culture.
- **Canggu**: The hub for digital nomads, with beaches, cafes, surfing, and nightlife. Suitable for those who enjoy energy and don't mind noise. The downside is heavy traffic.
- **Seminyak**: A bit more upscale, with great restaurants, spas, and beach clubs. Quieter than Canggu.
- **Nusa Dua**: A high-end resort area. If you have a generous budget and want to purely relax, this could be an option, but it's more suited for couples or families—solo travel here might be a bit dull.
- **Jimbaran**: Famous for sunset seafood barbecues.

**My recommendation**: If your trip is short (5–7 days), stay in **Ubud + Canggu** for a few days each to experience two completely different sides of Bali.

## 2. How to Handle Transportation?

Traveling solo in Bali can be quite challenging, especially when it comes to transportation. Honestly: **Bali's traffic is terrible, and it's not very pedestrian-friendly.**

https://youtu.be/ePutHL_Ob1Q

Here are a few solutions:

1. **Gojek / Grab**: Southeast Asia's version of Uber, it's cheap and convenient. Calling a motorbike is the most cost-effective option (around USD$1–2 from Ubud city center to nearby attractions). However, during peak hours (especially in Canggu), you might have to wait longer for a ride.
2. **Renting a motorbike**: If you have an international driver's license (and enough riding experience), this is the most liberating way to get around. It costs about USD$3–5 per day. **But be warned**: Bali's traffic is chaotic, and road conditions vary, so it's not recommended for beginners.
3. **Private car hire**: Around USD$40–60 per day (8–10 hours), this is suitable for longer trips (like Ubud's mountainous areas or Uluwatu). While it's not cheap for solo travelers, you can split the cost with hostel mates.

https://youtu.be/lIV-T2jYhVk

**My recommendation**: Use Gojek for short trips and hire a private car or join a local tour for longer journeys.

## 3. Dining Alone Without Awkwardness

Bali's restaurants are very welcoming to solo diners.

https://youtu.be/VuG8s-u_I0Q

- **Warung (local eateries)**: Sit at the counter and enjoy Nasi Goreng (fried rice) or Mie Goreng (fried noodles) for USD$2–3, which is both cheap and delicious.
- **Healthy vegetarian restaurants**: These are everywhere in Ubud, many with counter seating or shared long tables.
- **Night markets / street stalls**: Perfect for grabbing a bite while walking around, highly recommend satay and Babi Guling (roast pork rice).

The only thing to note is that some upscale restaurants or beach clubs might not welcome solo diners taking up large tables, so it's best to ask or choose a counter seat.

## 4. Is It Safe? Honest Advice for Solo Travelers

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775351807418-3e530ee91368827f35ce3d2046220e06.jpg)

Bali is generally safe, but there are a few things to keep in mind:

- **Always wear a helmet when riding a scooter**: Police will stop you, and it’s genuinely dangerous.
- **Beware of monkeys**: In places like Uluwatu and the Sacred Monkey Forest, monkeys can snatch glasses, phones, and food. Don’t turn your back on them.
- **Keep valuables close**: Especially your passport, wallet, and phone. Use the hostel safe if available.
- **Don’t accept drinks from strangers**: This applies everywhere.
- **Avoid walking alone in dark alleys late at night**: Especially in more remote areas of Canggu or Kuta.

**A small reminder**: Balinese people are generally friendly and warm, but those who are overly eager to chat or insist on taking you to their "friend’s shop" usually have ulterior motives. Politely decline.

## Five: The Best Part of Solo Travel—Meeting New People

Traveling alone doesn’t mean you have to stay alone.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775352447434-54bd8eecd33bfc05d58f6767e3816f0a.jpg)

- **Stay in hostels**: Ubud and Canggu have many great hostels with common areas, free breakfast, yoga classes, and coworking spaces… perfect for striking up conversations. Recommendations: Ubud Raya, Puri Garden, Tribal Bali.
- **Join day tours**: Like hiking Mount Batur for sunrise, snorkeling, or taking a Thai cooking class. Many others in the group are solo travelers too.
- **Yoga/meditation classes**: Ubud is Asia’s yoga hub, and people naturally chat before and after classes.
- **Digital nomad cafés in Canggu**: Bring your laptop, sit at the bar or shared tables, and conversations flow easily.

## Six: My Suggested Solo Travel Itinerary (6 Days, 5 Nights)

- **Day 1–3 Ubud**: Explore the palace, Sacred Monkey Forest, Tegallalang Rice Terraces, join a yoga class, watch a Legong dance performance, and enjoy vegan food.

https://youtu.be/JtxZHtijEkE

- **Day 4–6 Canggu**: Surf (hire an instructor if needed), watch the sunset at the beach, stroll Batu Bolong Beach, visit beach clubs (La Brisa is fantastic), and soak in the café culture.

https://youtube.com/shorts/f0N6FE2dxys

- **A day trip**: From Ubud, hire a car to visit Jatiluwih Rice Terraces + Ulun Danu Beratan Temple, or from Canggu, head to Uluwatu’s hidden beach for sunset + Kecak dance.

https://youtu.be/hQbBTktqEqY

## 7. Final Reminder: Slow Down

Many first-time solo travelers try to pack their itineraries full. But in Bali, **the most beautiful moments are often the empty ones** — sitting发呆 at a cafe by the rice fields, watching the waves at the beach without doing anything, chatting with new friends at the hostel until late at night.

The meaning of solo travel isn't about how many attractions you visit, but how comfortable you are with yourself.

Bali will gently show you: being alone can also feel complete.
