Are you considering traveling solo to Bali? Let me tell you first: **This is definitely a great idea.**

Bali is one of the most suitable Southeast Asian destinations for solo travel in my opinion. It's safe, tourist-friendly, reasonably priced, and being 'alone' here is never awkward. Whether you want to quietly unwind or meet new friends, Bali can fulfill your desires.

![Solo Travel in Bali: A Paradise That Can Be Equally Exciting](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775350592267-mixcollage-05-apr-2026-07-55-am-578.jpg)

Here are practical tips I've gathered from my own experiences for solo travelers:

***

## 1. Where to Stay? Choosing the Right Area is Crucial

![1. Where to Stay? Choosing the Right Area is Crucial](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775351493354-mixcollage-05-apr-2026-08-11-am-1312.jpg)

Bali is vast, and different areas offer completely different vibes:

- **Ubud**: The top choice for solo travelers. Yoga, meditation, healthy eating, rice field views—walking alone on the streets feels natural, and you'll find many solo travelers like yourself. Plenty of cafes and a thriving hostel culture.
- **Canggu**: A hub for digital nomads, featuring beaches, cafes, surfing, and nightlife. Suitable for those who enjoy energy and don't mind noise. The downside is heavy traffic congestion.
- **Seminyak**: A bit more upscale, with excellent restaurants, spas, and beach clubs, quieter than Canggu.
- **Nusa Dua**: A luxury resort area, ideal if you have a generous budget and want to purely relax, but it's more suited for couples or families and can be a bit dull for solo travelers.
- **Jimbaran**: Famous for sunset seafood barbecues.

**My recommendation**: If your trip is short (5–7 days), split your stay between **Ubud and Canggu** to experience two completely different sides of Bali.

## 2. How to Handle Transportation?

Traveling solo in Bali can be quite challenging. Let's be honest: **Bali's transportation system is poor, and it's not very pedestrian-friendly.**

https://youtu.be/ePutHL_Ob1Q

Here are a few solutions:

1. **Gojek / Grab**: Southeast Asia's version of Uber, it's cheap and convenient. Hiring a motorbike is the most cost-effective option for solo travelers (approximately USD$1–2 from Ubud city center to nearby attractions). However, during peak hours (especially in Canggu), you might have to wait longer for a ride.
2. **Renting a motorbike**: If you have an international driver's license (and sufficient riding experience), this is the most flexible way to get around. It costs about USD$3–5 per day. **But be warned**: Bali's traffic is chaotic, and road conditions vary, so it's not recommended for beginners.
3. **Private car hire**: Approximately USD$39–58 per day (8–10 hours), ideal for longer trips (such as Ubud's mountainous areas or Uluwatu). While it's not cheap for solo travelers, you can split the cost with hostel mates.

https://youtu.be/lIV-T2jYhVk

**My recommendation**: Use Gojek motorbikes for short distances, and hire a private car or join a local tour for longer trips or day excursions.

## Three: Dining Alone Without Awkwardness

Bali's restaurants are very solo-traveler-friendly.

https://youtu.be/VuG8s-u_I0Q

- **Warung (local small eateries)**: Sit at the counter and enjoy Nasi Goreng (fried rice) or Mie Goreng (fried noodles) for about USD$2–3, which is both cheap and delicious.
- **Healthy vegetarian restaurants**: These are everywhere in Ubud, many with counter seating or shared long tables.
- **Night markets / street stalls**: Perfect for grabbing a bite while walking around. Recommended dishes include satay and Babi Guling (roast suckling pig).

The only thing to note is that some high-end restaurants or beach clubs might not welcome solo travelers occupying large tables. It's best to ask beforehand or opt for counter seating.

## Four: Is It Safe? Honest Advice for Solo Travelers

![Four: Is It Safe? Honest Advice for Solo Travelers](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775351807418-3e530ee91368827f35ce3d2046220e06.jpg)

Bali is generally safe, but there are a few things to keep in mind:

- **Always wear a helmet when riding a scooter**: Police will stop you, and it's genuinely dangerous.
- **Be cautious of monkeys**: In places like Uluwatu and the Sacred Monkey Forest, monkeys may snatch glasses, phones, or food. Don’t turn your back on them.
- **Keep valuables close**: Especially your passport, wallet, and phone. Use the hostel safe if available.
- **Don’t accept drinks from strangers**: This applies everywhere.
- **Avoid walking alone in dark alleys late at night**: Particularly in more remote areas of Canggu or Kuta.

**A small reminder**: Balinese people are generally friendly and warm, but those who are overly eager to chat or insist on taking you to their "friend’s shop" usually have ulterior motives. Politely decline.

## Five: The Best Part of Solo Travel: Meeting New People

Traveling alone doesn’t mean you have to stay alone.

![Five: The Best Part of Solo Travel: Meeting New People](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775352447434-54bd8eecd33bfc05d58f6767e3816f0a.jpg)

- **Stay in hostels**: Ubud and Canggu have many fantastic hostels with common areas, free breakfast, yoga classes, and coworking spaces… making it easy to strike up conversations. Recommendations: Ubud Raya, Puri Garden, Tribal Bali.
- **Join day tours**: Like hiking Mount Batur for sunrise, snorkeling, or taking a Thai cooking class. Many in the group are also solo travelers.
- **Yoga/meditation classes**: Ubud is Asia’s yoga hub, and people naturally chat before and after classes.
- **Digital nomad cafes in Canggu**: Bring your laptop to work, sit at the bar or shared tables, and conversations flow easily.

## Six: My Suggested Solo Travel Itinerary (6 Days, 5 Nights)

- **Day 1–3 Ubud**: Explore the palace, Sacred Monkey Forest, Tegallalang Rice Terraces, join a yoga class, watch a Legong dance performance, and enjoy vegan food.

https://youtu.be/JtxZHtijEkE

- **Day 4–6 Canggu**: Surf (hire an instructor if needed), watch the sunset at the beach, stroll Batu Bolong Beach, visit beach clubs (La Brisa is great), and soak in the café culture.

https://youtube.com/shorts/f0N6FE2dxys

- **One-day excursion**: From Ubud, hire a car to visit Jatiluwih Rice Terraces + Ulun Danu Beratan Temple, or from Canggu, head to Uluwatu’s hidden beach for sunset + Kecak dance.

https://youtu.be/hQbBTktqEqY

## 7. Final Reminder: Slow Down

Many first-time solo travelers tend to pack their itineraries full. But in Bali, **the most beautiful moments are often the ones left blank** — sitting in a cafe by the rice fields daydreaming, watching the waves at the beach doing nothing, chatting with friends you just met at the hostel until late at night.

The essence of solo travel isn't about how many attractions you visit, but how comfortable you are with yourself.

Bali will gently show you: being alone can also feel complete.
