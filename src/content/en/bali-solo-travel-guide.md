For getting around alone, make good use of **Gojek ride-hailing** or private drivers. Are you thinking about traveling to Bali alone? Let me tell you right away: **This is a fantastic idea.**

Bali is one of my top Southeast Asian destinations for solo travel. It's safe, tourist-friendly, reasonably priced, and being 'alone' never feels awkward here. Whether you want to quietly unwind or meet new friends, Bali has you covered.

## 1. Where to Stay? Choosing the Right Area Matters

![一、住哪裡？選對區域很重要](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775351493354-mixcollage-05-apr-2026-08-11-am-1312.jpg)

Bali is large, and different areas have completely different vibes:

- **Ubud**: Top choice for solo travelers. Yoga, meditation, healthy food, rice field views—you'll feel at ease walking around alone, surrounded by other solo travelers. Plenty of cafes and a thriving hostel culture.
- **Canggu**: A hub for digital nomads, with beaches, cafes, surfing, and nightlife. Great for those who love energy and don't mind noise. The downside is heavy traffic.
- **Seminyak**: A bit more upscale, with excellent restaurants, spas, and beach clubs, quieter than Canggu.
- **Nusa Dua**: A high-end resort area. If you have a generous budget and want pure relaxation, consider it, but it's more suited for couples or families—solo travel might feel a bit dull here.
- **Jimbaran**: Famous for sunset seafood barbecues.

**My advice**: If you have limited time (5–7 days), split your stay between **Ubud and Canggu** for a few days each to experience two completely different sides of Bali.

## 2. How to Get Around?

This is the biggest headache for solo travelers in Bali. Honestly: **traffic is terrible, and it's not very walkable.**

https://youtu.be/ePutHL_Ob1Q

A few solutions:

1. **Gojek / Grab**: Southeast Asia's version of Uber—cheap and convenient. Renting a scooter solo is the best deal (from Ubud center to nearby attractions, it's about USD$1–2). However, during peak hours (especially in Canggu), you might wait longer for a ride.
2. **Rent a scooter**: If you have an international driver's license (and enough riding experience), this is the most freeing option. It costs about USD$3–5 per day. **But be warned**: Bali's traffic is chaotic and road conditions vary—not recommended for beginners.
3. **Hire a private driver**: About USD$39–58 per day (8–10 hours), ideal for longer trips (like Ubud's highlands or Uluwatu). While pricey for one person, you can split costs with hostel buddies.

https://youtu.be/lIV-T2jYhVk

**My advice**: Use Gojek scooters for short trips, and hire a driver or join a local tour for longer journeys or day trips.

## 3. Dining Solo Without Awkwardness

Bali's restaurants are very solo-diner friendly.

https://youtu.be/VuG8s-u_I0Q

- **Warung (local eateries)**: Sit at the counter and enjoy Nasi Goreng (fried rice) or Mie Goreng (fried noodles) for about USD$2–3 per serving—cheap and delicious.
- **Healthy vegetarian cafes**: Ubiquitous in Ubud, many have counter seating or communal tables.
- **Night markets / street stalls**: Perfect for eating on the go. Try satay and Babi Guling (suckling pig).

One thing to note: some upscale restaurants or beach clubs don't welcome solo diners taking up a large table—just ask first or opt for a seat at the bar.

## 4. Is It Safe? Honest Advice for Solo Travelers

![四、安全嗎？給獨旅者的真心話](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775351807418-3e530ee91368827f35ce3d2046220e06.jpg)

Bali is generally safe, but there are a few things to keep in mind:

- **Always wear a helmet when riding a scooter**: Police will fine you, and it's genuinely dangerous.
- **Watch out for monkeys**: In places like Uluwatu and the Sacred Monkey Forest, monkeys will snatch glasses, phones, and food. Don't turn your back on them.
- **Keep valuables on you at all times**: Especially your passport, wallet, and phone. Use the hostel safe if available.
- **Don't accept drinks from strangers**: This applies everywhere.
- **Avoid walking alone down dark alleys late at night**: Especially in more secluded parts of Canggu or Kuta.

**A quick tip**: Balinese people are generally warm and friendly, but if someone is overly eager to chat and insists on taking you to a 'friend's shop,' they usually have an agenda. A polite decline is all it takes.

## 5. The Best Part of Solo Travel: Making New Friends

Coming alone doesn't mean you have to go it alone the whole time.

![五、獨旅最棒的事：認識新朋友](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775352447434-54bd8eecd33bfc05d58f6767e3816f0a.jpg)

- **Stay at a hostel**: Ubud and Canggu have amazing hostels with common areas, free breakfast, yoga classes, and coworking spaces—it's easy to strike up conversations. Top picks: Ubud Raya, Puri Garden, Tribal Bali.
- **Join a day tour**: Like hiking Mount Batur for sunrise, snorkeling, or a Balinese cooking class. Many participants are also solo travelers.
- **Take a yoga or meditation class**: Ubud is Asia's yoga hub—people naturally chat before and after class.
- **Canggu's digital nomad cafés**: Bring your laptop, sit at the bar or a shared table, and conversations flow easily.

## 6. My Suggested Solo Itinerary (6 Days, 5 Nights)

- **Days 1–3: Ubud**: Explore the Royal Palace, Sacred Monkey Forest, Tegallalang Rice Terraces, take a yoga class, watch a Legong dance performance, and enjoy vegan food.

https://youtu.be/JtxZHtijEkE

- **Days 4–6 Canggu**: Surfing (instructors available), watching the sunset at the beach, exploring Batu Bolong Beach, hitting up beach clubs (La Brisa is amazing), and enjoying the café culture.

https://youtube.com/shorts/f0N6FE2dxys

- **One-Day Mini Adventure**: From Ubud, hire a driver to visit Jatiluwih Rice Terraces + Ulun Danu Beratan Temple, or from Canggu, head to Uluwatu’s hidden beaches for sunset + a Kecak dance performance.

https://youtu.be/hQbBTktqEqY

## 7. A Final Reminder: Slow Down

Many first-time solo travelers try to pack their itinerary full. But in Bali, **the most beautiful moments are often the blank spaces**—sitting in a café by the rice fields, staring at the waves on the beach doing nothing, or chatting late into the night with new friends from your hostel.

The point of solo travel isn’t how many sights you see, but how comfortable you are with yourself.

Bali will gently remind you: being alone can feel complete.
