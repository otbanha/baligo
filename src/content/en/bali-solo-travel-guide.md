Are you considering traveling solo to Bali? Let me tell you first: **This is definitely a great idea.**

Bali is one of the most suitable Southeast Asian destinations for solo travel in my opinion. It's safe, tourist-friendly, reasonably priced, and being 'alone' here is never awkward. Whether you want to quietly unwind or meet new friends, Bali can satisfy your needs.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775350592267-mixcollage-05-apr-2026-07-55-am-578.jpg)

Here are practical tips I've compiled for solo travelers based on my personal experiences:

***

## 1. Where to Stay? Choosing the Right Area is Crucial

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775351493354-mixcollage-05-apr-2026-08-11-am-1312.jpg)

Bali is vast, and different areas have completely different vibes:

- **Ubud**: The top choice for solo travel. Yoga, meditation, healthy eating, rice field views—walking alone on the streets feels natural, and everywhere you go, you'll find fellow solo travelers. Plenty of cafes and a thriving hostel culture.
- **Canggu**: A hub for digital nomads, offering beaches, cafes, surfing, and nightlife. Suitable for those who enjoy energy and don't mind noise. The downside is heavy traffic congestion.
- **Seminyak**: Slightly more upscale, with excellent restaurants, spas, and beach clubs. Quieter than Canggu.
- **Nusa Dua**: A high-end resort area. If you have a generous budget and want pure relaxation, this could be an option, but it's more suited for couples or families—solo travel here might feel a bit dull.
- **Jimbaran**: Famous for its sunset seafood barbecues.

**My recommendation**: If your trip is short (5–7 days), split your stay between **Ubud and Canggu** to experience two completely different sides of Bali.

## 2. How to Handle Transportation?

Traveling solo in Bali comes with its biggest headache. Let's be honest: **Bali's transportation is terrible, and it's not very walker-friendly.**

https://youtu.be/ePutHL_Ob1Q

Here are some solutions:

1. **Gojek / Grab**: Southeast Asia's version of Uber, affordable and convenient. Calling a motorcycle ride is the most cost-effective option for solo travelers (from Ubud city center to nearby attractions costs around USD$1-2). However, during peak hours (especially in Canggu), you might have to wait longer for a ride.
2. **Rent a motorcycle**: If you have an international driver's license (and sufficient riding experience), this offers the most freedom. Costs about USD$3-5 per day. **But be warned**: Bali's traffic is chaotic and road conditions vary, so it's not recommended for beginners.
3. **Private car hire**: Around USD$39-58 per day (8-10 hours), ideal for longer trips (like Ubud mountain areas or Uluwatu). While not cheap for solo travelers, you can split the cost with hostel mates.

https://youtu.be/lIV-T2jYhVk

**My recommendation**: Use Gojek for short distances, and hire a private car or join a local tour for longer day trips.

## Three: Dining Alone Without Awkwardness

Bali's restaurants are very solo-traveler friendly.

https://youtu.be/VuG8s-u_I0Q

- **Warung (local eateries)**: Sit at the counter and enjoy Nasi Goreng (fried rice) or Mie Goreng (fried noodles) for USD$2-3 per plate, both cheap and delicious.
- **Healthy vegetarian restaurants**: Plentiful in Ubud, many offer counter seating or shared long tables.
- **Night markets / street stalls**: Perfect for solo travelers to eat while walking around. Recommended dishes include satay and Babi Guling (roast pork rice).

Just one thing to note: Some upscale restaurants or beach clubs might not welcome solo travelers occupying large tables. It's best to ask beforehand or choose counter seating.

## Four: Is It Safe? Honest Advice for Solo Travelers

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775351807418-3e530ee91368827f35ce3d2046220e06.jpg)

Bali is generally safe, but there are a few things to keep in mind:

- **Always wear a helmet when riding a scooter**: Police will stop you, and it's genuinely dangerous.
- **Beware of monkeys**: At places like Uluwatu and the Sacred Monkey Forest, monkeys may snatch glasses, phones, or food. Don't turn your back on them.
- **Keep valuables close**: Especially your passport, wallet, and phone. Use the hostel safe if available.
- **Don't accept drinks from strangers**: This applies everywhere.
- **Avoid walking alone in dark alleys late at night**: Especially in more remote areas of Canggu or Kuta.

**A small reminder**: Balinese people are generally friendly and warm, but those who are overly eager to chat and insist on taking you to 'a friend's shop' usually have ulterior motives. Politely decline.

## Five: The Best Part of Solo Travel—Meeting New People

Traveling alone doesn't mean you have to be alone the entire time.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775352447434-54bd8eecd33bfc05d58f6767e3816f0a.jpg)

- **Stay in hostels**: Ubud and Canggu have many fantastic hostels with common areas, free breakfast, yoga classes, and coworking spaces—great for striking up conversations. Recommendations: Ubud Raya, Puri Garden, Tribal Bali.
- **Join day tours**: Like hiking Mount Batur for sunrise, snorkeling, or taking a Thai cooking class. Many in the group are also solo travelers.
- **Yoga/meditation classes**: Ubud is Asia's yoga hub, and people naturally chat before and after classes.
- **Digital nomad cafes in Canggu**: Bring your laptop to work, sit at the bar or shared tables, and easily start conversations.

## Six: My Suggested Solo Travel Itinerary (6 Days, 5 Nights)

- **Day 1–3 Ubud**: Explore the palace, Sacred Monkey Forest, Tegallalang Rice Terraces, join a yoga class, watch a Legong dance performance, and enjoy vegan food.

https://youtu.be/JtxZHtijEkE

- **Day 4–6 Canggu**: Surf (you can hire an instructor), watch the sunset at the beach, stroll Batu Bolong Beach, visit beach clubs (La Brisa is great), and soak in the café culture.

https://youtube.com/shorts/f0N6FE2dxys

- **A day trip**: From Ubud, hire a car to visit Jatiluwih Rice Terraces and Ulun Danu Beratan Temple, or from Canggu, head to Uluwatu's hidden beach for sunset and a Kecak dance performance.

https://youtu.be/hQbBTktqEqY

## 7. Final Reminder: Slow Down

Many first-time solo travelers try to pack their itineraries full. But in Bali, **the most beautiful moments are often the empty ones** — sitting in a cafe by the rice fields daydreaming, watching the waves at the beach doing nothing, chatting late into the night with friends you just met at the hostel.

The meaning of solo travel isn't about how many attractions you visit, but how comfortable you are with yourself.

Bali will gently show you: being alone can also feel complete.
