In Bali, motorcycles are a common mode of transportation for locals, especially in places like Ubud, where locals often ride through the fields. Many tourists, upon seeing this, might be tempted to think that motorcycles are both economical and convenient, allowing for free exploration of Bali.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_ff2d79a6384a7682bb6d83c4de623131.jpg)

## Is Riding a Motorcycle in Bali Safe?

Short answer: It depends. If you've never ridden a motorcycle before and are only on a short vacation, we don't recommend trying it; but if you're an experienced rider and confident, why not give it a go!

Riding a motorcycle in Bali offers a unique enjoyment, cruising through rice fields, feeling the breeze, and admiring the surrounding beauty, it's unparalleled. But don't forget, Bali's traffic conditions are completely different from other areas, especially in busy urban areas. Locals or vacationing foreigners often drive by intuition, leading to precarious situations. You might also encounter traffic jams or sudden heavy rains. Be mentally prepared!

## What Are the Costs of Renting a Motorcycle?

Motorcycle rental prices in Bali vary depending on the model, age of the bike, and location.

**Short-term Rental Costs**

- Scoopy or Vario 110cc: 70,000–110,000 IDR (approximately $6) per day
- Vario 125cc: 100,000–150,000 IDR (approximately $7) per day
- NMax: 100,000–200,000 IDR (approximately $8) per day

**Long-term Rental Costs** If planning an extended stay in Bali, opting for long-term rentals is usually more cost-effective.

- Scoopy or Vario 110cc: 1,100,000 IDR (approx. $70) per month
- Vario 125cc: 1,500,000 IDR (approx. $95) per month
- NMax: 2,000,000 IDR (approx. $125) per month

**Fueling Up** Street vendors in Bali often sell gasoline in old vodka bottles, with prices around 10,000 IDR (approx. $0.6) per liter.

## Reasons Why Riding a Scooter Isn't Recommended for Bali Travel 🚫🏍️

However, for foreign visitors, riding a scooter in Bali carries significant risks. Here are several reasons why we don't recommend scooters as the primary mode of transportation:

### 1. Heavy Traffic and Complex Rules 🚦

Navigating Bali's traffic isn't easy, especially in popular areas like Kuta, Jimbaran, and Seminyak, where congestion is common. Additionally, Bali follows right-hand driving, which differs from Taiwan or China. For visitors accustomed to left-hand driving, adapting to the local traffic flow can be challenging. Add to this the constant flow of vehicles, rare traffic lights, and drivers who switch lanes during jams... all these factors make scooter riding a higher-risk activity.

### 2. Unpredictable Driving by Foreign Tourists 🌍

Bali's roads are filled not only with locals but also many foreign tourists. Many of these tourists may have never ridden scooters in their home countries, leading to inexperience and unstable driving. This often results in reckless behavior on the roads, posing significant safety hazards. Even if you're a skilled rider, encountering these unpredictable drivers can still put you at risk.

### 3. International Driver's License and Safety Gear Requirements 📄

Riding a scooter in Bali requires an international driver's license; otherwise, you may be stopped by local police and fined. Additionally, Bali mandates that helmets must be worn while riding scooters. However, many tourists overlook this due to discomfort, which is both unsafe and could result in fines.

### 4. High Medical Costs 💰

According to the owner of [Mr. Lobster Seafood Restaurant](/blog/mr-lobster-seafood-restaurant-bali/), who operates in Bali, he often encounters tourists injured in scooter accidents. Due to their injuries, they are unable to participate in other activities and end up resting and dining at his restaurant. It’s important to note that medical costs in Bali are extremely high. If you’re unfortunate enough to get injured, the medical expenses could significantly increase your travel budget, putting a damper on your trip. (Personal experience: A friend once suffered a fractured ankle, and a simple cast/bandage cost 12,000,000 IDR, approximately USD$800.)

### 5. Road Conditions and Natural Factors 🌧️

Road conditions in Bali are unpredictable, especially in Ubud and rural areas where roads are narrow and muddy. Weather changes can quickly worsen road conditions. During the rainy season, sudden downpours can make roads slippery, and Bali’s mountainous terrain adds to the challenge. For tourists unfamiliar with the local terrain, slips and loss of balance are common risks. Bali’s weather is highly variable, so even on seemingly sunny days, sudden rainstorms or strong winds can occur.

### 6. Affordable Alternatives 🚖

Bali offers many affordable transportation options, such as private car hires, ride-hailing scooters, or ride-hailing cars. Compared to renting scooters, these options are safer and more convenient, making them a more reliable choice for tourists unfamiliar with local traffic.

## Key Safety Tips for Scooter Rentals

If you still insist on using a scooter as your mode of transportation, here are some essential reminders to keep in mind:

- **Always wear a helmet!** No matter how uncomfortable or unattractive it may seem, wearing a helmet is a must! Many accidents occur due to overconfidence or driving under the influence.
- **Drive on the left side:** Remember, Bali follows right-hand drive/left-side traffic rules!
- **Avoid hanging your handbag:** Bag snatching incidents are common in the area.
- **Obtain an international driver’s license:** While many rental shops don’t check for licenses, you could be fined if stopped by the police.
- **Purchase health insurance:** Medical expenses are high locally, and insurance provides security in case of accidents.
- **Provide only a photocopy of your passport:** Never hand over the original passport to the rental shop.

## **Inspect the Scooter’s Condition**

### Devil’s in the Details (Pre-Ride Inspection)

Don’t just take the keys and ride off! Before starting the engine or leaving the shop, take out your phone, start recording, and carefully inspect the following points while documenting everything.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_de43e176127f414dfd12cd2155b28955.png)

**1. Exterior Condition (Are You the Scratch King?)**

- **Walk around the scooter:** Examine the front, side panels, rear, and mirrors for any scratches, paint chips, dents, or cracks.
- **Pay special attention:** Check if the screws on the body panels have been tampered with or are loose, as this could indicate a previous accident.
- **Recording tip:** Point out each flaw with your finger while recording, and ideally, ask the owner or staff to appear in the video and give a thumbs-up, confirming they acknowledge these are pre-existing damages.

**2. Basic Functionality Test**

- **Mirrors:** Are they securely fastened? Is the view clear? Many local scooters have loose mirrors, which would fail inspection in Taiwan but are common here. Tighten them or consider switching scooters.
- **Lighting system:** Have a friend stand in front and behind the scooter to test the **headlights (high/low beam), turn signals, and brake lights**. In Bali, driving without lights at night is dangerous, and malfunctioning turn signals increase the risk of rear-end collisions.
- **Horn:** Give it a press. In Bali, honking is usually a "reminder" rather than a "provocation," so the horn must work.

**3. Mechanics and Tires (Safety Essentials)**

- **Brakes:** Test both the front and rear brakes. Are they too loose or too tight? Any unusual noises?
- **Tires:** Check the tread depth. If they're worn smooth, you'll definitely skid on wet roads! Insist on a different bike.
- **Dashboard:** After starting the engine, ensure the speedometer and fuel gauge are functioning properly.

**4. Compartment and Accessories**

- **Helmet:** Usually, a basic helmet is provided. Check if the straps are intact and the inner lining is clean. If hygiene is a concern, consider bringing a lightweight helmet liner from Taiwan or buying an inexpensive shower cap at a local convenience store.
- **Ignition:** Does it start smoothly? Can the handlebar lock?

## Essential Documents (Getting These Wrong Means Trouble)

In Bali, police checks are common when riding a scooter. If you don't have the correct documents, they'll likely demand a 'fine' (aka coffee money), ranging from IDR 50,000 to IDR 500,000.

**1. International Driving Permit (IDP) — Most Important!**

- **Taiwan Driver’s License:** You must first obtain an **International Driving Permit** (available at the DMV, usually issued within a day for trips under 100 km). Note that the pink booklet issued by the DMV must be used alongside your original Taiwan car or motorcycle license.
- **Important:** A simple translation of your Taiwan driver’s license is **not valid** here! Make sure you have an 'International Driving Permit.'
- **Category A:** Ensure that Category A (motorcycle) is checked on your IDP.

**2. Passport and Visa**

- Carry a photocopy of your passport (keep the original locked in your hotel safe). If the police insist on seeing the original, you can retrieve it later.

**3. Rental Agreement**

- Keep the small receipt given by the shop, which includes the license plate number and rental period, behind your phone case or in your wallet for verification if needed.

## Insurance Verification Steps (So You Won’t Cry If Something Happens)

Many people think the insurance included with car rentals is all-encompassing, but that’s not the case! Car rentals in Bali usually come in three states:

**1. Understand the English in the contract**

- **Loss Damage Waiver (LDW) / Theft Insurance:** How much will you pay if the car is stolen?
- **Collision Damage Waiver (CDW) / Collision Insurance:** Will you have to pay a deductible if the car is damaged?
- **TPL (Third Party Liability) / Third-Party Liability Insurance:** This is the most important! If you hit someone or damage someone else’s property, the insurance company will cover the other party. Make sure this is included in the contract.

**2. What is the 'Excess'?**

- Rental car insurance usually has a deductible (Excess/Deposit). For example, if the policy states an excess of 2 million IDR, and the repair costs 3 million IDR, you only pay 2 million, and the insurance company covers 1 million.
- **Ask the rental shop:** Always ask, "If I crash, how much do I pay?" If the amount is unreasonably high, consider paying extra for "zero excess" full coverage.

**3. Take a photo of the insurance sticker with your phone**

- In Bali, legally rented scooters usually have a government-mandated compulsory insurance sticker on the windshield. **Take a photo of it!** It includes the policy number and validity period. If something happens, this number can help you verify basic coverage at the hospital or police station.

## Conclusion

While renting a scooter is indeed the most economical and convenient way to explore Bali, safety should always be the top priority when traveling abroad. Especially in a foreign land, if an unfortunate accident occurs, not only are you unfamiliar with the surroundings, but you may also face exorbitant medical costs, making it not worth the risk. We recommend considering safer modes of transportation when exploring this beautiful island, so you can truly enjoy Bali's scenery and culture without worries.
