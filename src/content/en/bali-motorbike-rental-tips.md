In Bali, motorcycles are a common mode of transportation for locals, especially in places like Ubud, where locals often ride through the fields. Many tourists, upon seeing this, may be tempted by the idea that motorcycles are both economical and convenient, allowing for free exploration of Bali.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_ff2d79a6384a7682bb6d83c4de623131.jpg)

## Is Riding a Motorcycle in Bali Safe?

Short answer: It depends. If you've never ridden a motorcycle before and are only on a short holiday, we do not recommend trying it; but if you're an experienced rider and feel confident, why not give it a go!

Riding a motorcycle in Bali offers a unique enjoyment, weaving through rice fields, feeling the breeze, and admiring the surrounding beauty, it's unparalleled. But don't forget, Bali's traffic conditions are completely different from other places, especially in busy urban areas. Locals or vacationing foreigners often drive by intuition, leading to precarious situations. You might also encounter traffic jams or sudden heavy rains while riding. Be mentally prepared!

## How Much Does It Cost to Rent a Motorcycle?

Motorcycle rental fees in Bali vary depending on the model, age of the bike, and location.

**Short-term Rental Fees**

- Scoopy or Vario 110cc: 70,000–110,000 IDR (approximately $6) per day
- Vario 125cc: 100,000–150,000 IDR (approximately $7) per day
- NMax: 100,000–200,000 IDR (approximately $8) per day

**Long-term Rental Costs** If planning an extended stay in Bali, opting for long-term rentals is usually more cost-effective.

- Scoopy or Vario 110cc: 1,100,000 IDR (approx. $70) per month
- Vario 125cc: 1,500,000 IDR (approx. $95) per month
- NMax: 2,000,000 IDR (approx. $125) per month

**Fuel** Street vendors in Bali often sell gasoline in old vodka bottles, costing about 10,000 IDR (approx. $0.6) per liter.

## Reasons Not to Ride Motorcycles in Bali 🚫🏍️

However, for foreign tourists, riding motorcycles in Bali carries significant risks. Here are several reasons why we advise against choosing motorcycles as the primary mode of transportation:

### 1. Heavy Traffic and Complex Rules 🚦

Bali's traffic environment is challenging, especially in popular areas like Kuta, Jimbaran, and Seminyak, where severe congestion is common. Additionally, Bali drives on the right side of the road, unlike Taiwan or China. For tourists accustomed to left-hand driving, adapting to the local traffic flow can be quite difficult. Factors such as vehicles coming and going, rare traffic lights, and drivers riding into oncoming lanes during traffic jams add to the higher risks of riding motorcycles.

### 2. Unstable Driving by Foreign Tourists 🌍

Bali's roads are filled not only with locals but also many foreign tourists, some of whom may have never ridden motorcycles in their home countries. Their lack of experience and unstable driving can lead to reckless behavior on the roads, posing significant safety hazards. Even if you are skilled, encountering these foreign tourists can still put you at risk due to their unpredictable driving.

### 3. International Driver's License and Safety Gear Requirements 📄

To ride a scooter in Bali, you must have an international driver's license; otherwise, you may be stopped by local police and fined. Additionally, Bali mandates that helmets must be worn while riding scooters. However, many tourists overlook this rule due to discomfort, which is not only unsafe but could also result in fines.

### 4. High Medical Costs 💰

According to the owner of [Mr. Lobster](/blog/mr-lobster-seafood-restaurant-bali/), a restaurant operator in Bali, he often encounters tourists injured in scooter accidents. Due to their injuries, they are unable to participate in other activities and can only rest and dine at his restaurant. It's important to note that medical costs in Bali are extremely high. If you're unfortunate enough to get injured, the medical expenses could significantly increase your travel budget, putting a damper on your trip. (Personal experience: A friend once suffered an ankle fracture, and a simple cast/bandage cost IDR 12,000,000, approximately USD$800.)

### 5. Road Conditions and Natural Factors 🌧️

Road conditions in Bali are unpredictable, especially in Ubud and rural areas, where roads are narrow, muddy, and can deteriorate rapidly with changing weather. During the rainy season, weather changes are swift, and sudden downpours can make roads slippery. Combined with Bali's mountainous terrain, this poses a risk for tourists unfamiliar with the area, who may easily slip or lose balance. Bali's climate is highly variable and difficult to predict, so even on seemingly sunny days, be prepared for sudden rainstorms or strong winds.

### 6. Affordable Alternatives 🚖

Bali offers many affordable transportation options, such as private car hires, ride-hailing scooters, or ride-hailing cars. Compared to renting scooters, these options are safer and more convenient, making them a more reliable choice for tourists unfamiliar with local traffic.

## Key Safety Tips for Renting Scooters

If you still insist on using a scooter as your means of transportation, please pay attention to the following reminders:

- **Always wear a helmet!** No matter how uncomfortable or unattractive it may seem, wearing a helmet is a must! Many accidents occur due to overconfidence or driving under the influence.
- **Drive on the left side:** Remember, Bali drives on the right-hand side/left lane!
- **Avoid hanging your handbag on your body:** Bag snatching incidents are common in the area.
- **You need an international driver's license:** Although many rental shops don't check for licenses, you might be fined if stopped by the police.
- **Purchase health insurance:** Medical expenses are expensive locally, and insurance can provide coverage in case of accidents.
- **Only provide a copy of your passport:** Never hand over the original passport to the rental shop.

## **Check the Scooter's Condition**

### The Devil's in the Details (Pre-Ride Inspection)

Don't just take the keys from the owner and ride off! Before starting the engine or leaving the shop, take out your phone, turn on the video recording mode, and carefully inspect the following key points while recording for evidence.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_de43e176127f414dfd12cd2155b28955.png)

**1. Exterior Condition (Are You the Scratch King?)**

- **Walk around the scooter:** Carefully inspect the front, side panels, rear, and mirrors for any scratches, paint chips, dents, or cracks.
- **Pay special attention:** Check if the screws on the body panels have been pried open or are loose, as this could indicate previous accidents.
- **Recording tip:** While recording, point out each flaw with your finger, and ideally, ask the owner or staff to be in the frame giving a thumbs-up to acknowledge that these are pre-existing damages.

**2. Basic Functionality Test**

- **Mirrors:** Are they securely fastened? Is the view clear? Many local scooters have loose mirrors, which would never pass inspection in Taiwan but are common here. Tighten them or consider switching to another scooter.
- **Lighting system:** Have a friend stand in front and behind the scooter to test the **headlights (high/low beam), turn signals, and brake lights**. In Bali, driving without lights at night is dangerous, and non-functional turn signals can lead to rear-end collisions.
- **Horn:** Give it a press. In Bali, honking is usually a "reminder" rather than a "provocation," so the horn must work.

**3. Mechanical and Tire Condition (Safety Essentials)**

- **Brakes:** Test both the front and rear brakes. Are they too loose or too tight? Any unusual noises?
- **Tires:** Check the tread depth. If they're worn smooth, you'll definitely skid on wet roads! Insist on a different bike if needed.
- **Dashboard:** After starting the engine, check if the speedometer and fuel gauge are functioning properly.

**4. Storage and Accessories**

- **Helmet:** Usually comes with a half-shell helmet. Check if the straps are intact and if the inner lining is clean. If hygiene is a concern, consider bringing a lightweight helmet liner from Taiwan or buying an inexpensive shower cap at a local convenience store to use as padding.
- **Ignition:** Does it start smoothly? Can the handlebar lock be engaged?

## Essential Documents (Bring the wrong ones and prepare to be extorted)

In Bali, police checkpoints are common. If you don't have the correct documents, they'll often demand a 'fine' (also known as coffee money), ranging from IDR 50,000 to IDR 500,000.

**1. International Driving Permit (IDP) — Most Important!**

- **Taiwan Driver's License:** You must first obtain an **International Driving Permit** (available at the Motor Vehicles Office, usually issued within a day for trips under 100 km). Note that the small pink booklet issued by the Motor Vehicles Office must be used together with your original Taiwan car or motorcycle license.
- **Important:** A simple translation of your Taiwan driver's license is **not valid** here! Make sure you have an 'International Driving Permit.'
- **Category A:** Ensure that Category A (motorcycles) is checked on your International Driving Permit.

**2. Passport and Visa**

- Carry a photocopy of your passport (recommend keeping the original locked in your hotel safe). If the police insist on seeing the original, you can retrieve it later.

**3. Rental Agreement**

- Keep the small receipt from the rental shop with the license plate number and rental period, either behind your phone case or in your wallet for verification.

## Insurance Verification Steps (So You Don't Cry When Things Go Wrong)

Many people think the insurance included with car rentals is all-encompassing, but that's not the case! Bali car rentals typically fall into three categories:

**1. Understand the English on the contract**

- **Loss Damage Waiver (LDW) / Theft Insurance:** How much will you pay if the car is stolen?
- **Collision Damage Waiver (CDW) / Collision Insurance:** Will you have to pay a deductible if the car is damaged?
- **TPL (Third Party Liability) / Third-Party Liability Insurance:** This is the most important! If you hit someone or damage someone else's property, the insurance company will compensate the other party. Make sure this is included in the contract.

**2. What is a 'deductible'?**

- Rental car insurance usually has a deductible (Excess/Deposit). For example, if the policy states a deductible of 2 million Indonesian Rupiah, and the repair costs 3 million, you only pay 2 million, and the insurance company covers the remaining 1 million.
- **Ask the rental shop:** Always ask, "If I crash, how much do I pay?" If the amount is ridiculously high, consider paying extra for "zero deductible" comprehensive insurance.

**3. Take a photo of the insurance sticker with your phone**

- In Bali, legally rented motorcycles usually have a government-mandated compulsory insurance sticker on the windshield. **Take a photo of it!** It contains the policy number and validity period. If something happens, this number can help you verify basic coverage at the hospital or police station.

## Conclusion

While renting a scooter is indeed the most economical and convenient way to explore Bali, safety should always be the top priority when traveling abroad. Especially in a foreign land, if an unfortunate accident occurs, not only are you unfamiliar with the surroundings, but you may also face exorbitant medical costs, making it a lose-lose situation. We recommend considering safer modes of transportation while exploring this beautiful island, so you can truly enjoy Bali's scenery and culture without worries.
