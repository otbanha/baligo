In Bali, motorcycles are a common mode of transportation for locals, especially in places like Ubud, where locals often ride through the fields. Many tourists, seeing this, might be tempted by the idea that motorcycles are both economical and convenient for freely exploring Bali.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_ff2d79a6384a7682bb6d83c4de623131.jpg)

## Is Riding a Motorcycle in Bali Safe?

Short answer: It depends. If you've never ridden a motorcycle before and are only here for a short vacation, we don't recommend trying it; but if you're an experienced rider and feel confident, go for it!

Riding a motorcycle in Bali offers a unique enjoyment, cruising through rice fields, feeling the breeze, and admiring the surrounding scenery—it's unparalleled. But don't forget, Bali's traffic conditions are completely different from other areas, especially in busy urban zones. Locals or vacationing foreigners often drive by intuition, leading to precarious situations. You might also encounter traffic jams or sudden heavy rains. Be mentally prepared!

## What Are the Costs of Renting a Motorcycle?

Motorcycle rental costs in Bali vary depending on the model, age of the bike, and location.

**Short-term Rental Costs**

- Scoopy or Vario 110cc: 70,000–110,000 IDR (approx. $6) per day
- Vario 125cc: 100,000–150,000 IDR (approx. $7) per day
- NMax: 100,000–200,000 IDR (approx. $8) per day

**Long-term Rental Costs** If planning an extended stay in Bali, long-term rentals are often more economical.

- Scoopy or Vario 110cc: 1,100,000 IDR (~USD$70) per month
- Vario 125cc: 1,500,000 IDR (~USD$95) per month
- NMax: 2,000,000 IDR (~USD$125) per month

**Fuel** Street vendors in Bali often sell gasoline in old vodka bottles, priced at around 10,000 IDR (~USD$0.6) per liter.

## Reasons Not to Ride a Scooter in Bali 🚫🏍️

However, for foreign visitors, riding a scooter in Bali carries significant risks. Here are several reasons why we don’t recommend it as a primary mode of transportation:

### 1. Heavy Traffic and Complex Rules 🚦

Bali’s traffic environment is challenging, especially in popular areas like Kuta, Jimbaran, and Seminyak, where severe congestion is common. Additionally, Bali follows right-hand driving, unlike Taiwan or China. For visitors accustomed to left-hand driving, adapting to local traffic flow can be difficult. Factors like chaotic vehicle movements, rare traffic lights, and drivers switching to opposite lanes during jams further increase the risks of riding a scooter.

### 2. Unstable Driving by Foreign Tourists 🌍

Bali’s roads are shared not only with locals but also with many foreign tourists. Many of these tourists may have never ridden a scooter in their home countries, leading to inexperience and unstable driving. This often results in reckless behavior on the roads, posing significant safety hazards. Even if you’re skilled, encountering these unpredictable drivers can still put you at risk.

### 3. International Driver's License and Safety Gear Requirements 📄

To ride a motorcycle in Bali, you must hold an international driver's license; otherwise, you risk being stopped by local police and fined. Additionally, Bali mandates that motorcycle riders wear helmets. However, many tourists overlook this rule due to discomfort, which is both unsafe and could result in fines.

### 4. High Medical Costs 💰

According to the owner of [Mr. Lobster Seafood Restaurant](/blog/mr-lobster-seafood-restaurant-bali/) in Bali, he often encounters tourists injured in motorcycle accidents. Due to their injuries, they are unable to participate in other activities and end up resting and dining at his restaurant. It's important to note that medical costs in Bali are extremely high. If you're unfortunate enough to get injured, the medical expenses could significantly increase your travel budget, putting a damper on your trip. (Personal experience: A friend once suffered an ankle fracture, and a simple cast/bandage cost 12,000,000 IDR, approximately USD$800.)

### 5. Road Conditions and Natural Factors 🌧️

Road conditions in Bali are unpredictable, especially in Ubud and rural areas where roads are narrow, muddy, and can deteriorate quickly with weather changes. During the rainy season, weather shifts rapidly, and sudden downpours can make roads slippery. Combined with Bali's mountainous terrain, this poses a risk for tourists unfamiliar with the area, who may easily lose balance or slip. Bali's climate is also highly variable, so even on seemingly clear days, be prepared for sudden rainstorms or strong winds.

### 6. Affordable Alternatives 🚖

Bali offers many affordable transportation options, such as private cars, ride-hailing motorcycles, or ride-hailing cars. Compared to renting motorcycles, these options are safer and more convenient, making them a more reliable choice for tourists unfamiliar with local traffic.

## Key Safety Tips for Renting Motorcycles

If you're still determined to use a scooter as your main mode of transportation, here are some essential reminders to keep in mind:

- **Always wear a helmet!** No matter how uncomfortable or unattractive it may seem, wearing a helmet is non-negotiable. Many accidents happen due to overconfidence or driving under the influence.
- **Drive on the left side:** Remember, Bali follows right-hand drive rules, so stay on the left side of the road!
- **Avoid carrying bags on your body:** Bag snatching is common in the area.
- **Obtain an international driver's license:** While many rental shops don’t check, you could be fined if stopped by the police.
- **Purchase health insurance:** Medical costs are high locally, and insurance ensures you’re covered in case of an accident.
- **Only provide a photocopy of your passport:** Never hand over your original passport to the rental shop.

## **Inspect the Scooter’s Condition**

### The Devil’s in the Details (Pre-Ride Inspection)

Don’t just take the keys and ride off! Before starting the engine or leaving the shop, take out your phone, start recording, and carefully inspect the following points while documenting everything.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_de43e176127f414dfd12cd2155b28955.png)

**1. Exterior Condition (Are You the Scratch King?)**

- **Walk around the scooter:** Examine the front, side panels, rear, and mirrors for any scratches, paint chips, dents, or cracks.
- **Pay special attention:** Check if the screws on the body panels have been tampered with or are loose—this could indicate a past accident.
- **Recording tip:** As you record, point out each flaw with your finger. It’s best to include the owner or staff in the video giving a thumbs-up to confirm these are pre-existing damages.

**2. Basic Functionality Test**

- **Mirrors:** Are they securely fastened? Is the view clear? Many local scooters have loose mirrors, which wouldn’t pass inspection in Taiwan but are common here. Tighten them or request a different scooter.
- **Lighting system:** Have a friend stand in front and behind the scooter to test the **headlights (high and low beam), turn signals, and brake lights**. Driving without lights at night in Bali is dangerous, and non-functional turn signals increase the risk of rear-end collisions.
- **Horn:** Give it a press. In Bali, honking is usually a “reminder” rather than a “provocation,” so the horn must work.

**3. Mechanical and Tire Condition (Safety Essentials)**

- **Brakes:** Test both the front and rear brakes. Do they feel too loose or tight? Any unusual noises?
- **Tires:** Check the tread depth. If they're worn smooth, you'll definitely skid on wet road markings! Insist on a different bike.
- **Dashboard:** After starting the engine, check if the speedometer and fuel gauge are functioning properly.

**4. Storage Compartment & Accessories**

- **Helmet:** Usually, they provide half helmets. Check if the straps are intact and if the inner lining is clean. If hygiene is a concern, consider bringing a lightweight helmet liner from Taiwan or buy an inexpensive shower cap at a local convenience store to use as a barrier.
- **Ignition:** Does it start smoothly? Can the steering lock be engaged?

## Essential Documents (Getting these wrong could lead to extortion)

In Bali, police checks are common while riding. If you can't present the correct documents, they'll often directly ask for a 'fine' (commonly referred to as coffee money), ranging from IDR 50,000 to IDR 500,000.

**1. International Driving Permit (IDP) — Most Important!**

- **Taiwan Driver's License:** You must first obtain an **International Driving Permit** (available at the Motor Vehicles Office, usually issued within a day for distances under 100 km). Note that the small pink booklet issued by the office must be used alongside your original Taiwan car or motorcycle license.
- **Important:** A mere translation of your Taiwan driver's license **is not valid** here! Make sure to have your 'International Driving Permit' ready.
- **Category A:** Ensure that Category A (motorcycles) is checked on your IDP.

**2. Passport & Visa**

- Carry a photocopy of your passport (it's advisable to lock the original in your hotel safe and carry the copy). If the police insist on seeing the original, you can retrieve it later.

**3. Rental Agreement**

- Keep the small receipt from the rental shop with the license plate number and rental period written on it. Store it behind your phone case or in your wallet for easy access when needed.

## Insurance Verification Steps (So You Won't Cry in Case of an Accident)

Many people think the insurance included with car rentals is all-encompassing, but that's not the case! Bali car rentals typically fall into three categories:

**1. Understand the English Terms in the Contract**

- **Loss Damage Waiver (LDW) / Theft Insurance:** How much would you have to pay if the car is stolen?
- **Collision Damage Waiver (CDW) / Collision Insurance:** Would you have to pay a deductible if the car is damaged in a collision?
- **TPL (Third Party Liability) / Third-Party Liability Insurance:** This is the most important! If you hit someone or damage someone else's property, the insurance company will cover the costs. Make sure this is included in the contract.

**2. What is a 'Deductible'?**

- Rental car insurance usually comes with a deductible (Excess/Deposit). For example, if the policy states a deductible of 2 million IDR, and the repair costs 3 million IDR, you only pay 2 million, and the insurance company covers the remaining 1 million.
- **Ask the Rental Shop:** Always ask, "If I crash, how much do I pay?" If the amount seems unreasonably high, consider paying extra for "zero deductible" comprehensive insurance.

**3. Take a Photo of the Insurance Sticker with Your Phone**

- In Bali, legally rented motorcycles usually have a government-mandated insurance sticker on the windshield. **Take a photo of it!** It contains the policy number and validity period. In case of an accident, this number can help you verify basic coverage at the hospital or police station.

## Conclusion

While renting a scooter is indeed the most economical and convenient way to explore Bali, safety should always be the top priority when traveling abroad. Especially in a foreign land, if an unfortunate accident occurs, not only are you unfamiliar with the surroundings, but you may also face exorbitant medical costs, making it not worth the risk. It's recommended to consider safer transportation options when exploring this beautiful island, allowing you to truly enjoy Bali's scenery and culture without worries.
