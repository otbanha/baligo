**"Requesting to Share Your Mobile Hotspot"** could be a scam or inappropriate behavior tactic, especially in public places or tourist areas. Staying alert when encountering such requests is wise, particularly in crowded public spaces! Here are some common scam or risk scenarios:

---

### 📶 Possible Hotspot Sharing Scam Techniques:

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_cdcce95419c06ccd0f04d397e1a75f49.png)

### 1. **Inducing Hotspot Sharing to Steal Data**

A stranger might pretend to have no internet, a dead phone, or act pitiful to get you to turn on your hotspot. Once connected, if your phone or hotspot settings are improper (e.g., open network, unencrypted), they might attempt to hack your device, steal data, or install malicious software.

### 2. Conducting Illegal Activities Using Your IP Address

The person might use your hotspot to connect to the internet for illegal activities, such as downloading pirated content, hacking, or browsing illegal sites. These actions will leave your device's IP address as the trace, potentially making you the target of investigations.

### 3. **Mobile Hotspot-Induced Billing Trap**

If you're using mobile data for your hotspot, prolonged usage by others can quickly deplete your data, leading to unexpectedly high charges, especially dangerous when roaming internationally.

### 4. **Switching Roles: Pretending to Offer a Hotspot for Phishing**

It could also work the other way: scammers set up what appears to be a free Wi-Fi or hotspot (like "Free_WiFi" or "Hotel_Guest") to lure people into connecting, then use man-in-the-middle (MITM) attacks to steal login credentials and credit card information.

---

### 🚨 How to Protect Yourself?

- **Never share your hotspot with strangers**, even if they seem like tourists or students.
- If you really want to help, assist them with opening Google Maps or looking up information, but don’t let them operate your phone themselves.
- Always set a strong password when enabling a hotspot, and avoid using default credentials.
- Avoid connecting to unfamiliar Wi-Fi hotspots offered by strangers, especially unencrypted networks.
- Install antivirus software and keep your system updated to prevent remote device intrusions.

---

### ✍️ Practical Reminder (Travel Scenarios)

In public places like **Bali Airport, cafés, beaches, or shopping malls**, if a stranger asks you to share your hotspot, **politely but firmly decline**.

### 🇬🇧 English Version (For Foreign Tourists)

> “Sorry, I have limited data and can't share my hotspot. I hope you understand!”

Or more explicitly:

> “I'm on a prepaid plan with very little data, so I'm afraid I can't share it. Maybe try asking at a nearby café?”

In Bali, a popular tourist destination, you'll encounter all kinds of people. While it's important to remain kind-hearted during your travels, protecting yourself is even more crucial. When strangers ask to share your mobile hotspot, it may seem like a simple favor, but it could potentially expose you to risks. Whether you're at the airport, hotel lobby, or a beachside café, don't let a momentary act of kindness become the starting point for your personal data being compromised or your bills skyrocketing.

Remember during your journey—caution isn't coldness; it's the most basic form of self-protection.
