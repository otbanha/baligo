Taking elderly parents on an international trip may seem like a heartwarming gesture of filial piety, but for many, it can turn into a thankless and exhausting experience. According to surveys, over half of families have felt stressed during such trips, with some even leaving behind painful memories. This is because traveling with elders often goes beyond tourism—it involves physical, psychological, and even familial challenges.

## Why Is Traveling with Elders So Complicated?

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_e3af29c3d44c3341f926bc65755d9b29.png)

### **1. The Challenge of Physical Differences**

Elders often don’t have the same physical stamina as younger people. What you might consider a relaxing itinerary could be a significant physical burden for them. For example, a schedule packed with long walks or uphill climbs could leave elders exhausted or even unwell. One extreme example shared online involved a family trip to Jimbaran Beach in Bali to watch the sunset. The elders couldn’t navigate the sandy terrain just a few meters from the parking lot and ended up waiting there, leaving everyone feeling helpless.

### **2. Differences in Dietary Preferences**

Many elders are conservative with their food choices and may struggle with foreign cuisines. One family took their elders to Thailand, but the elders couldn’t handle the spicy and sour flavors. They spent the entire trip searching for Chinese restaurants and even brought along instant noodles and preserved snacks, completely missing out on the local culinary experience and dampening everyone’s enthusiasm.

### **3. Elders’ Travel Expectations Differ from Younger Generations**

Elders often prefer comfortable and traditional activities, such as visiting temples, gardens, or historical sites, while younger travelers might lean toward photo ops or extreme adventures. A friend once shared that they booked a helicopter tour over Bali’s Bukit Peninsula, but their parents backed out due to fear of heights, turning what was meant to be a trip highlight into a generational conflict.

### **4. The Burden of Caregiving**

Traveling with elderly parents often feels less like relaxation and more like a journey with "doubled responsibilities." One traveler shared their experience of staying at a resort in Seminyak, Bali, hoping to let their parents unwind. However, due to their father’s high blood pressure, they had to urgently search for a pharmacy in the middle of the night and even involve a local doctor, turning what was supposed to be a romantic getaway into a stressful ordeal.

### **5. The Impact of Elders’ "Psychological Gap"**

Some elderly parents may feel anxious in unfamiliar foreign environments, becoming overly sensitive to minor issues. One reader shared how they took their parents to Japan and planned to use the subway. However, their mother felt "neglected" because the traveler was busy discussing routes with others, and the crowded subway made her uncomfortable. She became upset, feeling that her child wasn’t considerate enough, leading to constant emotional reassurance throughout the trip. As a result, their planned subway sightseeing itinerary was nearly abandoned.

## Painful Experiences: Cases of Elders’ Uncooperativeness

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_c0d8fb61ba227fd4d75c613228e45179.png)

When traveling with elderly parents, differences in habits or psychological gaps often lead to uncooperative behavior, causing the entire trip to stall. Here are a few real-life stories that many readers might relate to:

### **1. "Insisting on Early Mornings, While Everyone Else is Exhausted"**

One family went on vacation to Bali, planning to take it slow and enjoy the island’s laid-back vibe. However, the elderly parent insisted on waking up at 5 AM every morning, demanding that the whole family join them to watch the sunrise. While everyone reluctantly complied on the first day, by the second day, everyone was physically drained, and the trip’s joy was replaced by a "military-like schedule." Eventually, the children and the parent argued over whether to wake up early, creating an awkward atmosphere.

### **2. "Mother Refused the Outdoor Pool Because It Was ‘Dirty’"**

A pair of siblings took their mother to a famous infinity pool hotel in Bali, only for their mother to refuse to swim, claiming the pool was "outdoor and dirty." She preferred to stay in the room drinking tea. In the end, the family had to split up, turning what should have been a memorable trip into an awkward and fragmented experience.

### **3. "If the Attraction Doesn't Suit Their Taste, They Just Walk Away"**

A couple took their elderly parents to Ubud, Bali, to visit the famous rice terraces, believing the natural beauty would leave a lasting impression. However, upon arrival, the parents refused to get out of the car, complaining that the terraces were "too sunny and not photogenic." They stayed inside scrolling through their phones, questioning why they weren’t taken to an air-conditioned mall instead. The children’s highly anticipated trip was instantly dampened.

### **4. "Stubbornness Leads to Conflict with the Guide"**

A family once hired a private driver for a Bali tour. The driver followed the agreed-upon itinerary, but the elderly parents suddenly demanded to visit a different location. When the driver explained that the detour would be inconvenient and incur extra costs, the parents argued, accusing him of "scamming" them. The children had to step in to mediate, leaving everyone feeling awkward and frustrated.

### **5. "Insisting on Carry-On Luggage They Can’t Handle"**

A family traveled to Seminyak, Bali, hoping for a relaxing vacation. They suggested using one large suitcase for everyone’s belongings, but the elderly parents insisted on bringing their own carry-on luggage, saying they "didn’t want to share." At the airport, however, the parents couldn’t carry their bags and demanded help, blaming the luggage design for their struggle, much to the children’s exasperation.

### **6. "Rigid Eating Habits Ruin the Travel Mood"**

One traveler shared that their parents insisted on eating rice every day during their Bali trip and couldn’t tolerate the local spiced cuisine. As a result, the family spent mealtimes searching for Chinese restaurants, disrupting their culinary exploration. The parents would complain, "Why isn’t there normal food here?" turning the trip into a stressful experience.

### **7. "Too Tired to Take Photos, Leaving the Family Portrait Incomplete"**

A family planned a special photoshoot on Sanur Beach, Bali, to capture cherished memories. On the day of the shoot, however, the elderly parents refused to participate, citing the heat and wind messing up their hair. Despite the family’s pleas, the parents remained stubborn, leaving the group photo incomplete and the children deeply disappointed.

### **8. "Demanding Immediate Responses Without Considering the Situation"**

During a family water activity in Bali, the parents suddenly asked for water. However, the children, who had just changed into swimwear and were about to enter the water, couldn't assist immediately and asked them to wait a few minutes. This led to the elders becoming upset, even questioning whether the children still had "filial piety." In the end, everyone's mood was dampened by this small incident, completely ruining the atmosphere of the entire activity.

### **9. "Disliking the Itinerary and Showing Displeasure Throughout"**

A family arranged for their parents to attend a cultural dance performance in Bali, thinking it would allow them to experience local culture. However, the elders found the performance too long and boring, yawning multiple times during the show and even quietly complaining in the audience, drawing attention from others. The children felt extremely embarrassed, making the entire performance unbearable.

***

These "painful experiences" may seem minor, but when accumulated during a trip, they can leave the children feeling exhausted and even defeated. However, understanding and communication are key. Although such trips may not be perfect, with the right mindset, they can still become important memories for family bonding.

## How to Reduce "Effort Without Reward" Situations?

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_7d15cb1eb2935e503e5fa653a8b8eb9e.jpg)

### **1. Avoid Overpacking the Itinerary: Don’t Turn the Trip into a Marathon**

"Painful Experience":

A family traveled to Bali, planning to visit Kuta, Seminyak, and Ubud all in one day. However, the elderly members of the family became exhausted from the constant getting in and out of the car due to the tight schedule, even feeling dizzy and unwell midway. Eventually, the whole family had to cut the trip short and return to the hotel to rest. What was supposed to be an eagerly anticipated journey turned into a chaotic experience.

**Recommendation**: It's best to plan for 2-3 attractions per day, especially since traffic in Bali can be complicated. Try to avoid long-distance travel.

### **2. Avoid High Floors or Hotels Without Elevators: Save Energy and Avoid Risks**

**Painful Experience**:

A family booked a hotel in Ubud with a great view and chose a room on a high floor. However, the elderly members struggled with climbing the stairs due to mobility issues, which even affected their knee health, causing inconvenience throughout the trip.

**Recommendation**: Choose hotels with elevators or stay in lower-floor rooms to avoid excessive stair climbing for the elderly.

### **3. Don’t Make the Elderly Wait, Especially in Hot Weather**

**Painful Experience**:

A family traveling in Bali thought it would be convenient and cost-effective to use ride-hailing apps (Gojek/Grab) within the city. However, due to traffic congestion, the family had to wait under the scorching sun for over 20 minutes. The elderly members suffered from heatstroke, felt unwell, and lost interest in the rest of the trip.

**Recommendation**: When traveling with the elderly, it’s best to opt for private car services to avoid making them wait.

### **4. Avoid Attractions with Many Stairs**

**Cautionary Tale**:

A couple brought their elderly parents to visit Besakih Temple in Bali, only to discover upon arrival that reaching the main structure required climbing numerous stairs. The elderly parents became fatigued midway, with one nearly collapsing from exhaustion, forcing them to abandon the visit.

**Advice**: Before heading out, research whether attractions involve extensive walking or stair climbing. Scenic spots like rice terraces or waterfalls should also be assessed for suitability based on the elderly's stamina. Refer to "[Complete Guide to Senior-Friendly Travel Destinations in Bali](/blog/bali-senior-friendly-travel-guide/)"

### **5. Consider Dietary Preferences and Restrictions**

**Cautionary Tale**:

A family tried the local specialty Babi Guling (roast pig) in Bali, but the elderly parents couldn't adjust to the unfamiliar flavors. They ended up watching others eat while remaining hungry, creating an awkward situation for everyone.

**Advice**: Check restaurant menus in advance and choose establishments with diverse options or those catering to elderly dietary needs.

### **6. Carry Essential Medications for the Elderly**

**Cautionary Tale**:

During one trip, an elderly family member suddenly experienced stomach pain, but the family had forgotten to bring stomach medicine. Unable to quickly purchase suitable medication at a local pharmacy, the entire itinerary was delayed, leaving everyone feeling helpless.

**Recommendation**: Essential medications for elderly family members, such as blood pressure medication, stomach medicine, painkillers, and mosquito repellent, must be prepared before departure.

### **7. Avoid Scheduling Stimulating or Adventurous Activities**

**Painful Experience**:

One family took their elderly relatives on a river tubing activity in Bali, thinking it would be a fun experience. However, they didn’t anticipate having to climb down a long flight of stairs, and the elderly member refused to continue! They were left unsure of what to do with the already paid-for activity.

**Recommendation**: When traveling with elderly family members, focus on relaxed sightseeing activities.

### **8. Elderly Family Members Should Avoid Getting Wet or Damp**

**Painful Experience**:

One family traveled to Bali during the rainy season, but due to an overly rushed itinerary, the elderly family member got soaked multiple times. They caught a cold after returning to the hotel, disrupting the entire travel plan.

**Recommendation**: Always carry rain gear, and during the rainy season, ensure the itinerary allows ample time to prevent elderly family members from becoming overly fatigued or getting wet.

### **9. Avoid Noisy Areas, Prioritize Peace and Comfort**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family stayed at a hotel in the bustling Kuta area of Bali. The constant music from nearby bars and nightclubs made it impossible for the elderly to rest, resulting in several sleepless nights and a noticeable decline in their mood.

**Recommendation**: Choose hotels away from busy areas, such as Sanur or Jimbaran, where the environment is quieter and more comfortable.

### **10. Plan Breaks for Long Drives**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family traveled from Nusa Dua in the south to Lovina in the north to watch dolphins, a journey that took 3-4 hours one way. The elderly experienced back pain from sitting too long and began questioning whether the trip was worth it.

**Recommendation**: For drives longer than 2 hours, plan stops along the way to allow the elderly to stretch or rest.

### **11. Ensure Convenient Access to Restrooms**

**Painful Experience**:

A family visiting the rice terraces in Bali found themselves in a difficult situation due to the limited number of restrooms in the area. An elderly family member had to hold in their urge to use the bathroom, which eventually led to discomfort and complaints about the entire trip.

**Advice**: Remind elderly family members to use the restroom before heading out. When hiring a private car, communicate with the driver to arrange rest stops along the way. Additionally, always confirm the location of restrooms upon arriving at a destination.

### **12. Avoid Overcrowded Places**

**Painful Experience**:

A family took their elderly relatives to watch the sunset at Uluwatu in Bali, only to find the area overly crowded. The elderly couldn't find a place to rest and were nearly pushed over in the crowd, making the entire experience very unpleasant.

**Advice**: Popular spots in Bali often attract large crowds. When traveling with elderly family members, consider visiting during off-peak hours or choosing quieter locations, such as Jimbaran Beach for sunset instead of the crowded Uluwatu.

### **13. Reduce Decision-Making Pressure on Elders**

**Painful Experience**:

A couple, fearing their elderly family member's displeasure, frequently asked for their opinions on where to eat or how to plan the next activity. This constant decision-making pressure led the elder to feel overwhelmed, eventually responding with "whatever you decide," but their mood had already been affected.

**Advice**: Don't leave all decisions to the elders. Plan options in advance and ask for their preferences moderately to reduce the pressure they feel about "needing to make decisions."

### **14. Prepare Sun Protection Items Like Hats and Sunscreen**

**Painful Lesson**:

One family took their elders to a scenic spot with little shade for photos. Due to the hot weather and forgetting to bring sun protection items, the elders' skin got sunburned and stung, making them unwilling to participate in outdoor activities afterward.

**Advice**: It's best to schedule elders' activities in the early morning, considering both their energy levels and Bali's hot weather, especially between noon and 2 PM. Hats, sunscreen, and sunglasses are essential for elders' outdoor activities. Reapply sunscreen every 2-3 hours to avoid sunburn.

### **15. Don’t Set the Air Conditioner Too Cold or Too Hot**

**Painful Lesson**:

One family stayed at a hotel in Bali and set the air conditioner too low for comfort. As a result, the elders caught a cold at night, which even affected their subsequent travel plans.

**Advice**: Adjust the hotel air conditioner to a moderate temperature, ideally between 24-26°C, to avoid it being too cold or too hot. Similarly, the car's air conditioning should also be adjusted according to the elders' comfort.

### **16. Reserve Time for Naps or Rest**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family planned a full-day itinerary from 6 AM to 9 PM, which left their elderly members visibly exhausted by midday. With the tight schedule allowing no time for rest, they had to cut the trip short and return to the hotel early.

**Recommendation**: Always allocate 1-2 hours of rest time in daily itineraries, allowing elderly members to nap or relax and avoid fatigue from prolonged activities. Areas like Sanur or Ubud in Bali offer many cafes and hotels with comfortable resting spaces, making them excellent choices.

### **17. Provide Comfortable Shoes and Clothing**

**Painful Lesson**:

While visiting Monkey Forest, an elderly family member developed blisters from walking in uncomfortable shoes, forcing them to return to the hotel early.

**Recommendation**: Prepare shoes that fit the elderly’s feet comfortably, avoiding new or hard-soled shoes. Clothing should be lightweight and breathable, with additional items like raincoats, warm jackets, or swimwear adjusted based on the itinerary.

### **18. Care for Elderly Emotions, Avoid Neglect**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family was busy taking Instagram-worthy photos at Seminyak Beach in Bali, leaving an elderly member waiting alone. Feeling neglected, the elder became upset, affecting the overall travel mood.

**Recommendation**: During the trip, interact more with the elderly to help them feel included in the itinerary, avoiding long periods where they might feel "left out." You can arrange family photo sessions in certain settings or share your travel experiences with them, making them feel valued.

### **19. Prepare Emergency Contact Information in Advance**

**Painful Lesson**:

During a family trip, an elderly member suddenly got separated from the group. Since they didn’t have a phone, it took two hours to find them, filled with anxiety and worry.

**Recommendation**: Prepare important contact details in advance, such as the hotel address, local driver’s phone number, and emergency contacts. You can also create a small card that says, "I'm lost! Help me to call my family," along with the phone number. Keep this information in a small pouch for the elderly to carry, ensuring you’re prepared for unexpected situations.

### **20. Maintain Patience and Flexibility, Adjust Your Mindset When Needed**

When traveling with the elderly, patience is key. They might complain due to discomfort or anxiety during the trip. In such moments, it’s important to stay calm, empathize, and focus on creating a pleasant atmosphere rather than resisting their emotions. For example, if they feel the itinerary is too rushed, you can adjust the schedule and save some attractions for future trips.

## Final Reminder: Every Moment on the Journey is a Memory

Although traveling abroad with the elderly requires attention to many details, these shared moments will likely become your most cherished memories. With proper preparation, patient communication, and reasonable compromises, the journey can become smoother and more meaningful. Relax your mindset and view this trip as a "bonding and relationship-maintaining journey." No matter how the process unfolds, the memories will be sweet! 😊
