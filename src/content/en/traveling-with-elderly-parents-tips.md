Taking elderly parents on international trips may seem like a heartwarming gesture of filial piety, but for many, it can turn into a thankless endeavor. Surveys show that over half of families have experienced stress during such trips, often leaving behind painful memories. Traveling with elders isn't just about tourism—it often involves physical, psychological, and familial challenges.

## Why Is Traveling with Elders So Challenging?

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_e3af29c3d44c3341f926bc65755d9b29.png)

### **1. The Challenge of Physical Differences**

Most elders aren't as physically fit as younger travelers. What you consider a relaxing itinerary might be a huge physical burden for them. For example, schedules with lots of walking or uphill climbs can leave elders exhausted or even unwell. One extreme case shared online involved a family trip to Jimbaran Beach in Bali to watch the sunset. The elders struggled to walk just a few meters on the sandy terrain and ended up waiting in the parking lot, leaving everyone feeling helpless.

### **2. Differences in Dietary Preferences**

Many elders are conservative with their diets and may struggle with foreign cuisine. One family took their elders to Thailand, but the elders couldn't handle the spicy and sour flavors. They spent the entire trip searching for Chinese restaurants and even brought instant noodles and preserved snacks, completely missing out on local culinary experiences and dampening everyone's enthusiasm.

### **3. Elders' Sightseeing Expectations Differ from Younger Travelers**

Elders often prefer comfortable and traditional activities, such as visiting temples, gardens, or historical sites, while younger travelers might prioritize photo ops or extreme adventures. A friend once shared that they booked a helicopter tour over Bali's Bukit Peninsula, but their parents backed out due to fear of heights, turning what was meant to be a trip highlight into a generational conflict.

### **4. The Burden of Caregiving Responsibility**

Traveling with elderly parents often feels less like relaxation and more like "double the responsibility." One traveler shared how they booked a resort in Seminyak, Bali, hoping their parents could unwind. However, when their father’s blood pressure spiked at night, they had to urgently search for a pharmacy and even consult a local doctor, turning what was meant to be a romantic getaway into a stressful ordeal.

### **5. The Impact of Elders' Psychological Discomfort**

Some elderly travelers may feel anxious in unfamiliar environments, becoming overly sensitive to minor issues. One reader recounted taking their parents to Japan and planning to use the subway. However, their mother felt "neglected" when the traveler was busy discussing directions with others, and the crowded subway ride only added to her discomfort. This led to tension throughout the trip, with much of the planned itinerary abandoned to manage emotions.

## Painful Experiences: Cases of Elders Being Uncooperative

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_c0d8fb61ba227fd4d75c613228e45179.png)

When traveling with elderly parents, differences in habits or psychological discomfort can lead to uncooperative behavior, stalling the entire trip. Here are a few real-life stories that many readers might relate to:

### **1. Insisting on Early Mornings While Everyone Else Is Exhausted**

One family vacationing in Bali planned to take it slow and enjoy the island’s laid-back vibe. However, the elder insisted on waking up at 5 AM every morning to watch the sunrise, dragging the whole family along. While everyone reluctantly complied on the first day, by the second day, exhaustion set in, and the trip’s joy was replaced by a "military-style schedule." Arguments over whether to wake up early created an awkward atmosphere.

### **2. Mother Refuses to Use the Outdoor Pool Due to Fear of Dirt**

A pair of siblings took their mother to a famous infinity pool hotel in Bali, only for her to declare the pool "outdoor and dirty" and refuse to swim. She opted to stay in the room drinking tea instead. The family ended up splitting up, turning what should have been a shared experience into an awkward memory.

### **3. "If the Attraction Isn't Their Cup of Tea, They Won't Go"**

A couple took their elderly parents to visit the rice terraces in Ubud, Bali, believing the natural beauty would leave a lasting impression. However, upon arrival, the parents refused to get out of the car, complaining that the terraces were "too sunny and not photogenic." They stayed inside scrolling through their phones and even questioned why they weren’t going to "an air-conditioned mall" instead. The children’s eagerly anticipated trip was instantly dampened.

### **4. "Stubborn Opinions Lead to Conflicts with the Guide"**

A family once hired a private driver for their Bali trip, with a pre-arranged itinerary covering major attractions. However, the elderly parents suddenly demanded to visit different locations. When the driver explained that the route wouldn’t be convenient and that changing the plan would incur additional costs, the parents not only refused to understand but also argued with the driver, accusing him of "scamming" them. The children had to step in to mediate, leaving everyone feeling awkward and frustrated.

### **5. "Insist on Carrying Hand Luggage, But Can't Handle It"**

A family took their parents to Seminyak, Bali, hoping for a relaxing vacation. They suggested using one large suitcase for everyone’s belongings, but the parents insisted on bringing their own carry-on luggage, stating they "didn’t want to mix their things with the kids’." However, at the airport, the parents couldn’t manage their luggage and asked the children for help, even blaming the luggage design for being impractical. The children were left exasperated.

### **6. "Stubborn Eating Habits Ruin the Travel Mood"**

One reader shared that their parents insisted on eating rice every day during their Bali trip and couldn’t tolerate the local spiced cuisine. As a result, every mealtime turned into a search for Chinese restaurants, disrupting the family’s culinary experience. The parents would also complain, "Why don’t these places have normal food?" making the entire trip stressful.

### **7. "Too Tired to Take Photos, Leaving the Family Portrait Incomplete"**

A family planned a special photoshoot on Sanur Beach in Bali to capture cherished memories. However, on the day of the shoot, the elderly parents refused to participate, citing the hot weather and wind messing up their hair. Despite the family’s efforts to persuade them, the parents remained stubborn, resulting in an incomplete family portrait. The children couldn’t help but feel disappointed.

### **8. "Demanding Immediate Attention Without Considering the Situation"**

While a family was participating in water activities in Bali, the parents suddenly said they wanted water. However, the children, who had just changed into swimsuits and were ready to get into the water, couldn't help immediately and asked them to wait a few minutes. This led to the elders becoming unhappy, even questioning whether the children still had "filial piety." In the end, everyone's mood was ruined by this small incident, completely affecting the atmosphere of the entire activity.

### **9. "Disliking the Itinerary and Showing Displeasure Throughout"**

A family arranged for their parents to attend a cultural dance performance in Bali, thinking it would allow them to experience local traditions. However, the elders complained that the performance was too long and boring, yawning multiple times during the show and even whispering complaints in the audience, drawing attention from those around them. The children felt extremely embarrassed, making the entire performance unbearable.

***

These "painful experiences" may seem minor, but when accumulated during a trip, they can leave the children feeling exhausted and even defeated. However, understanding and communication are key. Although such trips may not be perfect, adjusting one's mindset can still turn them into important memories of family bonding.

## How to Reduce "Effort Without Reward" Situations?

### **1. Don’t Make the Itinerary Too Packed: Avoid Turning the Trip into a "Marathon"**

**Painful Experience**:

A family traveled to Bali, planning to visit Kuta, Seminyak, and Ubud all in one day. However, the elderly members of the family became exhausted from the constant getting in and out of the car due to the tight schedule. One even felt dizzy and unwell midway through the trip, forcing the entire family to cut their itinerary short and return to the hotel to rest. What was supposed to be an exciting journey turned into a chaotic experience.

**Recommendation**: It’s best to plan only 2-3 attractions per day, especially since Bali’s traffic conditions can be complicated. Avoid long-distance travel as much as possible.

### **2. Avoid High Floors or Hotels Without Elevators: Save Energy and Reduce Risks**

**Painful Experience**:

A family booked a hotel in Ubud with a great view and chose a room on a high floor. However, the elderly members struggled with the stairs due to mobility issues, which even affected their knee health, making the entire trip inconvenient.

**Recommendation**: Choose hotels with elevators or stay on lower floors to avoid excessive stair climbing for elderly family members.

### **3. Don’t Make Elderly Family Members Wait, Especially in Hot Weather**

**Painful Experience**:

A family traveling in Bali thought it would be convenient and cost-effective to use ride-hailing apps (Gojek/Grab) within the city. However, due to traffic congestion, they ended up waiting under the scorching sun for over 20 minutes. An elderly family member suffered from heatstroke, felt unwell, and lost interest in the rest of the trip.

**Recommendation**: When traveling with elderly family members, it’s best to opt for private car services to avoid making them wait.

### **4. Avoid Attractions with Stairs**

**Painful Lesson**:

A couple brought their elderly parents to visit Besakih Temple in Bali, only to discover upon arrival that reaching the main structure required climbing numerous stairs. The elderly parents grew tired midway and nearly fell due to exhaustion, forcing them to abandon the visit.

**Advice**: Before heading out, research whether the attraction involves long walks or stair climbing. For places like rice terraces or waterfalls, assess whether the elderly have sufficient stamina. Check out our "[Complete Guide to Senior-Friendly Travel Destinations in Bali](/blog/bali-senior-friendly-travel-guide/)"

### **5. Consider Dietary Preferences and Restrictions**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family tried the local specialty Babi Guling (roast suckling pig) in Bali, but the elderly parents couldn’t adjust to the foreign flavors. They ended up sitting hungry while others enjoyed the meal, creating an awkward situation for everyone.

**Advice**: Check restaurant menus in advance and choose places with diverse options or dishes that cater to the dietary needs of elderly travelers.

### **6. Carry Essential Medications for Elderly Travelers**

**Painful Lesson**:

During one trip, an elderly family member suddenly experienced stomach pain, but the family had forgotten to bring stomach medicine. Unable to quickly purchase suitable medication at a local pharmacy, the entire itinerary was delayed, leaving everyone feeling helpless.

**Advice**: Essential medications for elderly family members, such as blood pressure medication, stomach medicine, pain relievers, and mosquito repellent, should always be prepared before departure.

### **7. Avoid Scheduling Stimulating or Adventurous Activities**

**Painful Experience**:

One family took their elderly relatives on a Bali river tubing activity, expecting it to be a fun experience. However, they didn’t anticipate having to climb down a long flight of stairs, and the elderly member refused to continue! The prepaid activity left them unsure of what to do.

**Advice**: When traveling with elderly family members, prioritize relaxed sightseeing activities.

### **8. Elderly Family Members Should Avoid Getting Wet in Rain or Damp Conditions**

**Painful Experience**:

A family traveled to Bali during the rainy season and, due to an overly rushed itinerary, their elderly family member got soaked in the rain multiple times. Upon returning to the hotel, they caught a cold, disrupting the entire travel plan.

**Advice**: Always carry rain gear, and during the rainy season, ensure the itinerary allows ample time to prevent elderly family members from becoming overly fatigued or getting wet.

### **9. Avoid Noisy Areas, Prioritize Peace and Comfort**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family stayed at a hotel in the bustling Kuta area of Bali. The constant music from nearby bars and clubs at night made it impossible for the elderly to rest, resulting in several sleepless nights and a decline in mood.

**Recommendation**: Choose hotels away from busy areas, such as Sanur or Jimbaran, where the environment is relatively quiet and comfortable.

### **10. Plan Breaks for Long Drives**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family traveled from Nusa Dua in the south to Lovina in the north to watch dolphins, a journey that took 3-4 hours one way. The elderly experienced back pain from the long drive and began to question whether the trip was worth it.

**Recommendation**: For drives longer than 2 hours, arrange for stops along the way to allow the elderly to stretch or rest.

### **11. Ensure Convenient Access to Restrooms**

**Painful Experience**:

A family visiting the rice terraces in Bali found themselves in a difficult situation due to the limited number of restrooms in the area. An elderly family member had to hold their bladder for an extended period, eventually leading to discomfort and complaints about the entire trip.

**Advice**: Remind elderly family members to use the restroom before heading out. When hiring a private car, communicate with the driver to arrange rest stops along the way. Additionally, always confirm the location of restrooms upon arriving at a destination.

### **12. Avoid Overcrowded Places**

**Painful Experience**:

A family took their elderly relatives to Uluwatu in Bali to watch the sunset, but the area was so crowded that the elderly couldn't find a place to rest and were nearly knocked over by the crowd, making the entire experience unpleasant.

**Advice**: Popular spots in Bali often attract large crowds. When traveling with elderly family members, consider visiting during off-peak hours or opting for quieter locations, such as watching the sunset at Jimbaran Beach instead of the crowded Uluwatu.

### **13. Reduce Decision-Making Pressure on Elders**

**Painful Experience**:

A couple, fearing their elderly family member's displeasure, constantly sought their opinion on decisions like where to eat or what to do next. This led to the elder feeling pressured, eventually saying "Whatever you decide," but their mood had already been affected.

**Advice**: Don't leave all decisions to the elders. Plan options in advance and simply ask for their preferences to reduce the pressure they might feel about "needing to make decisions."

### **14. Prepare Sun Protection Items Like Hats and Sunscreen**

**Painful Lesson**:

One family took their elders to a scenic spot with little shade for photos. Due to the hot weather and forgetting to bring sun protection, the elders' skin got sunburned and stung, making them unwilling to participate in outdoor activities afterward.

**Advice**: It's best to schedule elders' activities early in the morning, considering both their energy levels and Bali's hot weather, especially between noon and 2 PM. Hats, sunscreen, and sunglasses are essential for elders during outdoor activities. Reapply sunscreen every 2-3 hours to avoid sunburn.

### **15. Don’t Set the Air Conditioning Too Cold or Too Hot**

**Painful Lesson**:

One family stayed at a hotel in Bali and set the air conditioning too low for comfort, resulting in the elders catching a cold at night, which even affected their subsequent travel plans.

**Advice**: Adjust the hotel air conditioning to a moderate temperature, ideally between 24-26°C, to avoid it being too cold or too hot. Similarly, adjust the car's air conditioning according to the elders' comfort.

### **16. Reserve Time for Naps or Rest**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family planned a full-day itinerary from 6 AM to 9 PM, but the elderly members were visibly exhausted by midday. With the tight schedule leaving no room for rest, they had to cut the trip short and return to the hotel early.

**Recommendation**: Always allocate 1-2 hours of rest time in the daily itinerary, allowing elderly family members to nap or relax and avoid fatigue from prolonged activities. Areas like Sanur or Ubud in Bali offer many cafes and hotels with comfortable resting spaces, making them great options.

### **17. Provide Comfortable Shoes and Clothing**

**Painful Lesson**:

During a visit to Monkey Forest Park, an elderly family member developed blisters from wearing uncomfortable shoes during extended walking, forcing them to return to the hotel early.

**Recommendation**: Prepare comfortable shoes that fit the elderly's feet well, avoiding new or hard-soled shoes. Clothing should be lightweight and breathable, with additional items like raincoats, warm jackets, or swimwear adjusted based on the itinerary.

### **18. Attend to Elderly Emotions, Avoid Neglect**

**Painful Lesson**:

While the family was busy taking influencer-style photos at Seminyak Beach in Bali, an elderly member was left waiting alone. Feeling neglected, they became upset, which dampened the mood for the rest of the trip.

**Recommendation**: Engage more with elderly family members during the trip to help them feel included, avoiding long periods where they might feel 'left out.' Arrange for family photos in some settings or share your travel experiences with them, making them feel valued.

### **19. Prepare Emergency Contact Information in Advance**

**Painful Experience**:

During a family trip, an elderly member suddenly got separated from the group. The elder didn't have a mobile phone, and it took two hours to find them, filled with anxiety and distress.

**Recommendation**: Prepare important contact details in advance, such as the hotel address, local driver's phone number, and emergency contact numbers. Alternatively, write a small card saying 'I'm lost! Help me to call my family.' with the phone number included. Provide this information for the elder to carry in their waist bag to avoid panic in case of emergencies.

### **20. Maintain Patience and Tolerance, Adjust Your Mindset When Necessary**

Traveling with elderly family members requires patience above all. They might complain due to discomfort or anxiety during the trip. It's essential for younger family members to stay calm, empathize, and focus more on creating a pleasant atmosphere rather than confronting negative emotions. For instance, if the elder feels the itinerary is too rushed, consider adjusting the order of activities or saving some attractions for the next trip.

## Final Reminder: Every Moment on the Journey is a Memory

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_696668bc21571e4b94503611a5a3bc51.png)

Despite the numerous details to pay attention to when traveling abroad with elderly family members, these shared travel times are likely to become the most precious memories in the future. Through prior preparation, patient communication, and appropriate compromises, the journey can become smoother and more meaningful. Try to relax and view this trip as a 'journey of repairing and maintaining family bonds.' Believe that no matter how the process unfolds, the memories will be sweet! 😊
