Taking elderly parents on international trips may seem like a warm and filial gesture on the surface, but for many, it can turn into a thankless experience. According to surveys, over half of families have felt stressed during such trips, even leaving behind 'painful' memories. This is because traveling with elders often goes beyond just tourism—it comes with physical, psychological, and even familial challenges.

## Why Is Traveling with Elders So Complicated?

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_e3af29c3d44c3341f926bc65755d9b29.png)

### **1. The Challenge of Physical Differences**

Most elders are not as physically fit as younger people. What you consider a relaxing itinerary might be a huge physical burden for them. For example, if the schedule involves a lot of walking or uphill climbs, it could leave them exhausted or even unwell. One extreme case shared online involved a family visiting Jimbaran Beach in Bali to watch the sunset. The elderly parent struggled to walk the roughly 10 meters of sandy terrain to reach the shore and ended up waiting in the parking lot, leaving everyone feeling helpless.

### **2. Differences in Eating Habits**

Many elders are conservative with their food preferences and may struggle to adapt to foreign cuisines. One family took their elders to Thailand, but the elders couldn’t handle the sour and spicy flavors. They spent the entire trip searching for Chinese restaurants and even brought along instant noodles and preserved snacks, completely missing out on the local culinary experience and dampening everyone’s enthusiasm.

### **3. Elders’ ‘Tourism Expectations’ Differ from Younger Generations**

Elders’ travel preferences often lean toward comfort and tradition, such as visiting temples, gardens, or historical sites, while younger generations might prioritize photo opportunities or adventurous activities. A friend once shared that they arranged a helicopter tour over Bali’s Bukit Peninsula, but their parents, afraid of heights, opted out. What was meant to be a highlight of the trip turned into a 'generational conflict trigger.'

### **4. The Burden of Caregiving Responsibility**

Traveling with elderly parents often feels less like relaxation and more like "double the responsibility." One traveler shared their experience of staying at a resort in Seminyak, Bali, hoping to let their parents unwind. However, when their father’s blood pressure spiked in the middle of the night, they had to urgently search for a pharmacy and even involve a local doctor. What was supposed to be a romantic getaway quickly turned into a stressful ordeal.

### **5. The Impact of Elders’ "Psychological Gap"**

Some elderly parents may feel anxious in unfamiliar foreign environments, becoming overly sensitive to minor issues. One reader shared how they took their parents to Japan and planned to use the subway. However, their mother felt "neglected" because the traveler was busy discussing directions with others, and the crowded subway added to her discomfort. She became upset, feeling that her child wasn’t considerate enough, leading to a trip filled with emotional reassurance and derailing their planned subway sightseeing.

## Painful Experiences: Cases of Elders’ Uncooperativeness

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_c0d8fb61ba227fd4d75c613228e45179.png)

When traveling with elderly parents, differences in habits or psychological gaps often lead to uncooperative behavior, causing the entire trip to stall. Here are a few real-life stories that many readers might relate to:

### **1. "Insisting on Waking Up Early, While the Whole Family Is Exhausted"**

One family vacationing in Bali planned to take it slow and enjoy the island’s laid-back vibe. However, the elderly parent insisted on waking up at 5 a.m. every morning, demanding that the whole family join them to watch the sunrise. While everyone reluctantly complied on the first day, by the second day, exhaustion set in, and the trip’s joy was replaced by a "military-style schedule." Eventually, arguments broke out between the children and the parent over whether to wake up early, creating an awkward atmosphere.

### **2. "Mother Refused the Infinity Pool, Calling It Dirty"**

A pair of siblings took their mother to a famous infinity pool hotel in Bali, only for her to refuse to swim, calling the pool "outdoor and dirty." She opted to stay in the room drinking tea instead. In the end, the family had to split up, turning what should have been a memorable trip into an awkward and fragmented experience.

### **3. "If the Spot Doesn't Suit You, Just Walk Away"**

A couple took their elderly parents to Ubud, Bali, to visit the famous rice terraces, thinking the natural beauty would leave a lasting impression. However, upon arrival, the parents refused to get out of the car, complaining that the terraces were "too sunny and not photogenic." They stayed inside scrolling through their phones and questioned why they weren’t taken to "an air-conditioned mall" instead. The children’s excitement for the trip was quickly dampened.

### **4. "Stubbornness Leads to Conflict with the Guide"**

A family once hired a private driver for a Bali tour, with a pre-arranged itinerary to visit major attractions. Midway, the elderly parents suddenly demanded to change destinations. When the driver explained that the detour would be inconvenient and incur extra costs, the parents argued, accusing him of "scamming" them. The children had to step in to mediate, leaving everyone feeling awkward and frustrated.

### **5. "Insist on Carrying Hand Luggage, But Can’t Handle It"**

A family took their parents to Seminyak, Bali, hoping for a relaxing vacation. They suggested using one large suitcase for everyone’s belongings, but the parents insisted on bringing their own carry-on luggage, claiming they "didn’t want to share." At the airport, however, the parents couldn’t manage their bags and asked the children for help, even blaming the luggage design for being impractical. The children were left exasperated.

### **6. "Rigid Eating Habits Ruin the Travel Mood"**

One traveler shared that their parents insisted on eating rice every day during their Bali trip and couldn’t tolerate the local spiced cuisine. As a result, the family spent mealtimes searching for Chinese restaurants, disrupting their culinary exploration. The parents would complain, "Why isn’t there normal food here?" turning the trip into a stressful experience.

### **7. "Too Tired to Take Photos, Leaving Family Pictures Incomplete"**

A family planned a special photoshoot on Sanur Beach, Bali, to capture cherished memories. On the day of the shoot, however, the elderly parents refused to participate, citing the heat and wind messing up their hair. Despite the family’s efforts to persuade them, the parents remained stubborn, leaving the group photo incomplete. The children were left deeply disappointed.

### **8. "Demanding Immediate Responses Without Considering the Situation"**

While participating in water activities in Bali with their family, the parents suddenly asked for water. However, the children, who had just changed into their swimsuits and were about to enter the water, couldn't help immediately and asked them to wait a few minutes. This led to the elders becoming unhappy, even questioning whether the children still had "filial piety." In the end, everyone's mood was spoiled by this small incident, completely affecting the atmosphere of the entire activity.

### **9. "Disliking the Itinerary and Showing Displeasure Throughout"**

A family arranged for their parents to attend a cultural dance performance in Bali, thinking it would allow them to experience local traditions. However, the elders found the performance too long and boring, yawning multiple times during the show and even quietly complaining in the audience, drawing attention from those around them. The children felt extremely embarrassed, making the entire performance unbearable.

***

These "painful experiences" may seem minor, but when accumulated during a trip, they often leave the children feeling exhausted and even defeated. However, understanding and communication are key. Although such journeys may not be perfect, with the right mindset, they can still become important memories for family bonding.

## How to Reduce "Effort Without Appreciation" Situations?

### **1. Avoid Overpacking the Itinerary: Don’t Turn the Trip into a Marathon**

"Painful Experience":

A family traveled to Bali, planning to visit Kuta, Seminyak, and Ubud in a single day. However, the elderly members became exhausted from the constant getting in and out of the car due to the tight schedule, even feeling dizzy and unwell midway. The family had to cut the trip short and return to the hotel to rest. What was supposed to be an exciting journey turned into a chaotic experience.

**Recommendation**: It's best to plan 2-3 attractions per day, especially since Bali's traffic can be complicated. Avoid long-distance travel as much as possible.

### **2. Avoid High Floors or Hotels Without Elevators: Save Energy, Reduce Risks**

**Painful Experience**:

A family booked a hotel in Ubud with a great view and chose a room on a high floor. However, the elderly members struggled with the stairs due to mobility issues, even affecting their knee health, making the entire trip inconvenient.

**Recommendation**: Choose hotels with elevators or stay on lower floors to avoid excessive stair climbing for elderly family members.

### **3. Don’t Make Elderly Family Members Wait, Especially in Hot Weather**

**Painful Experience**:

A family traveling in Bali thought using ride-hailing apps (Gojek/Grab) in the city would be convenient and cost-effective. However, due to traffic, the family had to wait under the scorching sun for over 20 minutes. An elderly member suffered from heatstroke, affecting their mood and interest in the rest of the trip.

**Recommendation**: When traveling with elderly family members, opt for private car services to avoid making them wait.

### **4. Avoid Attractions with Stairs**

**Painful Lesson**:

A couple took their elderly parents to visit Bali's Besakih Mother Temple, only to discover upon arrival that reaching the main structure required climbing numerous stairs. The elderly parents became fatigued midway, with one nearly collapsing from exhaustion, forcing them to abandon the visit.

**Advice**: Before heading out, research whether the attraction involves extensive walking or stair climbing. For sites like rice terraces or waterfalls, assess whether the elderly have sufficient stamina. Refer to "[Complete Guide to Bali Travel Destinations Suitable for Seniors](/blog/2024-11-27-6746742bfd897800018d4906/)"

### **5. Consider Dietary Preferences and Restrictions**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family tried Bali's famous dish, Babi Guling (roast suckling pig), but the elderly parents couldn't adjust to the unfamiliar flavors. They ended up sitting hungry while watching others eat, creating an awkward situation for everyone.

**Advice**: Check restaurant menus in advance and choose places with diverse options or dishes that cater to the dietary needs of elderly travelers.

### **6. Carry Essential Medications for Elderly Travelers**

**Painful Lesson**:

During one trip, an elderly family member suddenly experienced stomach pain, but the family had forgotten to bring stomach medicine. Unable to quickly purchase suitable medication at a local pharmacy, the entire itinerary was delayed, leaving everyone feeling helpless.

**Advice**: Essential medications for elderly family members, such as blood pressure medication, stomach medicine, pain relievers, and mosquito repellent, must be prepared before departure.

### **7. Avoid Scheduling Stimulating or Adventurous Activities**

**Painful Experience**:

One family took their elderly relatives on a river tubing activity in Bali, thinking it would be a fun experience. However, they didn’t anticipate having to climb down a long flight of stairs, and the elderly family member refused to continue! They were left unsure what to do with the already paid-for activity.

**Advice**: When traveling with elderly family members, focus on relaxed sightseeing activities.

### **8. Elderly Family Members Should Not Get Wet or Damp**

**Painful Experience**:

One family traveled to Bali during the rainy season. Due to an overly rushed itinerary, the elderly family member got soaked multiple times. Upon returning to the hotel, they caught a cold from being chilled, which disrupted the entire travel plan.

**Advice**: Always carry rain gear, and during the rainy season, ensure the itinerary allows ample time to prevent elderly family members from becoming overly fatigued or getting wet.

### **9. Avoid Noisy Areas, Seek Peace and Comfort**

**Painful Experience**:

A family stayed at a hotel in the bustling Kuta area of Bali. The constant music from nearby bars and nightclubs at night made it impossible for the elderly to rest, resulting in several sleepless nights and a decline in their mood.

**Recommendation**: Choose hotels away from busy areas, such as Sanur or Jimbaran, where the environment is relatively quiet and comfortable.

### **10. Plan Breaks for Long Drives**

**Painful Experience**:

A family traveled from Nusa Dua in the south to Lovina in the north to watch dolphins, a one-way trip taking 3-4 hours. The elderly experienced back pain from the long drive and began to question whether the entire trip was worth it.

**Recommendation**: For drives longer than 2 hours, arrange for mid-journey stops to allow the elderly to stretch or rest.

### **11. Ensure Convenient Access to Restrooms**

**Painful Experience**:

A family visiting the rice terraces in Bali found themselves in an uncomfortable situation due to the limited number of restrooms in the area. An elderly member had to hold their bladder for an extended period, ultimately leading to physical discomfort and complaints about the entire trip.

**Advice**: Remind elderly family members to use the restroom before heading out. When hiring a private car, communicate with the driver to arrange rest stops along the way. Additionally, always confirm the location of restrooms upon arriving at a destination.

### **12. Avoid Overcrowded Places**

**Painful Experience**:

A family took their elderly relatives to watch the sunset at Uluwatu in Bali, only to find the area excessively crowded. The elderly struggled to find a place to rest and were nearly knocked over in the crowd, making the entire experience unpleasant.

**Advice**: Popular spots in Bali often attract large crowds. When traveling with elderly family members, consider visiting during off-peak hours or opting for quieter locations, such as Jimbaran Beach for sunset instead of the bustling Uluwatu.

### **13. Reduce Decision-Making Pressure on Elderly**

**Painful Experience**:

A couple, fearing their elderly parents' displeasure, frequently sought their opinions on decisions like where to eat or how to plan the next activity. This constant questioning led to significant stress for the elderly, who eventually responded with 'whatever you decide,' though their mood had already been affected.

**Advice**: Don't leave all decisions to the elders. Plan options in advance and simply ask for their preferences, reducing the pressure they might feel to make decisions.

### **14. Prepare Sun Protection Items Like Hats and Sunscreen**

**Painful Lesson**:

One family took their elders to a scenic spot with little shade for photos. Due to the hot weather and forgetting to bring sun protection, the elders' skin got sunburned and stung, making them unwilling to participate in outdoor activities afterward.

**Advice**: It's best to schedule elders' activities early in the morning, considering both their energy levels and Bali's hot weather, especially between noon and 2 PM. Hats, sunscreen, and sunglasses are essential for elders' outdoor activities. Reapply sunscreen every 2-3 hours to avoid sunburn.

### **15. Avoid Setting the Air Conditioner Too Cold or Too Hot**

**Painful Lesson**:

One family stayed at a hotel in Bali and set the air conditioner too low for comfort, resulting in the elders catching a cold at night, which even affected their subsequent travel plans.

**Advice**: Adjust the hotel's air conditioner to a moderate temperature, ideally between 24-26 degrees Celsius, to avoid it being too cold or too hot. Similarly, adjust the car's air conditioning according to the elders' comfort.

### **16. Reserve Time for Naps or Rest**

**Painful Lesson**:

A family planned a full-day itinerary from 6 AM to 9 PM, which left their elderly members visibly exhausted by midday. With the tight schedule allowing no time for rest, they had to cut the trip short and return to the hotel early.

**Recommendation**: Always allocate 1-2 hours of rest time in the daily itinerary for elderly members to nap or relax, preventing fatigue from prolonged activities. Areas like Sanur or Ubud in Bali offer many cafes and hotels with comfortable resting spaces, making them excellent choices.

### **17. Provide Comfortable Shoes and Clothing**

**Painful Lesson**:

While visiting the Monkey Forest, an elderly family member developed blisters from wearing uncomfortable shoes during extended walking, forcing them to return to the hotel early.

**Recommendation**: Prepare comfortable shoes that fit the elderly's feet well, avoiding new or hard-soled shoes. Additionally, opt for lightweight, breathable clothing and adjust based on the itinerary—consider bringing raincoats, warm jackets, or swimwear as needed.

**18. Care for Elderly Emotions, Avoid Neglect**

**Painful Lesson**:

While the family was busy taking Instagram-worthy photos at Seminyak Beach in Bali, they unintentionally left an elderly member waiting alone. Feeling neglected, the elder became upset, affecting the overall travel mood.

**Advice**: During the trip, interact more with elderly family members to help them feel included in the itinerary, avoiding long periods where they might feel "left out." You can arrange for family photos in certain settings or share your travel experiences with them, making them feel valued.

### **19. Prepare Emergency Contact Information in Advance**

**Painful Lesson**:

During a family trip, an elderly member suddenly got separated from the group. Since they didn’t have a phone, it took two hours to locate them, filled with anxiety and worry.

**Advice**: Prepare important contact details in advance, such as the hotel address, local driver’s phone number, and emergency contacts. You can also write a small card saying, "I'm lost! Help me to call my family," and include a phone number. Have the elderly carry this information in a waist pouch to avoid panic in unexpected situations.

### **20. Maintain Patience and Understanding, Adjust Your Mindset When Needed**

The most important thing when traveling with elderly family members is patience. They might complain due to discomfort or anxiety during the trip. In such moments, it’s essential to stay calm, empathize, and focus on creating a pleasant atmosphere rather than confronting their emotions. For example, if they feel the itinerary is too rushed, you can adjust the activity order or save certain attractions for a future trip.

## Final Reminder: Every Moment on the Journey is a Memory

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_696668bc21571e4b94503611a5a3bc51.png)

Although traveling abroad with elderly family members requires attention to many details, these shared moments will likely become cherished memories in the future. Through careful preparation, patient communication, and reasonable compromises, you can make the journey smoother and more meaningful. Try to relax and view this trip as a "bonding and relationship-maintaining journey." No matter how the process unfolds, the memories will surely be sweet! 😊
