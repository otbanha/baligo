Honestly, I feel like 2026 is flying by. I was just writing about Nyepi Day at the start of the year, and now we're already approaching June—Bali's annual 'massive crowd season' is about to kick off again.

And to be frank, I've noticed something clear in recent years: **Bali's peak season is quietly starting earlier**. Everyone used to agree the high season was from June to mid-September, but now people start pouring in as early as late May, without even waiting for the official summer break.

## Numbers Don't Lie: What Does 65,000 People at the Airport in a Day Mean?

At the peak of the high season, Ngurah Rai International Airport can see up to 65,000 international passengers in a single day. What does that number mean? It's roughly the equivalent of an Airbus A380 carrying 500 passengers landing 130 times a day.

A more direct indicator is hotel occupancy rates. Hotels in the Kuta area—one of Bali's oldest tourist zones—already hit **60% to 70%** occupancy in May. That means even before the 'official peak season' starts, the best room types at popular hotels are already being snapped up.

Ketut Darmayasa, General Manager of Grand Istana Rama Hotel, mentioned in an interview that the industry has been preparing for this long holiday wave since the start of the year, and this is just the expected tipping point.

## Why Is Bali Still So Popular Despite Global Turmoil?

The geopolitical situation in the Middle East in 2026 has indeed cast a shadow over many travel markets, but Bali is an interesting exception. Looking at the data, demand for travel to Bali hasn't dropped—it's actually been steadily rising.

From my own observations, Bali holds a kind of 'strategic flexibility' for Asian markets (especially Taiwan, Singapore, Malaysia, and Australia)—short flight times, easy visas, manageable costs, and diverse activities. Whether it's surfing, diving, yoga, villa vacations, or Instagram-worthy spots, almost every type of travel can find its place here. When other long-haul destinations make travelers hesitate, Bali naturally becomes the top choice.

## The USD is up, hotel prices are up—so why hasn't it affected travelers yet?

Recently, the strengthening of the US dollar against the Indonesian rupiah should theoretically push up hotel prices in Bali. But interestingly, current hotel rates and occupancy rates haven't clearly reflected the exchange rate changes.

The reason is actually simple: **Most travelers have already booked and paid for their rooms**. Darmayasa also bluntly stated that the current price increases are not due to exchange rates, but because of **high demand**—this is basic supply and demand, not much to do with the USD.

The Bali Provincial Tourism Office (led by Head I Wayan Sumarajaya) also mentioned that they are studying the potential long-term impact of a strong USD on tourism and will continue to monitor global economic and geopolitical changes.

## Peak Season Survival Guide: What to Expect When You Arrive in Bali

After all the data, let's talk about something more practical—**what does Bali actually look like during peak season?** Living here, I see the same script play out every year. If it's your first time visiting during peak season, here are a few things you really need to prepare for.

### ✈️ Airport Customs: Budget double the time—no exaggeration

Bali's Ngurah Rai Airport isn't actually that big, and during peak season, the bottlenecks mainly occur in three places:

**1. Immigration** During peak season, 65,000 people enter daily. Plus, most travelers now use eVOA (Electronic Visa on Arrival). If you haven't applied online beforehand and do it on the spot, expect to queue for at least an extra hour. My advice:

- Apply for eVOA (US$35) online a week before departure at [evisa.imigrasi.go.id](https://evisa.imigrasi.go.id)
- Print out or save offline copies of your passport page, return flight ticket, and QR code
- Upon arrival, head straight to the 'e-VOA Processed' lane to save a ton of time

**2. Baggage Claim** During peak season, luggage comes out as slowly as traffic jams. Waiting 40 minutes is common. If you've bought a SIM card or rented WiFi, this is the perfect time to get it activated.

**3. Departure Customs (This One's Worse)** For your return flight, make sure to **arrive at the airport 3.5 hours early**—not the usual 3 hours. During peak season, check-in counters routinely have 100 people in line. Add in X-ray security, passport control, and the walk to the gate, and time really slips away.

> 💡 **Pro Tip**: Indonesian customs now strictly check **liquids and medications**. I've seen real cases of Taiwanese travelers having unknown medicines confiscated or even fined upon entry.

### 🚗 Traffic Nightmare: Bali in Peak Season Is Worse Than Taipei's Xinyi District

I can't stress this enough: **Bali's traffic during peak season is absolutely hellish**.

The main roads in Kuta, Seminyak, and Canggu are gridlocked from 4 PM to 8 PM. The 9-kilometer drive from Seminyak to Canggu takes 30 minutes without traffic, but during peak season, it can take two hours. The roads in and out of Ubud are a disaster too, and around Uluwatu after a Kecak fire dance performance, the whole street turns into a parking lot.

Practical tips:

- **For airport rides, book a car at least 3 hours before departure**. Kuta to the airport is only 5 kilometers in a straight line, but during peak season, it can take 90 minutes
- **Use Grab or Gojek whenever possible**—prices are transparent and you can estimate time (still stuck in traffic, but at least you won't get ripped off by drivers)
- **For Instagram-famous areas like Canggu and Uluwatu, stay within the area**—commuting daily will drive you crazy
- **Avoid long-distance travel during peak afternoon hours (4-7 PM)**—use that time to lounge by the pool or get a spa treatment at your hotel
- **Don't rent a scooter without a license or experience**—too many tourists crash during peak season, and medical bills without insurance are a nightmare

### 🛒 Stocking Up at Hotel & Supermarkets: The Good Stuff Really Gets Wiped Out

This is something many don't consider: **even supermarkets and hotel amenities run out of stock during peak season.**

**Common Hotel Scenarios**:

- Popular room types (ocean view, pool villa) are booked up 2-3 months before peak season
- Breakfast crowds explode; **8-10 AM is the rush hour**—aim for before 7:30 AM or after 10 AM
- Hotel minibar restocking can't keep up; you might need to hit a nearby 7-Eleven for beer
- Shuttle buses, spa appointments, and restaurant reservations **need to be booked a day in advance**

**Supermarket Reality** (especially at tourist favorites like Pepito, Coco, and Grand Lucky):

- **Imported items** (Korean ramen, Japanese snacks, Taiwanese seasonings) are often out of stock on shelves during peak season
- **Baby products** (specific formula brands, diapers) run out easily—bring enough from home if traveling with kids
- **Sunscreen, aloe vera gel, and anti-diarrhea meds** are consumables that sell fast—grab them when you see them
- **Alcohol**, especially popular craft beers (Stark, Kura Kura), is frequently out of stock during peak season

> 💡 **Must-Stock Items**: On your first day at the hotel, head to Indomaret or Circle K to stock up on water, snacks, sunscreen, and insect repellent. During peak season, the hotel minibar really does get emptied by guests, and restocking can't keep up.

### ⚠️ Scam Alert: Peak Season Is Prime Time for Scams—Learn These Tips

During peak season, tourist crowds bring out the scammers. Here are some common pitfalls **Taiwanese travelers often fall into**:

**1. Currency Exchange Scams** If you see signs like 'No Commission' or 'Best Rate' with exchange rates that seem too good to be true in back-alley shops, **nine out of ten are scams**. The classic trick is shortchanging you by 500,000 or 1,000,000 Indonesian rupiah using sleight of hand while counting.

✅ **Only use licensed exchangers like PT. Central Kuta Money Changer, BMC, or Dirgahayu Valuta Prima**—they have official permits and are found across tourist areas. Check our [map of legal money changers](/map/money-changer/) on the site.

> Quick reference: [《Ultimate Bali Currency Exchange & Money Management Guide》](/en/blog/bali-currency-exchange-guide/)

**2. Airport Taxi Touts** As soon as you exit arrivals, people will rush up shouting 'Taxi? Transport?'—these are the infamous **taxi mafia**, charging 3-5 times the price of a legitimate Grab ride.

✅ **Walk to the designated Grab/Gojek pickup area across from the airport** to book a ride, or arrange hotel transfers in advance.

**3. Fake Guides & Souvenirs** At major spots like Tanah Lot or Uluwatu, people claiming to offer 'free tours' will approach you, then demand payment afterward. Many 'handmade silver jewelry' or 'natural batik' souvenirs are actually factory-made goods from China, marked up at exorbitant prices.

✅ **First, ask the price; second, haggle down to half.** If they won't budge, walk away—there are plenty of stalls to compare.

**4. Scooter Rental Traps** Some rental shops will nitpick when you return the scooter (claiming 'this scratch' or 'this is broken') to demand compensation.

✅ **When picking up the car, use your phone to record a video of the entire vehicle from all angles**, saving the date and time stamp. If there's a dispute when returning the car, just show the video.

**5. Restaurants with 'Tourist Menus'** Some restaurants have two menus, pulling out the pricier one when they see Asian faces.

✅ **Check the menu photos on Google Maps reviews before entering**, or ask the waiter for the 'Indonesian menu.'

**6. ATM Skimming** Peak season is also peak season for skimmers.

✅ **Only use ATMs inside banks, or inside convenience stores like Indomaret or Circle K**, or ATMs near banks with security guards. Avoid standalone machines on the street.

## Conclusion: Peak season is coming—only those who prepare will have fun

Bali during peak season is beautiful, lively, and full of energy, but it's also more expensive, more crowded, and requires more planning. Living here myself, I watch waves of tourists come and go each year, and the biggest difference is never 'how much budget you have,' but '**whether you did your homework in advance**.'

Here's a quick summary of the key checklist:

- ✅ Book accommodation a month early, lock in the rate before the exchange rate rises (check out our [300+ accommodation recommendations](/blog?cat=%E4%BD%8F%E5%AE%BF%E6%8E%A8%E8%96%A6))
- ✅ Apply for eVOA online to save 1 hour of queuing
- ✅ Allow 3 hours to get to the airport, and 3.5 hours to return
- ✅ Keep your accommodation and activity areas as close as possible to avoid long-distance travel
- ✅ Only exchange money at legal Money Changers
- ✅ Bring enough imported snacks, baby products, and medicine from Taiwan
- ✅ Record a video of your scooter rental for evidence; don't ride without a license
- ✅ Use Grab/Gojek for short rides
- ✅ Travel insurance is a must, with medical coverage being the most important
