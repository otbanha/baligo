Honestly, I also feel like 2026 is flying by. It seems like just yesterday I was writing about Nyepi Day, and now we're approaching June—Bali's annual 'crowd explosion season' is about to begin again.

And frankly, I've noticed something significant in recent years: **Bali's peak season is quietly shifting earlier**. While the traditional peak was recognized as June to mid-September, now the influx starts as early as late May, well before the official summer break.

### Numbers Speak: What Does 65,000 People at the Airport in a Day Mean?

At the peak of the season, Ngurah Rai International Airport can see up to 65,000 international travelers in a single day. To put that into perspective, that's like 130 fully loaded Airbus A380s landing each day.

A more direct indicator is hotel occupancy rates. Hotels in Kuta—one of Bali's oldest tourist areas—are already hitting **60% ~ 70%** occupancy in May. This means that even before the 'official peak season', the best rooms in popular hotels are already being snapped up.

Ketut Darmayasa, General Manager of Grand Istana Rama Hotel, mentioned in an interview that the industry has been preparing for this holiday surge since the beginning of the year, and now it's just the expected explosion point.

### Why is Bali Still So Hot Despite Global Turmoil?

The geopolitical situation in the Middle East in 2026 has indeed cast a shadow over many tourism markets, but Bali stands out as an interesting exception. Looking at the data, demand for Bali hasn't dropped; instead, it continues to rise.

From my own observations, Bali has a unique appeal to the Asian market (especially Taiwan, Singapore, Malaysia, and Australia) — it's a destination that offers both adventure and relaxation. The flight time is short, visas are simple, costs are manageable, and there's a wide variety of activities available. Whether it's surfing, diving, yoga, villa stays, or Instagram-worthy spots, Bali caters to almost every type of traveler. When other long-haul destinations make people hesitate, Bali naturally becomes the first choice.

### The US Dollar is Rising, So Are Hotel Prices — Why Haven't Travelers Been Affected?

Recently, the US dollar has strengthened against the Indonesian rupiah, which theoretically should push up hotel prices in Bali. However, interestingly, hotel rates and occupancy haven't significantly reflected the exchange rate changes yet.

The reason is quite simple: **most travelers have already booked and paid for their stays**. Darmayasa also straightforwardly stated that the current rise in hotel prices isn't due to the exchange rate but rather **high demand** — it's basic supply and demand, not directly tied to the US dollar.

The Bali Tourism Board (headed by I Wayan Sumarajaya) has also mentioned that they are studying the potential long-term impact of the stronger US dollar on tourism and will continue to monitor global economic and geopolitical changes.

### Peak Season Survival Guide: What to Expect in Bali

After discussing the data, let's get more practical — **what does Bali actually look like during peak season?** Living here, I see the same scenario play out every year. If it's your first time visiting during peak season, here are some things you should mentally prepare for.

#### ✈️ Airport Customs: Please Allow Double the Time, Seriously

Bali's Ngurah Rai Airport isn't particularly large, and during peak season, bottlenecks mainly occur in three areas:

**1. Immigration** With 65,000 arrivals daily during peak season, and most travelers now using **eVOA** (electronic visa on arrival), if you haven't applied online beforehand and need to do it on-site, **expect to queue for at least an extra hour**. My advice:

- Apply for eVOA (US$35) online at [evisa.imigrasi.go.id](https://evisa.imigrasi.go.id) one week before departure
- Print out or save offline copies of your passport page, return ticket, and QR Code
- Upon arrival, use the 'e-VOA Already Applied' lane to save significant time

**2. Baggage Carousel** During peak season, luggage comes out as slowly as traffic. Waiting 40 minutes is often the norm. This is a good time to activate your SIM card or WiFi if you've purchased one.

**3. Departure Immigration (This is worse)** For your return flight, **arrive at the airport 3.5 hours early**, not the standard 3 hours. During peak season, check-in queues of 100 people are common, and with X-ray security, passport checks, and walking distance to gates, time really gets eaten up.

> 💡 **Reminder**: Indonesian customs are now strictly checking **liquids and medications**. I've seen Taiwanese travelers have unidentified medications confiscated or even fined upon entry.

#### 🚗 Traffic Gridlock: Bali's Peak Season is Worse Than Taipei's Xinyi District

I need to emphasize this strongly: **Bali's traffic during peak season is truly hellish**.

The main roads in Kuta, Seminyak, and Canggu are jam-packed from 4 PM to 8 PM. The 9 km road from Seminyak to Canggu takes 30 minutes without traffic, but can take two hours during peak season. Ubud's access roads are also a nightmare, and Uluwatu becomes a parking lot after Kecak fire dance performances.

Practical advice:

- **For airport transfers, book at least 3 hours before departure**. While Kuta is only 5 km from the airport in a straight line, peak season traffic can take up to 90 minutes.
- **Always use Grab or Gojek** for transparent pricing and estimated travel times (though you’ll still hit traffic, at least you won’t get scammed by drivers).
- **If you’re staying in trendy areas like Canggu or Uluwatu, it’s best to stay locally**. Daily commutes can be exhausting.
- **Avoid long-distance travel during the golden hours (4-7 PM)**. Use this time to relax by the pool or enjoy a spa treatment.
- While scooters are fast, **don’t rent one if you don’t have a license or experience**. Peak season sees too many accidents, and medical bills without insurance can be a nightmare.

#### 🛒 Hotel & Supermarket Supplies: The Good Stuff Goes Fast

Many don’t realize this: **Supermarkets and hotel amenities can run out during peak season**.

**Common Hotel Scenarios**:

- Popular room types (ocean view, poolside villas) are booked 2-3 months in advance during peak season.
- Breakfast crowds explode, **8-10 AM is peak time**. Aim for before 7:30 AM or after 10 AM.
- Mini-bar restocking can’t keep up. If you want beer, you might need to grab it from a nearby 7-Eleven.
- Shuttle services, spa bookings, and restaurant reservations **should be secured a day in advance**.

**Supermarket Reality** (especially at Pepito, Coco, and Grand Lucky, popular with tourists):

- **Imported items** (Korean ramen, Japanese snacks, Taiwanese seasonings) are often out of stock during peak season.
- **Baby products** (specific brands of formula, diapers) are prone to shortages. If traveling with kids, bring enough from home.
- **Sunscreen, aloe vera gel, and anti-diarrheal meds** are fast-moving essentials—buy them when you see them.
- **Alcohol**, especially popular craft beers (Stark, Kura Kura), often runs out during peak season.

> 💡 **Must-Have Essentials**: On your first day at the hotel, head to Indomaret or Circle K to stock up on water, snacks, sunscreen, and insect repellent. During peak season, the mini-bar gets emptied quickly, and restocking can’t keep up.

#### ⚠️ Avoiding Scams: Peak Season is Scam Season, Here's What to Watch Out For

During peak season, tourists flock in and so do scam artists. I've compiled a list of **common pitfalls Taiwanese travelers encounter**:

**1. Black Market Money Changers** Seeing signs like "No Commission" or "Best Rate" at small alleyway currency exchanges with unrealistically good rates? **Nine times out of ten, it's a scam**. The classic trick is to shortchange you by 500,000 or 1,000,000 IDR while counting the money.

✅ **Only use legitimate PT. Central Kuta Money Changer, BMC, Dirgahayu Valuta Prima** - these are licensed operators found throughout tourist areas. Check out our [legitimate money changer map](/map/money-changer/) for reference.

> Quick Reference: [Bali Currency Exchange and Money Management Ultimate Guide](/blog/bali-currency-exchange-guide/)

**2. Airport Taxi Scams** As soon as you exit arrivals, people will rush up saying "Taxi? Transport?" These are the infamous **taxi mafia**, charging 3-5 times the regular Grab fare.

✅ **Walk across to the designated Grab/Gojek pickup area** or arrange hotel transfer in advance.

**3. Fake Guides & Counterfeit Souvenirs** At major attractions like Tanah Lot and Uluwatu, people claiming to offer "free tours" will approach you, then demand payment afterward. Many "handmade silver" and "authentic batik" items in souvenir shops are actually mass-produced in Chinese factories, sold at inflated prices.

✅ **Always ask the price first, then immediately offer half**. If they won't budge, walk away - there are plenty of vendors to compare.

**4. Scooter Rental Traps** Some rental shops will nitpick when you return the vehicle ("There's a scratch here," "This is broken") and demand compensation.

✅ **Record a video of the entire scooter from all angles when picking it up**, save it with date and time stamps, and show the video directly if there's any dispute upon return.

**5. "Tourist Menu" at Restaurants** Some restaurants have two sets of menus, presenting the more expensive version to Asian-looking customers.

✅ **Check the menu photos in Google Maps reviews before entering**, or ask the waiter for the "Indonesian menu."

**6. ATM Skimming** Peak season is also skimmer season.

✅ **Only use ATMs inside banks or convenience stores like Indomaret and Circle K**, or ATMs near banks with security guards. Avoid standalone machines on the street.

Bali during peak season is beautiful, lively, and full of energy, but it also becomes more expensive, crowded, and requires more planning. Living here, I see waves of tourists coming and going every year, and the biggest difference isn't "how much budget you have," but rather "**whether you've done your homework in advance**."

Here's a quick summary of key points for this trip:

- ✅ Book accommodations a month early to lock in prices before exchange rates rise (check out our [300+ accommodation recommendations](https://gobaligo.id/blog?cat=%E4%BD%8F%E5%AE%BF%E6%8E%A8%E8%96%A6))

- ✅ Allow 3 hours for airport arrival, 3.5 hours for departure
- ✅ Concentrate accommodation and activity areas to avoid long-distance travel
- ✅ Only exchange money at licensed Money Changers
- ✅ Bring enough imported snacks, baby products, and medications from Taiwan
- ✅ Record video evidence when renting scooters, don't ride without a license
- ✅ Use Grab/Gojek for short trips
- ✅ Must purchase travel insurance, with medical coverage being most important

Peak season in Bali can indeed test travelers' patience, but if you do your homework and avoid pitfalls, the island will still welcome you with its most beautiful side.
