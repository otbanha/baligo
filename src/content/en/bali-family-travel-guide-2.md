For many Taiwanese families, Bali is the first destination to take their children abroad—airfare isn't too expensive, the climate is warm, and hotels have pools, which sounds perfect. But when you actually start packing, you'll find one problem after another: Can you buy formula in Bali? Which cold medicines should you bring? Does the brand of diapers matter?

This article compiles our years of experience traveling to Bali with kids, along with practical information used by local families, into a preparation checklist to ensure you're ready to go.

***

### Pre-Trip Planning: Don't Underestimate the Importance of Itinerary Planning

The biggest pitfall when traveling with young children is 'thinking you'll figure it out when you get there.' Although Bali has abundant tourism resources, many things children need aren't readily available or require special effort to find. Before departure, we recommend:

- Confirming flight options (the impact of long direct flights vs. layovers on young children varies greatly)
- Checking if accommodations provide cribs and have children's play facilities
- Planning daily schedules in advance, ensuring they're not too rushed, as toddlers need nap time

Budget planning is also crucial. Traveling with kids costs more than traveling with adults, especially if you need to buy medical supplies or baby products on the island, which are often more expensive than in Taiwan. ### The Flight Challenge: Packing for Toddlers

Flying with toddlers can be the most stressful part of the trip, but proper preparation can significantly reduce the pressure.

Flight aids: For children aged 1-3, consider bringing an inflatable footrest for them to lie flat or a ride-on suitcase that doubles as a bed (like JetKids). Note that not all airlines permit these, so confirm beforehand. For infants under one year, bassinet seats can be reserved, but applications should be made weeks in advance.

In-flight hydration: The air in the cabin is very dry, so remember to bring baby lotion and saline nasal spray to keep your baby's nasal passages moist. Ear pressure changes significantly during takeoff and landing, and breastfeeding or giving a bottle is the most effective way to relieve it. For older children, candy can help.

Luggage packing tip: Prepare a small bag with multiple pockets filled with snacks, small toys, stickers, and little books. Let your child slowly explore and find things—the process of discovery itself can pass a lot of time.

Baby carrier: A good-quality, natural-material carrier is very practical for moving around the airport and Bali. Many places in Bali are not stroller-friendly, so a carrier offers much more flexibility.

### Baby food and formula: How much to bring and can you buy it locally?

This is a major concern for many moms. Honestly, Bali isn't a place where you can't find anything, but there are definitely some pitfalls to avoid.

**Formula**: If your baby requires a special formula (hydrolyzed protein, hypoallergenic), it's strongly recommended to bring enough with you, as options on the island are very limited. Regular formula can be found in supermarkets and pharmacies, with common brands including Nutrilon, Lactogen (Nestlé), and Enfamil, which also offers a lactose-free version. Australian organic brands like Bellamy's (cow's milk) and Karihome (goat's milk) are also available but tend to be pricey.

**Baby food jars**: It's advisable to bring enough meat purees, especially turkey, which is almost impossible to find in Bali. Some imported supermarkets (like Pepito) occasionally carry Heinz or Bellamy's fruit purees, but they are mostly vegetable and fruit flavors and cost about twice as much as in Taiwan. If your baby is still in the baby food stage, buying a blender and preparing local ingredients might be a more practical approach once you arrive in Bali.

**Dairy products**: The overall quality of dairy products in Bali isn't ideal, with high sugar content or high prices. If your child is used to yogurt or kefir, consider bringing cultures to make your own. Plant-based milk options are plentiful, and lactose-free milk brands like Milk life are also available.

**Health food stores**: Bali Buda is a chain of organic health food stores across the island where you can find various grains (including buckwheat, which is rare in Taiwan), gluten-free, and dairy-free products. It's a great supply station for families with young children.

### Diapers and hygiene products

**Diapers**: The brand selection is quite different from Taiwan. The better options are Korean and Japanese brands like Genki, Makuku, and Merries. Some places also carry biodegradable Ecoboom. Note that most Merries sold on the island are locally produced in Indonesia, and the quality differs. The authentic Japanese-made white packaging is only available at select outlets like Clandy's or Bali Hypermart, or you can order online via Shopee/Tokopedia. Swim diaper pants are mostly available online, so it’s recommended to bring enough from Taiwan.

**Size Reminder**: Many diaper brands tend to run narrow, so consider sizing up.

**Other Baby Supplies**: Offline stores include Balonku, Genyc, and Mothercare for baby products. For baby furniture, visit Informa, and for tableware and storage, check out IKEA (available in-store or via their app, with delivery options).

**Special Skincare Products**: If your child requires specific shampoos or creams for skin conditions (e.g., Mustela), it’s best to bring them from Taiwan, as options on the island are limited.

**Hats**: Sun hats for toddlers are essential. It’s advisable to bring your trusted hat from home—while hats in Bali’s markets are cheap, many are made with thick materials and have poorly designed straps that don’t stay put on a child’s head.

### Medications: Prepare This List Carefully

Most pharmacies in Bali sell generic drugs, and brand-name medications are available but cost two to three times more, often requiring a prescription. If your child relies on long-term medication, be sure to bring an adequate supply.

Other recommended medications to bring:

- **Digestive**: Simethicone (e.g., Gas-X) for bloating, probiotics, intestinal adsorbents, digestive enzymes
- **Wound Care**: Disinfectant spray, healing ointment, band-aids, liquid skin adhesive
- **Throat & Cold**: Throat anti-inflammatory spray, throat lozenges, saline nasal rinse, fever reducers/pain relievers
- **Eyes**: Artificial tears or anti-inflammatory eye drops
- **Nose**: Saline nasal spray, decongestants
- **Allergies & Insect Bites**: Antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine/Zyrtec), anti-itch gel
- **For Babies**: Teething relief gel, nasal aspirator
- **Miscellaneous**: COVID/flu rapid tests, thermometer

Most importantly: **Remember to purchase travel medical insurance before your trip**. Private hospitals in Bali are decent, but costs are very high, and without insurance, unexpected medical expenses can hit your wallet hard.

Bali is truly a great destination for traveling with young children—as long as you're well-prepared. Nowadays, most basic necessities can be found in Bali, though brand options are limited, some items need to be ordered online, and some are more expensive than in Taiwan. Bring essentials like pureed meat, special formula, regular medications, favorite hats, and a few familiar toys from home—everything else can be managed once you arrive.

Having familiar items around helps children adapt much faster; and with thorough preparation, adults can truly relax and enjoy the trip.
