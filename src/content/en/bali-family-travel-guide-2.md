For many Taiwanese families, Bali is the first destination for traveling abroad with children—airfare isn't too expensive, the climate is warm, and hotels have pools, which sounds perfect. But when you actually start packing, you'll find problems popping up one after another: Can you buy formula in Bali? What cold medicines should you bring? Does the brand of diapers matter?

This article integrates our years of experience traveling to Bali with children and practical information used by families on the island into a preparation checklist to set your mind at ease.

***

### Pre-Trip Planning: Don't Underestimate the Importance of Itinerary Planning

The biggest pitfall when traveling with toddlers is 'thinking we'll figure it out when we get there.' Although Bali is rich in tourism resources, many things toddlers need are not readily available or require special effort to find. It's recommended to:

- Confirm flight options (the impact of long direct flights vs. layovers on toddlers is significant)
- Confirm if accommodations provide cribs and have children's play facilities
- Plan the daily itinerary pace in advance, not too rushed, as toddlers need nap time

Budget planning is also crucial; traveling with children incurs more expenses than adults, especially if medical and toddler supplies are purchased on the island, prices are often higher than in Taiwan. ### The Flight Challenge: How to Prepare for Traveling with Toddlers

The flight is often the most stressful part of traveling with young children, but being well-prepared can significantly reduce the pressure.

Flight aids: For children aged 1-3, consider bringing an inflatable footrest to allow them to lie flat or a ride-on suitcase that doubles as a bed (like JetKids). However, note that not all airlines permit these, so it’s best to confirm before departure. For infants under one year, you can book a bassinet seat, but this needs to be requested weeks in advance.

In-flight hydration: Cabin air is very dry, so remember to bring baby lotion and saline nasal spray to keep your baby’s nasal passages moist. During takeoff and landing, ear pressure changes are noticeable—breastfeeding or giving a bottle is the most effective way to relieve discomfort, while older children can be given candy.

Packing tip: Prepare a small bag with multiple compartments filled with snacks, small toys, stickers, and little books for your child to explore on their own — the process of discovery itself can keep them entertained for quite some time.

Baby carrier: A good-quality, natural-material baby carrier is highly practical for moving around the airport and Bali. Many places in Bali aren't stroller-friendly, so a carrier offers much more flexibility.

### Baby food and formula: How much to bring and can you buy it locally?

This is a major concern for many moms. Honestly, Bali isn't a place where you can't find anything, but there are definitely some pitfalls to avoid.

**Formula**: If your baby requires special formulas (hydrolyzed protein, hypoallergenic), it's strongly recommended to bring enough supply, as options on the island are very limited. Regular formula can be found in supermarkets and pharmacies, with common brands including Nutrilon, Lactogen (Nestlé), and Enfamil, the latter also offering a lactose-free version. Australian organic brands Bellamy's (cow milk) and Karihome (goat milk) are also available but tend to be pricier.

**Baby food jars**: It's advisable to bring enough meat purees, especially turkey, which is almost impossible to find in Bali. Some imported supermarkets (like Pepito) occasionally stock Heinz or Bellamy's fruit purees, but they're mostly vegetable and fruit flavors and cost about twice as much as in Taiwan. If your baby is still in the baby food stage, buying a blender and preparing local ingredients might be a more practical approach once you're in Bali.

**Dairy products**: The overall quality of dairy products in Bali isn't ideal, often being high in sugar or expensive. If your child is used to yogurt or kefir, consider bringing cultures to make your own. Plant-based milk options are plentiful, and lactose-free milk brands like Milk life are available.

**Health food stores**: Bali Buda is a chain of organic health food stores across the island where you can find various grains (including buckwheat, which is rare in Taiwan), gluten-free, and dairy-free products, making it a great supply stop for families with young children.

### Diapers and hygiene products

**Diapers**: The brand selection differs from Taiwan. Better options include Korean and Japanese brands like Genki, Makuku, and Merries, with some places also offering biodegradable Ecoboom. Note that most Merries sold on the island are locally produced in Indonesia, with varying quality. The authentic Japanese-made white packaging is only available at select outlets (like Clandy's or Bali Hypermart) or can be ordered online via Shopee/Tokopedia. Swim diapers are almost exclusively available online, so it's recommended to bring enough from Taiwan.

**Size Reminder**: Many diaper brands run narrow, so consider sizing up.

**Other Baby Essentials**: For baby products, check out Balonku, Genyc, and Mothercare stores. For baby furniture, visit Informa, and for tableware and storage, IKEA is a good option (available in-store or via their app, with home delivery).

**Specialized Skincare**: If your child requires specific shampoos or creams for skin conditions (e.g., Mustela), it's best to bring them from Taiwan, as options on the island are limited.

**Hats**: Sun protection hats for toddlers are essential. Bring your trusted one from home—while hats in Bali's markets are cheap, many are made from thick materials with poorly designed straps that slip off easily.

### Medications: Prepare This List Carefully

Most pharmacies in Bali sell generic drugs, and brand-name medications are available but cost two to three times more, often requiring a prescription. If your child is on long-term medication, be sure to bring enough.

Other recommended items to pack:

- **Digestive**: Simethicone (e.g., Gas-X) for bloating, probiotics, intestinal adsorbents, digestive enzymes
- **Wound Care**: Disinfectant spray, healing ointment, band-aids, liquid skin adhesive
- **Throat & Cold**: Throat anti-inflammatory spray, lozenges, saline nasal rinse, fever reducers/pain relievers
- **Eyes**: Artificial tears or anti-inflammatory eye drops
- **Nose**: Saline nasal spray, decongestants
- **Allergies & Insect Bites**: Antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine/Zyrtec), itch-relief gel
- **Baby-Specific**: Teething gel, nasal aspirator
- **Miscellaneous**: COVID/flu rapid tests, thermometer

Most importantly: **Remember to purchase travel medical insurance before your trip**. Private hospitals in Bali are decent but very expensive, and without insurance, unexpected medical costs can hit your wallet hard.

## **Additionally, Here Are Some Extra Tips:**

- Bring your child's favorite toys and activities on the plane to help keep them entertained.
- In hot weather, make sure your child drinks plenty of water and apply sunscreen.
- Before visiting temples or other religious sites, teach your child to respect local culture.

Bali is truly a great destination for traveling with toddlers — as long as you're well-prepared.
