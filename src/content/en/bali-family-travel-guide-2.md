For many Taiwanese families, Bali is the first destination to take their children abroad—airfare is not too expensive, the climate is warm, and hotels have pools, which sounds perfect. But when you actually start packing, you'll find one problem after another: Can you buy formula in Bali? What cold medicines should you bring? Does the brand of diapers matter?

This article combines our years of experience taking children to Bali with practical information used by local families on the island, compiling a preparation checklist to ensure you can set off with peace of mind.

### Pre-Trip Planning: Don’t Underestimate the Importance of Itinerary Planning

The biggest pitfall when traveling with young children is 'thinking we’ll figure it out when we get there.' Although Bali has abundant tourism resources, many things needed for young children are not readily available or require special effort to find. It is recommended to:

- Confirm flight options (the impact of long direct flights vs. layovers on young children is significant)
- Confirm if accommodations provide cribs and have children’s play facilities
- Plan daily itinerary pace in advance, not too rushed, as young children need nap time

Budget planning is also crucial; traveling with children incurs more expenses than adults, especially if medical and child care items are purchased on the island, prices are often higher than in Taiwan. ### The Flight Challenge: Preparing Toddler Essentials

Flying with toddlers can often be the most stressful part of the trip, but being well-prepared can significantly reduce the pressure.

Flight essentials: For children aged 1-3, consider bringing an inflatable footrest to allow them to lie flat and rest, or a ride-on suitcase that doubles as a bed (like JetKids). However, note that not all airlines permit these, so it's best to confirm before departure. For infants under one year old, bassinet seats can be reserved, but applications need to be made several weeks in advance.

In-flight hydration: Cabin air is very dry, so remember to bring baby lotion and saline nasal spray to keep your baby's nasal passages moist. During takeoff and landing, ear pressure changes are noticeable. Breastfeeding or giving a bottle is the most effective way to alleviate discomfort, while older children can be given candy.

Packing tips: Prepare a small bag with multiple compartments filled with snacks, small toys, stickers, and little books for your child to slowly explore—the process of discovery itself can pass a lot of time.

Baby carrier: A good quality, natural material baby carrier is very practical for moving around the airport and Bali. Many places in Bali are not stroller-friendly, making a carrier much more flexible.

### Baby food and formula: How much to bring and what's available locally?

This is a major concern for many mothers. Honestly, Bali isn't lacking in everything, but there are definitely some pitfalls to avoid.

**Formula**: If your baby requires special formulas (hydrolyzed protein, hypoallergenic), it's strongly advised to bring enough, as options on the island are very limited. Regular formulas can be found in supermarkets and pharmacies, including common brands like Nutrilon, Lactogen (Nestlé), and Enfamil, which also offers a lactose-free version. Australian organic brands Bellamy's (cow milk) and Karihome (goat milk) are available but priced higher.

**Baby food jars**: It's recommended to bring enough meat purees, especially turkey, which is almost impossible to find in Bali. Some imported supermarkets (like Pepito) occasionally stock Heinz or Bellamy's fruit purees, but they are mostly vegetable and fruit flavors and cost about twice as much as in Taiwan. If your baby is still in the baby food stage, buying a blender to prepare local ingredients might be a more practical approach after arriving in Bali.

**Dairy products**: The overall quality of dairy products in Bali is not ideal, with high sugar content or high prices. If your child is used to yogurt or kefir, consider bringing cultures to make your own. Plant-based milk options are plentiful, and lactose-free milk brands like Milk life are also available.

**Health food stores**: Bali Buda is a chain of organic health food stores across the island, offering a variety of grains (including buckwheat, which is rare in Taiwan), gluten-free, and dairy-free products, making it a great supply station for families with young children.

### Diapers and Hygiene Products

**Diapers**: The brand options are quite different from Taiwan. Some of the better choices are Korean and Japanese brands like Genki, Makuku, and Merries. Some places also carry biodegradable options like Ecoboom. Note that most Merries sold on the island are locally produced in Indonesia, and the quality differs. The authentic Japanese-made white packaging is only available at select stores like Clandy's or Bali Hypermart. You can also order online via Shopee/Tokopedia. Swim diapers are mostly available online, so it's recommended to bring enough from Taiwan.

**Size Tip**: Many diaper brands tend to run narrow, so consider sizing up.

**Other Baby Products**: Offline stores include Balonku, Genyc, and Mothercare for baby supplies. For baby furniture, check out Informa. For utensils and storage, IKEA is a good option (available in-store or via their app with delivery support).

**Special Skincare Products**: If your child requires specific shampoos or creams for skin conditions (e.g., Mustela), it's best to bring them from Taiwan, as the selection on the island is limited.

**Hats**: Sun hats for toddlers are essential. It's recommended to bring your child's usual hat from home—while hats in Bali markets are cheap, many are made from thick materials with poorly designed straps, making them impractical for kids.

### Medications: Prepare This List Carefully

Pharmacies in Bali mostly sell generic drugs. Brand-name medications are available but cost two to three times more, and many require prescriptions. If your child is on long-term medication, make sure to bring an adequate supply.

Other recommended medications to bring:

- **Digestive**: Simethicone (e.g., Gas-X) for bloating, probiotics, intestinal adsorbents, digestive enzymes
- **Wounds**: Disinfectant spray, healing ointment, band-aids, liquid skin adhesive
- **Throat & Cold**: Throat anti-inflammatory spray, throat lozenges, saline nasal rinse, fever-reducing painkillers
- **Eyes**: Artificial tears or anti-inflammatory eye drops
- **Nose**: Saline nasal spray, vasoconstrictors
- **Allergies & Insect Bites**: Antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine / Zyrtec), anti-itch gel
- **For Children**: Teething relief gel, nasal aspirator
- **Other**: COVID/flu rapid tests, thermometer

The most important point: **Remember to purchase travel medical insurance before departure**. Private hospitals in Bali are of decent quality, but the costs are very high. Without insurance, unexpected incidents can be financially burdensome.

## **Additionally, here are some extra tips:**

- Bring your child’s favorite toys and activities on the plane to keep them entertained.
- In hot weather, make sure your child drinks plenty of water and apply sunscreen.
- Before visiting temples or other religious sites, teach your child to respect local culture.

Bali is truly a great destination for traveling with toddlers—as long as you’re well-prepared. Nowadays, most basic necessities can be found in Bali, though brand options are limited, some items need to be ordered online, and some are more expensive than in Taiwan. Having familiar items around helps children adapt quickly; with proper preparation, adults can truly relax and enjoy the trip.
