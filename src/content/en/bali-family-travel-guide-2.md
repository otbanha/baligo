For many Taiwanese families, Bali is the first overseas destination with kids—airfare isn’t too expensive, the weather is warm, and hotels have pools, which sounds perfect. But when you start packing, you’ll realize the challenges one after another: Can you buy formula in Bali? What medicines should you bring? Does the diaper brand matter?

This article combines our years of experience traveling to Bali with kids and practical information used by local families to create a preparation checklist for a stress-free trip.

***

### Pre-Trip Planning: Don’t Underestimate the Importance of Itinerary Planning

The biggest pitfall when traveling with toddlers is thinking 'we’ll figure it out when we get there.' While Bali is rich in tourism resources, many things toddlers need aren’t readily available or require special effort to find. Before departure, we recommend:

- Confirm flight options (long-haul direct flights vs. layovers can make a big difference for toddlers)
- Check if accommodations provide cribs and have children’s play facilities
- Plan daily itineraries in advance, keeping them relaxed as toddlers need nap time

Budget planning is also crucial. Traveling with kids costs more than traveling solo, especially for medical and baby supplies, which can be more expensive in Bali than in Taiwan. [Extended Reading: How Much Cash Should You Prepare for a Bali Trip?](/blog/bali-travel-cash-budget-guide/)

### Choosing the Right Accommodation: Half the Success of Family Travel

Bali has numerous family-friendly hotels offering cribs, Kids Clubs, children's pools, and kid's menus. For families with toddlers, it's advisable to prioritize villas with fenced pools or private pools for enhanced safety.

### The Flight Challenge: Packing for Toddlers

Flying with toddlers can be the most stressful part of a trip, but proper preparation can significantly reduce the pressure.

Flight aids: For children aged 1-3, consider bringing an inflatable footrest to allow them to lie flat or a ride-on suitcase that doubles as a bed (like JetKids). Note that not all airlines permit these, so confirm beforehand. For infants under one year, bassinet seats can be reserved but require advance booking several weeks prior.

In-flight hydration: The air in the cabin is very dry, so remember to bring baby lotion and saline nasal spray to keep your baby's nasal passages moist. During takeoff and landing, ear pressure changes are noticeable—breastfeeding or giving a bottle is the most effective way to relieve discomfort. For older children, offering candy can help.

Luggage bag hack: Prepare a small bag with multiple pockets filled with snacks, small toys, stickers, and little books. Let your child explore and dig through it—the process of discovery itself can kill a lot of time.

Baby carrier: A good-quality, natural-material baby carrier is incredibly practical for moving around airports and Bali. Many places in Bali aren't stroller-friendly, so a carrier offers much more flexibility.

### Baby food and formula: How much to bring and can you buy it locally?

This is a major concern for many moms. Honestly, Bali isn't a place where you can't find anything, but there are definitely some pitfalls to avoid.

**Formula**: If your baby requires special formulas (hydrolyzed protein, hypoallergenic), strongly recommend bringing enough supply, as options on the island are very limited. Regular formula can be found in supermarkets and pharmacies, with common brands like Nutrilon, Lactogen (Nestlé), and Enfamil (which also offers a lactose-free version). Australian organic brands Bellamy's (cow milk) and Karihome (goat milk) are available but tend to be pricey.

**Baby food jars**: It's advisable to bring enough meat purees, especially turkey, which is nearly impossible to find in Bali. Some imported supermarkets (like Pepito) occasionally stock Heinz or Bellamy's fruit purees, but they're mostly fruit and vegetable flavors and cost about twice as much as in Taiwan. If your baby is still in the puree stage, buying a blender and using local ingredients might be a more practical approach once you're in Bali.

**Dairy products**: The overall quality of dairy products in Bali isn't ideal—they're either high in sugar or expensive. If your child is used to yogurt or kefir, consider bringing starter cultures to make your own. Plant-based milk options are plentiful, and lactose-free milk brands like Milk life are available.

**Health food stores**: Bali Buda is a chain of organic health food stores across the island, offering a variety of grains (including buckwheat, which is rare in Taiwan), gluten-free, and dairy-free products. It's a great supply stop for families with young children.

### Diapers and hygiene products

**Diapers**: The brand selection is quite different from Taiwan. The better options are Korean and Japanese brands like Genki, Makuku, and Merries. Some places also carry biodegradable options like Ecoboom. Note that most Merries sold on the island are locally produced in Indonesia, and the quality differs. The authentic Japanese-made white packaging is only available at select stores like Clandy's or Bali Hypermart, or you can order online via Shopee/Tokopedia. Swim diaper styles are mostly available online, so it's recommended to bring enough from Taiwan.

**Size Tip**: Many diaper brands run narrow, so consider sizing up.

**Other Baby Products**: Offline stores include Balonku, Genyc, and Mothercare for baby supplies. For baby furniture, check out Informa. For utensils and storage, IKEA is a good option (you can order in-store or via their app, with delivery available).

**Special Skincare Products**: If your child requires specific shampoos or creams for skin conditions (e.g., Mustela), it's best to bring them from Taiwan, as options on the island are limited.

**Hats**: Sun hats for toddlers are essential. Bring your trusted hat from home—Bali market hats are cheap, but many are too thick and have poorly designed straps that slip off easily.

### Medications: Prepare This List Carefully

Most pharmacies in Bali sell generic drugs. Brand-name medications are available but cost two to three times more, and many require a prescription. If your child is on long-term medication, be sure to bring enough.

Other recommended medications to pack:

- **Digestive**: Simethicone (e.g., Gas-X) for bloating, probiotics, intestinal adsorbents, digestive enzymes
- **Wound Care**: Disinfectant spray, healing ointment, band-aids, liquid skin glue
- **Throat & Cold**: Throat anti-inflammatory spray, throat lozenges, saline nasal rinse, fever/pain relievers
- **Eyes**: Artificial tears or anti-inflammatory eye drops
- **Nose**: Saline nasal spray, decongestants
- **Allergies & Insect Bites**: Antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine / Zyrtec), itch-relief gel
- **Baby-Specific**: Teething gel, nasal aspirator
- **Miscellaneous**: COVID/flu rapid tests, thermometer

Most importantly: **Remember to purchase travel medical insurance before your trip**. Private hospitals in Bali are decent but very expensive. Without insurance, unexpected medical costs can hit your wallet hard.

## **Here are some additional tips:**

- Bring your child's favorite toys and activities on the plane to keep them entertained.
- In hot weather, make sure your child drinks plenty of water and apply sunscreen.
- Before visiting temples or other religious sites, teach your child to respect local culture.

Bali is truly a great destination for traveling with toddlers—as long as you're well-prepared. Nowadays, most basic necessities can be found in Bali, though brand options are limited, some items need to be ordered online, and certain things are more expensive than in Taiwan. Bring essentials like pureed meat, special formula, regular medications, favorite hats, and a few familiar toys from home—everything else can be managed once you arrive.

Having familiar items around helps children adapt much faster; and when parents are well-prepared, the trip becomes truly relaxing.
