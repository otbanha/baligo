Bali, this picturesque island, attracts travelers from around the world. In our Facebook group, [Chihming Lai](https://www.facebook.com/profile.php?id=100008235455950), who is not a professional traveler or travel agency employee, makes a special trip to Bali every six months to relax, enjoy the sun, beer, beaches, and bikinis. For him, Bali's unique slow-paced atmosphere is the island's greatest charm. Below, he shares his personal travel experiences and practical tips.

(The following content is adapted and reprinted with permission from [Chihming Lai](https://www.facebook.com/profile.php?id=100008235455950))

## **Pre-Trip Preparations:**

Before departure, he recommends applying for an **e-visa** online. When entering with a Taiwanese passport, you can scan your passport for facial recognition, allowing for quick and hassle-free immigration.

After collecting your luggage and before exiting the airport, make sure to have your **electronic customs declaration form** ready. It's crucial to complete this form in advance, as scanning it at customs can significantly speed up the process.

As for the **tourist tax**, he believes it's a personal choice whether to pay or not. He personally supports environmental initiatives like turtle releases, despite concerns about fund usage, as it contributes to conservation efforts. The tax is just USD$10, equivalent to a few beers.

## **Upon Arrival in Bali:**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_e13870a67da8a8539a2df155af5a63e5.jpg)

1. Don't pack your itinerary too tightly. Since you'll only be here briefly, it's better to slow down and savor the local pace of life. Wander aimlessly, sip a cool drink, and let your mind breathe.
2. Currently, Bali offers a more serene environment, and the beach pollution has improved significantly.
3. For those wondering which attractions to visit, his advice is that while the Monkey Forest and Ubud Palace are worth a visit, five minutes is enough—no need to spend too much time there. Instead, just across the road from the palace is the Ubud Market, where wandering might be more enjoyable. As for the rice terraces, they can be seen along the way, so visiting them is optional. The journey itself is the scenery, so focus on enjoying it and relaxing.

## **Accommodation and Itinerary Suggestions:**

1. He strongly advises against overloading your schedule when staying in a villa, as it defeats the purpose and expense. Villas represent leisure—enjoy the pool and sunshine, sip a drink, and relax. Only venture out if you feel like it. There's no need to leave early in the morning and return late in the evening, especially if you're only staying for a night or two. Staying in a villa is about resting, appreciating the scenery, and enjoying the slow pace—don't overdo it.
2. The length of your Bali trip should dictate your itinerary; avoid rushing through everything. Traffic here is often congested, and the roads are narrow, making it nearly impossible to visit all the popular spots.
3. He always travels independently, staying in villas without changing accommodations. A 10-day villa stay costs about the same as a 5-day, 4-night hotel stay in Taiwan. Independent travel offers more flexibility, freeing you from rigid tour packages.
4. Language barriers shouldn't be a major concern—a friendly smile often works wonders. Plus, your phone can be a handy translation tool.
