Bali's Bingin Beach, a favorite among tourists and surfers, is set for major changes. The government announced that over 40 structures in the area, due to illegal land occupation and violations of spatial planning regulations, will be forcibly demolished starting July 21, 2025.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_e640f066067761e239d9a5078ca6aaf9.jpg)

Businesses in the Bingin area have been receiving formal notices from the Badung Regency Government over the past two months, urging them to rectify building structures and land occupation practices. However, the government ultimately determined that most structures did not comply with regulations and decided to proceed with legal action.

Satpol PP (Bali Civil Police) stated that investigations revealed these buildings were mostly illegally constructed without proper permits, with some even occupying state-owned land.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_d544e0f8149e61f7127439067bf1ae52.jpg)

Badung Regent Adi Arnawa emphasized that residents and business owners were well aware that these structures were built on non-private land and must take responsibility for the outcome. He stressed the government's firm stance on fully implementing the demolition recommendations based on provincial and Satpol PP investigations.

He also called on the public to avoid unnecessary interference or incitement of opposition, stating, 'Our community knows very well what land they were using. Now, it's simply about restoring the land to its proper use.'

The government also highlighted that businesses had been notified through various means, including letters, public notices, and WhatsApp messages, to prevent claims of ignorance as an excuse.

An anonymous Bingin Beach business owner expressed frustration, noting that the area was once just a fishing village and had gradually invested significant resources into tourism infrastructure. He lamented, 'If we were violating regulations, why didn’t the government stop us over the past 30 years? Now that everything is being torn down, shouldn’t the government also reflect on its actions?'

The demolition affects 45 structures, including guesthouses, restaurants, tourist facilities, and beach villas. Violations involve illegal land occupation, breaches of environmental and spatial regulations, and impacts on natural landscape conservation areas.
