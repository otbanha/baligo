The incident happened on the streets of Ubud. They were originally planning to exchange money at 'BMC' recommended by their hotel, but along the way, they passed an exchange booth advertising a rate of '16675', significantly higher than the normal rate of around 16325 that day.

One member of the group thought it was a great rate and went in first. Everything seemed normal. But when other members followed, the nightmare began...

## The Magic Trick Revealed: How Fellow Travelers' Money Was Stolen!

This unscrupulous exchange booth's technique was quite sophisticated. Only after the incident did the group members realize what had happened:

- One traveler exchanging $500 had 2 million IDR (about USD$130) stolen

- Another exchanging $1000 lost 4 million IDR (about USD$260)

Here's how they did it:

## ****Bali Money Exchange Safety Tips: Protect Your Hard-Earned Money****

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_790c4aaf4dab053d3e6bae094881f2f9.jpg)

Based on suggestions from fellow travelers, here are some safety guidelines:

### ****Choose Reputable Money Changers****

1. Many recommend "BMC" or "Central Kuta" money changers. While their rates may not be the best, trust and safety are paramount (look for shops with the green "PVA Authorized Money Changer" sign).
2. Some always go to "MPB" for exchanges, where staff sometimes offer better rates than advertised (e.g., 16,325 listed but 16,500 given).
3. This kind of scam isn’t new—don’t be tempted by attractive rates. Always count all the money at once, and don’t let the staff touch it after you’ve counted. Stick to large, reputable chains!

### ****Observe the Shop Environment****

1. Always choose a physical shop with glass windows.
2. Avoid street vendors without proper shops or small alley shops offering suspiciously good rates.
3. Compare several shops to understand the normal exchange rate range for the day.
4. Shops with average rates along the way are relatively safer.
5. Ensure it’s "no commission" and that there are no hidden fees.

### ****Insist on "Eyes on the Money, Hands on the Counter"****

1. No matter how "helpful" the staff tries to be, firmly refuse any assistance in organizing the bills.
2. After counting yourself, recount before putting the money in your wallet.
3. Don’t give up your vigilance out of politeness.

### ****How to Handle Suspicious Situations****

1. If something bad happens, remember: don't be shy about making a scene!
2. Shout loudly to attract attention
3. In Indonesia, if you make enough noise to gather a crowd, bystanders will naturally help resolve the situation
4. Call the Tourist Police for assistance: `(+62) 361 224 111` or `(+62) 361 759 687`
5. If you get scammed while exchanging money in Bali, you can call the police, and the Tourist Police take such incidents that damage tourism very seriously. Based on current cases, although these scams (sleight of hand, shortchanging) usually involve relatively small amounts, reporting or threatening to report often has the following effects:

- Recover losses: Many scammers operate on the assumption that tourists won't bother making a fuss. When you call the police or threaten to do so, most illegal money changers will choose to refund your money immediately to avoid being shut down or jailed.
- Blacklist: Bali authorities have been cracking down hard on illegal money changers in recent years. Your report can get these shady businesses blacklisted or even shut down.

6. SOP for Dealing with Scams
If you find yourself shortchanged, follow these steps:

- Stay calm and don't leave the scene: It's hard to prove you were shortchanged after you've left.
- Record evidence: If possible, start recording during the argument, capturing the shop's sign and the staff's face.
- Protest firmly: Point out directly that the amount is incorrect. If they refuse to make up the difference, call immediately and tell the staff: "I am calling the Tourist Police now."

## Editor's Honest Words

Bali is truly beautiful - Ubud's cultural arts, Kuta's beach sunsets, Seminyak's Instagram-worthy cafes... but don't let a bad money exchange experience ruin your wonderful vacation.

Although our group lost some money this time, fortunately they discovered it immediately and it didn't affect their subsequent itinerary. They also hope to warn others through their experience: those shops advertising "exceptionally good exchange rates" are often traps. Rather than risking it for a few hundred thousand extra IDR, choose safe and reliable official money changers. After all, what's most important when traveling abroad is having fun and peace of mind, right?
