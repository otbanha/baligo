The incident happened on the streets of Ubud. The group originally planned to exchange money at "BMC," recommended by their hotel, but along the way, they passed a money changer advertising a rate of "16675," which was significantly higher than the day's standard rate (around 16325). One member thought it was a rare opportunity and went in to exchange money. Everything seemed normal at first, but when other group members followed suit, the nightmare began...

## **The Magic Trick Revealed: This is How the Group's Money Was Stolen!**

The scam was quite sophisticated, and the group members only realized what had happened afterward:

- One member who exchanged $500 had **2 million** IDR (approximately USD$130) stolen
- Another member who exchanged $1000 lost **4 million** IDR (approximately USD$260)

How did they do it? Here's how it unfolded:

1. The staff first counted the money in front of you
2. You then counted it yourself to confirm
3. While you were focused on counting, the staff "helpfully" stacked the bills neatly
4. In that split second of stacking, the bills were magically stolen!
5. The entire process took less than three seconds, as fast as a magic trick

## **Bali Money Exchange Safety Guidelines: Protect Your Hard-Earned Money**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_790c4aaf4dab053d3e6bae094881f2f9.jpg)

Based on suggestions from netizens, here are some safety guidelines:

### **Choose Reputable Money Changers**

1. Netizens highly recommend "BMC" or "Central Kuta" money changers. While the exchange rate might not be the best, honesty and safety are paramount (look for shops with the green "PVA Authorized Money Changer" sign).
2. Some people regularly go to "MPB" for exchanges, where staff might even offer better rates than advertised (e.g., advertised 16325, given 16500).
3. This kind of incident isn’t new, so don’t be tempted by attractive rates. Always count all the money yourself, and don’t let the staff touch it again after you’ve counted. It’s best to stick to large chain stores!

### **Observe the Shop Environment**

1. Always choose a physical shop with glass windows.
2. Avoid street vendors without proper shops or small alleys offering suspiciously good rates.
3. Compare several shops to understand the normal exchange rate range for the day.
4. Shops with average rates along the way are relatively safer.
5. Make sure it’s "no commission" and that there are no hidden fees.

### **Insist on "Eyes on the Money, Hands on the Counter"**

1. No matter how "helpful" the staff tries to be in organizing the bills, firmly refuse.
2. After counting yourself, recount before putting the money in your wallet.
3. Don’t give up your insistence out of politeness.

### **How to Handle Situations Like This**

1. If something bad happens, remember: don't let pride get in the way!
2. Shout loudly to attract attention from passersby
3. In Indonesia, if you make enough noise to gather a crowd, locals will naturally help resolve the situation
4. Contact the Tourist Police for assistance: `(+62) 361 224 111` or `(+62) 361 759 687`
5. If you get scammed while exchanging money in **Bali**, **you can call the police**, and the Tourist Police take such incidents that damage tourism very seriously. Based on current cases, although these scams (sleight of hand, shortchanging) usually involve small amounts, reporting or threatening to report often has the following effects:

- **Recover losses:** Many scammers operate on the assumption that tourists won't bother making a fuss. When you call the police or threaten to do so, most illegal money changers will immediately **return the money** to avoid being shut down or jailed.
- **Blacklisting:** Bali authorities have been cracking down hard on illegal money changers in recent years. Your report can get the shady establishment blacklisted or even shut down.

6. **Recommended SOP after being scammed**
   If you find that money is missing, follow these steps:

- **Stay calm and don't leave the scene:** It's hard to prove they shortchanged you after you've left.
- **Record evidence:** If possible, start recording during the argument, capturing the store sign and the staff's face.
- **Protest firmly:** Point out directly that the money count is incorrect. If they refuse to make up the difference, call immediately and tell the staff: **"I am calling the Tourist Police now."**
