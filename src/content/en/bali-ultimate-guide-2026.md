## 🛂 Must-Do Before Departure

### 1. Passport and Visa: Confirm 2 Months in Advance

Your passport must have at least **6 months of validity remaining**. Indonesian customs is strict about this—even one day past the requirement and you'll be turned away.

**Three visa options:**

- **Visa-Free**: Taiwanese passport holders can stay up to 30 days without a visa (but need to apply online)
- **eVOA (Electronic Visa on Arrival)**: Apply online before departure, approval takes about 2-3 business days, valid for 30 days—**best for first-timers**
- **VoA (Visa on Arrival)**: Process upon arrival at Bali, with on-site queues taking 15 to 30 minutes

**I strongly recommend the eVOA** because:

- You can go straight to the automated gate upon arrival (no 30-minute queue)
- It's only $5 cheaper, but saves you tons of time
- Customs policies are tightening in 2026, so having it ready avoids any hassle

### 2. Travel Insurance: Seriously, Don't Skip It

Medical standards in Bali aren't as good as in Taiwan. If you end up hospitalized there, it could cost you between USD$16,000 and USD$32,000. Plus, Bali has plenty of mosquito-borne diseases.

**What your insurance should cover:**

- Accidental injuries (common for hiking and water sports)
- Medical expenses (including overseas hospitalization)
- Trip interruption / Lost luggage

### 3. Flight Planning: Avoid Peak Seasons to Save 1/3 of Your Budget

**When is it most expensive?**

- Summer and winter breaks (July, August)
- Chinese New Year (late January to February)
- Christmas holidays (mid-December to early January)
- Qingming and Labor Day long weekends (early April)

**When is it cheap?**

- May to June (tail end of rainy season, before peak season)
- September to October (just after peak season)
- November (start of rainy season)

Tickets are usually **best purchased 3–6 weeks in advance**. Don’t rush for the first flight of the day—flights departing between 2–5 PM are often 20–30% cheaper.

💡 **Common beginner mistake**: thinking budget airlines are always cheaper. In reality, you’ll pay extra for luggage and seat selection, which can add up. Direct flights with STARLUX, China Airlines, or EVA Air often offer better value.

### 4. Bali Tourist Levy

Starting in 2024, every foreign visitor to Bali must pay a tourist levy of 150,000 Indonesian rupiah.

**How to pay?**

- Easiest: Pay online before departure (balilevy.baliprov.go.id)
- Next best: Pay upon arrival at the airport (dedicated counters available)
- Not recommended: Wait until you reach a tourist attraction, as random checks may occur

Be sure to keep your receipt—you may be asked to show it at attractions.

## 💰 Pre-Trip Checklist

### 5. Currency Exchange: Don't Exchange at Your Departure Bank

[https://youtu.be/KPGb6MmwfZw](https://youtu.be/KPGb6MmwfZw)

**Exchange rates at overseas banks are usually terrible**, and fees are high. Smart approach:

1. **Bring USD** to Bali and exchange a small amount first.
2. **Or exchange at legal money changers in Bali**.
3. **Avoid roadside exchange booths** (a breeding ground for scams—countless tourists have been ripped off).

### 6. Packing List: What to Bring and What to Leave Behind

**Must-Bring Items (You'll Actually Use):**

- Sunscreen (SPF 50+ minimum)
- Mosquito repellent (lotion works better than spray)
- Light rain gear (sudden showers are common in the rainy season)
- Basic medications (cold medicine, stomach meds, anti-diarrhea pills)
- Light jacket (indoor air conditioning can be chilly)
- Photocopy of your passport (just in case of loss)

**Skip These (You Can Find Them Cheaply in Bali):**

- Face masks (inexpensive jelly masks are everywhere)
- Cosmetics (plenty of affordable brands)
- Daily essentials (hotels and supermarkets have them covered)

**Banned Items (Customs Will Confiscate or Fine You):**

- Any drugs or narcotics
- Meat products (even instant noodles from Taiwan need ingredient checks)
- Alcoholic beverages over 5 liters
- Wildlife products

### 8. Voltage & Plugs: 220V, Bring an Adapter

Bali uses **220V voltage**, while Taiwan uses 110V.

**Smart Move:**

- Bring a universal adapter (get one that supports multiple country standards)
- Or buy one locally in Bali (usually the cheapest option)

Most laptops and phone chargers support 110-240V auto-conversion (check the charger for details), but always double-check hair dryers and curling irons.

## 🗺️ Trip Planning Essentials

### 9. How Many Days Are Best? First-timers: 5–7 Days

**3 days or less:** You'll be stuck in the south (Kuta, Seminyak) — a real shame.
**5–7 days:** South + Ubud, you'll experience about 60% of Bali.
**10+ days:** Only then can you explore the east, west, and outer islands.

For first-timers, I highly recommend **6 days and 5 nights** — just the right amount of time without feeling rushed.

## ✈️ Arriving in Bali

### 14. The Correct Process from Landing to Exiting the Airport

![14. 下飛機到出機場的正確流程](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-05/1778988530785-_5.png)

**Don't wander off—follow this sequence:**

1. **Follow the "Arrival" signs** (not Departure)
2. **E-gate holders use the automated immigration gates** (fast track)
3. **Visa-on-arrival holders queue at the Visa on Arrival counter** (to get your visa)
4. **Pass through immigration check** (passport stamping)
5. **Head to the baggage carousel** (find your flight number)
6. **Fill out the AIAC customs declaration form** (online only)
7. **Go through customs** (usually just a quick glance and you're through)
8. **Pay the Bali tourist tax** (if you haven't paid it in advance)

**The whole process takes at most 30-60 minutes if there's no queue**, so don't stress.

### 15. Transportation Options from the Airport to Your Hotel

**Four options:**

| Option | Cost | Pros | Cons |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |

### 16. Stomach Issues and Adjusting to Local Conditions

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

**The most common issue in Bali is Bali Belly (traveler's diarrhea).**

**Prevention is better than cure:**

- Drink only bottled water (supermarkets are everywhere and cheap)
- Avoid raw street food (be cautious with fresh salads and fruit platters)
- Food cooked at high temperatures is generally safe

**If you do get it:**

- Bring anti-diarrheal medication (safer to bring from Taiwan)
- Drink electrolyte beverages (available at Bali supermarkets)
- Rest for half a day; it usually clears up within 24 hours

### 17. How to Convert Prices?

**Quick Conversion Formula**: Remove the last three zeros from the Indonesian Rupiah amount, then multiply by 2 to get roughly the amount in New Taiwan Dollars (NT$).

**Examples:**

- 100,000 IDR → 100 × 2 = NT$200
- 500,000 IDR → 500 × 2 = NT$1,000
- 1,000,000 IDR → 1000 × 2 = NT$2,000

💡 With this formula, you won't be shocked by those five-digit numbers when ordering.

### 18. How to Tip?

[https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY](https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY)

**Indonesia doesn't have a 'mandatory tipping' culture, but 'thank-you tips' are very welcome.**

- **Hotel Bellhop**: USD$1-2 or 20,000 IDR
- **SPA Massage Therapist**: 10% of the bill (or directly give 20,000~50,000 IDR)
- **Restaurant Server**: 10% of the bill (often a 4-10% service charge is already included, so you can skip this)
- **Taxi**: Round up to the nearest thousand (give the change if you have it, no need if you don't)
- **Guide/Private Driver**: 50,000~100,000 IDR per day

**The simplest rule**: Whenever someone does something for you, give a small tip. It doesn't have to be much, but it's greatly appreciated.

### 23. Cultural Taboos and Respect

**Religiously Sensitive Areas:**

- Wear a sarong when entering temples (usually provided for free)
- Do not touch or climb on sacred statues
- Traditionally, women should not enter temples during menstruation (though most temples don’t check)
- If you see a ceremony in progress, take photos from a distance and avoid disturbing it

**Everyday Etiquette:**

- Use your right hand for eating and handing things over
- Do not point your feet at others
- Remove shoes before entering a home
- Show respect to elders

**Dress Code:**

- Bikinis are fine on the beach
- Cover shoulders and knees when entering temples or villages
- For formal occasions, men can wear a polo shirt

### Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

## Q: How many days are best for a first trip to Bali?

**A:** 5–7 days is the best choice for beginners. Less than 3 days feels rushed and you can only explore the south; 10 days or more lets you see the entire island. With **6 days and 5 nights**, you can experience the south (beaches, shopping) plus the north (culture, highlands) — the most comfortable balance without getting too exhausted.

## Q: What's the budget for a Bali trip?

## Q: Can I use New Taiwan Dollars in Bali?

**A:** No. Bali only uses **Indonesian Rupiah (IDR)**. New Taiwan Dollars are not accepted anywhere, and it's hard to find exchange places. It's best to bring US dollars and exchange them for Rupiah once you're in Bali.

## Q: Is Bali safe? Are there robberies or scams?

**A:** Overall, Bali is safer than many Southeast Asian cities. Robberies are rare, and scams mainly target unwary tourists (like black-market money changers or fake drivers). As long as you follow basic safety tips — avoid walking alone in dark alleys at night, don't use street-side black-market exchange, and verify driver IDs — you'll almost certainly have no issues.

## Q: Can I visit Bali during the rainy season?

**A:** Absolutely. The rainy season (November–March) usually only brings **brief afternoon showers lasting 1–2 hours**, and attractions remain open. In fact, the rainy season has perks: fewer crowds, hotels 40–50% cheaper, the greenest rice terraces, and more relaxing spa experiences. Many seasoned travelers specifically choose this time. The downsides are unpredictable mountain weather and the possibility of last-minute cancellations for some outdoor activities.

👉 [**Complete Rainy Season Guide**](/en/blog/2026-bali-indoor-activities/)

## Q: Do I need a visa for my first trip to Bali? Can I get a visa on arrival?

**A:** Taiwanese passport holders have two options:

1. **eVOA (Electronic Visa on Arrival)** — apply online 2–3 days before departure (**highly recommended**)
2. **Visa on Arrival** — apply at the airport (around USD$39)

The advantage of the eVOA is that you can use the automated gate upon arrival (no need to queue for 45 minutes at the manual counter).

Hong Kong, Singapore, and Malaysia passport holders get 30 days visa-free.

## Q: Should I tip in Bali? How much?

**A:** Tipping is not mandatory in Indonesia, but it's always appreciated. Here's a rough guide:

- Bellhop: 20,000 IDR
- SPA/massage: 50,000 IDR
- Restaurants: If service charge is included, no need to tip extra
- Taxi: Round up to the nearest 5,000 or 10,000 IDR
- Private driver/guide: 50,000–100,000 IDR per day

The key is: tip anyone who helps you out. The amount doesn't have to be big — it's the gesture that counts.

## Q: Which area is best to stay in Bali for first-timers?

**A:** It depends on your travel style:

- **Kuta**: Lively, budget-friendly, backpacker hub
- **Seminyak**: Boutique shops, upscale dining, Instagram-worthy spots
- **Canggu**: Cafés, youthful vibe, surfing
- **Ubud**: Culture, wellness, away from the crowds
- **Nusa Dua**: Five-star hotels, family-friendly, resorts
- **Jimbaran**: Sunsets, seafood, romantic dates

For beginners, the top recommendation is a **Seminyak + Ubud** combo, letting you enjoy both the beach and culture.
