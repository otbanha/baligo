## 🛂 Must-Do Before Departure
### 1. Passport & Visa: Confirm 2 Months in Advance
Your passport must have **at least 6 months of validity remaining**. Indonesian customs is strict about this—even one day short and you'll be turned away.

**Three visa options:**
- **Visa-Free**: Taiwanese passport holders can stay up to 30 days without a visa (but need to declare online)
- **eVOA (Electronic Visa on Arrival)**: Apply online before departure, approval takes about 2-3 business days, valid for 30 days—**best for beginners**
- **VoA (Visa on Arrival)**: Apply upon arrival in Bali, with on-site queues of 15 to 30 minutes

**I strongly recommend the eVOA** because:
- You can use the automated gate upon arrival (no 30-minute queue)
- It's only $5 more expensive, but saves you tons of time
- Customs policies are tightening in 2026, so having it ready avoids any hassle

### 1. 護照與簽證：提前2個月確認

你的護照至少要剩**6個月以上的有效期**。印尼海關對這個要求很嚴格，過期一天都會被擋。

**簽證有三種選擇：**

- **免簽證**：台灣護照持有人停留 30 天內免簽（但需要在線申報）
- **電子簽證 eVOA**：出發前在線申請，約2-3個工作日核准，停留 30 天，**新手最推薦**
- **落地簽 VoA**：抵達峇里島後辦理，現場排隊可能要 15 分鐘到 30 分鐘

**強烈建議選電子簽證**，因為：

- 抵達後直接走自動通關閘門（不用排隊排 30 分鐘）
- 費用只便宜 5 美金，節省時間卻值超多
- 2026 年海關政策越來越嚴，先備好絕對沒坑

### 2. Travel Insurance: Don't Skip It

Medical standards in Bali aren't as high as in Taiwan. If you end up hospitalized locally, it could cost you USD$16,000 to USD$32,000. Plus, Bali has plenty of mosquito-borne diseases.

**What your insurance should cover:**

- Accidental injuries (common for hiking and water sports)
- Medical expenses (including overseas hospitalization)
- Trip interruption / lost luggage

Check this out: [Insurance Guide for Travelers from Taiwan, Hong Kong, Macau, and Singapore: Key Tips on Overseas Medical and Trip Interruption Claims](/en/blog/bali-travel-insurance-guide/)

### 3. Flight Planning: Avoid Peak Seasons to Save 1/3 of Your Budget

**When is it most expensive?**

- Summer and winter breaks (July, August)
- Chinese New Year (late January to February)
- Christmas holidays (mid-December to early January)
- Tomb Sweeping Day and Labor Day long weekends (early April)

**When is it cheaper?**

- May to June (end of rainy season, before peak season)
- September to October (just after peak season)
- November (start of rainy season)

Tickets are usually **best purchased 3–6 weeks in advance**. Don't rush for the first flight of the day—opting for departures between 2–5 PM can save you 20–30%.

💡 **Common beginner mistake**: "Budget airlines are cheaper." In reality, you'll pay extra for baggage and seat selection, which often makes them no cheaper than full-service carriers. Direct flights with STARLUX, China Airlines, or EVA Air can actually be a better deal.

### 4. Bali Tourist Levy

Starting in 2024, every foreign visitor to Bali must pay a tourist levy of 150,000 Indonesian Rupiah.

**How to pay?**

- Easiest: Pay online before departure (balilevy.baliprov.go.id)
- Next best: Pay at the airport upon arrival (dedicated counters available)
- Not recommended: Wait until you reach a tourist attraction, as random checks may occur

Be sure to keep your receipt—you may be asked to show it at attractions.

### 6. Packing List: What to Bring and What to Leave Behind

**Must-Bring Items (You'll Actually Use):**

- Sunscreen (SPF 50+ minimum)
- Insect repellent (lotion type works better than spray)
- Light rain gear (sudden showers are common in the rainy season)

- Face masks (cheap jelly masks are everywhere on the streets)
- Cosmetics (plenty of affordable brands)
- Daily essentials (hotels have them, and supermarkets stock them too)

**Prohibited Items (Customs Will Confiscate or Fine You):**

- Any type of drugs
- Meat products (even instant noodles from Taiwan need ingredient checks)
- Alcoholic beverages exceeding 5 liters
- Wildlife products

### 9. How Many Days Are Best? For First-Timers, I Recommend 5–7 Days

**Under 3 days:** You'll be stuck in the south (Kuta, Seminyak) — a real shame.
**5–7 days:** South + Ubud, you'll experience about 60% of Bali.
**10+ days:** Only then can you explore the east, west, and outer islands.

For first-timers, I highly recommend **6 days and 5 nights** — it's just the right amount of time without feeling rushed.

## ✈️ Arriving in Bali

### 14. The Right Process from Landing to Exiting the Airport

![14. 下飛機到出機場的正確流程](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-05/1778988530785-_5.png)

**Don't wander off—follow this sequence:**

1. **Follow the 'Arrival' signs** (not Departure)
2. **E-gate holders use the automated immigration gates** (fast track)
3. **Visa-on-arrival holders queue at the Visa on Arrival counter** (to get your visa)
4. **Pass through immigration check** (passport stamping)
5. **Head to the baggage carousel** (find your flight number)
6. **Fill out the AIAC customs declaration form** (online only)
7. **Clear customs** (usually just a quick glance and you're through)
8. **Pay the Bali tourism tax** (if you haven't paid it in advance)

**The whole process takes at most 30-60 minutes if there's no queue**, so no need to stress.

### 16. Stomach Issues and Adjusting to Local Conditions

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

**One of the most common issues in Bali is Bali Belly (traveler's diarrhea).**

**Prevention is better than cure:**

- Only drink bottled water (supermarkets are everywhere and cheap)
- Avoid raw street food (be cautious with fresh salads and fruit platters)
- High-temperature cooked food is generally safe

**If you do get it:**

- Bring anti-diarrhea medication (safer to bring from Taiwan)
- Drink electrolyte beverages (available at Bali supermarkets)
- Rest for half a day; it usually resolves within 24 hours

### 17. How to Convert Prices?

**Quick Conversion Formula**: Remove the last three zeros from the Indonesian Rupiah amount, then multiply by 2. The result is roughly equivalent to New Taiwan Dollars.

**Examples:**

- 100,000 IDR → 100 × 2 = NT$200
- 500,000 IDR → 500 × 2 = NT$1,000
- 1,000,000 IDR → 1000 × 2 = NT$2,000

💡 With this formula, you won't be shocked by five-digit prices when ordering.

### 18. How to Tip?

[https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY](https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY)

**Indonesia has no 'mandatory tipping' culture, but 'thank-you tips' are very welcome.**

- **Hotel Bellhop**: USD$1-2 or 20,000 IDR
- **SPA Massage Therapist**: 10% of the bill (or simply give 20,000–50,000 IDR)
- **Restaurant Server**: 10% of the bill (often a 4-10% service charge is already included, so you can skip it)
- **Taxi Driver**: Round up to the nearest convenient amount (if there's change, give it; if not, no need)
- **Guide/Private Driver**: 50,000–100,000 IDR per day

**The simplest rule**: Whenever someone does something for you, give a small tip. It doesn't have to be much, but it's always appreciated.

### 23. Cultural Taboos and Respect

**Religiously Sensitive Areas:**

- Enter temples wearing a sarong (usually provided for free)
- Do not touch or climb on sacred statues
- Traditionally, women should not enter temples during their menstrual period (though most temples don't check)

**Daily Etiquette:**

- Use your right hand for eating and handing things over
- Do not point your feet at others
- Remove shoes when entering a home
- Show respect to elders

**Dress Code:**

- Bikinis are fine on the beach
- Cover shoulders and knees when entering temples or villages
- For formal occasions, men can wear a polo shirt

### Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

## Q: How many days are best for a first trip to Bali?

**A:** 5–7 days is the best choice for beginners. Less than 3 days will feel rushed, and you can only explore the south; more than 10 days is needed to fully experience the entire island. With **6 days and 5 nights**, you can enjoy the south (beaches, shopping) plus the north (culture, mountains), offering the most comfortable pace without excessive fatigue.

## Q: What's the budget for a Bali trip?

## Q: Can I use New Taiwan Dollars in Bali?

**A:** No. Bali only uses **Indonesian Rupiah (IDR)**. New Taiwan Dollars are not accepted locally, and it's hard to find exchange places. It's best to bring US Dollars and exchange them for Rupiah in Bali.

## Q: Is Bali safe? Are there robberies or scams?

**A:** Overall, Bali is safer than many Southeast Asian cities. Robberies are rare, and scams mainly target unwary tourists (like black-market currency exchange or fake drivers). As long as you follow basic safety advice—avoid walking alone in dark alleys at night, don't use roadside black-market exchanges, and verify driver identities—you'll almost certainly have no issues.

## Q: Can I visit Bali during the rainy season?

**A:** Absolutely. The rainy season (November to March) usually brings **just 1–2 hours of afternoon showers**, and attractions remain open. In fact, the rainy season has advantages: fewer tourists, hotels 40–50% cheaper, the greenest rice terraces, and more relaxing spa experiences. Many seasoned travelers specifically choose this season. The downsides are unpredictable mountain weather and the possibility of some outdoor activities being canceled on short notice.

👉 [**Complete Rainy Season Guide**](/en/blog/2026-bali-indoor-activities/)

## Q: Do I need a visa for my first trip to Bali? Can I get a visa on arrival?

**A:** Taiwanese passport holders have two options:

1. **eVOA (Electronic Visa on Arrival)** – Apply online 2–3 days before departure (**highly recommended**)
2. **Visa on Arrival** – Get it at the airport (around USD$39)

The advantage of the eVOA is that you can use the automated gate upon arrival (no need to queue for 45 minutes at the manual counter).

Hong Kong, Singapore, and Malaysia passport holders enjoy 30-day visa-free entry.

## Q: Should I tip in Bali? How much?

**A:** Tipping is not mandatory in Indonesia, but it’s always appreciated. Here’s a suggested guide:

- Bellhop: 20,000 IDR
- SPA massage: 50,000 IDR
- Restaurants: If a service charge is already included, no need to tip extra
- Taxis: Round up to the nearest 5,000 or 10,000 IDR
- Private driver/guide: 50,000–100,000 IDR per day

The key is to tip anyone who helps you out—amounts don’t need to be large, but a gracious attitude goes a long way.

## Q: Which area is best to stay in Bali for first-timers?

**A:** It depends on your travel style:

- **Kuta**: Lively, budget-friendly, backpacker hub
- **Seminyak**: Boutique shops, upscale dining, Instagram-worthy
- **Canggu**: Cafés, youthful vibe, surfing
- **Ubud**: Culture, wellness, away from crowds
- **Nusa Dua**: Five-star hotels, family-friendly, resorts
- **Jimbaran**: Sunsets, seafood, romantic dates

For beginners, the top recommendation is a **Seminyak + Ubud** combo, letting you enjoy both the beach and culture.
