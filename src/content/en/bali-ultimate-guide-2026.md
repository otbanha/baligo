## 🛂 Must-Do Before Departure

### 1. Passport & Visa: Confirm 2 Months in Advance

Your passport must have **at least 6 months of validity remaining**. Indonesian customs are very strict about this requirement—even one day expired could get you denied.

**There are three visa options:**

- **Visa-Free Entry**: Taiwanese passport holders can stay up to 30 days visa-free (but must complete an online declaration)
- **Electronic Visa on Arrival (eVOA)**: Apply online before departure, approval takes about 2-3 business days, valid for 30 days—**most recommended for first-timers**
- **Visa on Arrival (VoA)**: Processed upon arrival in Bali, may take 15 to 30 minutes in line

**Strongly recommend choosing the eVOA** because:

- You can use the automated immigration gates upon arrival (no need to wait in line for 30 minutes)
- It’s only $5 cheaper, but the time saved is worth so much more
- Customs policies are getting stricter by 2026, so being prepared is essential

### 2. Travel Insurance: Don’t Skip It

The medical standards in Bali are not as good as in Taiwan. Hospitalization there could cost USD$16,000-32,000. Additionally, Bali has many mosquito-borne diseases.

**Insurance Coverage Essentials:**

- Accidental injury (common for hiking and water activities)
- Medical expenses (including overseas hospitalization)
- Trip interruption/lost luggage

Check this out: [Must-read for Taiwan, Hong Kong, Malaysia & Singapore travelers! Insurance guide, overseas medical & travel inconvenience claim tips](/blog/bali-travel-insurance-guide/)

### 3. Flight Planning: Avoid Peak Periods to Save 1/3 of Your Budget

**When are prices highest?**

- Summer & winter vacations (July, August)
- Lunar New Year (late January-February)
- Christmas holidays (mid-December to early January)
- Qingming & Labor Day holidays (early April)

**When are prices lower?**

- May-June (end of rainy season, before peak season)
- September-October (just after peak season)
- November (start of rainy season)

Booking flights **3-6 weeks in advance** is usually most cost-effective. Don't aim for the first flight of the day - flights departing between 2-5 PM are often 20-30% cheaper.

💡 **Common Misconception for Beginners**: "Budget airlines are cheaper." The truth is, budget airlines charge extra for luggage and seat selection, and when you add it all up, they may not be cheaper than traditional airlines. Direct flights with Starlux, China Airlines, or EVA Air might actually be more cost-effective.

### 4. Bali Tourist Levy

Starting in 2024, every foreign visitor entering Bali will be required to pay a tourist levy of IDR 150,000.

**How to Pay?**

- Easiest: Pay online before departure (balilevy.baliprov.go.id)
- Next: Pay directly at the airport upon arrival (dedicated counters available)
- Not recommended: Wait until you're at a tourist site to pay, as random checks may occur

Be sure to keep your receipt, as you may be asked to show it at tourist sites.

## 💰 Pre-Trip Checklist

### 5. Currency Exchange: Don’t Exchange at Your Local Bank

[https://youtu.be/KPGb6MmwfZw](https://youtu.be/KPGb6MmwfZw)

1. **Bring USD** and exchange a small amount first in Bali.
2. **Or exchange at legitimate money changers in Bali**.
3. **Avoid roadside money changers** (hotbeds for scams, countless tourists have been cheated).

### 6. Packing List: What to Bring and What to Skip

**Must-bring items (you’ll actually use them):**

- Sunscreen (SPF 50+ minimum)
- Mosquito repellent (lotion works better than spray)
- Light rain gear (frequent showers during the rainy season)
- Basic medication (cold medicine, stomach medicine, anti-diarrheal)
- Light jacket (indoor air conditioning can be chilly)
- Passport copy (in case of loss)

**Don't Bring (You Can Buy These Cheaply in Bali):**

- Face masks (cheap jelly masks are everywhere)
- Cosmetics (plenty of affordable brands)
- Daily necessities (hotels provide them, and supermarkets have them too)

**Prohibited Items (Customs Will Confiscate or Fine):**

- Anything related to drugs
- Meat products (even instant noodles from Taiwan need ingredient checks)
- Alcoholic beverages exceeding 5 liters
- Products made from wild animals

### 8. Voltage & Plugs: 220V, Bring the Right Adapter

Bali uses **220V voltage**, while Taiwan uses 110V.

**Smart Tips:**

- Bring a universal adapter (buy one that supports multiple countries)
- Or buy one locally in Bali (usually the cheapest option)

Most laptop and phone chargers support automatic voltage conversion between 110-240V (check if it’s written on the charger), but always double-check for hair dryers and curling irons.

## 🗺️ Trip Planning Essentials

### 9. How Many Days Are Ideal? First-Timers Should Aim for 5-7 Days

**Less than 3 days:** Only enough time to explore the south (Kuta, Seminyak), which is a shame
**5-7 days:** South + Ubud, experiencing 60% of Bali
**10+ days:** Enough time to explore the east, west, and outer islands

For first-timers, I highly recommend **6 days and 5 nights** — it’s the perfect duration without feeling rushed.

### 11. When is the best time to visit?

**Dry Season (April-October)**

- Weather: Sunny, clear skies
- Crowds: High (peak season)
- Airfare: Expensive
- Suitability: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Rainy Season (November-March)**

- Weather: Frequent afternoon showers (not all day)

**Five Peak Seasons:**

1. **Summer & Winter Breaks** (July, August) - Families flock in

## ✈️ Arriving in Bali

### 14. The Correct Process from Landing to Exiting the Airport

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-05/1778988530785-_5.png)

**Don’t wander around—follow these steps in order:**

1. **Follow the "Arrival" signs** (not Departure)
2. **E-Visa holders use the automated gates** (fast track)
3. **Visa on Arrival holders queue at the Visa on Arrival counter** (to process your visa)
4. **Pass through immigration** (get your passport stamped)
5. **Collect your luggage at the carousel** (look for your flight number)
6. **Fill out the AIAC customs declaration form** (online only)
7. **Go through customs** (usually just a quick glance)
8. **Pay the Bali tourism tax** (if not paid in advance)

**The entire process takes 30-60 minutes if there’s no queue**, so don’t stress.

### 16. Stomach Issues and Digestive Problems

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

**The most common issue in Bali is Bali Belly (traveler's diarrhea).**

**Prevention is Key:**

- Drink only bottled water (widely available and cheap in supermarkets)
- Avoid raw foods from street vendors (be cautious with fresh salads and fruit platters)
- Foods cooked at high temperatures are generally safe

**If You Do Get Sick:**

- Bring anti-diarrheal medication (it's safer to bring from home)
- Drink electrolyte beverages (available in Bali supermarkets)
- Rest for half a day; symptoms usually improve within a day

## 💡 Local Tips

### 17. How to Convert Currency?

**Quick Conversion Formula**: Remove the last three zeros from the Indonesian Rupiah amount, then multiply by 2 to get an approximate value in USD.

**Example:**

- 100,000 IDR → 100 × 2 = USD$6
- 500,000 IDR → 500 × 2 = USD$32
- 1,000,000 IDR → 1000 × 2 = USD$64

💡 With this formula, you won’t be shocked by the "OMG, it’s a 5-digit number" when ordering food.

### 18. How to Tip?

[https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY](https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY)

**Indonesia doesn’t have a "mandatory tipping" culture, but "gratitude tips" are appreciated.**

- **Hotel Bellboy**: USD$1-2 or 20,000 IDR
- **SPA Masseuse**: 10% of the bill (or directly give 20,000~50,000 IDR)
- **Restaurant Waiter**: 10% of the bill (usually, a 4-10% service charge is already included, so you can skip this)
- **Taxi Driver**: Round up (give change if you have it, otherwise, it’s fine)
- **Tour Guide/Private Driver**: 50,000~100,000 IDR per day

**The simplest rule**: Always tip when someone helps you. It doesn't need to be much, but it's greatly appreciated.

### 23. Cultural Taboos and Respect

**Religious Sensitive Areas:**

- Wear a sarong when entering temples (usually provided for free)
- Do not touch or climb sacred statues
- Traditionally, women should not enter temples during menstruation (though temples usually don't check)
- If you see a ceremonial ritual, take photos from a distance and do not disturb

**Daily Etiquette:**

- Use your right hand for eating and handing things over
- Do not point your feet at others
- Remove shoes before entering homes
- Show respect to elders

**Dress Code:**

- Bikinis are fine at the beach
- Wear clothing that covers shoulders and knees when entering temples or villages
- Polo shirts are acceptable for men in formal settings

## Frequently Asked Questions FAQ

### Q: How many days are ideal for a first trip to Bali?

**A:** 5-7 days is the best choice for beginners. Less than 3 days will feel rushed and limit you to the southern area; more than 10 days are needed to explore the entire island. **6 days and 5 nights** allows you to experience both the south (beaches, shopping) and the north (culture, mountains) comfortably without feeling overly fatigued.

### Q: What’s the budget for a Bali free-and-easy trip?

**A:** The per person budget for 6 days and 5 nights can range from $200 to $2,000. We recommend using the tool below:

👉 [**Detailed Budget Calculator**](/bali-budget-calculator/)

### Q: Can I use Taiwanese dollars in Bali?

**A:** No. Bali only uses **Indonesian Rupiah (IDR)**. Taiwanese dollars are not accepted locally, and it's difficult to find places to exchange them. We recommend bringing US dollars to Bali and exchanging them for Rupiah there.

### Q: Is Bali safe? Will I get robbed or scammed?

**A:** Overall, Bali is safer than many cities in Southeast Asia. Robberies are rare, and scams mainly target unwary tourists (such as black market currency exchange or fake drivers). As long as you follow basic safety advice—avoid walking alone in secluded alleys at night, don’t use black market currency exchange, and verify driver identities—you’ll likely have no issues.

### Q: Can I visit Bali during the rainy season?

**A:** Absolutely. During the rainy season (November-March), it’s usually **just 1-2 hours of afternoon showers**, and attractions remain open. In fact, the rainy season has its advantages: fewer tourists, hotels are 40-50% cheaper, rice terraces are at their greenest, and Spas are more relaxing. Many seasoned travelers specifically choose the rainy season to visit. The downside is unpredictable mountain weather and the possibility of some outdoor activities being canceled last minute.

👉 [**Complete Rainy Season Guide**](/blog/2026-bali-indoor-activities/)

### Q: Do I need to apply for a visa before my first trip to Bali? Can I get a visa on arrival?

**A:** There are two options for Taiwanese passport holders:

1. **eVOA (Electronic Visa on Arrival)** (apply online 2-3 days before departure, **highly recommended**)
2. **Visa on Arrival** (apply at the airport upon arrival, around USD$39)

The advantage of the eVOA is that you can use the automated immigration gates upon arrival (no need to queue for 45 minutes at the manual counters).

Hong Kong, Singapore, and Malaysia citizens enjoy 30-day visa-free entry.

### Q: Is tipping expected in Bali? How should I tip?

**A:** Tipping is not mandatory in Indonesia, but it will make service staff very happy. Suggested tipping amounts:

- Porters: IDR 20,000
- Spa/massage: IDR 50,000
- Restaurants: No need to tip if service charge is already included
- Taxis: Round up the fare
- Private drivers/guides: IDR 50,000~100,000 per day

The key is to 'tip those who help you' — the amount doesn't need to be large, but the gesture is what matters most.

### Q: Where is the best area to stay for first-time visitors to Bali?

**A:** It depends on your travel style:

- **Kuta**: Lively, affordable, popular with backpackers
- **Seminyak**: Boutiques, upscale dining, Instagram hotspots
- **Canggu**: Cafes, youthful vibe, surfing
- **Ubud**: Culture, wellness, away from the crowds
- **Nusa Dua**: Five-star hotels, family-friendly, resorts
- **Jimbaran**: Sunsets, seafood, romantic dinners

For beginners, we recommend combining **Seminyak + Ubud** to experience both beaches and culture.
