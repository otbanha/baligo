## 🛂 Must-Do Before Departure

### 1. Passport & Visa: Confirm 2 Months in Advance

Your passport must have **at least 6 months of validity remaining**. Indonesian customs are very strict about this requirement - even one day expired will get you denied.

**There are three visa options:**

- **Visa-Free**: Taiwan passport holders can stay up to 30 days visa-free (but need to complete online declaration)
- **Electronic Visa eVOA**: Apply online before departure, approved in about 2-3 business days, valid for 30 days - **most recommended for first-timers**
- **Visa on Arrival VoA**: Apply upon arrival in Bali, may take 15-30 minutes in line

**Strongly recommend choosing the electronic visa** because:

- Walk straight through automated gates upon arrival (no 30-minute queues)
- Only $5 cheaper, but saves so much more in time
- Customs policies are getting stricter by 2026, better to be prepared

### 2. Travel Insurance: Don't Skip It

The medical standards in Bali are not as good as in Taiwan. Hospitalization there could cost USD$16,000-32,000. Additionally, Bali has many mosquito-borne diseases.

**Insurance Coverage Essentials:**

- Accidental injury (common for hiking and water activities)
- Medical expenses (including overseas hospitalization)
- Trip interruption/lost luggage

Check this out: [Essential Guide for Taiwan, Hong Kong, Malaysia & Singapore Travelers: Insurance Tips, Overseas Medical Coverage & Claim Avoidance](/blog/bali-travel-insurance-guide/)

### 3. Flight Planning: Avoid Peak Seasons to Save 1/3 of Your Budget

**When Are Prices Highest?**

- Summer and winter vacations (July, August)
- Chinese New Year (late January-February)
- Christmas holidays (mid-December to early January)
- Qingming and Labor Day holidays (early April)

**When Are Prices Lower?**

- May-June (end of rainy season, before peak season)
- September-October (just after peak season)
- November (start of rainy season)

Booking flights **3-6 weeks in advance** usually offers the best deals. Avoid the first flight of the day; afternoon departures between 2-5 PM are often 20-30% cheaper.

💡 **Common Misconception for Beginners**: "Budget airlines are cheaper." The truth is, budget airlines charge extra for luggage and seat selection, and when you add it all up, they may not be cheaper than traditional airlines. Direct flights with Starlux, China Airlines, or EVA Air can actually be more cost-effective.

### 4. Bali Tourist Levy

Starting in 2024, every foreign tourist entering Bali will need to pay a tourist levy of IDR 150,000.

**How to Pay?**

- Easiest: Pay online before departure (balilevy.baliprov.go.id)
- Next Option: Pay directly at the airport upon arrival (dedicated counters available)
- Not Recommended: Wait until you're at a tourist site to pay, as random checks may occur

Be sure to keep your receipt, as you may be asked to show it at tourist sites.

## 💰 Pre-Trip Checklist

### 5. Currency Exchange: Don’t Exchange Money at Your Local Bank

[https://youtu.be/KPGb6MmwfZw](https://youtu.be/KPGb6MmwfZw)

**Exchange rates at overseas banks are usually terrible**, and the fees are high. Smart approach:

1. **Bring USD** and exchange a small amount upon arrival in Bali.
2. **Or exchange at authorized money changers in Bali**.
3. **Avoid small roadside exchange booths** (hotbeds for scams, countless tourists have been ripped off)

- 5 days: Bring USD 500-700 is enough
- 7 days: Bring USD 700-1000
- (Unless you plan to eat at Michelin-starred restaurants or stay in five-star hotels every day)

💡 **Most common pitfall for beginners**: "I'll bring Taiwanese dollars to Bali!" Don't be naive. Not only is it hard to find shops in Bali that accept Taiwanese dollars, but even if someone does, the exchange rate will be ridiculously bad.

### 6. Packing Checklist: What to Bring and What Not to Bring

**Essential items (you'll actually use them):**

- Sunscreen (SPF 50+ minimum)
- Mosquito repellent (lotion works better than spray)
- Light rain gear (frequent showers during rainy season)
- Basic medications (cold medicine, stomach medicine, anti-diarrheal)
- Light jacket (air conditioning indoors can be cold)
- Passport copy (in case of loss)

**Don't Bring (You Can Buy These in Bali, and They're Cheap):**

- Face masks (cheap jelly masks are everywhere)
- Cosmetics (plenty of affordable brands)
- Daily necessities (hotels provide them, and supermarkets have them too)

**Prohibited Items (Customs Will Confiscate or Fine):**

- Anything related to drugs
- Meat products (even instant noodles from Taiwan need to be checked for ingredients)
- Alcoholic beverages exceeding 5 liters
- Products made from wild animals

### 8. Voltage & Plugs: 220V, Bring the Right Adapter

Bali uses **220V voltage**, while Taiwan uses 110V.

**Smart Tips:**

- Bring a universal adapter (get one that supports multiple countries)
- Or buy one locally in Bali (usually the cheapest option)

Most laptop and phone chargers support automatic 110-240V conversion (check if it’s written on the charger), but always confirm for hair dryers and curling irons.

### 9. How Many Days Are Ideal? First-Timers Recommend 5-7 Days

**Less than 3 days:** Only enough time to explore the south (Kuta, Seminyak), which is a pity
**5-7 days:** South + Ubud, experiencing 60% of Bali
**10+ days:** Enough to explore the east, west, and outer islands

For first-timers, I recommend a **6-day 5-night** trip — it's the perfect duration without feeling rushed.

## ✈️ Arriving in Bali

### 14. Correct Process from Landing to Exiting the Airport

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-05/1778988530785-_5.png)

**Don’t wander around; follow these steps:**

1. **Follow the 'Arrival' signs** (not Departure)
2. **E-Visa holders use the automated gates** (fast track)
3. **Visa on Arrival holders queue at the Visa on Arrival counter** (to get your visa)
4. **Pass through immigration** (get your passport stamped)
5. **Collect your luggage at the carousel** (look for your flight number)
6. **Fill out the AIAC customs declaration form** (online only)
7. **Pass through customs** (usually just a quick glance)
8. **Pay the Bali tourism tax** (if not paid in advance)

**The whole process takes 30-60 minutes if there’s no queue**, so don’t stress.

### 16. Stomach Issues and Bali Belly

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

**The most common problem in Bali is Bali Belly (traveler's diarrhea).**

**Prevention is better than cure:**

- Only drink bottled water (cheap and widely available in supermarkets)
- Avoid raw foods from street vendors (be cautious with fresh salads and fruit platters)
- High-temperature cooked foods are generally safe

**If you do get sick:**

- Bring anti-diarrheal medication (safer to bring from Taiwan)
- Drink electrolyte beverages (available in Bali supermarkets)
- Rest for half a day; symptoms usually improve within a day

## 💡 Practical Local Knowledge

### 17. How to Convert Currency?

**Quick Conversion Formula:** Remove the last three zeros from the Indonesian Rupiah amount, then multiply by 2 to get an approximate value in New Taiwan Dollars.

**Examples:**

- 100,000 IDR → 100 × 2 = NT$200
- 500,000 IDR → 500 × 2 = NT$1,000
- 1,000,000 IDR → 1000 × 2 = NT$2,000

💡 With this formula, you won't be shocked by those '5-digit numbers' when ordering food.

### 18. How to Tip?

[https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY](https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY)

**Indonesia doesn't have a 'mandatory tipping' culture, but 'gratitude tips' are warmly welcomed.**

- **Hotel Bellboys:** USD 1-2 or 20,000 IDR
- **SPA Masseuses:** 10% of the bill (or simply give 20,000~50,000)
- **Restaurant Waitstaff:** 10% of the bill (usually, a 4-10% service charge is already added, so this can be omitted)
- **Taxi Drivers:** Round up (give the change if you have it, otherwise, it's fine)
- **Tour Guides/Private Drivers:** 50,000~100,000 per day

**The simplest rule**: Always tip when someone helps you. Not much, but it's greatly appreciated.

### 23. Cultural Taboos and Respect

**Religious Sensitive Areas:**

- Wear a sarong when entering temples (usually provided for free)
- Do not touch or climb on sacred statues
- Traditionally, women should not enter temples during menstruation (though most temples do not check)
- If you see a ritual ceremony, take photos from a distance and do not disturb

**Daily Etiquette:**

- Use your right hand for eating and handing things
- Do not point your feet at others
- Remove shoes when entering homes
- Show respect to elders

**Dress Code:**

- Bikinis are fine at the beach
- Wear clothing that covers shoulders and knees when entering temples or villages
- Polo shirts are acceptable for men in formal settings

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: How many days are ideal for a first trip to Bali?

**A:** 5-7 days is the best choice for beginners. Less than 3 days will feel rushed, limiting you to the southern area; more than 10 days are needed to explore the entire island. **6 days and 5 nights** allows you to experience both the south (beaches, shopping) and the north (culture, mountains) comfortably without feeling overly fatigued.

### Q: What’s the budget for a Bali free-and-easy trip?

**A:** The per person budget for 6 days and 5 nights can range from USD$200 to USD$2,000. We recommend using the tool below:

👉 [**Detailed Budget Calculator**](/bali-budget-calculator/)

### Q: Can I use Taiwanese dollars in Bali?

**A:** No. Bali only uses **Indonesian Rupiah (IDR)**. Taiwanese dollars are not accepted locally, and it's difficult to find places to exchange them. We recommend bringing US dollars to Bali and exchanging them for Rupiah there.

### Q: Is Bali safe? Will I get robbed or scammed?

**A:** Overall, Bali is safer than many cities in Southeast Asia. Robberies are rare, and scams mainly target careless tourists (e.g., black market currency exchange, fake drivers). As long as you follow basic safety tips—don’t walk alone in secluded alleys at night, avoid black market currency exchange, verify driver identities—you’ll likely have no issues.

### Q: Can I visit Bali during the rainy season?

**A:** Absolutely. During the rainy season (November-March), it’s usually **just 1-2 hours of afternoon showers**, and attractions remain open. In fact, the rainy season has advantages: fewer tourists, hotels are 40-50% cheaper, rice terraces are at their greenest, and Spas are more relaxing. Many experienced travelers specifically choose the rainy season. The downside is unpredictable mountain weather and potential cancellations of some outdoor activities.

👉 [**Complete Rainy Season Guide**](/blog/2024-01-06-6598c6fffd89780001047d76/)

### Q: Do I need to apply for a visa before my first trip to Bali? Can I get a visa on arrival?

**A:** There are two options for Taiwanese passport holders:

1. **eVOA Electronic Visa** (apply online 2-3 days before departure, **most recommended**)
2. **Visa on Arrival** (apply at the airport, around USD$39)

The advantage of the eVOA is that you can go directly through the automatic gates upon arrival (no need to wait in the 45-minute manual queue).

Hong Kong, Singapore, and Malaysia enjoy 30-day visa-free entry.

### Q: Is tipping expected in Bali? How should I tip?

**A:** Indonesia doesn't have a mandatory tipping culture, but tipping will make service staff very happy. Suggested tipping amounts:

- Porter: 20,000 IDR
- Spa massage: 50,000 IDR
- Restaurant: No need to tip extra if service charge is included
- Taxi: Round up the fare
- Private driver/guide: 50,000\~100,000 IDR per day

The key is "tip those who help you" — the amount doesn't need to be large, attitude is most important.

### Q: Where's the best area to stay for first-time visitors to Bali?

**A:** It depends on your travel style:

- **Kuta**: Lively, affordable, popular with backpackers
- **Seminyak**: Boutiques, upscale dining, Instagram hotspots
- **Canggu**: Cafes, youthful vibe, surfing
- **Ubud**: Culture, wellness, away from the crowds
- **Nusa Dua**: Five-star hotels, family-friendly, resorts
- **Jimbaran**: Sunsets, seafood, romantic dinners

For beginners, we recommend combining **Seminyak + Ubud** to experience both the beach and culture.
