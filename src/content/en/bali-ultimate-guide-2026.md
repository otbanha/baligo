## 🛂 Must-Do Before Departure
### 1. Passport & Visa: Confirm 2 Months in Advance
Your passport must have at least **6 months of validity remaining**. Indonesian customs is strict about this—even one day past the requirement and you'll be turned away.

**Three visa options:**
- **Visa-Free**: Taiwan passport holders can stay up to 30 days without a visa (but must submit an online declaration)
- **eVOA (Electronic Visa on Arrival)**: Apply online before departure; approval takes about 2-3 business days. Valid for 30 days. **Highly recommended for first-timers**
- **VoA (Visa on Arrival)**: Apply upon arrival at Bali; expect a 15- to 30-minute wait in line

**I strongly recommend the eVOA** because:
- You can use the automated gate upon arrival (no 30-minute queue)
- It's only $5 more expensive, but saves you tons of time
- Customs policies are tightening in 2026; having it ready in advance avoids any hassle

### 1. 護照與簽證：提前2個月確認

你的護照至少要剩**6個月以上的有效期**。印尼海關對這個要求很嚴格，過期一天都會被擋。

**簽證有三種選擇：**

### 2. Travel Insurance: Seriously, Don't Skip It

Medical standards in Bali aren't as good as in Taiwan. If you end up hospitalized locally, it could cost you between USD$16,000 and USD$32,000. Plus, Bali has quite a few mosquito-borne diseases.

**What your insurance should cover:**

- Accidental injury (common for hiking and water sports)
- Medical expenses (including overseas hospitalization)
- Trip interruption / lost luggage

### 3. Flight Planning: Avoid Peak Seasons to Save 1/3 of Your Budget

**When is it most expensive?**

- Summer and winter breaks (July, August)
- Chinese New Year (late January to February)
- Christmas holidays (mid-December to early January)
- Qingming and Labor Day long weekends (early April)

**When is it cheap?**

- May to June (end of rainy season, before peak season)
- September to October (just after peak season)
- November (start of rainy season)

For the best deals, book your flight **3–6 weeks in advance**. Skip the first morning flights and aim for departures between 2–5 PM — they're often 20–30% cheaper.

💡 **Common beginner mistake**: thinking budget airlines are always cheaper. In reality, once you add luggage and seat selection fees, they may not beat traditional carriers. Direct flights on STARLUX, China Airlines, or EVA Air can actually be a better deal.

### 4. Bali Tourist Levy

Starting in 2024, every foreign visitor to Bali must pay a tourist levy of 150,000 Indonesian Rupiah.

**How to pay?**
- Easiest: Pay online before departure (balilevy.baliprov.go.id)
- Next best: Pay at the airport upon arrival (dedicated counters available)
- Not recommended: Wait until you reach attractions, as random checks may occur

Make sure to keep your receipt — you may be asked to show it at certain attractions.

## ✈️ Arriving in Bali

### 14. The Right Way to Get from the Plane to the Airport Exit

![14. 下飛機到出機場的正確流程](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-05/1778988530785-_5.png)

**Don't wander off—follow this order:**

1. **Follow the 'Arrival' signs** (not Departure)
2. **E-visa holders: use the automated gates** (fast track)
3. **Visa-on-arrival holders: queue at the Visa on Arrival counter** (to get your visa)
4. **Pass through immigration** (get your passport stamped)
5. **Collect your luggage at the carousel** (look for your flight number)
6. **Fill out the AIAC customs declaration form** (online only)
7. **Pass through customs** (usually just a quick glance)
8. **Pay the Bali tourism tax** (if you haven't paid it in advance)

**The whole process takes at most 30–60 minutes if there's no queue**, so no need to stress.

### 16. Stomach Issues and Bali Belly

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

**The most common problem in Bali is Bali Belly.**

**Prevention is better than cure:**

- Only drink bottled water (sold cheaply everywhere in supermarkets)
- Avoid raw street food (be cautious with fresh salads and fruit platters)
- Well-cooked food is generally safe

**If you do get it:**

- Bring anti-diarrhea medication (better to bring from home)
- Drink electrolyte beverages (available at Bali supermarkets)
- Rest for half a day; it usually clears up within 24 hours

## 💡 Local Tips & Know-How

### 17. How to Convert Prices?

**Quick conversion trick**: Remove the last three zeros from the Indonesian rupiah amount, then multiply by 2 to get an approximate value in New Taiwan Dollars.

**Examples:**

- 100,000 IDR → 100 × 2 = NT$200
- 500,000 IDR → 500 × 2 = NT$1,000
- 1,000,000 IDR → 1000 × 2 = NT$2,000

💡 Use this trick, and you won't be shocked by a five-digit price tag when ordering.

### 18. How to Tip?

[https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY](https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY)

**Indonesia doesn't have a mandatory tipping culture, but tips as a token of gratitude are warmly welcomed.**

- **Hotel Bellhop**: USD 1–2 or 20,000 IDR
- **SPA Massage Therapist**: 10% of the service fee (or directly give 20,000–50,000 IDR)
- **Restaurant Server**: 10% of the bill (often a 4–10% service charge is already included, so you can skip)
- **Taxi**: Round up to the nearest 5,000 IDR (if there's change, leave it; if not, no need)
- **Guide/Private Driver**: 50,000–100,000 IDR per day

**The Golden Rule**: Whenever someone does something for you, give a little tip. It doesn't have to be much, but it's always appreciated.

### 23. Cultural Taboos & Respect

**Religious Sensitivities:**

- Wear a sarong when entering temples (usually provided for free)
- Do not touch or climb on sacred statues
- Traditionally, women on their period should not enter temples (though most temples don’t check)
- If you see a ceremony in progress, take photos from a distance and avoid disturbing it

**Everyday Etiquette:**

- Use your right hand for eating and handing things over
- Don’t point your feet at others
- Remove shoes before entering homes
- Show respect to elders

**Dress Code:**

- Bikinis are fine on the beach
- Cover shoulders and knees when entering temples or villages
- For formal occasions, a polo shirt is acceptable for men

### Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

## Q: How many days is best for a first trip to Bali?

**A:** 5–7 days is the best choice for beginners. Less than 3 days is too rushed—you can only explore the south; 10 days or more is needed to see the whole island. **6 days and 5 nights** lets you experience the south (beaches, shopping) plus the north (culture, mountains), with the most comfortable pacing and minimal fatigue.

## Q: Can I use New Taiwan Dollars in Bali?

**A:** No. Bali only uses **Indonesian Rupiah (IDR)**. New Taiwan Dollars are not accepted anywhere, and it's hard to find exchange points. It's best to bring US dollars and exchange them for rupiah once you're in Bali.

## Q: Is Bali safe? Are there robberies or scams?

**A:** Overall, Bali is safer than many Southeast Asian cities. Robberies are rare, and scams mainly target unwary tourists (like black market money changers or fake drivers). As long as you follow basic safety tips—don't walk alone in dark alleys at night, avoid street money changers, and verify driver IDs—you'll almost certainly have no issues.

## Q: Can I visit Bali during the rainy season?

**A:** Absolutely. The rainy season (November to March) usually just means **1–2 hours of afternoon showers**, and attractions remain open. In fact, the rainy season has its perks: fewer tourists, hotels 40–50% cheaper, the greenest rice terraces, and more relaxing spa experiences. Many seasoned travelers specifically choose to go during this time. The downsides are unpredictable mountain weather and the possibility of some outdoor activities being canceled on short notice.

👉 [**Complete Rainy Season Guide**](/en/blog/2026-bali-indoor-activities/)

## Q: Do I need a visa for my first trip to Bali? Can I get a visa on arrival?

**A:** Taiwanese passport holders have two options:

1. **eVOA (Electronic Visa on Arrival)** – Apply online 2–3 days before departure (**highly recommended**)
2. **Visa on Arrival** – Get it at the airport (around USD$39)

The advantage of the eVOA is that you can go straight to the automated gate upon arrival (no need to queue 45 minutes at the manual counter).

Hong Kong, Singapore, and Malaysia passport holders get 30 days visa-free.

## Q: Do I need to tip in Bali? How much?

**A:** Tipping is not mandatory in Indonesia, but it’s always appreciated. Here’s a rough guide:

- Bellhop: 20,000 IDR
- SPA massage: 50,000 IDR
- Restaurants: If service charge is already included, no need to tip extra
- Taxi: Round up to the nearest 5,000 or 10,000 IDR
- Private driver/guide: 50,000–100,000 IDR per day

The key is to tip anyone who helps you out—amounts don’t need to be big, but attitude matters most.

## Q: Which area is best to stay in Bali for first-timers?

**A:** It depends on your travel style:

- **Kuta**: Lively, budget-friendly, backpacker hub
- **Seminyak**: Boutique shops, upscale dining, Instagram-worthy spots
- **Canggu**: Cafés, youthful vibe, surfing
- **Ubud**: Culture, wellness, away from the crowds
- **Nusa Dua**: Five-star hotels, family-friendly, resorts
- **Jimbaran**: Sunsets, seafood, romantic dates

For first-timers, the top recommendation is a **Seminyak + Ubud** combo, giving you both beach and culture.
