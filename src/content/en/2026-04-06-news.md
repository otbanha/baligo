**Bali** is currently in the transitional period between the dry and wet seasons (Peralihan). Although the island is gradually entering the dry season, the Indonesian Meteorological Agency (BMKG) has issued a **yellow alert** today, warning areas including Kuta and Ubud of a **45% - 60%** chance of brief thunderstorms, strong winds, and lightning risks in the afternoon.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775432045241-3ceb1d6524f69d9f46e44e81f7d6e105.jpg)

In terms of temperature, the daytime high is around **31°C**, with the humidity reaching 80%, making it feel quite muggy. At night, it drops to a comfortable **24°C**. The southern coastal areas (Uluwatu, Nusa Dua) are influenced by the Australian monsoon, with winds shifting to east-southeast and wave heights of about 1-2 meters. The sea conditions are generally stable, but it is advised to stay away from thunderstorm areas. Overall, the morning to noon period is the golden time for outdoor activities, and it is recommended to carry light rain gear and apply sunscreen.

Due to the Middle East conflict and international airspace turbulence, experts warn that Bali and Thailand will be the regions most affected by increased long-haul flight costs. Airlines have already started adjusting routes or adding fuel surcharges.

Culturally, today coincides with a challenging day in the Balinese calendar, considered unsuitable for weddings or groundbreaking ceremonies. Last Saturday marked the end of **Saraswati Day**, and schools and temples across the island are still immersed in the atmosphere of academic worship. On April 8th, **Pagerwesi** Day will be celebrated, with residents strengthening spiritual protection and inner balance rituals. In terms of security, a British man wanted by Interpol was arrested at the airport last week and is currently undergoing extradition proceedings. The Tourism Board has announced a target of attracting 6.63 million international tourists by 2026, shifting focus to promoting high-quality tourism.
