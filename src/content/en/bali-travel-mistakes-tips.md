These mistakes can not only affect the travel experience but also waste valuable time and money. Below are 18 common errors and practical tips to help you enjoy a more pleasant and stress-free journey.

## Itinerary Planning

### 1. Overpacking Your Schedule

> **Mistake**: Many travelers underestimate the size of Bali and try to visit all the attractions in a short time, resulting in a rushed itinerary that doesn’t allow them to fully enjoy each location.

Although Bali is an island, it’s actually quite large and diverse, offering everything from southern beaches to northern mountains, each with its unique charm. When planning your itinerary, choose areas based on your stay duration:

- **One week or less**: Pick 1-2 main accommodation bases to minimize moving around.
- **Two weeks**: Cover 3-4 accommodation bases, allowing moderate travel time.
- **Three weeks or more**: Plan up to 5-6 locations, but avoid making the schedule too overwhelming.

Try to reduce frequent changes in accommodation to avoid wasting time packing and moving, which can leave you exhausted.

### 3. Scheduling Island Hopping at the End

**Mistake**: Many travelers plan their trips to the Nusa Islands (Nusa Lembongan, Nusa Ceningan, Nusa Penida) or the Gili Islands towards the end of their journey. However, if bad weather causes ferry cancellations, it could disrupt your return plans.

To avoid delays in your return due to weather-related ferry cancellations, it’s advisable to schedule these island visits during the middle or early part of your trip. This way, any disruptions won’t affect your overall travel plans.

## Accommodation and Transportation

### 5. Staying in Kuta

> **Mistake**: Kuta is known for its vibrant nightlife, but it's overly commercialized, crowded, and noisy, making it less ideal for travelers seeking relaxation.

Choose alternative accommodation areas based on your needs:

- **[Nusa Dua](/blog/nusa-dua-guide/)**: If you love five-star resorts and private beaches, this is the perfect choice.

### 6. Risks of Renting a Car/Motorcycle for Self-Driving

> **Mistake**: Renting a motorcycle is convenient and cost-effective

## Safety and Health

To ensure your Bali trip is safe and comfortable, it’s important to avoid common health and safety pitfalls. Additionally, respecting local culture and eco-tourism ethics can help you connect more deeply with Bali. Below are some common mistakes and suggestions regarding safety, health, and cultural etiquette.

### 7. Bank Card Security

**Mistake**: Using credit cards for large purchases without considering potential fraud risks.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0648b8fa6386a0758be5f8b1fa666c66.jpg)

Some areas in Bali may pose a risk of credit card fraud, so it's recommended to protect your funds using the following methods:

- **Use a debit card with limited funds**: Keep the balance on your debit card below USD$300 to minimize losses from fraud.
- **Online bank transfers**: When making large payments or purchases, transfer additional funds via online banking to prevent having too much money on the card.
- **Use electronic payments**: Whenever possible, use e-wallets like Gojek to reduce the use of physical cards.

### 8. Mosquito Protection

**Mistake**: Ignoring the threat of mosquitoes

Dengue fever is not uncommon in Bali, so mosquito protection is crucial. Here are some essential items and tips:

- **Insect repellent**: Carry insect repellent with you and use it frequently, especially during outdoor activities.
- **Wear long sleeves and pants**: Mosquitoes are most active from evening to night, so it's advisable to wear protective clothing.
- **Secure doors and windows**: Ensure your accommodation's doors and windows are tightly closed, or use mosquito nets to prevent mosquitoes from entering.
- **Be aware of dengue fever risks**: If you feel unwell, such as having a fever or muscle pain, seek medical attention immediately to check for dengue fever.

### 9. Dealing with the Rainy Season

> **Mistake**: Ignoring Bali's rapid weather changes during the rainy season and failing to prepare accordingly.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_c86655f684007466ca064fac6b244c4f.png)

Bali's rainy season typically occurs from November to April. While it doesn't rain every day, the weather changes quickly, often shifting suddenly from sunny to heavy downpours.

- **Carry a raincoat**: A lightweight raincoat is more practical than an umbrella, as heavy rains come suddenly and are often accompanied by strong winds.
- **Plan morning activities**: The weather is usually more stable in the morning, so it's advisable to schedule outdoor activities before noon to avoid sudden afternoon showers.

### 10. Monkey Safety Guidelines

https://youtube.com/shorts/tpPWsvsH-Eo

> **Mistake**: Ignoring the potential dangers of monkeys and carrying valuables or food while interacting with them.

In some areas of Bali, such as Uluwatu (Uluwatu Temple) and the Ubud Monkey Forest, monkeys are common 'residents.' While they may appear cute, they can be aggressive, especially when interested in valuables.

- **Do not carry valuables or food**: Monkeys are particularly attracted to food and shiny objects, so avoid bringing these items with you.
- **Avoid eye contact**: Making eye contact with monkeys can be extremely dangerous, as some may perceive it as a challenge.
- **Protect glasses and phones**: Monkeys often snatch items like glasses and phones from tourists, so keep them secure in monkey areas.
- **Maintain distance**: While monkeys are often seen as friendly, remember they are wild animals and keep a safe distance.

## Cultural Etiquette

### 11. Respect Local Culture

> **Mistake**: Ignoring local religious and cultural etiquette, leading to disrespectful behavior.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_2051bf25b8c56908609a59e3c8151f2a.jpg)

Bali is a cultural hub of Hinduism, and locals place great importance on religious rituals and traditions. Respecting their customs is crucial for visitors. Here are some key cultural etiquettes:

- **Temple Dress Code**: When entering temples, dress modestly and wear a sarong (temples usually offer free rentals).
- **Avoid Stepping on Offerings**: On Bali's streets, you'll often see small basket-like offerings. These are daily offerings made by locals, so be careful not to step on them.

Further reading: [Understanding Bali's Offering Rituals: Canang Sari](/blog/bali-canang-sari-explained/)

- **Taboo Behaviors**: Do not touch someone's head, as it is considered disrespectful in local culture. Additionally, using your left hand to give or receive items is seen as impolite.
- **Remove Shoes Indoors**: When entering a local's home or certain shops, remove your shoes as a sign of respect.
- **Learn Basic Greetings**: Learning a few simple phrases in the local language, such as "thank you," can help you build rapport with locals.
- - Indonesian "Thank you": **Terima kasih**
    - Balinese "Thank you": **Suksma**

### 12. Ethical Animal Tourism

> **Mistake**: Participating in unethical animal tourism activities that harm animals.

Bali offers many animal-related tourist activities, but many involve inhumane treatment of animals, sparking significant controversy. To protect wildlife, it's recommended to avoid the following activities:

- **Zoos and Marine Parks**: Many animals in zoos and marine parks are kept in confined spaces, unable to move freely.
- **Elephant Parks**: Some parks offer elephant rides, but these elephants are often mistreated and in poor health. Bali has banned elephant riding across the island starting in 2026.
- **Wildlife Performances**: Wild animals in performances are often subjected to cruel training to perform unnatural behaviors.

Support activities that respect animals' natural habitats, such as:

- **Lovina Dolphin Watching**: Here, dolphins roam freely in open waters rather than being confined to aquariums.
- **Sea Turtle Watching in Natural Habitats**: Join organizations or activities that support wildlife conservation to observe sea turtles in the wild without interfering with their natural lives.

## **Practical Tips**

When traveling in Bali, adequate preparation and understanding of local conditions can make your journey more enjoyable and help avoid potential hassles. Below are some practical tips for traveling in Bali to help you have a smoother vacation.

### 13. Currency Exchange

>   **Mistake**: Exchanging cash before departure or at the airport.

https://youtu.be/KPGb6MmwfZw

- **Exchange After Arrival**: Refer to the "[Bali Currency Exchange Guide](/blog/bali-currency-exchange-guide/)" to learn how and where to exchange currency.
- **Avoid Carrying Large Amounts of Cash**: Carrying too much cash increases risks; using a debit card or electronic payments is safer.

### 14. Bargaining Tips

>   **Mistake**: Accepting the first offer at markets or street vendors without understanding local bargaining culture.

- **Negotiable Prices for Non-Fixed Items**: In many open-air markets and small stalls in Bali, prices are not fixed, and you can negotiate appropriately.
- **Avoid Accepting the First Offer**: Sellers often quote higher prices; try to lower the price reasonably and purchase once both parties agree.
- **Support the Local Economy**: While bargaining is common, avoid excessive haggling to respect local vendors and support their livelihoods.

### 15. Weather Awareness

>   **Mistake**: Ignoring Bali's weather changes and failing to check the forecast in advance.

- **Follow BMKG (Bali Meteorology, Climatology, and Geophysics Agency)** on their [official Instagram account](https://www.instagram.com/infobmkg/): They post daily English weather forecasts for tourists.
- **Use the Accuweather App**: Often provides more accurate predictions than Google.
- **Check weather on Windy.com**: Ideal for viewing Bali's weather forecasts, especially for wave and wind speed information.
- **Morning activities are best**: Bali's weather is usually more stable in the morning, making it a great time for outdoor activities.
- **Afternoons are prone to rain**: Especially during the rainy season, brief showers often occur in the afternoon, so it's recommended to schedule indoor activities or rest during this time.

### 16. Packing Tips

>   **Mistake**: Bringing too many unnecessary clothes or ignoring the local weather characteristics.

Based on Bali's climate and activity needs, here are the essentials:

- **Lightweight and breathable clothing**: Since Bali is hot and humid, pack thin, breathable outfits to stay comfortable in the heat.
- **Swimwear**: Whether or not you plan to stay near the beach, swimwear is a must—Bali has countless beautiful beaches and pools to enjoy.
- **Simple flats or sandals**: Most activities in Bali, like exploring markets, strolling on the beach, or visiting temples, can be easily done in flats or sandals.
- **Light jacket for mountainous areas**: If you plan to visit Ubud or Munduk, evenings can be cooler, so a light jacket will keep you comfortable.

### 17. Motorcycle Safety

https://youtu.be/lIV-T2jYhVk

> **Mistake**: Neglecting motorcycle safety measures.

- **Always Wear a Helmet**: Whether you're driving yourself or riding as a passenger, wearing a helmet is mandatory. It's not just the law—it's a crucial safety measure.
- **Request a Spare Helmet When Booking Rides**: When using ride-hailing services like Gojek or Grab for motorcycle rides, always ask the driver to provide a helmet.
- **Follow Traffic Rules**: The road conditions and traffic regulations in Bali may differ from those in your home country. Drive cautiously and adhere to local traffic laws.

> Further Reading: [Essential for Short Trips! Hailing Motorcycle Taxis with Apps, Saving Time and Effort!](/blog/bali-travel-app-motorcycle-taxi/)

### 18. Ignoring Local Food Safety

> **Mistake**: Not paying attention to food hygiene and casually eating at roadside stalls or unclean places.

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

- **Choose Clean Restaurants or Well-Reviewed Street Stalls**: Bali offers many delicious local snacks and restaurants, but not all places meet hygiene standards. Opt for restaurants with good cleanliness or stalls that come recommended.
- **Be Mindful of Drinking Water**: Never drink tap water directly. Choose bottled water or ensure the restaurant provides safe drinking water.
- **Avoid Raw or Undercooked Food**: Especially in remote areas or unfamiliar restaurants, raw and undercooked food can pose a risk of stomach discomfort. Prioritize fully cooked meals.
- **Carry Stomach Medicine**: If you have a sensitive stomach, carry common medications like anti-diarrheal pills or probiotics for emergencies.
