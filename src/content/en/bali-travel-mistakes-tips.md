After multiple explorations and observations, we've noticed that tourists often make certain mistakes while traveling in Bali. These mistakes not only affect the travel experience but can also waste valuable time and money. Here are 18 common errors and practical tips to help you enjoy a more pleasant and worry-free journey.

## Itinerary Planning

### 1. Overly Rushed Itinerary

> **Mistake**: Many tourists underestimate the size of Bali and try to visit all the attractions in a short period, resulting in an overly rushed itinerary that doesn't allow them to fully enjoy each location.

Although Bali is an island, it is actually quite large and diverse, offering everything from southern beaches to northern mountains, each with its unique charm. When planning your itinerary, consider choosing suitable areas based on your stay duration:

- **One week**: Select 1-2 main accommodation bases to minimize frequent moves.
- **Two weeks**: Cover 3-4 accommodation bases, allowing moderate time for travel.
- **Three weeks or more**: Plan up to 5-6 locations, but be careful not to overload your schedule.

Try to reduce frequent changes in accommodation to avoid wasting time packing and moving, which can leave you exhausted.

### 3. Saving Island Hopping for the End

**Mistake**: Many travelers schedule trips to the Nusa Islands (Nusa Lembongan, Nusa Ceningan, Nusa Penida) or the Gili Islands towards the end of their journey. However, bad weather leading to canceled boat trips could disrupt your return plans.

To avoid delays in your return due to weather-related boat cancellations, it's advisable to plan these island-hopping adventures for the middle or early part of your trip. This way, any potential delays won't affect your overall travel schedule.

>   **Mistake**: Kuta is known for its vibrant nightlife, but it's overly commercialized, crowded, and noisy, making it less ideal for travelers seeking relaxation.

> **Mistake**: Renting a motorcycle is convenient and cost-effective

## Safety and Health

To make your Bali trip safer and more comfortable, it’s crucial to avoid common health and safety pitfalls. Additionally, respecting local culture and eco-tourism ethics can help you connect more deeply with Bali. Below are some common mistakes and suggestions regarding safety, health, and cultural etiquette.

### 7. Bank Card Security

**Mistake**: Making large purchases with a credit card without considering the risk of fraud.

![7. Bank Card Security](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0648b8fa6386a0758be5f8b1fa666c66.jpg)

Some areas in Bali may pose a risk of credit card fraud, so it's recommended to protect your funds using the following methods:

- **Use a debit card with limited funds**: Keep the balance on your debit card below USD$300 to minimize potential losses from fraud.
- **Online bank transfers**: For large payments or purchases, transfer additional funds via online banking to prevent having too much money on the card.
- **Use electronic payments**: Whenever possible, use e-wallets like Gojek to reduce the use of physical cards.

### 8. Mosquito Protection

**Mistake**: Ignoring the threat of mosquitoes

Dengue fever is not uncommon in Bali, so mosquito protection is crucial. Here are some essential items and recommendations:

- **Mosquito repellent**: Carry mosquito repellent with you and use it frequently, especially during outdoor activities.
- **Wear long sleeves and pants**: Mosquitoes are most active from dusk to night, so it's advisable to wear protective clothing.
- **Secure doors and windows**: Ensure that your accommodation's doors and windows are tightly closed, or use mosquito nets to prevent mosquitoes from entering.
- **Be aware of dengue fever risk**: If you feel unwell, such as having a fever or muscle pain, seek medical attention immediately to check for dengue fever.

### 9. Dealing with the Rainy Season

>   **Mistake**: Ignoring the rapid weather changes during Bali's rainy season and failing to prepare accordingly.

Bali's rainy season typically occurs from November to April. While it doesn't rain every day, the weather changes quickly, often shifting suddenly from sunny to heavy rain.

- **Carry a raincoat**: A lightweight raincoat is more practical than an umbrella, as the rain comes suddenly and is often accompanied by strong winds.
- **Plan morning activities**: The weather is usually more stable in the morning, so it's advisable to schedule outdoor activities before noon to avoid sudden afternoon showers.

### 10. Monkey Safety Guidelines

https://youtube.com/shorts/tpPWsvsH-Eo

>   **Mistake**: Ignoring the potential dangers of monkeys and carrying valuables or food while interacting with them.

In certain areas of Bali, such as Uluwatu (Uluwatu Temple) and the Monkey Forest in Ubud, monkeys are common "residents." While they may appear cute, they can be aggressive, especially when interested in valuables.

- **Do not carry valuables or food**: Monkeys are particularly interested in food and shiny objects, so avoid bringing these items with you.
- **Do not make eye contact**: Making eye contact with monkeys is highly dangerous, as some may perceive it as a challenge.
- **Protect your glasses and phone**: Monkeys often snatch items like glasses and phones from tourists, so keep them secure in monkey-populated areas.
- **Maintain distance**: While monkeys are often seen as friendly, remember they are wild animals and keep a safe distance.

## Cultural Etiquette

### 11. Respect Local Culture

> **Mistake**: Ignoring local religious and cultural etiquette, leading to disrespectful behavior.

![11. Respect Local Culture](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_2051bf25b8c56908609a59e3c8151f2a.jpg)

Bali is a cultural hub of Hinduism, where locals place great importance on religious rituals and traditions. Respecting their customs is especially important for visitors. Here are some key cultural etiquettes:

- **Temple Dress Code**: When entering temples, dress modestly and wear a sarong (temples usually offer free rentals).
- **Avoid Stepping on Offerings**: On the streets of Bali, you’ll often see small basket-like offerings. These are daily offerings made by locals, so be sure to avoid stepping on them.

Further Reading: [Understanding Bali’s Offering Rituals: Canang Sari](/en/blog/bali-canang-sari-explained/)

- **Taboo Behaviors**: Do not touch someone’s head, as it is considered disrespectful in local culture. Additionally, using your left hand to pass items is seen as impolite.
- **Remove Shoes Indoors**: When entering locals’ homes or certain shops, remove your shoes as a sign of respect.
- **Learn Basic Greetings**: Learning a few simple phrases in the local language, such as "thank you," will help you connect more warmly with locals.
  - Indonesian "Thank you": **Terima kasih**
  - Balinese "Thank you": **Suksma**

### 12. Ethical Animal Tourism

> **Mistake**: Participating in unethical animal tourism activities that harm animals.

Bali offers many animal-related tourist activities, but many involve inhumane treatment of animals, sparking significant controversy. To protect wildlife, it’s recommended to avoid the following activities:

- **Zoos and Marine Parks**: Many animals in zoos and marine parks are confined to small spaces, unable to move freely.
- **Elephant Parks**: Some parks offer elephant rides, but these elephants are often mistreated and in poor health. Bali has banned elephant riding across the island starting in 2026.
- **Wildlife Performances**: Wild animals in performances are often subjected to cruel training to perform unnatural behaviors.

Support activities that respect animals' natural habitats, such as:

- **Lovina Dolphin Watching**: Here, dolphins roam freely in open waters rather than being confined to aquariums.
- **Natural Habitat Turtle Watching**: Join organizations or activities that support wildlife conservation to observe wild turtles without interfering with their natural lives.

## **Practical Tips**

When traveling in Bali, being well-prepared and understanding local conditions can make your journey more enjoyable and help avoid potential hassles. Below are some practical tips for traveling in Bali to ensure a smoother vacation.

### 13. Currency Exchange

>   **Mistake**: Exchanging cash before departure or at the airport.

https://youtu.be/KPGb6MmwfZw

- **Exchange Upon Arrival**: Refer to the "[Bali Currency Exchange Guide](/en/blog/bali-currency-exchange-guide/)" to learn how and where to exchange currency.
- **Avoid Carrying Large Amounts of Cash**: Carrying too much cash increases risks; using debit cards or electronic payments is safer.

### 14. Bargaining Tips

>   **Mistake**: Accepting the first price offered at markets or street vendors without understanding local bargaining culture.

- **Negotiable Prices**: In many open-air markets and small stalls in Bali, prices are not fixed, and you can bargain appropriately.
- **Avoid Accepting the First Offer**: Sellers often quote higher prices; try to negotiate a lower price and purchase only when both parties are satisfied.
- **Support the Local Economy**: While bargaining is common, avoid excessively lowballing to respect local vendors and support their livelihoods.

### 15. Weather Awareness

> **Mistake**: Ignoring Bali's weather changes and not checking the forecast in advance.

- **Follow BMKG's (Bali Meteorology, Climatology, and Geophysics Agency)** [official Instagram account](https://www.instagram.com/infobmkg/): They post daily English weather forecasts for tourists.
- **Use the Accuweather App**: Often provides more accurate predictions than Google.
- **Check weather on Windy.com**: Great for Bali's weather forecasts, especially for wave and wind speed information.
- **Morning activities are ideal**: Bali's weather is usually more stable in the morning, making it the best time for outdoor activities.
- **Afternoons are prone to rain**: Especially during the rainy season, brief showers are common in the afternoon, so it's recommended to schedule indoor activities or rest during this time.

### 16. Packing Tips

> **Mistake**: Bringing too many unnecessary clothes or ignoring the local weather characteristics.

Based on Bali's climate and activity needs, here are the essentials:

- **Lightweight and breathable clothing**: Due to Bali's hot and humid climate, pack light and airy outfits to stay comfortable in the heat.
- **Swimwear**: Whether or not you plan to stay near the beach, swimwear is a must, as Bali offers countless beautiful beaches and pools to enjoy.
- **Simple flats or sandals**: Most activities in Bali, such as exploring markets, strolling on the beach, or visiting temples, can be comfortably done in flats or sandals.
- **A light jacket for mountainous areas**: If you plan to visit places like Ubud or Munduk, evenings can be cooler, so a light jacket will keep you comfortable.

### 17. Motorcycle Safety

https://youtu.be/lIV-T2jYhVk

>   **Mistake**: Ignoring motorcycle safety measures.

- **Always wear a helmet**: Whether you're driving yourself or riding as a passenger, wearing a helmet is mandatory. This is not only a legal requirement but also a crucial safety measure.
- **Request a spare helmet when booking rides**: When using ride-hailing services like Gojek or Grab for motorcycle rides, always ask the driver to provide you with a helmet.
- **Follow traffic rules**: The road conditions and traffic regulations in Bali may differ from those in your home country. Drive cautiously and make sure to adhere to local traffic laws.

### 18. Ignoring local food safety

>   **Mistake**: Not paying attention to food hygiene and casually eating at roadside stalls or unclean places.

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

- **Choose clean restaurants or well-reviewed street vendors**: Bali offers many delicious local snacks and restaurants, but not all places meet hygiene standards. Opt for restaurants with good hygiene practices or vendors that come recommended.
- **Be cautious with drinking water**: Never drink tap water directly. Always choose bottled water or ensure that the restaurant provides safe drinking water.
- **Avoid raw or undercooked food**: Especially in remote areas or unfamiliar restaurants, raw and undercooked food can pose a risk of stomach discomfort. Prioritize fully cooked meals.
- **Carry stomach medicine**: If you have a sensitive stomach, bring along common stomach remedies like anti-diarrheal medication or probiotics for emergencies.
