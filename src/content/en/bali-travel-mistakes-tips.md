## Itinerary Planning

### 1. Overpacked Itinerary

>   **Mistake**: Many travelers underestimate Bali's size and try to cram in every attraction in a short time, leading to a rushed trip where you can't truly savor each spot.

Though Bali is an island, it's surprisingly large and diverse—from southern beaches to northern highlands, each area has its own unique charm. When planning, choose regions based on your stay length:

- **One week or less**: Pick 1-2 main bases to minimize moving around.
- **Two weeks**: Cover 3-4 bases with reasonable travel time between them.
- **Three weeks or more**: Max out at 5-6 locations, but be careful not to overload your schedule.

Try to avoid frequent accommodation changes, as constant packing and moving can eat up time and leave you exhausted.

### 3. Saving Island Hopping for Last

> **Mistake**: Many travelers schedule trips to the Nusa Islands (Lembongan, Ceningan, Penida) or the Gili Islands at the end of their trip. But if bad weather cancels ferries, it could disrupt your return plans.

To avoid weather-related ferry cancellations messing with your departure, plan these island trips for the middle or early part of your journey. That way, even if delays happen, they won’t derail your entire itinerary.

## Accommodation & Transportation

### 5. Staying in Kuta

> **Mistake**: Kuta is famous for its vibrant nightlife, but it’s overly commercialized, crowded, and noisy—far from ideal for travelers seeking relaxation.

Choose alternative accommodations based on your needs:

- [**Seminyak**](/en/blog/seminyak-guide/): Home to many stylish boutique hotels and upscale restaurants, perfect for travelers seeking a refined getaway.
- [**Canggu**](/en/blog/canggu-guide/): Known as a surfer's paradise, with a laid-back vibe and charming cafes, ideal for younger crowds.
- [**Sanur**](/en/blog/sanur-guide/): Quieter than Kuta, great for families and travelers looking to escape the hustle and bustle.
- [**Nusa Dua**](/en/blog/nusa-dua-guide/): If you love five-star resorts and private beaches, this is the perfect choice.

### 6. Risks of Renting a Car or Scooter

> **Mistake**: Renting a scooter is convenient and saves money.

https://youtube.com/shorts/9uSjE2FVGQs

Bali's roads are narrow and winding, designed mainly for scooters, making driving a car risky for visitors unfamiliar with local traffic. While renting a scooter is convenient, accidents happen frequently, leading to injuries that can disrupt your trip and result in hefty medical bills. Given the high risks of self-driving, consider these alternatives:

- **Grab/Gojek**: These local ride-hailing apps are very convenient and affordable.
- **Private Driver**: Hiring a private driver in Bali is not only convenient but also reasonably priced, ideal for long-distance travel or day trips.

## Safety & Health

To make your Bali trip safer and more comfortable, it's important to avoid common health and safety pitfalls. On the other hand, respecting local culture and eco-tourism ethics can help you build a deeper connection with Bali. Below are some common mistakes and tips regarding safety, health, and cultural etiquette.

### 7. Card Security

> **Mistake**: Using credit cards for large purchases without considering the risk of fraud.

![7. 銀行卡安全](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0648b8fa6386a0758be5f8b1fa666c66.jpg)

In some areas of Bali, there is a risk of credit card fraud. Here are ways to protect your funds:

- **Use a debit card with limited funds**: Keep your debit card balance under USD$300 to minimize potential losses from fraud.
- **Online bank transfers**: For large payments or purchases, transfer additional funds via online banking to avoid keeping a high balance on your card.
- **Use digital payments**: When possible, use e-wallets like Gojek to reduce the use of physical cards.

### 8. Mosquito Protection

> **Mistake**: Ignoring the threat of mosquitoes

Dengue fever is not uncommon in Bali, so mosquito protection is essential. Here are some must-haves and tips:

- **Insect repellent**: Carry insect repellent with you and use it frequently, especially during outdoor activities.
- **Wear long sleeves and pants**: Mosquitoes are most active from dusk to night, so wear protective clothing.
- **Keep doors and windows closed**: Ensure your accommodation is sealed or use mosquito nets to keep mosquitoes out.
- **Watch for dengue symptoms**: If you feel unwell, such as fever or muscle pain, seek medical attention immediately to check for dengue fever.

### 9. Dealing with the Rainy Season

> **Mistake**: Ignoring Bali’s rapid weather shifts during the rainy season and failing to prepare accordingly.

![9. 應對雨季](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_c86655f684007466ca064fac6b244c4f.png)

Bali’s rainy season typically runs from November to April. While it doesn’t pour every day, the weather can change in an instant—switching from sunshine to a downpour without warning.

- **Carry a raincoat**: A lightweight raincoat is more practical than an umbrella, as storms arrive suddenly and often bring strong winds.
- **Plan morning activities**: Mornings tend to be more stable, so schedule outdoor adventures early to avoid sudden afternoon showers.

### 10. Monkey Safety Tips

https://youtube.com/shorts/tpPWsvsH-Eo

> **Mistake**: Underestimating the potential danger of monkeys and bringing valuables or food near them.

In parts of Bali, such as Uluwatu Temple and the Ubud Monkey Forest, monkeys are common ‘residents.’ Though they look cute, they can be aggressive—especially when it comes to shiny objects or food.

- **Don’t bring valuables or food**: Monkeys are especially drawn to food and shiny items, so keep these out of sight.
- **Avoid eye contact**: Staring at a monkey can be seen as a challenge and is very risky.
- **Protect glasses and phones**: Monkeys often snatch glasses, phones, and other items—keep them secure in monkey areas.
- **Keep your distance**: While monkeys may seem friendly, remember they are wild animals and should be observed from a safe distance.

## Cultural Etiquette

### 11. Respect Local Culture

> **Mistake**: Ignoring local religious and cultural etiquette, leading to disrespectful behavior.

![11. 尊重當地文化](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_2051bf25b8c56908609a59e3c8151f2a.jpg)

Bali is a stronghold of Hindu culture, and locals place great importance on religious ceremonies and traditions. Respecting their customs is crucial for visitors. Here are some key cultural etiquette tips:

- **Temple Dress Code**: When entering temples, dress modestly and wear a sarong (temples usually offer free rental services).
- **Avoid Stepping on Offerings**: You'll often see small basket-like offerings on the streets of Bali—these are daily offerings made by locals, so be sure not to step on them.

Further reading: [Understanding Bali's Offering Tradition: Canang Sari](/en/blog/bali-canang-sari-explained/)

- **Taboo Actions**: Do not touch anyone's head, as it's considered disrespectful in local culture. Also, offering items with your left hand is seen as impolite.
- **Remove Shoes Indoors**: When entering a local's home or some shops, take off your shoes as a sign of respect.
- **Learn Basic Greetings**: Picking up a few simple local phrases like "thank you" can help you connect more easily with locals.
  - Indonesian "thank you": **Terima kasih**
  - Balinese "thank you": **Suksma**

### 12. Animal Tourism Ethics

> **Mistake**: Participating in animal tourism activities that are unethical and cause harm to animals.

Bali offers many animal-related tourism activities, but quite a few involve inhumane treatment of animals and have sparked significant controversy. To protect wildlife, it's recommended to avoid the following activities:

- **Zoos and Marine Parks**: Many animals in zoos and marine parks are confined to cramped spaces, unable to roam freely.
- **Elephant Parks**: Some parks offer elephant rides, but these elephants are often mistreated and in poor health. Since 2026, Bali has banned elephant riding across the island.
- **Wildlife Shows**: The animals in these shows are often subjected to cruel training to perform unnatural behaviors.

Support activities that respect animals' natural habitats, such as:

- **Lovina Dolphin Watching**: Here, dolphins swim freely in open waters, not confined to aquariums.
- **Natural Habitat Sea Turtle Observation**: Join organizations or activities that support wildlife conservation and observe sea turtles in the wild without interfering with their natural lives.

## **Practical Tips**

When traveling in Bali, thorough preparation and understanding of local conditions can make your trip smoother and more enjoyable, while also helping you avoid potential pitfalls. Here are some practical tips for traveling in Bali to help you have a hassle-free vacation.

### 13. Currency Exchange

> **Mistake**: Exchanging money before departure or at the airport.

https://youtu.be/KPGb6MmwfZw

- **Exchange After Arrival**: Check out the "[Bali Currency Exchange Guide](/en/blog/bali-currency-exchange-guide/)" for tips on how and where to exchange money.
- **Avoid Carrying Large Amounts of Cash**: Carrying too much cash increases risk; using a debit card or digital payment is safer.

### 14. Bargaining Tips

> **Mistake**: Accepting the first price when shopping at markets or street stalls, without understanding local bargaining culture.

- **All non-fixed-price items are negotiable**: In many open-air markets and small stalls across Bali, prices aren’t set in stone—you can haggle.

- **Don’t accept the first offer**: Vendors usually start with a higher price. Try to negotiate a fair reduction and only buy once you’ve both agreed on a price.

- **Support the local economy**: While bargaining is a common practice, avoid pushing prices too low. Respect local vendors to help sustain their livelihoods.

### 15. Weather Awareness

>   **Mistake**: Ignoring Bali’s changing weather and not checking conditions in advance.

- **Follow BMKG (Bali Meteorology, Climatology, and Geophysics Agency)** [**on Instagram**](https://www.instagram.com/infobmkg/): They post daily English-language travel weather forecasts.
- **Use the AccuWeather app**: It often provides more accurate predictions than Google.
- **Check weather on Windy.com**: Great for Bali forecasts, especially wave and wind conditions.
- **Morning activities are best**: Bali’s weather is usually more stable in the morning, making it ideal for outdoor plans.
- **Afternoons are more likely to rain**: Especially during the rainy season, brief showers often hit in the afternoon, so plan indoor activities or rest for that time.

### 16. Packing Tips

>   **Mistake**: Bringing too many unnecessary clothes or ignoring local weather patterns.

Based on Bali’s climate and activities, here are the essentials:

https://youtu.be/0M0voLfFLrQ

- **Lightweight, breathable clothing**: Bali’s hot and humid weather calls for thin, airy fabrics to keep you comfortable in the heat.
- **Swimwear**: Whether or not you’re staying near the beach, swimwear is a must—Bali has countless stunning beaches and pools to enjoy.
- **Simple flats or sandals**: Most activities in Bali, like market browsing, beach walks, or temple visits, are easy in flat shoes or sandals.
- **A light jacket for the highlands**: If you’re heading to Ubud or Munduk, evenings can be cooler, so a thin jacket will come in handy.

https://youtu.be/lIV-T2jYhVk

> **Mistake**: Ignoring motorcycle safety measures.

- **Always wear a helmet**: Whether you're driving or riding pillion, a helmet is a must. It's not only a legal requirement but also a crucial safety measure.
- **Request a spare helmet when booking a ride**: When using ride-hailing apps like Gojek or Grab, if you're taking a motorcycle, be sure to ask the driver for a helmet.
- **Follow traffic rules**: Road conditions and traffic regulations in Bali may differ from those in your home country. Drive cautiously and adhere to local traffic laws.

### 18. Ignoring Local Food Safety

> **Mistake**: Not paying attention to food hygiene, casually eating at street stalls or unclean places.

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

- **Choose clean restaurants or well-reviewed street stalls**: Bali offers many delicious local snacks and eateries, but not all places meet hygiene standards. Opt for restaurants with good cleanliness or recommended vendors.
- **Watch your drinking water**: Never drink tap water directly. Choose bottled water or ensure the restaurant provides safe drinking water.
- **Avoid raw or undercooked food**: Especially in remote areas or unfamiliar restaurants, raw and undercooked food can pose a risk of stomach discomfort. Prioritize cooked dishes.
- **Carry stomach medication**: If you have a sensitive stomach, bring along common gastrointestinal medicines like anti-diarrheal pills or probiotics, just in case.
