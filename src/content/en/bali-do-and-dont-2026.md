For first-time visitors to Bali, many only think of beaches, sunsets, and delicious satay—completely overlooking how seemingly harmless actions can be major faux pas on the island.

Having lived in Bali for several years, I've seen countless tourists make cultural blunders out of ignorance, and some even get deported for more serious violations. Trust me, spending 5 minutes understanding these points before your trip is absolutely worth it.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-05/1777688893131-779059604094166643.png)

***

## Things You Should Do More of in Bali

**Respect Local Culture and Dress Codes**

Bali is the heart of Hindu culture, where religion permeates every aspect of life. When visiting temples or attending ceremonies, remember to wear a sarong around your waist and cover your shoulders. Many temples offer free sarong rentals at the entrance, so there's no need to buy one.

**Experience Festivals with the Balinese**

If you happen to encounter an Odalan temple celebration or a Ngaben cremation ceremony, it's truly a rare experience—but remember to keep your distance, observe quietly, and avoid rushing into the crowd for photos.

**Only Exchange Money at Licensed Currency Exchange Offices**

This is something I really want to emphasize. Many places outside the airport and on the streets offer seemingly great exchange rates, but you might find out later that they've sneakily deducted a bunch of fees or even removed some bills from your stack. Licensed currency exchange offices are the safe choice.

**Try Bali's Local Cuisine**

Babi guling (crispy roast pork), Nasi campur, Bebek betutu—these are dishes you won't find anywhere else. Supporting local small businesses is also one of the most meaningful ways to travel.

**Learn a Few Indonesian Phrases**

"Terima kasih" (thank you), "Selamat pagi" (good morning)—just these two phrases will earn you extra smiles in markets, on the streets, and in restaurants. Locals really appreciate it, and you'll notice the difference in their demeanor immediately.

## Things You Should Never Do

**Working Without a Permit**

This has been the most common reason for foreigners being deported from Bali in recent years. Freelancing, opening a shop, taking on business through social media—without a proper work permit, it's all illegal. Getting caught could mean more than just a fine; you could be deported and blacklisted.

**Drugs**

Indonesia has a zero-tolerance policy towards drugs. Possession leads to severe punishment, and trafficking can result in the death penalty. Enough said.

**Stepping on Offerings (Canang Sari)**

Every morning, women in Bali place small woven coconut leaf baskets filled with offerings at their doorsteps, along roadsides, and in front of shops. This is part of their daily religious ritual—these are not trash nor mere decorations. Be mindful of where you step; accidentally crushing them is considered highly disrespectful.

https://youtube.com/shorts/TNB1hIHjsog

**Taking Inappropriate Poses at Sacred Sites**

In recent years, several foreigners have been expelled for this very reason. Whether it’s temples, altars, or statues—even if it’s for a perfect Instagram shot—it’s not appropriate to strike disrespectful poses. Keep the scenery intact, and keep the respect intact too.

**Riding Without a License**

Many people rent motorcycles in Bali, but if you don’t have an international or Indonesian driver’s license, getting stopped isn’t just about paying a fine—it could also affect your insurance claims. If something goes wrong and you’re left with nothing, that’s when real trouble begins.

https://youtu.be/lIV-T2jYhVk

**Over-Bargaining at Markets**

Haggling is part of the shopping culture in Bali, but it’s important to keep it friendly. The vendors don’t make much profit, so negotiating in a lighthearted, conversational manner keeps everyone happy. Aggressively driving down prices will only leave a bad impression.

## One Last Thing

What makes Bali enchanting isn’t just its beauty—it’s the island’s soul and rhythm. The more you’re willing to understand its culture, the more the island will reveal its best side to you.

Embark with respect, and you'll enjoy your journey more than any guidebook could offer.
