# Indonesian Petai: The Green Magic Bean That Smells Shocking but Tastes Addictive

When it comes to 'quirky ingredients' in Southeast Asia, Petai (Parkia speciosa) definitely makes the list. Those who smell it for the first time might frown, but once you fall in love with it, it's like being enchanted—the more you eat, the more you crave it.

## What Exactly is Petai?

Petai is the seed of the 'Beautiful Ball Flower Bean,' a leguminous plant. It is elongated, bright green, and comes in pods resembling oversized pea pods. Once opened, the beans inside are plump and round, emitting a strong, distinctive odor—some describe it as 'gas leakage' or 'sour smell,' but for Southeast Asians, it's a charming aroma.

In Indonesia, Malaysia, and southern Thailand, Petai is a common ingredient in home-cooked meals and street food. The most classic ways to enjoy it include:

- Eating it raw with Sambal chili sauce
- Stir-frying it with shrimp paste, chili, and shrimp
- Adding it to curries or Sambal sauce for stewing

## In Indonesia, We Actually Love 'Petai Fried Rice'

If you travel to Indonesia, you must try **Nasi Goreng Petai (Petai Fried Rice)**. At first glance, it might seem challenging, but when Petai is added to classic Indonesian fried rice and quickly stir-fried in a hot pan, its intense flavor mellows, releasing a subtle aroma reminiscent of nuts and fresh grass.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-05/1778028180819-nasigoreng_petai.jpeg)

The rice is savory and flavorful thanks to soy sauce, garlic, chili, and shrimp paste, paired with a few crunchy stink beans. When bitten into, their slight bitterness and crisp texture surprisingly balance the greasiness of the fried rice. At roadside stalls in Jakarta or Surabaya, a plate of Nasi Goreng Petai often comes with a fried egg, fried chicken, or shrimp crackers, making it a comforting dish for many Indonesians.

## What are the benefits of stink beans?

Despite their strong odor, stink beans are actually highly nutritious. Older generations often say, 'Eat stink beans, and your body won’t accumulate toxins.' Modern science also confirms their various benefits:

1. **Natural Antidepressant**
Stink beans are rich in tryptophan, a precursor to serotonin production in the brain. Eating them helps relax and improve mood, which is why some say 'eating stink beans makes you happy.'
2. **Stabilizes Blood Sugar**
Studies show that bioactive compounds in stink beans help reduce blood sugar fluctuations, and they are even used in traditional Indonesian diabetes management.
3. **Promotes Kidney Health**
Stink beans act as a natural diuretic, helping to flush out excess salt and waste from the body, benefiting those with high blood pressure or mild edema.
4. **Iron Supplement, Prevents Anemia**
They are high in iron, aiding red blood cell production, making them suitable for those with anemia or women after menstruation.
5. **Gut Health**
Rich in dietary fiber, they promote digestion and relieve constipation.

> ⚠️ Note: Stink beans contain high purine levels, so gout sufferers should consume them in moderation. Additionally, due to their diuretic effect, urine and sweat may have a noticeable odor after consumption, which is normal.

## In Indonesia, stink beans aren’t cheap

https://www.instagram.com/reel/DX-iT5Uq2ZZ/?utm_source=ig_web_copy_link&igsh=MzRlODBiNWFlZA==

You might be surprised, but stink beans aren’t exactly an affordable ingredient in Indonesia. Especially high-quality, plump fresh stink beans, **cost Rp. 25,000 per pod** (about USD$1.60). A single pod contains only about 6–12 beans, so buying a small handful can easily cost several dollars.

Why are they so expensive? Stink beans primarily come from wild or semi-cultivated areas near the forests of Sumatra and Java. Harvesting is difficult, requiring climbers to pick the massive pods, which can be 30–50 cm long. Combined with their short shelf life and susceptibility to damage during transport, fresh stink beans are often sold 'by the pod' in traditional markets, making them a mid-to-high-priced seasonal vegetable.

So next time someone in Indonesia offers you a stink bean dish, don’t hesitate—it’s a gesture of genuine hospitality!

## Worth Trying: An Adventurous Ingredient

Petai (stink beans) aren't for everyone, but like durian or natto, once you overcome the initial aroma barrier, they open up a whole new world of flavors. If you have the chance to visit Indonesia, try ordering a plate of **Nasi Goreng Petai**, or buy some fresh petai to eat raw with chili. You might find yourself, like us, going from frowning to smiling, falling in love with this 'charmingly pungent' green magic.
