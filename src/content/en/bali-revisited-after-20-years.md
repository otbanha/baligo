At the end of June 2024, I just returned from Bali, a trip filled with memories and surprises. For me, this was my second visit to Bali; the first was to get my Open Water diving certification. That trip was very tight, as the diving course took five days, leaving little time to explore elsewhere. This time, however, I came with my family, and it’s been twenty years since my last visit. Bali’s development stunned me—not only is it far more prosperous, but the number of Indonesians and tourists has more than tripled compared to two decades ago. Below are some of the memorable places and moments from our journey:

## Traffic in Bali

Traffic in Bali has always been a challenge. Chronic congestion often slows speeds to just 10 km/h, making itineraries hard to manage. We frequently had to patiently wait in traffic jams—if we weren’t in a hurry, we treated it as extra time to enjoy the scenery along the way!

## Taxis and Gojek

Twenty years ago, hailing a taxi in Bali cost a whopping $20 USD or more to start, which made me reluctant to leave the hotel after returning—the transportation costs alone were painful. Now things have improved with the Gojek ride-hailing app, making transportation much more affordable and convenient, greatly enhancing our travel experience.

## Ngurah Rai International Airport

Bali's airport is already new and quite beautiful. However, since its capacity still can't handle the massive crowds during peak season, the check-in process can take a long time, often over an hour. Therefore, it's recommended to arrive early at the airport for check-in when departing to avoid delays.

## Airport Shopping

Inside the airport, there's a small stall selling Oakley sunglasses and backpacks. Although the selection is limited, the prices are cheaper than in Taiwan. Based on my experience, the branded items at the airport should be authentic, so shopping here offers more peace of mind. Additionally, Billabong surfwear is also more affordable at the airport than in Taiwan, making it tempting to buy a few extra pieces.

## Bali's Coffee and Chocolate

Bali produces coffee and chocolate. JungleGold Bali offers many unique flavors; although the prices are a bit steep, the packaging is exquisite, making them great souvenirs to bring home as gifts. We visited Satria Coffee Plantation, where various spices are grown, and we got to see how cocoa fruit grows on trees—a rare experience for us.

At Satria Coffee Plantation, we also tried luwak coffee. Personally, I didn't find the luwak coffee particularly aromatic, but the experience was eye-opening. ## Pork Ribs Food Tour

We sampled plenty of delicious food in Bali, and Naughty Nuri's grilled pork ribs were absolutely amazing—we ate at both the Ubud and Sanur locations. The Sanur branch is larger and has air-conditioned rooms, making it very comfortable. Fat Chow Bali (Kuta) also served fantastic food; we ate there twice in Kuta, and each time we had to queue, but the wait was well worth it.

## Indonesian Cuisine

Our guide, Mr. Liega, recommended Warung Kampung for Indonesian cuisine. The food was delicious and affordable—freshly blended durian juice cost just NT$40 (about USD$1) a cup, giving us a real taste of local food culture.

## Bali Zoo

Although the zoo entrance fee was a bit steep—over NT$1,000 (about USD$32) per person—once inside, we found it beautifully integrated with Bali's unique architectural style. We toured the park in an air-conditioned exploration bus, which made the visit comfortable despite the heat, and we felt the ticket price was well worth it.

## Nightlife Experience

HardRock Cafe Bali carries on the vibe of Indonesian nightlife, with live band performances starting at 9:30 PM every night. The music is extremely loud—so much so that we couldn't stay long and left quickly.

## Whitewater Rafting Experience

We also joined a rafting activity in Bali. Although the price was on the higher side—nearly USD$65 per person—it included an all-you-can-eat local meal. Our guide managed to get us a 20% discount. While the river wasn't too dangerous, climbing up and down the stone steps required some physical effort. Even my 76-year-old mother managed to complete the experience smoothly.

## Spotting Manta Rays at Nusa Penida

To see manta rays at Nusa Penida, it's best to stay overnight on the island the day before. If you're staying elsewhere, like Kuta, you'll need to wake up as early as 5 AM. Since we didn't want to get up that early, we ended up skipping this activity.

## Bali's Culture

![Bali's Culture](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_d2bcc00956186161b74a5936edb7dc14.png)

In Bali, every household has its own temple, and the walls are adorned with many beautiful stone carvings. Our guide, Mr. Fovix, explained that locals pray twice a day, and no building in a home can be taller than the temple, as a sign of respect to the gods. This is also why most parts of Bali have no high-rise buildings.

## Summary

For those who love ocean views, beaches, and rice terrace scenery, Bali is a place you'll want to visit again and again. On my next trip, I plan to explore the local markets and experience the daily life of the locals. The basic monthly wage here is around USD$180 (as of 2024), and living that way would surely offer a different perspective. Through the local way of life, we might gain a deeper appreciation for the spirituality of this beautiful land. We are very grateful to our two guides, Mr. Fovix and Mr. Liega, who gave us invaluable advice. Mr. Fovix is attentive and speaks fluent English, while Mr. Liega is honest and has good English skills. They took us to many spots frequented by local tourists, helping us save a lot of money. Every time we ate, we invited them to join us, but they always politely declined or ordered something simple. We truly appreciate their thoughtful care, which left us with perfect memories.
