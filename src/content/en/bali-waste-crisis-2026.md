If you've recently searched for news about Bali, you might have seen scenes like this: garbage bags piled waist-high on the streets, rivers emitting foul odors, people burning waste in open spaces, hundreds of garbage trucks protesting in front of the governor's office...

This isn't some remote village, but what's happening in Denpasar and Kuta, the bustling tourist heart of Bali.

The trigger for the problem is a 40-year-old landfill reaching its end.

## Suwung Landfill: A Time Bomb That Lasted 40 Years

The Suwung Landfill is located about 10 kilometers northeast of Denpasar International Airport. In the past, it processed nearly 1,000 tons of waste daily, handling not just Denpasar's garbage but also waste from Badung, Gianyar, Tabanan, and other counties—meaning almost all the waste from Bali's southern tourist core area was sent here.

The problem is, this landfill has long been overloaded. Waste management expert Nur Azizah from the University of Indonesia points out that organic waste accounts for 70% of Suwung's waste, which is very dangerous—long-term accumulation of organic matter produces methane, which can cause explosions and landslides. Such accidents have happened several times, with a landslide at Indonesia's largest landfill (outside Jakarta) in March this year killing 7 people.

## April 1: A Ban Without Backup Plans

Starting April 1, 2026, the Suwung Landfill will completely stop accepting organic waste, with plans to permanently close on August 1. The policy is well-intentioned, but the reality is: the alternatives are not ready yet.

The outcome is predictable. Garbage has started piling up on the streets, attracting rats and spreading foul odors; desperate residents have begun burning waste in their yards or nearby open spaces, producing acrid black smoke that has raised public health concerns.

"People near my house have been burning garbage, and the smoke fills the air. The stench of burning plastic is very worrying," said Tyas Ardi, a resident of Denpasar.

It's not just homes—garbage has piled up along roadsides, riversides, parks, and even around schools. In some places, the trash hasn't been collected for weeks or even months.

## The Dilemma of Garbage Truck Drivers: Nowhere to Dump

Over 400 garbage truck drivers have joined forces, driving truckloads of foul-smelling organic waste directly to government offices in protest.

"It's our fault if we don't collect our clients' garbage, but if we do collect it, we don't know where to dump it—what are we supposed to do?" said protester I Wayan Tedi Brahmanca, voicing the frustration of all waste collection workers.

## Garbage Bags Piled Waist-High Near Kuta Beach

Near the parking lot and pedestrian walkways by Kuta Beach, bags of garbage have piled up. I Nyoman Arya Arimbawa, head of the Kuta Beach Tourism Management Office, said that while cleaning teams work to clear the trash, people keep dumping more, making it impossible to keep up. He noted that the garbage appears to have been transported from areas outside Kuta.

"There are a lot of rats here at night, and the smell is terrible... It's not a good sight," said Australian tourist Justin Butcher, who was at the scene.

## The Government's Response: Temporary Solutions, Long-Term Uncertainty

Facing waves of protest, the Environment Minister has finally compromised. The Suwung landfill will now receive organic waste twice a week, but this emergency measure will only last until July 31st — what happens after August remains unclear.

The long-term solution is to build a Waste-to-Energy facility in Benoa, which is expected to process 1,200 tons of waste daily. However, even if construction proceeds smoothly, it will take several years to become operational.

According to current regulations, those caught illegally dumping or burning garbage can face up to three months of detention and a fine of 50 million Indonesian Rupiah (approximately USD$1,600). But many residents say they have no other choice.

## First-hand feedback from our community members

Data and news reports are one thing, but the experiences of those who actually go are another. **Zhang Changyong**, a Taiwanese community member who just returned, said 'no big impact,' and **Li Yingjie** also mentioned 'not really.' **Fiona Wu** recently visited Sanur and said it was fine.

**PeacefulNectarine** put it aptly: 'It's actually okay. People there aren't really into recycling, but it's not overly dirty or disgusting either.'

These reflections highlight a reality: the impact of the garbage crisis varies greatly across different areas. In Nusa Dua and Sanur, where hotels are densely packed and management is stricter, the effect on the tourist experience is relatively limited. However, in places like Kuta and Denpasar city center, the problem is much more visible.

## Be mentally prepared if you're heading to Bali now

This isn't to say you shouldn't go. But if you have plans for Bali in the near future, here are a few things worth knowing:

**Prioritize staying in Nusa Dua or Sanur** — hotels in these areas have stricter waste management practices, and their geographical locations make it less likely for marine debris to drift in, keeping the streets relatively clean.

**The Kuta and Legian areas are currently less stable**, and travelers may encounter garbage piles or unpleasant odors when going out. Those with cleanliness concerns or traveling with children should be especially cautious.

**Do not casually hand over trash to roadside "collection" services**, as with limited resources, the final destination of this waste is uncertain.

The crisis triggered by the closure of the landfill in Bali is essentially the result of decades of infrastructure debt coming to a head. Policies have outpaced supporting facilities, leaving residents and waste management operators caught in the middle. No one can predict the situation after August—this island is racing against time.
