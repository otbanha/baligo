Recently, someone asked in our Facebook group: "Do I need to bring my own showerhead for bathing in Bali? Should I use bottled water for brushing my teeth?" The original poster said online information is polarized and wanted to hear from those who have actually been there. The comment section was very active, with experiences from various body types, accommodation types, and both "got sick" and "stayed healthy" stories.

I’ve summarized nearly 40 comments into three key points to give you peace of mind (or a heads-up).

### 🔹 Conclusion 1: **Showerhead—Most Say ‘No,’ but Filters Turn Yellow in Ubud/Homestays**

- **Most Comments**: No need to replace the showerhead; it’s fine to use as is.
- **But Some Experiences**: Bringing a filtered showerhead to **Ubud or homestays** resulted in visibly yellow filters with impurities. In **hotels**, the filters stayed clean.
- **Netizen Test**: Someone posted a photo of a filter after three nights in Ubud—it was really yellow.
- **Conclusion**: If you’re staying in high-end hotels or resorts, the water quality is usually fine. If staying in **Ubud villas, homestays, or if you have sensitive skin**, bring a filtered showerhead as insurance—it’s lightweight.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775969361098-shower_head.jpg)

### 🔹 Conclusion 2: **Brushing Teeth—Half Bottled Water, Half Tap Water, It Depends on ‘Your Constitution’**

- **Bottled Water Camp**: Rinse with bottled water after brushing for peace of mind.
- **Tap Water Camp**: Brush directly with tap water, no issues.
- **Harsh Reality**: Some shared that **even using bottled water, their friend still got sick**; others said they ate street food in India without problems but got diarrhea in Taiwan. **It really depends on the person.**
- **Advice**:
    - Sensitive stomach, first-time traveler, traveling with elderly or kids → **Stick to bottled water for brushing**.
    - Iron stomach, frequent traveler, staying in good hotels → Most say it’s fine.

### 🔹 Conclusion 3: **Ice, Street Food, Restaurant Drinks—High-Risk Items**

- **Ice**: Many say they drank restaurant ice without issues, but others warn: "Avoid ice in drinks" as basic self-protection.
- **Street Food**: Universally considered the biggest risk. Some fell victim to a roadside iced coffee, with symptoms appearing only after returning home. Grilled corn and noodle stalls were fine for some, but high-risk groups should skip.
- **Five-Star Hotel Water**: One traveler shared that the bathtub water in a five-star hotel had black particles, prompting them to use bottled water for brushing the entire trip. **Star ratings don’t guarantee water filtration quality**.

### 🧳 Essential Prevention Kit (Crowdsourced Edition)

- ✅ **Before & During Trip**: Take **probiotics** on an empty stomach every morning
- ✅ **Brushing Teeth**: Stick to bottled water to be safe
- ✅ **Showering**: Bring a filtered showerhead (especially if staying in Ubud or guesthouses)
- ✅ **Drinks**: Ice in restaurants is fine, but skip it at roadside stalls; canned drinks are safest
- ✅ **Stomach Medicine**: Bring from home (e.g., Zhang Guozhou Stomach Powder, anti-diarrheal meds)
- ✅ **If You Get Sick**: [**Check this quick self-help guide**](https://gobaligo.id/blog/2026-01-02-69523427fd89780001caa652/)

### ✍️ My Takeaway

**There’s no definitive answer to Bali’s water quality—it’s all about what your stomach can handle.**
If you’re prone to worry or traveling with family, **bring an extra filter, use bottled water for brushing, and avoid street food** for peace of mind.
If you have an iron stomach and a carefree attitude, relax and enjoy—most people don’t have issues.
**The bottom line: Pack stomach meds, then soak up Bali!** 🌴
