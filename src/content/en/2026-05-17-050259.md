## 🛂 Must-do Before Departure

### 1. Passport & Visa: Confirm 2 Months in Advance

Your passport must have **at least 6 months of validity remaining**. Indonesian customs are very strict about this requirement — even one day expired could get you denied.

**There are three visa options:**

- **Visa-Free Entry**: Taiwan passport holders can stay up to 30 days visa-free (but must complete an online declaration)
- **eVOA (Electronic Visa on Arrival)**: Apply online before departure, approval takes about 2-3 business days, valid for 30 days, **most recommended for first-timers**
- **VoA (Visa on Arrival)**: Apply upon arrival in Bali, may take 15-30 minutes in line

**Strongly recommend choosing eVOA** because:

- Walk straight through the automatic gates upon arrival (no 30-minute wait in line)
- Only $5 cheaper, but the time saved is worth so much more
- Customs policies are getting stricter by 2026, better to be prepared

### 2. Travel Insurance: Don’t Skip It

The medical standards in Bali are not as good as those in Taiwan. Hospitalization there could cost USD$16,000-32,000. Additionally, Bali has many mosquito-borne diseases.

**Insurance Coverage to Consider:**

- Accidental injuries (common for hiking and water activities)
- Medical expenses (including overseas hospitalization)
- Trip interruption/lost luggage

You can refer to this guide: [Essential Reading for Travelers from Taiwan, Hong Kong, Malaysia, and Singapore! Insurance Guide, Overseas Medical Coverage, and Travel Disruption Claims](/en/blog/bali-travel-insurance-guide/)

### 3. Flight Planning: Avoid Peak Periods to Save 1/3 of Your Budget

**When Are Prices Highest?**

- Summer and winter breaks (July, August)
- Chinese New Year (late January-February)
- Christmas holidays (mid-December to early January)
- Qingming and Labor Day holidays (early April)

**When Are Prices Lower?**

- May-June (end of rainy season, before peak season)
- September-October (just after peak season)
- November (start of rainy season)

Flights are usually cheapest when booked **3-6 weeks in advance**. Avoid the first flight of the day; flights departing between 2-5 PM are often 20-30% cheaper.

💡 **Common Misconception for Beginners**: "Budget airlines are cheaper." The truth is, budget airlines charge extra for luggage and seat selection, and when you add it all up, they may not be cheaper than traditional airlines. Direct flights with Starlux, China Airlines, or EVA Air might actually be more cost-effective.

### 4. Bali Tourist Levy

Starting in 2024, every foreign tourist entering Bali will need to pay a tourist levy of 150,000 Indonesian Rupiah.

**How to Pay?**

- Easiest: Pay online before departure (balilevy.baliprov.go.id)
- Next Option: Pay directly at the airport upon arrival (dedicated counters available)
- Not Recommended: Wait to pay at tourist attractions, as random checks may occur

Be sure to keep your receipt, as you may be asked to show it at tourist sites.

## 💰 Pre-Trip Checklist

[https://youtu.be/KPGb6MmwfZw](https://youtu.be/KPGb6MmwfZw)

1. **Bring USD** and exchange a small amount first in Bali.
2. **Or exchange at authorized money changers in Bali**.

### 6. Packing List: What to Bring and What to Skip

**Must-bring items (you’ll actually use these):**

- Sunscreen (SPF 50+ or higher)
- Mosquito repellent (lotion works better than spray)
- Light rain gear (frequent showers during the rainy season)
- Basic medication (cold medicine, stomach medicine, anti-diarrheal)
- Light jacket (air conditioning indoors can be chilly)
- Passport copy (in case you lose the original)

**Don't Bring (You Can Buy These Cheaply in Bali):**

- Face masks (cheap jelly masks are everywhere)
- Cosmetics (plenty of affordable brands)
- Daily necessities (hotels provide them, and supermarkets have them too)

**Prohibited Items (Customs Will Confiscate or Fine You):**

- Anything related to drugs
- Meat products (even instant noodles from Taiwan need ingredient checks)
- Alcoholic beverages exceeding 5 liters
- Products made from wild animals

### 8. Voltage & Plugs: 220V, Bring the Right Adapter

Bali uses **220V voltage**, while Taiwan uses 110V.

**Smart Tips:**

- Bring a universal adapter (get one that supports multiple countries)
- Or buy one locally in Bali (usually the cheapest option)

Most laptop and phone chargers support automatic conversion between 110-240V (check if it's written on the charger), but always confirm for hair dryers and curling irons.

## 🗺️ Trip Planning Essentials

### 9. How Many Days Are Ideal? First-Timers Recommend 5-7 Days

**Less than 3 days:** Only enough time to explore the south (Kuta, Seminyak), which is a pity
**5-7 days:** South + Ubud, experiencing 60% of Bali
**10+ days:** Enough time to explore the east, west, and outer islands

For first-timers, I highly recommend a **6-day, 5-night** trip — it’s the perfect duration without feeling rushed.

## ✈️ Arriving in Bali

### 14. The Correct Process from Landing to Exiting the Airport

![14. 下飛機到出機場的正確流程](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-05/1778988530785-_5.png)

**Don’t wander around—follow these steps:**

1. **Follow the 'Arrival' signs** (not Departure)
2. **E-Visa holders use the automated gates** (fast track)
3. **Visa on Arrival holders queue at the Visa on Arrival counter** (to get your visa)
4. **Pass through immigration** (get your passport stamped)
5. **Collect your luggage at the carousel** (look for your flight number)
6. **Fill out the AIAC Customs Declaration Form** (online only)
7. **Pass through customs** (usually just a quick glance)
8. **Pay the Bali Tourism Tax** (if not paid in advance)

**The entire process takes 30-60 minutes if there’s no queue**, so don’t stress.

### 16. Stomach Issues and Bali Belly

https://youtu.be/GivvqCXp11A

**The most common problem in Bali is Bali Belly (traveler's diarrhea).**

**Prevention is Better Than Cure**:

- Only drink bottled water (widely available and cheap in supermarkets)
- Avoid raw foods from street vendors (be cautious with fresh salads and fruit platters)
- Food cooked at high temperatures is generally safe

**If You Do Get Sick**:

- Bring anti-diarrheal medication (it's safer to bring from home)
- Drink electrolyte beverages (available in Bali supermarkets)
- Rest for half a day; you'll usually feel better within a day

## 💡 Local Knowledge

### 17. How to Convert Currency?

**Quick Conversion Formula**: Remove the last three zeros from the Indonesian Rupiah amount, then multiply by 2 to get an approximate value in USD.

**Example**:

- 100,000 IDR → 100 × 2 = USD$6
- 500,000 IDR → 500 × 2 = USD$32
- 1,000,000 IDR → 1000 × 2 = USD$64

💡 With this formula, you won't be shocked by 'OMG, why is it a 5-digit number?' when ordering food.

### 18. How to Tip?

[https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY](https://youtube.com/shorts/oBLpQ-MH4kY)

**Indonesia doesn't have a 'mandatory tipping' culture, but 'gratitude tips' are appreciated.**

- **Hotel Bellboy**: USD$1-2 or 20,000 IDR
- **SPA Masseuse**: 10% of the bill (or directly give 20,000~50,000 IDR)
- **Restaurant Waiter**: 10% of the bill (usually, a 4-10% service charge is already added, so it can be omitted)
- **Taxi Driver**: Round up (give change if you have it, otherwise don't worry)
- **Tour Guide/Private Driver**: 50,000~100,000 IDR per day

**The simplest rule**: Always tip when someone helps you. It doesn't need to be much, but it's greatly appreciated.

### 23. Cultural Taboos and Respect

**Religious Sensitive Areas:**

- Wear a sarong when entering temples (usually provided for free)
- Do not touch or climb on sacred statues
- Traditionally, women on their period should not enter temples (but most temples do not check)
- If you see a ceremony, take photos from a distance and do not disturb

**Daily Etiquette:**

- Eat and hand things with your right hand
- Do not point your feet at others
- Remove shoes when entering homes
- Show respect to elders

**Dress Code:**

- Bikinis are fine at the beach
- Cover shoulders and knees when entering temples or villages
- Polo shirts are acceptable for men in formal settings

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: How many days are ideal for a first trip to Bali?

**A:** 5-7 days is the best choice for beginners. Less than 3 days will feel rushed, limiting you to the southern part; 10 days or more allows you to explore the entire island. **6 days and 5 nights** lets you experience the south (beaches, shopping) + the north (culture, mountains) comfortably without overexertion.

### Q: What’s the budget for a Bali free-and-easy trip?

👉 [**Detailed Budget Calculator**](/en/bali-budget-calculator/)

### Q: Can I use Taiwanese dollars in Bali?

**A:** No. Bali only uses **Indonesian Rupiah (IDR)**. Taiwanese dollars are not accepted locally, and it's difficult to find places to exchange them. We recommend bringing US dollars to Bali and exchanging them for Rupiah there.

### Q: Is Bali safe? Will I get robbed or scammed?

**A:** Bali’s overall safety is better than many cities in Southeast Asia. Robberies are rare, and scams mainly target careless tourists (e.g., black market currency exchange, fake drivers). As long as you follow basic safety tips—avoid walking alone in secluded alleys at night, don’t use black market currency exchanges, verify driver identities—you’ll likely have no issues.

### Q: Can I visit Bali during the rainy season?

**A:** Absolutely. During the rainy season (November-March), it’s usually **just 1-2 hours of afternoon showers**, and attractions remain open. In fact, the rainy season has advantages: fewer tourists, hotels are 40-50% cheaper, rice terraces are at their greenest, and Spas are more relaxing. Many experienced travelers specifically choose the rainy season. The downside is unpredictable mountain weather and potential cancellations of some outdoor activities.

👉 [**Complete Rainy Season Guide**](/en/blog/2026-bali-indoor-activities/)

### Q: Do I need to apply for a visa before my first trip to Bali? Can I get a visa on arrival?

**A:** There are two options for Taiwanese passport holders:

1. **eVOA (Electronic Visa on Arrival)** (apply online 2-3 days before departure, **highly recommended**)
2. **Visa on Arrival** (apply at the airport, around USD$39)

The advantage of the eVOA is that you can go directly through the automated immigration gates upon arrival (no need to wait in the 45-minute manual queue).

Hong Kong, Singapore, and Malaysia enjoy visa-free entry for 30 days.

### Q: Is tipping expected in Bali? How should I tip?

**A:** Indonesia doesn't have a mandatory tipping culture, but tipping will make service staff very happy. Suggested tipping amounts:

- Porter: IDR 20,000
- Spa/Massage: IDR 50,000
- Restaurant: No need to tip if service charge is already included
- Taxi: Round up the fare
- Private driver/Guide: IDR 50,000~100,000 per day

The key is to "tip those who help you," the amount doesn't need to be large, attitude is most important.

### Q: Where is the best area to stay for first-time visitors to Bali?

**A:** It depends on your travel style:

- **Kuta**: Lively, affordable, popular with backpackers
- **Seminyak**: Boutiques, upscale restaurants, Instagram hotspots
- **Canggu**: Cafes, youthful vibe, surfing
- **Ubud**: Culture, wellness, away from the crowds
- **Nusa Dua**: Five-star hotels, family-friendly, resorts
- **Jimbaran**: Sunsets, seafood, romantic dinners

For first-timers, we recommend combining **Seminyak + Ubud** to experience both beaches and culture.
