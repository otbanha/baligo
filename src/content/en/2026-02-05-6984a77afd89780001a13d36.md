Based on the latest 2025 direct flight passenger statistics, Bali's tourism structure is slowly evolving, but some patterns remain unexpectedly stable—like the growing number of Singaporean visitors; Taiwanese travelers maintaining a consistent volume over two decades; and some previously dominant markets quietly shrinking.

Even more interesting is that Australia, long considered Bali's largest source of visitors, has been surpassed by Europe for the first time.

In this article, I’ll first guide you through **the changing trends of Bali’s international visitors in 2025**, and then clarify **Bali visa options and FAQs** that often confuse travelers.

No anxiety, no panic—just practical data and firsthand experiences to ensure you're fully prepared before your trip.

If you're planning a trip to Bali, this article will be incredibly useful.

According to official data, the total number of foreign visitors flying directly to Bali in 2025 is close to **7 million**, and there are some subtle shifts in the source structure that might surprise you.

Let’s start with something Taiwanese travelers will relate to. Taiwanese visitors to Bali have remained steady for nearly **20 years**, hovering around **120,000** annually—no sudden spikes, no significant drops. This is very 'Taiwanese'—not chasing trends, not swayed by hype, just quietly returning to what they love.

On the other hand, the once-dominant **mainland Chinese tourists** have dwindled to about **one-third** of their peak numbers. This shift is noticeable in airports, hotel demographics, and even the languages spoken by tour guides.

Here’s something I find particularly interesting: Australia has long been synonymous with Bali tourism—flights are frequent, the distance is short, and the vacation culture aligns perfectly. It’s held the top spot for years. But this time, European visitors have officially surpassed Australians, becoming Bali’s largest source of tourists.

This isn’t a sudden surge but the result of gradual growth over time.

This also reflects the changes I’ve observed in Bali in recent years:

- Long-term travelers are increasing, digital nomads are on the rise, and the off-season is no longer so quiet.
- The European habit of 'staying longer once they arrive' is changing the rhythm of the entire island.

## Here's another important point many people ask about: How to choose the EVOA electronic visa on arrival?

Currently, what most travelers struggle with isn't whether to get a visa, but 'which one to choose'.

The most common options right now are B1 and C1:

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_dd1d15ae602a759d1d7e072cbd771d4d.jpg)

**Quick Reference:** [Indonesia/Bali eVisa/eVOA Complete Guide: Application Steps and Precautions All in One Place!](/blog/2024-02-06-65c217c5fd897800010113af/)

### B1 Visa (Tourist Visa)

- Allows a stay of 30 days
- According to latest news, **short-term pure tourism does not require financial proof**

This visa is straightforward:

It's for traveling, vacationing, relaxing, taking photos, hitting the beaches, and enjoying great food.

But it **is not suitable for**:

Business meetings, contract signings, investment inspections, and medical-related activities.

### C1 Visa (Visit Visa)

- Allows a stay of 60 days
- Requires uploading a financial statement (PDF) showing a minimum of USD$2,000 from the last 3 months

The focus of the C1 visa is not 'leisure' but 'visiting'.

It includes exchanges, inspections, and non-employment-related business contacts, but still prohibits working.

## How should regular tourists choose?

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_d26a3e513ac8df95013e940223bf7017.png)

The answer is actually quite simple.

If your itinerary includes

- ✔ Travel
- ✔ Vacation
- ✔ Pure relaxation
- ✔ Travel not exceeding 30 days

**Choosing the B1 – Tourist Visa is completely sufficient.**

C1 is more suitable for those with complex travel purposes, longer stays, or those requiring an official 'visit purpose'.

Otherwise, preparing too much information just adds unnecessary hassle. However, it's crucial to note that **you must have your return flight ticket and accommodation details ready**! If you're randomly checked and your travel motives are deemed suspicious, you may be asked to provide proof of financial means or even denied entry! After all, there are quite a few cases of illegal stays in Bali.

## Bali Visa FAQ

### Q1: What type of visa do general tourists need for Bali?

If your purpose is simply tourism, vacation, relaxation, eating, and drinking, **just choose B1 (Tourist Visa)**, no need to overthink it. This is currently the choice for most short-term tourists.

### Q2: Does the B1 visa require proof of financial means?

According to the latest information, **the B1 visa for short-term tourism purposes does not require uploading proof of financial means**. However, it's still recommended to keep basic return flight and accommodation details on hand, just in case.

### Q3: If I want to stay in Bali longer, which one should I choose?

If you wish to stay for more than 30 days, or if your trip includes visiting friends, exchanges, or non-employment-related inspections, then you might consider **C1 (Visit Visa)**, which allows a stay of 60 days but requires uploading proof of financial means (PDF) showing over US$2,000 in the last 3 months.

### Q4: What is the biggest difference between B1 and C1?

In simple terms:

- B1 = Pure tourism
- C1 = Non-employment related visits

B1 is not suitable for business meetings, contract signings, or investment-related activities;

C1 emphasizes visits, exchanges, and inspections, but similarly does not permit work.

### Q5: Can I use a tourist visa to view properties or discuss collaborations?

Officially, it is not recommended to use B1 for any business or investment-related activities. If your trip involves 'business-related contacts,' C1 would be a safer choice.

### Q6: Can EVOA be applied for in Taiwan in advance?

Yes. EVOA (Electronic Visa on Arrival) can now be applied for online before departure,

making the entry process much smoother and reducing the need to stand in long queues.

**Quick Reference:** [Complete Guide to Indonesia/Bali eVisa/eVOA: Application Steps and Precautions All in One Place!](/blog/2024-02-06-65c217c5fd897800010113af/)

### Q7: Can EVOA be extended?

Yes. For B1 type visas, you can generally extend once for 30 days according to regulations. However, if you know you'll be staying for a long time from the start, choosing C1 directly might be more convenient.

### Q8: Is it mandatory to purchase a return ticket when entering Bali?

Officially, it's 'recommended to have a departure ticket.' In practice, most travelers aren't individually checked, but it's better to be prepared for peace of mind.

### Q9: What if I chose the wrong visa?

Changing your visa after entry is relatively complicated. The best approach is to select the correct one before departure.

If you're just traveling, don't overcomplicate it — **B1 – Tourist Visa** is all you need.

## Final Reminder

Bali's visa regulations occasionally undergo minor adjustments. It's safest to quickly check the latest announcements 1–2 weeks before departure. Based on most travelers' experiences, choosing the right visa makes entering Bali quite straightforward.
