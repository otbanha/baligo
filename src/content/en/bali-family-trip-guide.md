> The following content is adapted from a travelogue shared by group member [Lin Dinosaur](https://www.facebook.com/share/p/1DMYqVwzhq/):

**Bottom line**: A 7-day, 6-night family trip to Bali during the rainy season (January) is totally doable. The key is to **book a private villa in Ubud as a rainy-day base**, arrange a private driver to plan routes that avoid traffic jams, and pack mosquito repellent and stomach medicine (to prevent Bali belly). You can still enjoy outdoor activities like the Tirta Empul Temple cultural experience and ATV rides.

From January 11 to 17, 2026, our family of all ages embarked on a joyful Bali family trip. Before departure, many friends warned me about the rainy season and traffic congestion. Now that the trip is over, it's time to share my real experience and reflections.

First, about the **traffic issue** that everyone worries about: my conclusion is **it's much better than expected!** Since we were a large group, we rented a 14-seat minibus. Throughout the trip, we only encountered slow traffic for about two hours when entering a tourist-heavy area; the rest of the time, we moved smoothly. This taught me that in Bali, **choosing a private driver and planning the route wisely** can effectively avoid most traffic nightmares.

![暴雨來襲，趕快開車離開海邊！！經過一些低窪地區，路面都變成河流](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_f4f9f96d6c813ec68ba40960047d0ffa.jpg)

Heavy rain hit, so we quickly drove away from the beach!! Some low-lying areas turned into rivers on the road.

Now, let's talk about the **challenges and surprises of the rainy season**. Yes, we experienced intermittent rain on five out of seven days. But the rainy season isn't all bad—it actually brought some perks: cooler, more comfortable temperatures, lush and refreshing greenery, and fewer tourists, so we avoided the crowds. Of course, sudden downpours require quick adaptation (like the flood scene in the photo where we 'fled' from the beach—now it's just a funny memory 🤣🤣). Interestingly, the weather forecast seemed unreliable. For example, in Kuta, we received a **heavy rain warning**, but the two days we were there were mostly **sunny**, with only occasional evening showers. This taught us that in Bali, it's better to stay flexible than to worry about the weather. Our strategy was to always have a **rainy-day backup plan**, like switching to indoor attractions, museums, galleries, or markets, keeping the itinerary adaptable, with the rain becoming a soothing background soundtrack.

> **Quick Reference**: [Bali Rainy Season: What to Do When It Rains in Bali? 60 Rainy-Day Backup Plans](/en/blog/2026-bali-indoor-activities/)

Of course, there were a few things to get used to on this trip: the traffic in the city center isn't very pedestrian-friendly, and crossing the road feels like playing a real-life version of 'Frogger'; the countless zeros on Indonesian rupiah bills also nearly caused my brain's CPU to overheat and smoke during conversions ♨️

## First, let's share our itinerary:

### **Day 1: Arrival in Ubud, a Leisurely Start at the Villa**

After a long flight, we landed smoothly in Bali. The customs process was quicker than expected, and after picking up our luggage, we met our pre-booked private driver. As the car left the airport's hustle and bustle, the scenery gradually shifted from modern buildings to lush rice fields and intricate shrines, reminding us that we had truly arrived on the 'Island of the Gods.'

![第一天：抵達烏布，入住 Villa 的悠閒序幕](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_872fd38d85050291bdc0602b42d36dd9.jpg)

When we reached our booked **Villa** in Ubud, the fatigue of the journey instantly melted away at the sight before us. The villa was nestled in greenery, featuring a private pool and an open-sided bale (gazebo). The kids cheered as soon as they saw the pool, while the adults quickly unloaded the luggage, eager to brew some tea and sit in the pavilion, soaking in Ubud's unique tranquil air. That night, lulled by a natural symphony of crickets and frogs, we ended our relaxed first day, recharging for the adventures ahead.

The kids were curious about this 'lining up in the water' ritual, and we explained its religious significance—it was a great cultural experience.

We firmly remembered the rule: 'no eating, no staring, no touching,' observing their behavior from a distance. The kids were both nervous and thrilled. Luckily, the monkeys here were much tamer! Still, it was a vivid lesson in respecting wildlife.

### **Day 4: Exploring the Ancient Villages and Coastal Scenery of the East Coast**

Today, we set our sights on eastern Bali, experiencing a more raw and tranquil side of the island.

- **Tenganan Pegringsingan Ancient Village:** This well-preserved old village (Bali Aga) boasts a unique social structure and traditions. The streets are neatly aligned, exuding a quiet and solemn atmosphere. We watched villagers weaving the famous double-sided fabric, Geringsing, a complex craft with rustic colors. There was no commercial hustle here, only a deep sense of history—strolling through felt like a journey through time.
- **Tirta Gangga Water Palace:** Next, we visited the former royal water palace. It's a **photographer's paradise**! Intricate ponds, fountains, stone carvings, and water lilies complement each other, with plump koi fish swimming in the pools. Hopping across the stone stepping stones in the water was a fun experience for both adults and kids. The entire garden exuded a sense of zen and coolness, making it utterly charming.

The views were incredibly expansive, offering a panorama of azure waters and rocky reefs. Many locals and tourists were picnicking and flying kites here, creating a relaxed vibe. ## **Trip Notes & Practical Tips**

![旅程備忘錄與實用建議](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_2a859b507a199d6500bc5d3d47023c43.png)

Based on our firsthand experience, here are a few tips for those planning a trip in the future:

1. **Currency Exchange:** Locally, **exchanging US dollars for Indonesian Rupiah usually gives you a better rate than using Taiwan dollars or other currencies**. It's recommended to bring USD cash and exchange it at a reputable money changer in town. **Quick Reference:** [Indonesia/Bali Travel Currency Exchange Guide: What currency is used in Indonesia/Bali? How to get the best rate? Where to exchange?](/en/blog/bali-currency-exchange-guide/)
2. **Internet:** The **DJB physical SIM card** we used had very stable signal, with smooth connectivity throughout the trip—navigation, communication, and searching for info all worked without issues. It was quite reliable. **Quick Reference:** [【Essential for Bali Travel】How to Get Internet/SIM Card/eSIM in Bali?](/en/blog/bali-sim-card-esim-roaming-guide/)
3. **Shopping & Price Comparison:** **Supermarket prices aren't always cheaper than street vendors**, especially for souvenirs. Vendors usually allow bargaining, while supermarkets have fixed prices. It's best to compare between the two based on the item before buying.
4. **Transportation:** For short trips within the city or last-minute rides, **ride-hailing apps like Gojek or Grab are very convenient and affordable**, making them a great alternative to hiring a private driver. **Quick Reference:** [Grab or Gojek? Complete Guide to Registering, Using, and Topping Up Gojek & GoPay in Indonesia/Bali](/en/blog/indonesia-gojek-gopay-guide/)
5. **Mosquito & Insect Protection:** Bali has **plenty of flies and mosquitoes**, especially near fields or in the evening. **Insect repellent is an absolute daily essential**—be sure to carry it with you and reapply regularly. **Quick Reference:** [Essential Medicines & Supplies for Bali Travel: How to Buy Insect Repellent & Sunscreen?](/en/blog/bali-travel-medicine-checklist/)
6. **Digestive Health (Proven Effective):** To avoid traveler's stomach issues, we strictly followed these steps, and no one in our group had any digestive problems:
7. - **Take one probiotic every morning on an empty stomach.**

- **Use bottled water for drinking and brushing teeth.**
- Be extra careful when showering to **avoid accidentally swallowing tap water.**
- When eating out, **wipe utensils with alcohol wipes** (especially for kids).
- Choose restaurants that **prioritize hygiene and have good reviews, especially larger establishments.**
- Always **wash hands with soap/hand sanitizer** before meals.
