> The following content is adapted from a travelogue shared by group member [Lin Dinosaur](https://www.facebook.com/share/p/1DMYqVwzhq/):

**Bottom Line First**: A 7-day, 6-night family trip to Bali during the rainy season (January) is totally doable. The key is to **book a private villa in Ubud as a rainy-day base**, ask your driver to plan routes that avoid traffic jams, and pack mosquito repellent and stomach medicine (to prevent Bali belly). You can still enjoy outdoor activities like the Tirta Empul Temple experience and ATV rides.

From January 11 to 17, 2026, our family of all ages embarked on a joyful Bali family trip. Before we left, many friends warned us about the rainy season and traffic jams. Now that the trip is over, it's time to share my real experiences and reflections.

First, about the **traffic issue** everyone worries about: my conclusion is **it's much better than expected!** Since we were a large group, we hired a 14-seater minibus. Throughout the trip, we only encountered slow-moving traffic for about two hours when entering tourist-heavy areas; the rest of the time, we moved smoothly. This taught me that in Bali, **choosing a private car and planning your route wisely** can effectively avoid most traffic nightmares.

![暴雨來襲，趕快開車離開海邊！！經過一些低窪地區，路面都變成河流](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_f4f9f96d6c813ec68ba40960047d0ffa.jpg)

A sudden downpour hit—we quickly drove away from the beach!! In some low-lying areas, the roads turned into rivers.

Now, let's talk about the **challenges and surprises of the rainy season**. Yes, we experienced intermittent rain on five out of seven days. But the rainy season isn't all bad—it actually brought some perks: cooler, more comfortable temperatures, lush and fresh greenery, and fewer tourists, so we avoided the crowds. Of course, sudden heavy rain requires quick adaptation (like the flooded scene in the photo where we 'fled' from the beach—now it's just a funny memory 🤣🤣). Interestingly, weather forecasts seemed unreliable. For instance, in Kuta, we received a **heavy rain warning**, but the two days we were there were mostly **sunny**, with only occasional evening showers. This taught us that in Bali, it's better to stay flexible than to worry about the weather. Our strategy was to always have a **rainy-day backup plan**, like heading to indoor attractions, museums, galleries, or markets, keeping the itinerary adaptable. The sound of rain became a soothing background noise for our journey.

> **Quick Reference**: [Bali Rainy Season: What to Do When It Rains in Bali? 60 Rainy-Day Backup Plans](/en/blog/2026-bali-indoor-activities/)

Of course, there were a few things to get used to on this trip: the traffic in the city center isn't very pedestrian-friendly, crossing the street feels like playing a real-life version of 'Frogger'; and the countless zeros on Indonesian rupiah bills nearly caused my brain's CPU to overheat and smoke while converting ♨️

## First, let's share our itinerary:

### **Day 1: Arrival in Ubud, a Leisurely Start at the Villa**

After a long flight, we landed smoothly in Bali. The customs process was quicker than expected, and after picking up our luggage, we met our pre-booked private driver. As the car left the airport's hustle and bustle, the scenery gradually shifted from modern buildings to lush rice fields and intricate shrines, reminding us that we had truly stepped onto the 'Island of the Gods.'

![第一天：抵達烏布，入住 Villa 的悠閒序幕](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_872fd38d85050291bdc0602b42d36dd9.jpg)

When we arrived at our booked **Villa** in Ubud, the fatigue of the journey instantly melted away at the sight before us. The villa was nestled in greenery, featuring a private pool and an open-sided bale (gazebo). The kids cheered at the sight of the pool, while the adults quickly unloaded luggage, eager to brew some tea and sit in the pavilion, soaking in Ubud's unique tranquil air. That night, lulled by a natural symphony of insects and frogs, we ended our relaxed first day, recharging for the adventures ahead.

Watching local devotees and international visitors line up in the purification pools to bathe and pray, their expressions focused and devout, brought a sense of peace to our hearts. The kids were curious about this 'lining up in the water' ritual, and we explained its religious significance—it was a great cultural experience.

- **Delta Supermarket:** Refreshed after the massage, we headed to Delta Supermarket for some shopping. The store had a wide selection, from fresh produce and snacks to daily necessities. - **Dinner: Instant Noodles at Our Villa**
  It might sound simple, but in the villa's kitchen, the whole family cooked noodles together, adding eggs and vegetables, then sat around the table chatting about the day's adventures. **This unplanned, everyday moment became one of the warmest and most memorable parts of the trip.** Relaxed, carefree, and stress-free—it was the perfect end to a fulfilling day.

Today's itinerary was full of contrasts and surprises—from forests to rice terraces, ancient trees to palaces, experiencing Bali's many facets.

We firmly remembered the rule: 'no eating, no staring, no touching,' observing their behavior from a distance. The kids were both tense and thrilled. Luckily, the monkeys here were much tamer! Still, it was a vivid lesson in respecting wildlife.

### **Day 4: Exploring the Rustic Villages and Coastal Scenery of the East Coast**

Today, we set our sights on eastern Bali, experiencing a more pristine and tranquil side.

- **Tenganan Pegringsingan Ancient Village:** This well-preserved traditional village (Bali Aga) boasts a unique social structure and customs. The streets are neatly aligned, exuding a quiet and solemn atmosphere. We watched villagers weaving the famous double-sided fabric 'Geringsing,' a complex craft with rustic colors. There's no commercial hustle here, only a profound sense of history—strolling through feels like a journey through time.
- **Tirta Gangga Water Palace:** Next, we visited the former royal water palace. It's a **photographer's paradise**! Exquisite ponds, fountains, stone carvings, and water lilies complement each other, with plump koi fish swimming in the pools. Hopping across the stone stepping stones in the water is a fun experience for both adults and kids. The entire garden exudes a zen-like serenity and coolness, making it utterly charming.

- **Bukit Asah Desa Bugbug Seaside:** Finally, we headed to this hidden seaside gem on the east coast. It's not a sandy beach but a grassy coastal hill (Bukit) with expansive views overlooking the azure sea and rocky reefs. Many locals and tourists leisurely picnic and fly kites here, creating a relaxed vibe. We waited quietly for the sunset, experiencing a coastal atmosphere completely different from Kuta Beach.

### **Day 5: The Perfect Balance of Speed, Thrills, and Leisurely Shopping**

On the fifth day of the trip, we arranged the activity the younger members were most looking forward to.

- **ATV Off-Road Experience:** This was the day's highlight! We headed to the ATV course, and after a brief briefing from the instructor, we rode into the mountains, through fields, and along muddy trails. Crossing streams, climbing slopes, and diving down, the natural scenery along the way was incredibly pristine and beautiful. **For those who love thrills and adventure, this is an absolute 'must-recommend' activity!** Though we ended up covered in mud, the feeling of speeding freely and laughing out loud became one of the most vivid memories of the trip.

## **Trip Notes and Practical Tips**

Based on our firsthand experience, we've summarized a few tips for those planning a future trip:

1. **Currency Exchange:** Locally, **exchanging US dollars for Indonesian Rupiah usually offers a better rate than using Taiwanese dollars or other currencies.** It's recommended to bring USD cash and exchange it at a reputable money changer in the city. **Quick Reference:** [Indonesia/Bali Travel Currency Exchange Guide: What currency is used in Indonesia/Bali? How to get the best exchange rate? Where to exchange money?](/en/blog/bali-currency-exchange-guide/)
2. **Internet:** The **DJB physical SIM card** we used had a very stable signal, with smooth connectivity throughout the trip. Navigation, communication, and checking information were all hassle-free—very reliable. **Quick Reference:** [【Essential for Bali Travel】How to Get Internet/SIM Card/eSIM in Bali?](/en/blog/bali-sim-card-esim-roaming-guide/)
3. **Shopping Price Comparison:** **Supermarket prices aren't always cheaper than street vendors',** especially for souvenirs. Vendors usually allow bargaining, while supermarkets have fixed prices. It's best to compare between the two based on the item before buying.
4. **Transportation:** For short trips within the city or last-minute rides, **ride-hailing apps like Gojek or Grab are very convenient and affordable,** making them a great alternative to hiring a driver. **Quick Reference:** [Grab or Gojek? A Complete Guide to Registering, Using, and Topping Up Gojek and GoPay in Indonesia/Bali](/en/blog/indonesia-gojek-gopay-guide/)
5. **Mosquito and Insect Repellent:** Bali has **quite a few flies and mosquitoes,** especially near fields or in the early evening. **Insect repellent is an absolute daily essential**—be sure to carry it with you and reapply regularly. **Quick Reference:** [Essential Medicines and Supplies for Bali Travel: How to Buy Insect Repellent and Sunscreen?](/en/blog/bali-travel-medicine-checklist/)
6. **Digestive Health (Proven Effective):** To avoid traveler's stomach issues, we strictly followed these steps, and no one in our group experienced any discomfort:
7. - **Take a probiotic every morning on an empty stomach.**

- **Use bottled water for drinking and brushing your teeth.**
- Be extra careful when showering to **avoid accidentally swallowing tap water.**
- When eating out, **clean utensils with alcohol wipes** (especially for children).
- Choose restaurants that **prioritize hygiene and have good reviews, especially larger establishments.**
- Always **wash hands with soap or hand sanitizer** before meals.
