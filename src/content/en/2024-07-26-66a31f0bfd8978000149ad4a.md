## The Origin and History of Candi Bentar

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_c95607d6aa39c056b9b5f30917cf982a.jpg)

Have you ever noticed those unique traditional entrance structures without roofs while traveling in Bali or Java? These structures, known as Candi Bentar, are important symbols of local culture and history. Candi Bentar, meaning 'split temple gate,' is a distinctive traditional architectural form primarily used as entrances from outer courtyards to inner courtyards in temples, palaces, and other significant sites. These structures consist of two symmetrical buildings without a connecting roof, only linked at the base by steps. Candi Bentar is a common traditional architectural feature in Bali, originating from ancient Indonesian religious and cultural traditions, reflecting Bali's rich historical and artistic heritage. Although Hinduism has greatly influenced Bali's religion and architectural style, the specific form and design of Candi Bentar are products of Bali's local culture.

## The Religious and Cultural Significance of Candi Bentar

### Its Role in Balinese Religion

In Balinese religious life, Candi Bentar plays a crucial role. It marks the boundary between the mundane world and the sacred realm, serving as a necessary passage for people entering temples or palaces.

The design of Candi Bentar is a symbolic separation, representing the transition from the secular world to the sacred realm. They typically consist of two symmetrical tower-like structures, intended to create a sense of transition as people pass through the gate, as if moving from one world to another.

## The Role of Candi Bentar in Modern Bali

### Modern Applications and Variations

Despite being a traditional structure, Candi Bentar is still widely used in modern Bali and has undergone contemporary adaptations. For instance, you can spot Candi Bentar at Bali's airport, as well as in various hotels and resorts.

### Cultural Preservation and Development

The Balinese place great importance on preserving their cultural heritage. As a symbol of Balinese culture, Candi Bentar has been well-maintained and restored, continuing to play a role in new architectural projects.

## Why "Gate of Good and Evil" is a Mistranslation

![中文翻譯成"善惡門"是錯的!!](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0ec623b604c3e6b65802ef0969f02a4e.jpg)

The Chinese translation "Gate of Good and Evil" is incorrect!!

### Linguistic Explanation

Many people refer to it as the "Gate of Good and Evil," but from a linguistic perspective, this translation is inaccurate. It imposes a moral judgment on Candi Bentar, suggesting consequences for wrongdoing, which is not part of its original meaning.

Additionally, according to Balinese Hindu beliefs, spirits and evil entities can only move in straight lines and cannot turn or navigate around obstacles. The design of Candi Bentar typically consists of two symmetrical, separate tower-like structures with a gap between them. This design prevents spirits and evil entities from passing through, effectively stopping them in their tracks.

### Architectural Design Details

In the design of Candi Bentar, certain common details also serve protective functions. For example:

1. **Reliefs and Carvings**: The reliefs and carvings on the tower-like structures often depict Hindu deities, mythological stories, and protective symbols. These carvings are believed to possess the power to ward off evil.
2. **Gate Structure**: Candi Bentar is not a completely enclosed gate but has certain gaps. These gaps symbolize the transition into sacred space and form an invisible barrier that prevents ghosts and evil spirits from entering.

To more accurately convey the meaning of Candi Bentar, one might consider using terms like **'Yin-Yang Gate'** or **'Sacred Gate'**. This preserves its sacred connotations while avoiding the misinterpretations that moral judgments might introduce.

## Conclusion

Although Candi Bentar originated in ancient times, its design and aesthetic principles continue to be applied in modern architecture. Many contemporary architects incorporate these traditional elements into new designs, creating works that blend historical and modern sensibilities.

## **Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)**

**1. What is the historical background of Candi Bentar?** Candi Bentar dates back to the Majapahit era, initially used for religious and royal ceremonies, and gradually became an integral part of local architectural style.

**2. Why does Candi Bentar lack a roof?** The roofless design of Candi Bentar symbolizes a passage between heaven and earth, emphasizing the independence and connectivity of the structures on either side.

**3. Where can Candi Bentar be seen in Bali?** Candi Bentar can be found at many significant temples in Bali, such as Tirta Empul Temple in Ubud, Lempuyang Temple (the 'Gates of Heaven'), Besakih Temple, and Tanah Lot Temple.

**4. What is the difference between Candi Bentar and Paduraksa?** Candi Bentar lacks a roof and serves as the entrance to the outer areas of temples, while Paduraksa has a roof and is typically used for inner temple entrances.

5. **How is Candi Bentar correctly pronounced?**

The correct pronunciation of Candi Bentar is 'chan-dee Ben-tar'.

6. ****Can I visit Candi Bentar?****

Yes, many non-commercialized Candi Bentar structures are located in public temples and palaces, where visitors can admire their architectural beauty.
