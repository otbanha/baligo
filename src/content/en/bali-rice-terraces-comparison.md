## Introduction to Bali's Rice Terraces: Exploring Two Iconic Areas

Bali is renowned not only for its stunning beaches, vibrant cultural activities, and rich natural landscapes but also for its iconic rice terraces. These unique rice paddies offer not just breathtaking views but also a glimpse into the local farming culture and social structure. **Bali's two major rice terrace areas—Tegalalang Rice Terraces** and **Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**—are considered world wonders, allowing visitors to delve deep into Balinese rural life and experience the serene beauty of nature.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_681188d17bd939f1995e2b811dd9ea8d.jpg)

## **What Are Bali's Rice Terraces?**

Bali has rice terraces, and so does Taiwan! So why travel all the way to Bali to see them? Behind Bali's rice terraces lies an ancient and intricate irrigation system—the **Subak System**. Dating back to the 11th century, the Subak System has been recognized by UNESCO as a **World Cultural Heritage**. It is not only the foundation of local agriculture but also an integral part of Balinese culture and social structure.

Below, we’ll dive into the unique features and best ways to explore these two major rice terrace areas.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_4e4eb6216ccf69fd8413b958ab5010ec.png)

## **Tegalalang Rice Terrace**

Located north of Ubud, Tegalalang Rice Terrace is one of Bali's most iconic tourist attractions. Its grand yet picturesque terraced landscapes and abundant surrounding activities have drawn countless visitors, making it a must-visit spot for photography enthusiasts and nature lovers alike.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_04c9efaa4e64a1161cd73552f0fa0eaf.jpg)

### **Features of Tegalalang Rice Terrace**

The Tegalalang Rice Terraces are most renowned for their **stunning landscapes and unique geographical location**. The terraces vary in height and are mostly situated on hillsides. The Subak irrigation system ensures water flows smoothly through each layer of rice paddies, creating beautiful reflective effects, especially enchanting during sunrise and sunset. Additionally, the surroundings of Tegalalang are lush with diverse tropical plants, making the verdant scenery feel like a green paradise.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_4b230e91b2e502eebc023b2f37cd16aa.jpg)

### **Tips for Visiting Tegalalang Rice Terraces**

- **Visit in the morning or evening**: It's recommended to visit during early morning or late afternoon when the light is softest, offering the best visual experience. The cooler temperatures during these times also avoid the intense midday sun.
- **Opening hours**: Daily from 6:00 AM to 7:00 PM.
- **Join a local tour**: Opt for a local guided tour to learn about the history and workings of the Subak system and gain deeper insights into the local rice farming culture.
- **Try the swing**: The 'Bali Giant Swing' near Tegalalang Rice Terraces has become a popular photo spot. Experience the thrill of swinging while enjoying the magnificent terrace views.
- **Be cautious on the paths**: The trails at Tegalalang are quite steep, so wear comfortable shoes and watch your step.

### **Tickets and Transportation to Tegalalang Rice Terraces**

Currently, the entrance fee for Tegalalang Rice Terraces ranges from IDR 10,000 to IDR 50,000 (depending on different areas and ticket booths). From Ubud city center, it's convenient to reach by car or motorbike, taking approximately 20 to 30 minutes.

## **Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_03240ac24d35267825628f03033462da.jpg)

Located in central Bali, the Jatiluwih Rice Terraces are a vast and well-preserved rice field area. Compared to Tegalalang, Jatiluwih offers more expansive and endless terrace views, allowing visitors to immerse themselves in a magnificent green ocean. The scenery here is rustic and ideal for travelers who seek tranquility and natural beauty.

### **Features of Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_489574c7808e044a63eb6e36357de053.jpg)

The Jatiluwih Rice Terraces have been designated as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**, showcasing the essence of the Subak irrigation system through their natural and harmonious layout. Spread across vast fields surrounded by mountains, the terraces exude a serene and tranquil atmosphere. The rice paddies display a variety of colors throughout the seasons, from lush green to golden yellow, offering breathtaking natural beauty no matter when you visit.

### **Tips for Exploring Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

- **Circular Trail Hiking**: Jatiluwih Rice Terraces feature several trails of varying lengths, perfect for hiking. You can choose any trail and stroll through the rice fields, enjoying the fresh air and stunning natural scenery.
- **Bike Rentals**: Local bike rental services are available, allowing you to cycle through the terraces while soaking in the views. This activity is particularly popular among foreign tourists.
- **Savor Local Cuisine**: Nearby traditional restaurants serve Balinese dishes, where you can enjoy a meal while admiring the terraces and experiencing local hospitality.

### **Ticket Prices and Transportation for Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

The entrance fee for Jatiluwih Rice Terraces is approximately IDR 40,000. From major tourist areas in Bali like Seminyak or Kuta, it takes about 2 hours by car. It’s recommended to hire a private car or join a local tour to simplify transportation.

## **Comparison Between Tegallalang and Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

Tegallalang and Jatiluwih Rice Terraces each have their unique charm. Tegallalang is closer to Ubud, making it ideal for short visits, while Jatiluwih is more expansive and suited for travelers who enjoy in-depth exploration and serene natural settings. Whichever terraces you choose, you’ll experience Bali’s distinctive agricultural landscapes and rich cultural heritage.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_ac65861b32e9c3e850c3f44aff108470.jpg)

## **Best Seasons to Visit and Recommendations**

Bali's rice terraces are beautiful year-round, with each season offering a unique view. Generally, the dry season (May to September) is the best time to visit, as the weather is pleasant and sunny, and the terraces are at their most spectacular.

From the perspective of rice growth, **February to April** is when the young rice plants emerge, covering the terraces in a soft green-yellow hue, resembling a beautiful green carpet.

During **June to July** (known locally as Sasih Sada), farmers begin harvesting, and you'll witness them busily working in the fields, celebrating the rice harvest. This is the perfect time to experience local agricultural life.

However, these timings are not fixed, as local farmers adjust based on weather and rice growth conditions.

## **Photography Tips: How to Capture the Beauty of Rice Terraces**

- **Choose the golden hour**: The warm, soft light during sunrise or sunset adds depth to your photos. The period from 8 to 10 AM is ideal for shooting, as the sunlight is moderate, creating gentle shadows and avoiding overexposure. Similarly, the golden hour around 4 to 5 PM bathes the terraces in golden hues, creating a dreamy atmosphere.
- **Use a wide-angle lens**: The vast landscapes of the terraces are best captured with a wide-angle lens. If using a smartphone, try the panorama mode to capture the full grandeur.
- **Incorporate foreground and composition**: Include elements like rice stalks or local villagers in your frame to add storytelling and depth. If you encounter farmers at work, seize the moment to capture their labor, adding a sense of life to your photos. You can also photograph nearby temples, Subak irrigation channels, or ask friends in traditional Balinese attire to pose, enhancing the cultural vibe.
- **Play with layers**: The stepped structure of Bali's terraces allows you to create rich layers in your photos by incorporating foreground, midground, and background.
- **Leverage contrasting colors**: The vibrant green rice fields against the blue sky and white clouds create striking contrasts, making for compelling compositions.
- **Experiment with low angles**: Shooting from a crouched position or focusing on rice stalks adds dimension and highlights the intricate details of the fields.
- **Respect local residents**: Always ask for permission before photographing locals (a simple way to ask is: 'Foto, foto?').

## Additional Information: What is the Subak System?

The Subak system mentioned above is Bali's unique traditional irrigation method for rice terraces, with a history spanning over a thousand years. Since the 11th century, Subak has been at the heart of local agriculture and was even designated as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**. It is not merely a technical irrigation system but also a unique cultural symbol that integrates religious beliefs, social structures, and environmental protection. Rooted in Buddhism and water deity worship, the Subak system emphasizes harmony between humans and nature and fosters a spirit of cooperation and sharing within farming communities.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0ca73e6ea28937fc21a3beff6cc1905f.jpg)

The Subak system is collectively managed by local farmers to ensure fair distribution of water resources across each terrace area. Farmers hold meetings through Subak organizations to coordinate water allocation and conduct ceremonies each planting season to express gratitude to the water and mountain deities. The Subak system also effectively prevents water disputes and strengthens solidarity among farming communities.

![有名的Kanto Lampo瀑布並不是一個自然形成的瀑布，是因為蘇巴克灌溉系統的水溢出而形成](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_9ce193a07d5b914c7677aa9920e7b859.jpg)

The famous Kanto Lampo Waterfall is not a natural formation but rather the result of overflow from the Subak irrigation system.

This spot is a must-visit for capturing stunning photos! Check out Jay's [Kanto Lampo Waterfall Guide](https://youtu.be/-0KOzA9nPGY) for more.

### Water Sources of the Subak System

The Subak system primarily draws water from Bali's volcanic mountains, particularly Mount Bratan and Mount Batur/Mount Agung. These volcanic regions receive abundant rainfall, providing a stable water source for local streams and rivers. The Subak system ingeniously utilizes these natural water flows, channeling them to distribute water across each terrace layer, ensuring stable rice cultivation.

### Water Distribution Process

1. **Mountain Water Sources**: Rainwater collects in lakes within the volcanic mountains and flows downstream through rivers.
2. **Main Irrigation Channels**: The Subak system uses rivers and streams to construct main irrigation channels that direct water to the terraces.
3. **Distribution Channels**: The main water flow is allocated to each terrace area, with distribution channels ensuring even water distribution across each layer.
4. **Lakes Supplying Two Major Terrace Areas**: The Tegalalang Terraces primarily receive water from Lake Batur (Danau Batur), while the Jatiluwih Terraces are mainly supplied by Lake Bratan (Danau Beratan) near the Water Temple.

![水神廟所在的布拉坦湖供應著賈提魯維梯田區的灌溉用水](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_cb2b1f1de756cb57dc9c603d2552ddba.jpg)

Lake Bratan, where the Water Temple is located, provides irrigation water for the Jatiluwih Terrace area.

### The Sacred Water Ritual

Subak is not merely a technical irrigation system; it is deeply intertwined with local religious beliefs. Farmers pray to the water deity at the start of the planting season, conducting ceremonial rituals to express gratitude for the natural water sources and to pray for a bountiful harvest. These rituals symbolize the farmers' reverence and gratitude towards nature, adding a mystical aura to the terraced landscape.
