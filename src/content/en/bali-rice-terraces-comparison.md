## Introduction to Bali's Rice Terraces: Exploring the Two Major Terrace Regions

**Bottom line first**: **Tegalalang** is closer to Ubud, easy to reach, but more commercialized (including the famous Instagram swings). **Jatiluwih** is a UNESCO World Heritage site with more expansive and pristine scenery, but it's farther away and less crowded. Choose Tegalalang for Instagram-worthy photos, and Jatiluwih for stunning nature and fewer crowds. For both, it's best to avoid visiting during midday.

Bali is famous not only for its stunning beaches, vibrant cultural events, and rich natural landscapes, but its rice terraces are also iconic attractions. The unique stepped rice paddies offer not just a visual feast but also a glimpse into the local farming culture and social structure. **Bali's two major terrace regions—Tegalalang Rice Terraces** and **Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**—are world wonders that allow visitors to delve into Balinese rural life and experience the serene beauty of nature firsthand.

## **What Are Bali's Rice Terraces?**

Bali has rice terraces, and so does Taiwan! So why go all the way to Bali to see them? Behind Bali's terraces lies an ancient and complex irrigation system known as the **Subak system**. Originating in the 11th century, the Subak system has been recognized by UNESCO as a **World Cultural Heritage site**. It is not only the foundation of local agriculture but also an integral part of Balinese culture and social structure.

Below, we dive into the unique features of these two major terrace regions and the best ways to explore them.

## **Tegalalang Rice Terrace**

Located north of Ubud, the Tegalalang Rice Terrace is one of Bali's most iconic tourist attractions. Its stunning and picturesque layered landscapes, along with a wealth of nearby activities, draw countless visitors, making it a must-visit for photography enthusiasts and nature lovers alike.

![德格拉朗梯田（Tegalalang Rice Terrace）](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_04c9efaa4e64a1161cd73552f0fa0eaf.jpg)

### **Highlights of Tegallalang Rice Terraces**

The biggest highlight of the Tegallalang Rice Terraces is their **stunning landscape and unique location**. The terraces vary in height and are mostly set on hillsides, with the Subak irrigation system allowing water to flow smoothly through each layer of rice paddies, creating beautiful reflective water effects that are especially dreamy at sunrise and sunset. In addition, the area around the terraces is filled with diverse tropical plants, making the lush green scenery feel like a green paradise.

![德格拉朗梯田的特色](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_4b230e91b2e502eebc023b2f37cd16aa.jpg)

### **Tips for Visiting Tegallalang Rice Terraces**

- **Visit in the morning or at dusk**: It's best to go early in the morning or late in the evening, as the light is softer and the visual effects are at their best. Plus, the temperatures are cooler, avoiding the intense midday sun.
- **Opening hours**: 6:00 AM to 7:00 PM daily.
- **Join a local tour**: You can opt for a guided tour to learn about the history and operation of the Subak system, and gain deeper insight into the local rice terrace culture.
- **Try the swing**: The "Bali Giant Swing" next to the Tegallalang Rice Terraces has become a popular Instagram spot. You can experience the thrill of swinging while taking in the magnificent terrace views.
- **Watch your step**: The paths at Tegallalang are quite steep, so wear comfortable shoes and be mindful of your footing.

### **Tegallalang Rice Terraces Tickets and Transportation**

Currently, the entrance fee for Tegallalang Rice Terraces ranges from about 10,000 to 50,000 Indonesian Rupiah (depending on the area and ticket booth). If you're coming from Ubud city center, it's very convenient to get there by car or scooter, taking about 20 to 30 minutes.

## **Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

![賈提魯維梯田（Jatiluwih Rice Terraces）](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_03240ac24d35267825628f03033462da.jpg)

Jatiluwih Rice Terraces are located in central Bali, featuring vast and well-preserved rice fields. Compared to Tegallalang, Jatiluwih is more expansive, with endless terrace views that let visitors wander through a magnificent green ocean. The scenery here is rustic and perfect for travelers who love tranquility and natural beauty.

### **The Highlights of Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

![賈提魯維梯田的特色](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_489574c7808e044a63eb6e36357de053.jpg)

Listed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**, the Jatiluwih Rice Terraces showcase the essence of the Subak irrigation system through their naturally harmonious layout. The terraces here are vast and wide, surrounded by rolling hills, creating a peaceful and serene atmosphere. The rice paddies change color with the seasons, shifting from vibrant green to golden yellow, so no matter when you visit, you can experience the beauty and grandeur of nature.

### **Tips for Visiting Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

- **Circular Trail Hiking**: Jatiluwih offers several trails of varying lengths, perfect for exploring on foot. You can pick any path and wander through the rice paddies, breathing in the fresh air and soaking up the stunning natural scenery.
- **Bike Rental**: Local bike rentals are available, allowing you to cycle through the terraces while exercising and enjoying the views. This option is especially popular among foreign tourists.
- **Savor Local Cuisine**: Several traditional restaurants nearby serve Balinese dishes, where you can dine while overlooking the terraces and experiencing the warm hospitality of the locals.

### **Jatiluwih Rice Terraces: Tickets and Transportation**

The entrance fee for Jatiluwih Rice Terraces is around 50,000 Indonesian Rupiah. From major tourist areas in Bali like Seminyak or Kuta, it takes about 2 hours by car. It's recommended to hire a private driver or join a local tour to save the hassle of transportation.

## **Comparing Tegalalang and Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

Tegalalang and Jatiluwih each have their own charm. Tegalalang is closer to Ubud, making it ideal for travelers with limited time, while Jatiluwih is more expansive, perfect for those who love deep exploration and tranquil nature. Whichever terrace you choose, you'll experience Bali's unique agricultural landscape and rich cultural heritage.

![德格拉朗與賈提魯維梯田的比較](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_ac65861b32e9c3e850c3f44aff108470.jpg)

### Best Time to Visit and Tips

Bali's rice terraces are actually **beautiful year-round**, showcasing stunning scenery at different stages. However, it's important to note that the two major terraces have very different rice-growing ecosystems:

- **Tegalalang Rice Terraces (multi-crop system):** Farmers here stagger their planting throughout the year, so no matter which month you visit, you'll almost always see a mix of vibrant green seedlings, golden rice stalks, or water-filled paddies reflecting the sky—there's no real off-season.
- **Jatiluwih Rice Terraces (traditional red rice, one to two harvests per year):** This area preserves the most traditional farming calendar. Typically, **from February to April**, the rice seedlings grow rapidly, blanketing the entire valley in a carpet of fresh green. By **June to July** (locally known as Sasih Sada), the rice turns golden, marking the busy harvest season—the perfect time to experience traditional agricultural abundance.

**In summary:** If you want to avoid afternoon thunderstorms and capture the terraces under clear blue skies, the **dry season from May to September** offers the most stable weather for your visit. However, nature's rhythm isn't set in stone; local farmers still adjust their planting schedules based on rainfall and climate each year, making every visit a unique encounter!

## **Photography Tips: How to Capture the Beauty of the Rice Terraces**

- **Choose the golden hours:** The warm, soft light at sunrise or sunset adds depth to your images. The prime shooting window is from 8 to 10 AM, when the sun angle is just right, creating gentle shadows and avoiding harsh overexposure. Around 4 to 5 PM, the golden hour casts a warm glow over the terraces, turning the layered paddies a dreamy shade of gold.
- **Use a wide-angle lens:** The expansive landscape of the terraces is perfect for a wide-angle lens, allowing you to capture the full grandeur of the layered fields. If you're using a phone, try the panorama mode to get the sweeping view.
- **Incorporate foreground and composition:** Include foreground elements like rice stalks or local farmers to add storytelling and depth to your photos. If you happen to catch farmers at work, seize the moment—their sweat and effort bring a rich sense of life to the image. You can also shoot nearby small temples, Subak irrigation channels, or ask a friend in traditional Balinese attire to pose, enhancing the cultural vibe.
- **Leverage layering:** The hallmark of Bali's terraces is their stepped structure. Use foreground, midground, and background to create a sense of depth in your photos.
- **Play with contrasting colors:** The vivid green of the rice paddies against the blue sky and white clouds makes for a striking composition you shouldn't miss.
- **Try low-angle shots:** Crouch down or shoot the rice stalks from a low angle to add dimension and highlight the intricate details of the fields.
- **Respect the locals:** If you want to photograph farmers or villagers, always ask for permission first (a simple way to ask is: "Foto, foto?").

## Bonus: What Is the Subak System?

The Subak system mentioned above is Bali's unique traditional rice terrace irrigation method, with a history spanning over a thousand years. Since the 11th century, Subak has been the core of local agriculture and has been designated a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**. It is not merely a technical irrigation system but a unique cultural symbol that integrates religious beliefs, social structure, and environmental protection. Based on Buddhist and water deity beliefs, the Subak system emphasizes harmony between humans and nature, fostering a spirit of cooperation and sharing among farming communities.

![額外補充: 什麼是蘇巴克系統？](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0ca73e6ea28937fc21a3beff6cc1905f.jpg)

The Subak system is collectively managed by local farmers, ensuring fair water distribution across every terrace. Farmers hold meetings through Subak organizations to coordinate water allocation and perform ceremonies each planting season to thank the water and mountain gods for their blessings. This system effectively prevents water disputes and strengthens solidarity among farming communities.

![有名的Kanto Lampo瀑布並不是一個自然形成的瀑布，是因為蘇巴克灌溉系統的水溢出而形成](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_9ce193a07d5b914c7677aa9920e7b859.jpg)

The famous Kanto Lampo Waterfall is not a natural waterfall; it is formed by overflow from the Subak irrigation system.

This spot is a must-visit for taking stunning photos! Check out Xiao Jie's [Kanto Lampo Waterfall guide](https://youtu.be/-0KOzA9nPGY) for more details.

### The Water Source of Subak

The Subak system primarily draws water from Bali's volcanic mountain ranges, especially Mount Bratan, Mount Batur, and Mount Agung. These volcanic areas receive abundant rainfall, providing a steady water supply to local streams and rivers. The entire Subak system cleverly utilizes these natural water flows, channeling water to each terrace level to ensure stable rice growth.

### Water Distribution Process
1. **Mountain Water Sources**: Rainwater collects in lakes on volcanic mountains and flows downhill through rivers.
2. **Main Irrigation Channels**: The Subak system uses rivers and streams to build main irrigation channels that bring water into the terraces.
3. **Distribution Channels**: The main water flow is allocated to each terrace area, with distribution channels ensuring every level receives water evenly.
4. **Lakes Supplying Two Major Terrace Areas**: The Tegalalang terraces are primarily supplied by Lake Batur (Danau Batur), while the Jatiluwih terraces are mainly fed by Lake Beratan (Danau Beratan) at the Ulun Danu Temple.

Lake Beratan, home to the Ulun Danu Temple, supplies irrigation water to the Jatiluwih terrace area.

### Sacred Water Rituals

### 神聖的水祭儀式

Subak is not merely a technical irrigation system; it is deeply intertwined with local religious beliefs. At the start of each planting season, farmers pray to the water gods and hold ceremonial offerings to thank nature for the gift of water and pray for a bountiful harvest. These rituals symbolize the farmers' reverence and gratitude toward nature, adding a layer of mystique to the terraced landscape.
