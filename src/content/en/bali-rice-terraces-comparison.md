## Introduction to Bali's Rice Terraces: Exploring Two Major Terrace Areas

Bali is renowned not only for its stunning beaches, vibrant cultural activities, and rich natural landscapes but also for its iconic rice terraces. These unique rice paddies offer not only spectacular visual delights but also showcase local agricultural culture and social structures. **Bali's two major rice terrace areas—Tegalalang Rice Terraces** and **Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**—are considered world wonders, allowing visitors to delve deep into Balinese rural life and experience the serene beauty of nature.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_681188d17bd939f1995e2b811dd9ea8d.jpg)

## **What Are Bali's Rice Terraces?**

Bali has rice terraces, and so does Taiwan! So why travel all the way to Bali to see them? Behind Bali's rice terraces lies an ancient and intricate irrigation system—the **Subak System**. Originating in the 11th century, the Subak System has been designated as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**. It is not only the cornerstone of local agriculture but also an integral part of Balinese culture and social structure.

Below, we will delve into the features and best ways to explore these two major rice terrace areas.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_4e4eb6216ccf69fd8413b958ab5010ec.png)

## **Tegalalang Rice Terrace**

Located north of Ubud, Tegalalang Rice Terrace is one of Bali's most iconic tourist attractions. Its magnificent and picturesque terraced landscapes, along with a variety of surrounding activities, draw countless visitors, making it a must-visit spot for photography enthusiasts and nature lovers.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_04c9efaa4e64a1161cd73552f0fa0eaf.jpg)

### **Features of Tegalalang Rice Terrace**

The Tegalalang Rice Terraces are renowned for their **stunning landscapes and unique geographical location**. The terraces vary in height and are mostly situated on hillsides. The Subak irrigation system ensures water flows smoothly through each layer of rice fields, creating beautiful reflections, especially during sunrise and sunset, making the scene dreamlike and enchanting. Additionally, the terraces are surrounded by diverse tropical plants, immersing visitors in a lush green paradise.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_4b230e91b2e502eebc023b2f37cd16aa.jpg)

### **Tips for Visiting Tegalalang Rice Terraces**

- **Visit in the Morning or Evening**: It’s best to visit during early morning or late afternoon when the light is soft and the visual effects are most striking. The cooler temperatures during these times also make the visit more comfortable compared to the midday heat.
- **Opening Hours**: Daily from 6:00 AM to 7:00 PM.
- **Join a Local Tour**: Consider joining a local guided tour to learn about the history and workings of the Subak system and gain deeper insights into the local rice farming culture.
- **Try the Swing**: The 'Bali Giant Swing' near the Tegalalang Rice Terraces has become a popular Instagram spot. Experience the thrill of swinging while enjoying the breathtaking views of the terraces.
- **Be Cautious on the Paths**: The trails around the terraces can be steep, so wear comfortable shoes and watch your step.

### **Ticket Prices and Transportation for Tegalalang Rice Terraces**

Currently, the entrance fee for Tegalalang Rice Terraces ranges from 10,000 to 50,000 Indonesian Rupiah (depending on the area and ticket booth). From Ubud, it’s convenient to reach by car or motorbike, taking approximately 20 to 30 minutes.

## **Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_03240ac24d35267825628f03033462da.jpg)

Located in central Bali, the Jatiluwih Rice Terraces are a vast and well-preserved rice field area. Compared to Tegalalang, Jatiluwih offers a more expansive and endless landscape of terraces, allowing visitors to immerse themselves in a magnificent green ocean. The scenery here is rustic and perfect for travelers who seek tranquility and natural beauty.

### **Highlights of Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_489574c7808e044a63eb6e36357de053.jpg)

The Jatiluwih Rice Terraces, listed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**, showcase the essence of the Subak irrigation system through their natural and harmonious layout. Spread across vast fields surrounded by mountains, the terraces exude a serene and tranquil atmosphere. The rice fields display a variety of color changes throughout the seasons, from lush green to golden yellow, offering visitors a chance to experience the beauty and grandeur of nature at any time of year.

### **Tips for Visiting Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

- **Circular Trail Hiking**: Jatiluwih Rice Terraces feature several trails of varying lengths, perfect for hiking. You can choose any trail to stroll through the rice fields, enjoying the fresh air and stunning natural scenery.
- **Bike Rentals**: Local bike rental services are available, allowing you to cycle through the terraces while exercising and taking in the views. This method is particularly popular among foreign tourists.
- **Savor Local Cuisine**: Nearby traditional restaurants offer Balinese dishes, allowing you to enjoy the terrace views while dining and experiencing local hospitality.

### **Ticket Prices and Transportation to Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

The entrance fee to Jatiluwih Rice Terraces is approximately 40,000 IDR. From major tourist areas in Bali like Seminyak or Kuta, it takes about 2 hours by car. It’s recommended to hire a private car or join a local tour to save on transportation hassles.

## **Comparing Tegallalang and Jatiluwih Rice Terraces**

Tegallalang and Jatiluwih Rice Terraces each have their unique charm. Tegallalang is closer to Ubud, making it ideal for short visits, while Jatiluwih is more expansive, perfect for travelers who enjoy in-depth exploration and serene natural settings. Whichever terrace you choose, you’ll experience Bali’s unique agricultural landscapes and rich cultural heritage.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_ac65861b32e9c3e850c3f44aff108470.jpg)

## **Best Seasons to Visit and Recommendations**

Bali's rice terraces are beautiful year-round, with each season offering a unique landscape. Generally, the dry season (May to September) is the best time to visit, as the weather is pleasant and sunny, and the terraces are at their most spectacular.

From the perspective of rice growth, **February to April** is when the rice seedlings are growing, covering the terraces in a soft green-yellow hue, resembling a beautiful green carpet.

During **June to July** (locally known as Sasih Sada), farmers begin harvesting, and you’ll witness them busy in the fields, enjoying the sight of abundant rice yields. This is the perfect time to experience local agricultural life.

However, these timings are not fixed, as local farmers adjust based on weather and rice growth conditions.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_598c698b2d2bbe81b87963dc055fb955.jpg)

## **Photography Tips: How to Capture the Beauty of the Terraces**

- **Choose the golden hour**: The warm, soft light during sunrise or sunset adds depth to your photos. The period from 8 to 10 AM is ideal for shooting, as the sunlight is moderate, creating gentle shadows and avoiding overexposure. Similarly, the golden hour around 4 to 5 PM bathes the terraces in golden hues, creating a dreamy atmosphere.
- **Use a wide-angle lens**: The expansive views of the terraces are best captured with a wide-angle lens. If using a smartphone, try the panorama mode to capture the full grandeur.
- **Incorporate foreground and composition**: Include elements like rice stalks or local villagers in your frame to add storytelling and depth. If you encounter farmers at work, seize the moment to capture their labor, adding a sense of life to your photos. You can also photograph nearby temples, Subak irrigation channels, or friends dressed in traditional Balinese attire to enhance the cultural vibe.
- **Play with layers**: The stepped structure of Bali’s terraces allows you to use foreground, midground, and background to create rich layers in your photos.
- **Utilize contrast**: The vibrant green rice fields against the blue sky and white clouds create striking contrasts, making for compelling compositions.
- **Try low-angle shots**: Crouching or shooting from a low angle can add dimensionality and highlight the intricate details of the rice fields.
- **Respect local residents**: If you wish to photograph local farmers or villagers, always ask for permission first (a simple way to ask is: "Foto, foto?").

## Additional Information: What is the Subak System?

The Subak system mentioned above is Bali's unique traditional irrigation method for rice terraces, with a history spanning over a thousand years. Since the 11th century, Subak has become the core of local agriculture and has been designated as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**. It is not merely a technical irrigation system but also a unique cultural symbol that integrates religious beliefs, social structures, and environmental protection. Rooted in Buddhism and water deity worship, the Subak system emphasizes harmony between humans and nature and fosters a spirit of cooperation and sharing within farming communities.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0ca73e6ea28937fc21a3beff6cc1905f.jpg)

The Subak system is collectively managed by local farmers to ensure fair water distribution across each terrace area. Farmers hold meetings through Subak organizations to coordinate water allocation and conduct ceremonies each planting season to express gratitude to the water and mountain deities. The Subak system also effectively prevents water disputes, strengthening unity within farming communities.

![有名的Kanto Lampo瀑布並不是一個自然形成的瀑布，是因為蘇巴克灌溉系統的水溢出而形成](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_9ce193a07d5b914c7677aa9920e7b859.jpg)

The famous Kanto Lampo Waterfall is not a naturally formed waterfall but rather a result of water overflow from the Subak irrigation system.

This spot is a must-visit for capturing stunning photos! Check out Jay's [Kanto Lampo Waterfall Guide](https://youtu.be/-0KOzA9nPGY) for more tips.

### The Water Source of Subak

The Subak system primarily draws water from Bali's volcanic mountains, particularly Mount Bratan and Mount Batur/Mount Agung. These volcanic regions receive abundant rainfall, providing a stable water source for local streams and rivers. The Subak system ingeniously utilizes these natural water flows, channeling them to distribute water across terraced fields, ensuring stable rice cultivation.

### The Water Distribution Process

1. **Mountain Water Sources**: Rainwater collects in lakes within volcanic mountains and flows downstream through rivers.
2. **Main Irrigation Channels**: The Subak system uses rivers and streams to construct main irrigation channels that direct water to the terraces.
3. **Distribution Channels**: The main water flow is allocated to each terrace area, with distribution channels ensuring even water distribution across all levels.
4. **Lakes Supplying Two Major Terrace Areas**: The Tegalalang Terraces are primarily supplied by Lake Batur (Danau Batur), while the Jatiluwih Terraces are mainly irrigated by Lake Beratan (Danau Beratan) near the Ulun Danu Temple.

![水神廟所在的布拉坦湖供應著賈提魯維梯田區的灌溉用水](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_cb2b1f1de756cb57dc9c603d2552ddba.jpg)

Lake Beratan, where the Ulun Danu Temple is located, supplies irrigation water to the Jatiluwih Terrace area.

### The Sacred Water Ritual

Subak is not merely a technical irrigation system; it is deeply intertwined with local religious beliefs. Farmers pray to the water deities at the start of the planting season, conducting ceremonial rituals to express gratitude for the natural water sources and to pray for a bountiful harvest. These rituals symbolize the farmers' reverence and gratitude towards nature, adding an aura of mystique to the terraced landscape.
