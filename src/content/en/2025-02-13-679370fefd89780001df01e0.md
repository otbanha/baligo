**Sharing My Experience Traveling to Bali with My 70+ Year Old Mother from 1/13 to 1/17**

This time, I traveled to Bali with my mother in her 70s, a journey filled with warmth and challenges. From transportation and accommodation to food, every experience deepened my appreciation for Bali's unique charm while teaching me valuable lessons. ## Transportation

Transportation was one of my top concerns when traveling to Bali with my mother. Since many say Bali taxi drivers tend to overcharge tourists, I decided to use Grab for rides. My previous experience with Grab in Thailand was excellent, and Bali was no exception. However, the situation at Bali Airport was chaotic—as soon as we stepped out, we were swarmed by taxi drivers aggressively offering their services, even claiming to be Grab drivers and promising cheaper rates. This was particularly frustrating when traveling with an elderly parent.

My advice is to head left immediately after exiting the airport, where you’ll find a small parking lot where Grab drivers usually wait. This helps avoid the hassle of other taxi drivers and speeds up finding your ride. Still, some drivers may approach you during the wait—just firmly tell them you’ve already booked a car.

During our stay in Bali, we relied almost entirely on Grab. The app’s advantage is the abundance of available cars; you can usually get one within three minutes, no matter where you are. Most drivers speak decent English and are friendly, and fares are strictly based on the app’s pricing, so there’s no risk of being overcharged. The only minor issue is the scarcity of small change in Bali. Sometimes, we didn’t have exact change and simply told the driver to keep the difference, which was usually just a few dollars. The drivers were always appreciative.

That said, Bali’s traffic is truly headache-inducing 🤣. There’s no clear distinction between fast and slow lanes, and motorcycles weave through traffic unpredictably. Watching them dart between cars can make you feel like an accident is imminent. If you’re brave, you might try Grab’s motorcycle service, especially during peak hours when car traffic can be unbearable. Once, we traveled 20 kilometers between two locations, and it took a full two hours—both frustrating and amusing.

Overall, Grab is a reliable transportation option in Bali, especially for travelers with elderly companions—it’s convenient and safe. Just remember that Bali’s traffic is chaotic, so stay alert while driving and be extra cautious when crossing the street, paying attention to motorcycles and cars (unlike in Taiwan).

## Private Car Hire

During our Bali trip, hiring a private car was one of the most convenient and hassle-free transportation options we chose. We found our driver through recommendations in online forums, and the reviews were excellent. After experiencing it firsthand, I can confidently say it was worth every penny. Here are the details of our private car hire experience for your reference.

### Booking and Communication:

We contacted the driver via Line, and the communication was smooth and efficient. First, we informed him about our desired itinerary and the duration of the car hire. Based on our needs, he provided suggestions, listed the total cost, and outlined the itinerary in detail. The day before our trip, he reached out again to confirm that there were no changes to the plan. The best part? Payment was made only after the day's activities were completed, so there was no need to worry about prepayment.

### Itinerary and Costs:

The main purpose of our private car hire was to explore the Ubud area, and afterward, we asked the driver to drop us off at our hotel in another region. The total car hire duration was 10 hours, costing approximately USD$39, which I found very reasonable. Additionally, we asked the driver to arrange activities like ATV riding, river tubing, and jungle swings. He included the costs of these activities in the total price, which we confirmed beforehand. Payment for everything was made in one go at the end of the day, making it super convenient.

## Civet Coffee

---

During our Bali trip, the private car driver took us to a civet coffee plantation, which is one of the "classic experiences" many tourists encounter in Bali. Although this experience felt a bit commercialized, I still wanted to share my thoughts with everyone, so you can have another reference when planning your itinerary.

這次峇里島之旅，包車司機帶我們去了一個麝香貓咖啡園區，這也是許多遊客在峇里島會遇到的「經典行程」之一。雖然這個體驗有點商業化，但還是想跟大家分享一下我的感受，讓大家在安排行程時可以多一個參考。

### Driver's Recommendation:

During our private car tour, the driver casually asked if we wanted to try Kopi Luwak, emphasizing that it's a specialty of Bali and costs only USD$3 per cup, making it well worth a try. Given the affordable price and driven by curiosity, we agreed. The driver then took us to a plantation dedicated to Kopi Luwak.

### Plantation Tour and Tasting:

As soon as we entered the plantation, the staff enthusiastically explained the process of making Kopi Luwak, highlighting how natural and organic their coffee is, and how it undergoes "natural processing" by the civet cats. They guided us through the plantation, showing the relationship between the civet cats (actually palm civets) and the coffee beans, before leading us to the tasting area.

The tasting experience was quite interesting. In addition to Kopi Luwak, they also prepared a variety of other coffees and herbal teas for us to try. Each drink was accompanied by a detailed explanation, such as the flavor profile of the coffee or the benefits of the herbal tea. After finishing the tasting session, it did feel a bit awkward not to buy something.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_b47855e8d20e3f0b3936512c44f670a2.jpg)

### Controversy Surrounding Kopi Luwak:

However, while tasting, I couldn't help but recall the controversies surrounding Kopi Luwak that I had read about online. Many reports point out that these coffee beans are not entirely naturally sourced, as many civet cats are kept in cages and force-fed coffee beans, which is not animal-friendly. Additionally, Kopi Luwak is extremely expensive. While the tasting cup costs only USD$3, the actual purchase price might be prohibitive.

### Purchase Pressure and Actual Experience:

During the tasting, the staff enthusiastically promoted their products right in front of us, which created a bit of pressure. We ended up buying a small pack of mangosteen tea for around USD$13, which wasn't too expensive but also not exactly cheap. As for the Kopi Luwak, it did have a subtle sweetness and a smooth texture, but it didn't strike me as a "must-buy." If you're not particularly passionate about coffee or not interested in such commercialized tours, you can simply tell the private car driver to skip this stop, saving a lot of time.

### Experience Summary:

The civet coffee experience is considered a signature activity in Bali, but the underlying animal welfare issues and commercialized approach can be off-putting. If you're not particularly interested in coffee or don't want to spend too much time on shopping-oriented activities, it's recommended to communicate with your driver in advance to skip this part. After all, Bali has many more worthwhile attractions and activities to explore. Time is precious, so why not save it for creating even more beautiful memories!

## Food

Food was definitely one of the highlights that left a lasting impression on me during this trip to Bali! Bali's culinary culture is incredibly diverse, especially its local specialties, which are truly unforgettable. Below is my detailed sharing of this dining experience, hoping to provide some references for everyone.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_d7966ba428f0a4c7761ad02df03437bc.jpg)

### Dirty Duck Rice and Pork Ribs:

First, I highly recommend Bali's 'Dirty Duck Rice' and 'Pork Ribs.' Dirty Duck Rice is a traditional Balinese dish where the duck is marinated and cooked with spices for a long time, resulting in tender meat paired with fragrant rice, offering rich layers of flavor that keep you coming back for more. As for the pork ribs, they are crispy on the outside and tender on the inside, with a sweet sauce that's not greasy at all, making them perfect for meat lovers.

Interestingly, our driver told us that although most of Indonesia's population is Muslim and doesn't eat pork, over 85% of Bali's population practices Hinduism, so pork dishes are widely available here, which is quite rare in other parts of Indonesia. Additionally, Bali's lifestyle is relatively free, without the many clothing or dietary restrictions found in Islamic regions, making our journey even more relaxed and comfortable. The driver also mentioned that schools in Bali finish at noon, which made me feel that the pace of life here is really leisurely—sounds amazing 🤣.

### Traditional Indonesian Cuisine:

While Dirty Duck Rice and Pork Ribs amazed us, traditional Indonesian cuisine didn't quite hit the mark for our taste buds. We even signed up for a cooking class, spending a morning learning to make six or seven Indonesian dishes. There were a few Korean tourists in the class with us, and after tasting, everyone found the dishes too salty and oily, so we only took a couple of bites before stopping. However, the process of participating in the cooking class was still very fun, as experiencing the local cuisine firsthand is a unique travel experience.

## Currency Exchange

When traveling in Bali, currency exchange is a crucial aspect to pay attention to, especially since the local currency amounts are very large, and it's easy to get confused. Below are my currency exchange tips from this trip, hoping to help everyone handle money matters more smoothly.

### Bringing USD is Most Convenient:

It's recommended to **exchange USD in Taiwan first, then convert it to Indonesian Rupiah in Bali**. This is because the exchange rate for USD in Bali is relatively stable, and all currency exchange offices accept USD. This time, we brought USD to Bali and paid the visa fee directly with USD when applying for the visa on arrival at the airport, asking the staff to give change in Indonesian Rupiah. This way, we had some local currency to use as soon as we landed.

### Airport Exchange vs. City Exchange:

Although there are currency exchange offices at the airport, the rates are usually not as favorable as those in the city. Therefore, we only exchanged a small amount of Indonesian Rupiah for transportation from the airport to the city, and waited until we were in the city to exchange the rest at reputable currency exchange offices.

### Amount Representation and Conversion Tips:

The denominations of Indonesian Rupiah are very large, often reaching hundreds of thousands or even millions, which might be confusing for first-time visitors. Locals commonly represent the last three zeros with a "K". For example, 50,000 Indonesian Rupiah is written as 50K. The simplest way to convert between Indonesian Rupiah and USD is to multiply the number before "K" by 2 and add a little more, which gives you an approximate amount in USD. For example, 50K Indonesian Rupiah is roughly a little over USD$3, making it very convenient for calculations and avoiding mistakes when shopping or paying.

### Precautions:

When exchanging money, always choose reputable currency exchange offices to avoid stalls on the street, which may offer unfair rates or counterfeit bills. Additionally, it's best to count the money on the spot and confirm the amount before leaving. Some exchange offices may charge a fee, so it's advisable to ask about this before proceeding.

## Accommodation

This Bali accommodation experience really highlighted the importance of choosing the right location and surrounding environment. Here are some insights from my stay that might help you make better choices when planning your trip.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_a547a653ea0227a0f9908d938d74da36.jpg)

### Mosquito Issue:

First off, be warned that Bali has a lot of mosquitoes, and they're quite large! The humid climate, combined with the lush gardens and trees that hotels and villas maintain to create a tropical ambiance, also makes them a breeding ground for mosquitoes. Even in the more upscale villa we stayed in, mosquitoes managed to find their way inside. So, **insect repellent is an absolute must**. I recommend carrying it with you at all times, especially during dawn and dusk when mosquitoes are most active.

### Breakfast Service:

While the mosquito issue was a bit of a nuisance, the hotels and guesthouses we stayed at really excelled in their breakfast offerings. The selection was diverse, ranging from local specialties to Western breakfast options, all of high quality. I found breakfast to be particularly important in Bali accommodations because there were hardly any convenience stores or food stalls nearby. If you didn't eat enough at breakfast, you might have to wait until lunchtime to find something to eat. Therefore, I suggest paying special attention to whether breakfast is included when choosing your accommodation, as it can make your trip much more comfortable.

### Choosing the Right Location:

The biggest mistake we made was staying in a location that was quite far from the bustling areas, with almost no shops or restaurants nearby. Every time we wanted to go out, we had to call for a ride, which was very inconvenient. Bali's attractions are spread out, and transportation isn't the most convenient. If you're not staying in a central area, just the daily transportation costs can add up significantly. We spent about USD$40 each time we called for a ride, and once we were out, there was no way to come back to grab something or rest, which somewhat limited our itinerary planning.

Therefore, **it's highly recommended to consider the convenience of the surrounding area when choosing accommodation**. If you enjoy a lively atmosphere, you might want to stay in bustling areas like Kuta, Seminyak, or Ubud, where you'll find plenty of restaurants, shops, and evening entertainment. If you opt for a more remote villa or resort, be prepared to rely on Grab delivery or private car services for your daily needs.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_0391757a9b3ffecaea7406bc888c42e0.jpg)

### Accommodation Insights:

While Bali's accommodations are beautiful, mosquito issues and location choices are important considerations. Make sure to bring plenty of mosquito repellent and think carefully about the convenience of your chosen location. Staying in a remote area might increase transportation costs and inconvenience, so keep this in mind when planning your itinerary. We hope these tips help you find the perfect accommodation in Bali, making your trip even more enjoyable!
