### The Indonesian Ministry of Health recently announced stricter screening procedures for Bali and other areas due to the global rise in Mpox (formerly known as monkeypox) cases. Mpox is a highly contagious virus, and everyone needs to be extra cautious.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_69c98b8211e806d7a145a78b20e10aca.jpg)

On August 18, Acting Director General Yudhi Pramono of the Ministry of Health stated at a press conference that all foreign tourists entering Indonesia must now fill out a health questionnaire upon arrival. This questionnaire will inquire about their health status, recent contact history, and recent travel destinations.

The Indonesian government hopes this measure will reduce the risk of an Mpox outbreak in the country.

Pramono further explained, 'We will collect data based on the [travelers'] country of origin. If they feel unwell, we advise them not to continue their journey.' He emphasized that Indonesia remains highly vigilant against this virus and will take all necessary measures to protect public safety.

Additionally, Pramono reminded everyone that the virus's incubation period can last up to 34 days, so heightened vigilance is required at all entry points. Currently, the number of Mpox cases in Indonesia has decreased compared to last year, a positive trend the government aims to maintain.

As of 2024, Indonesia has confirmed 14 Mpox cases, with 74 suspected cases. This is a significant drop compared to the same period in 2023, which saw 73 confirmed cases and 240 suspected cases reported.

On August 14, WHO Director-General Dr. Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus declared the current Mpox outbreak a 'Public Health Emergency of International Concern,' urging countries to take appropriate measures to address the situation.

Currently, the virus has spread beyond the African continent, with confirmed cases reported in Sweden and Pakistan.

You can find more information about Mpox on the World Health Organization's website. If you've had skin-to-skin contact with an infected person or breathed in their breath during close contact, you may contract the virus. Additionally, touching items contaminated with the virus, such as bedding, clothing, or towels, can also lead to infection, as the virus can enter the body through the skin, respiratory tract, nose, mouth, and eyes.

Currently, Indonesia has not implemented any additional preventive measures, and it remains unclear whether further actions will be taken in the coming days or weeks. If you're concerned that you may have been exposed to the virus or are already showing symptoms, you should seek medical attention immediately.

Most people infected with Mpox do not require special treatment and only need simple supportive therapies, typically used to alleviate flu-like symptoms and body pain. In severe cases, antiviral medications may be necessary, and vaccines can be administered before or after exposure to prevent infection.
