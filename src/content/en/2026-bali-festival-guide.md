### March 18–24: Eid al-Fitr Holiday

- March 18–24: Indonesia's Eid al-Fitr (Idul Fitri 1447 Hijriah) — the most important national holiday in the country.

The country takes about ten days off. Eid al-Fitr is Indonesia's most significant holiday, celebrating the end of Ramadan and a time for Indonesians to spend with family and friends, akin to Chinese Lunar New Year.
Expect Bali to start filling up with locals from March 13 onwards, with crowds lasting about two weeks. This marks the first of Bali's five peak seasons — a holiday best avoided!

### **Galungan and Kuningan**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_c8fb868428b648667e4ff2774ba1d282.jpg)

- - **Date:** June 17 and June 27, 2026
    - **Description:** One of Bali's most important religious festivals, celebrating the victory of good over evil and the descent of ancestral spirits. This is one of Bali's most significant festivals, with Galungan on June 17 continuing until Kuningan ten days later, celebrating the creation of the universe and the triumph of good over evil. During this festival, towering penjor decorations bring vibrant energy to the streets, while abundant offerings, rituals, and celebrations offer a glimpse into the island's rich cultural and spiritual heritage. Families honor their ancestors at home, seeking their blessings, while ceremonies are held in temples to pay respects to the deities. Galungan, based on Bali's Pawukon calendar, also marks the day of cosmic creation. During this period, schools in Bali close for two weeks, highlighting its importance! Note that transportation such as ferries, private cars, and taxis may be affected! Many local guides and drivers take a few days off during the main celebrations, so it's best to book in advance. The day after Galungan (Manis Galungan) is when locals visit relatives or go out together, so you may find the roads particularly crowded. Large celebrations can also cause traffic congestion.

### **Bali Arts Festival - Pesta Kesenian Bali** [**Official Website**](https://pestakesenianbali.id/)

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_15ea6a6aa9cd30d097d8adf78a25f3cb.png)

- - **Date:** June 13 - July 11, 2026
    - **Description:** A month-long annual arts event showcasing dance, music, theater, and crafts. Every June, Bali hosts the Bali Arts Festival, Indonesia's largest cultural celebration, aimed at celebrating, preserving, and developing Balinese art and culture. This festival usually begins on the second Saturday of June and lasts for a month, having been held since 1979, making it one of Indonesia's oldest art festivals. The Bali Arts Festival includes various exhibitions, performances, and art-related activities designed to showcase Balinese culture and art. During the festival, many towns are decorated with traditional bamboo and coconut to celebrate. Outdoor stages and pavilions are set up across Bali to host talent performances. On these stages, you can enjoy dance, puppet shows, and music performances. The festival typically begins with a parade in Denpasar, followed by traditional music, then public exhibitions of paintings, sculptures, and photography, as well as craft exhibitions and food stalls offering Balinese cuisine.

### Christmas (Kelahiran Yesus Kristus)

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_601fcd7028841bd9bfdec12156d64e54.jpg)

A national holiday in Indonesia, with consecutive holidays on December 24 and 25. December 25 is Christmas Day and a national holiday in Indonesia. For Christians, Christmas is an important festival, and Indonesia has an average of nearly 10% Christians. Since Christmas and New Year's Day are close together, many Indonesians will go to great lengths to take time off, combining the two holidays into one long vacation. This gives them the opportunity to spend more time with family and friends and travel to other cities or countries. Indonesians have even coined a term called "Nataru," meaning "Christmas - New Year holiday," derived from Natal (Christmas) and tahun baru (New Year), to represent this holiday period.

## FAQ (Frequently Asked Questions)

**1. Does Bali's Nyepi Day affect tourism?**

Yes, on Nyepi Day, all commercial activities and transportation come to a halt, and even the airport closes. Visitors are required to stay within their hotels, but it’s a special opportunity to experience Balinese culture.
  **2. Do Bali's festival events require tickets?**

Most festival events are open to the public and free, especially ceremonies held in temples. However, appropriate attire and adherence to etiquette are required when participating.
  **3. When is the best time to attend Bali's cultural festivals?**

The best periods to experience multiple important festivals are from March to May and November to December, including Nyepi Day, Galungan, and Kuningan.
  **4. How can I respectfully participate in Bali's festivals?**

It’s recommended to wear traditional sarongs and sashes, and to observe local customs and taboos to avoid inappropriate behavior.
  **5. Are Bali's festivals suitable for families?**

Absolutely! Many festivals not only expose children to different cultural atmospheres but also strengthen family bonds through participation. However, some ceremonies may last a long time, so families should plan accordingly to avoid tiredness in children.
  **6. Do Bali's festivals affect transportation?**

Yes, especially during major festivals like Galungan or Kuningan, some streets may be temporarily closed for parades or events. It’s advisable to plan your itinerary early or opt for walking, which also allows for closer viewing of festival activities.
  **7. Can I photograph local festival ceremonies?**

Photography is generally allowed, but it’s polite to ask locals or guides first, especially in temples or during traditional ceremonies. Some ritual occasions may have restrictions, so maintaining respect and handling your camera discreetly is courteous.
  **8. What are some special foods to try during Bali's festivals?**

During festivals, visitors can savor many local traditional delicacies such as Babi Guling (roast suckling pig) and Lawar (a dish mixing vegetables and meat). These foods often carry cultural significance tied to festival rituals and are well worth trying.
  **9. How to avoid cultural conflicts during festivals?**

Respecting local culture is crucial. It's advisable for travelers to familiarize themselves with festival-related taboos in advance, such as maintaining silence on Nyepi Day and refraining from photographing forbidden scenes. Observing and adapting to local behaviors is also recommended.
  **10. How do Bali's festivals foster community connections?**

Festival activities are typically grand events for villages or communities, where residents come together to prepare and participate, strengthening emotional connections and enhancing community cohesion. Participating in these events not only offers cultural experiences but also allows travelers to feel the warmth and friendliness of local residents.
