## **Is There a Remote Worker Visa in Bali?**

Although Indonesian government officials have long been studying the possibility of a remote worker visa, they finally launched the 'Second Home' visa last year.

While the 'Second Home' visa is valid for 5 or 10 years, its stringent requirements make it somewhat out of reach for most remote workers. The most exaggerated condition is that you must have at least 2 billion Indonesian Rupiah (approximately USD$130,000) in an Indonesian bank account under your name as proof of funds, which cannot be touched. This visa also does not allow you to legally work for Indonesian companies or provide commercial services locally.

## The "Golden Visa" for Investors

Just launched on September 3rd by the Indonesian government, the "Golden Visa" program aims to attract foreign investment and stimulate the local economy. This program requires a minimum investment of $2.5 million for a minimum duration of 5 years. Applicants can obtain residency rights ranging from 5 to 10 years. Individual investors who contribute $2.5 million and establish a company will receive 5 years of residency, while increasing the investment to $5 million extends the residency period to 10 years.

For corporate applications, the minimum investment requirement is $25 million, with directors and designated personnel eligible for 5 years of residency. Doubling the investment extends the residency period to 10 years.

## **Practical Living Tips**

You've already arranged your work, accommodation, and visa. Now, let's talk about practical matters—how to manage daily life?

> **Transportation:** Rent a scooter: Renting a scooter in Bali is relatively cheap and straightforward (but remember, you must have an international driver's license). You'll definitely see many people on the road without helmets, but don't forget to wear yours. Police are cracking down on helmetless riding, but more importantly, Bali's roads can be dangerous, and wearing a helmet is one way to protect yourself.

> **Using Grab and Gojek Apps:** These apps are convenient and easy to use for booking rides, scooter rentals, and ordering food delivery. However, due to pressure from local taxi companies, these services may not be available at certain island attractions or remote areas.

> **Taxis:** Some taxi drivers in Bali may refuse to use the meter or claim it's 'broken' in order to negotiate higher fares verbally. Whenever possible, it's safest to use Blue Bird taxis, as their drivers always use the meter.

> **Staying Connected:** Purchase a local SIM card for the most affordable internet plans and reliable coverage. Telkomsel and XL are two of the best mobile network providers in Bali. Additionally, it's worth investing in a VPN service for use on public Wi-Fi networks and to bypass Indonesia's internet censorship, as some websites may be inaccessible.

> **Health and Safety:** While Bali has many excellent medical facilities, you should ensure you have sufficient travel health insurance to cover emergency overseas treatment. It's also important to note down emergency contact numbers and reputable hospitals in Bali.

> **Learning the Language:** Although many Indonesians in Bali speak English well, we recommend learning some Bahasa Indonesia to make it easier to befriend locals, get around, and enjoy meals at local eateries, which are often affordable and delicious. We mostly speak Indonesian in Bali!

> **Respecting Local Culture and Laws:** There are too many stories of foreigners in Bali getting into trouble for disrespecting local culture or breaking local laws. When in someone else's land, please respect their cultural norms and legal systems.
