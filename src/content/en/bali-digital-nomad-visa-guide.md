**Bottom line**: Indonesia **does not have a true digital nomad visa**. Currently viable options are: short-term use of a **tourist visa (VoA/e-VOA, 30 days extendable once)** to work remotely for an overseas employer (not allowed to be employed by an Indonesian company); long-term only the high-barrier 'Second Home' visa (requiring 2 billion Indonesian rupiah, about USD$129,000 in frozen deposits) or the 'Golden Visa' starting from a $2.5 million investment. The most popular areas for digital nomads are **Canggu, Ubud, Seminyak, and Sanur**, with plenty of coworking space options.

Bali, a popular travel destination with beautiful beaches, rich culture, and a serene atmosphere, attracts countless visitors. However, with the rise of remote work, more and more people want to vacation and work simultaneously in this paradise. Fortunately, Bali offers several options for you to enjoy your time here while staying productive.

## **Is There a Remote Worker Visa for Bali?**

Although Indonesian government officials have long been studying the possibility of a remote worker visa, they ultimately launched the 'Second Home' visa last year.

Although the 'Second Home' visa is valid for 5 or 10 years, its strict requirements make it a bit too far-fetched for most remote workers. The most extreme condition is that you must have at least 2 billion Indonesian Rupiah (approximately USD$129,000) in an Indonesian bank account under your name as proof of funds, which cannot be touched. This visa also does not allow you to legally work for an Indonesian company or provide commercial services locally.

## The 'Golden Visa' for Investors

Just launched on September 3, Indonesia's 'Golden Visa' program aims to attract foreign investment and stimulate the local economy. This program requires a minimum investment of USD$2.5 million for a minimum period of 5 years. Applicants can obtain residency for 5 to 10 years. Individual applicants who invest USD$2.5 million and set up a company will receive 5 years of residency, while if the investment amount increases to USD$5 million, the residency period extends to 10 years.

For applications under a corporate name, the minimum investment is USD$25 million, allowing directors and assigned personnel to obtain 5 years of residency. If the company doubles the investment amount, the residency period also increases to 10 years.

## **Practical Life Tips**

You've sorted out work, accommodation, and visas. Now, let's talk about the practical side of things—how to manage daily life?

> **Transportation:** Rent a scooter: Renting a scooter in Bali is relatively cheap and easy (but remember, you must have an international driver's license). You'll see many people riding without helmets, but never forget to wear yours. Police are cracking down on helmetless riding, and more importantly, Bali's roads can be dangerous—wearing a helmet is key to staying safe.

> **Using Ride-Hailing Apps like Grab and Gojek:** These apps are convenient and easy to use, allowing you to book cars and motorcycle taxis, as well as order food delivery. However, due to pressure from local taxi companies, these services may not be available at certain island attractions or remote areas.

> **Taking Taxis:** Some taxi drivers in Bali may refuse to use the meter or claim it's 'broken' to negotiate a higher fixed price. Whenever possible, use Blue Bird taxis—they are the most reliable, and their drivers always use the meter.

> **Staying Connected:** Buy a local SIM card for the most affordable data plans and reliable coverage. Telkomsel and XL are the two best mobile network providers in Bali. Additionally, it's worth spending a little on a VPN service to use when connecting to public Wi-Fi networks, and to bypass Indonesia's internet censorship, as some websites are inaccessible there.

> **Health and Safety:** While Bali has many excellent medical facilities, you should ensure you have adequate travel health insurance for overseas coverage in case of emergencies requiring treatment abroad. It's also important to note emergency contact numbers and reputable hospitals in Bali.

> **Learning the Language:** Although many Indonesians living in Bali speak English well, we recommend learning a bit of Indonesian—it will make it easier to make friends, get around, and enjoy meals at local eateries, which are often cheap and delicious. We almost always speak Indonesian in Bali!

> **Respecting Local Culture and Laws:** There are countless stories of foreigners in Bali getting into trouble for disrespecting local culture or breaking local laws. When you're on someone else's land, respect their culture and laws.
