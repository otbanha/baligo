## **Does Bali Have a Remote Work Visa?**

Although Indonesian government officials have long been studying the possibility of a remote work visa, they finally introduced the "Second Home" visa last year.

While the "Second Home" visa is valid for 5 or 10 years, its stringent requirements make it somewhat inaccessible for most remote workers. The most exaggerated condition is that you must have at least 2 billion Indonesian Rupiah (approximately USD$130,000) in an Indonesian bank account under your name as proof of funds, which cannot be touched. This visa also does not allow you to legally work for Indonesian companies or provide commercial services locally.

## The "Golden Visa" for Investors

The "Golden Visa" program, launched by the Indonesian government on September 3rd, aims to attract foreign investment and stimulate the local economy. This program requires a minimum investment of $2.5 million for a minimum duration of 5 years. Applicants can obtain residency rights ranging from 5 to 10 years. Individual investors who invest $2.5 million and establish a company will receive a 5-year residency, while increasing the investment to $5 million extends the residency to 10 years.

For corporate applications, the minimum investment requirement is $25 million, with directors and designated personnel eligible for a 5-year residency. If the company doubles its investment, the residency period also extends to 10 years.

## **Confirm Internet Connection**

If you enjoy working from home, a reliable internet connection is crucial. Some areas in Bali may have spotty network reception, so ensure your accommodation has stable Wi-Fi when choosing where to stay. You can inquire in advance with hotels or guesthouses about internet speed and reliability.

## **Practical Life Tips**

You've already sorted out work, accommodation, and visas. Now, let's talk about practical matters—how to manage daily life?

> **Transportation:** Rent a scooter: Renting a scooter in Bali is relatively cheap and straightforward (but remember, 'you must have an international driver's license'). You'll definitely see many people on the road without helmets, but don't forget to wear yours. Police are cracking down on helmetless riding, but more importantly, Bali's roads can be dangerous, and wearing a helmet is one way to protect yourself.

> **Use Ride-Hailing Apps like Grab and Gojek:** These apps are convenient and easy to use for calling rides and renting scooters, as well as ordering food delivery. However, due to pressure from local taxi companies, these services are not available at certain island attractions or remote areas.

> **Taxis:** Some taxi drivers in Bali may refuse to use the meter or claim that it's 'broken' in order to negotiate higher fares verbally. Whenever possible, it's most reliable to use Blue Bird taxis, as their drivers always use the meter.

> **Stay Connected:** Purchase a local SIM card for the most affordable internet plans and reliable signal coverage. Telkomsel and XL are two of the best mobile network providers in Bali. Additionally, it's worth spending a bit on a VPN service to use when connecting to public Wi-Fi networks and to bypass Indonesia's internet censorship, as some websites are inaccessible.

> **Health and Safety:** While Bali has many excellent medical facilities, you should ensure you have adequate travel health insurance for overseas treatment in case of emergencies. It's also important to note down emergency contact numbers and reputable hospitals in Bali.

> **Learn the Language:** Although many Indonesians living in Bali speak English well, we recommend learning a bit of Indonesian to make it easier to befriend locals, get around, and enjoy meals at local eateries, which are often inexpensive and delicious. We almost always speak Indonesian in Bali!

> **Respect Local Culture and Laws:** There are too many stories of foreigners in Bali getting into trouble for disrespecting local culture or breaking local laws. When in someone else's land, please respect their cultural laws.
