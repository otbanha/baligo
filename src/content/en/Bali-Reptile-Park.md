When people think of Bali, most imagine endless blue oceans, golden sunsets on sandy beaches, serene rice terraces, and sacred temples. These are indeed Bali's charming sides, but if you want to add a touch of raw, wild, even 'dinosaur-era' excitement to your trip, I sincerely invite you to venture deeper into Gianyar Regency.

There, hidden away from most tourists, lies a fantastic world—**Bali Reptile Park**.

![【Bali Family Attractions】Bali Reptile Park Guide: Get Up Clo](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-06/1780413643143-unnamed_5_.webp)

This is more than just a zoo; it's a living museum that condenses the reptile ecology of the Indonesian archipelago. Even better, it's **right next to Bali Bird Park**, sharing the same parking lot. Just a few steps and you can switch scenes, from the 'silent world of reptiles' to the 'colorful paradise of birds.' With good timing, you can easily enjoy two distinct yet equally wonderful animal kingdoms in one go.

## Why Is It Worth a Special Trip?

[https://youtu.be/Gt3_BFN1KsY?si=QwJsTRCx8zz7iTAO](https://youtu.be/Gt3_BFN1KsY?si=QwJsTRCx8zz7iTAO)

Bali Reptile Park is the island's first and only reptile-themed park. The park's design is very thoughtful, abandoning the cold iron cages and concrete floors of traditional zoos to create an immersive environment like a tropical rainforest. Walking along shaded paths with the sound of flowing water, you'll need to look carefully to spot the residents hidden among leaves and rock crevices. This sense of 'exploration' makes the entire journey full of surprises.

The park isn't huge, but the layout is smooth. A thorough visit takes about **2.5 to 3 hours**, and most visitors even stay for 4 hours. It's perfect as one stop on a Ubud day trip or as a midway point when traveling from Ubud to southern Kuta.

## Unique Interactive Experiences

This is what impressed me most about the Bali Reptile Park—'up-close encounters'!

During scheduled times (or you can ask the staff), under the guidance of professional keepers, you’ll have the chance to **touch and even drape a docile python over your shoulders**! The keepers will first explain safety rules in detail and stay by your side throughout. When those cool, dry, and finely textured snake scales glide across your skin, it’s an utterly surreal sensation. This isn’t something you can do just anywhere—it’s bound to be one of the most unique ‘trophies’ from your trip.

Watching crocodiles lunge out of the water to tear into chicken, or monitor lizards precisely snatch prey with their forked tongues, you’ll witness the raw reality of apex predators at the top of the food chain—both brutal and mesmerizing.

## Ultimate One-Day Two-Park Strategy: How to Plan Perfectly?

This is what I think is the smartest, best-value way to do it!

**【Morning】Bali Reptile Park (About 3 Hours)**
I recommend arriving **right when it opens at 9:00 AM**. The weather is cooler then, and the reptiles are more active. Head straight to the Komodo dragon and giant crocodile areas to snap photos before the crowds. The whole park takes about 3 hours to explore, wrapping up just before noon. By then, the sun is strong and you’ll be tired—perfect timing to move on to the next park.

**【Lunch】Dining at the Park**
Both parks have their own restaurants. The reptile park’s eatery is simpler, while the bird park offers more dining options, including air-conditioned indoor seating. You can grab lunch here and take a break.

**【Afternoon】Bali Bird Park – Recommended 3-4 hours**
After lunch, just walk next door to the bird park! This sprawling sanctuary is home to over **250 species and thousands of birds** from across the Indonesian archipelago and around the world. Step into massive aviaries where charming hornbills and parrots fly overhead; don’t miss the daily **parrot show** and **raptor show**—they’re spectacular! The park’s landscaping is lush with tropical vibes—waterfalls, lotus ponds, and traditional Balinese architecture make every shot look like a postcard.

## Must-Read Before You Go! FAQ

To help you plan your trip smoothly, we’ve compiled the most frequently asked questions:

**Q1: Do I need to buy separate tickets for the Reptile Park and Bird Park?**

**A:** Yes, the two parks are **independently ticketed**. Although they’re right next to each other, there’s no combo ticket. However, you can buy e-tickets separately through travel platforms like Klook or KKday, which are often cheaper than buying at the gate and save you waiting in line. If you only have time for one, I’d recommend the reptile park (since it’s more unique), but if you have enough time, I strongly suggest visiting both—they offer completely different experiences!

**Q2: How long does it take to explore both parks?**

**A:** The Reptile Park takes about 3 hours (for a detailed visit with photos and interactive experiences). The Bird Park is larger, so allow 3-4 hours (including shows, exploring the grounds, and taking photos). So visiting both parks in one day takes about 6-7 hours total. I recommend entering at 9:00 AM and finishing around 4:00 PM—plenty of time to take it easy.

**Q3: Is it suitable for kids? What age is best?**

**A:** Absolutely great for families! The parks are very kid-friendly, with smooth paths that are easy to push a stroller on. However, I'd recommend kids aged 3 and up will get the most out of it, as they can understand the concept of animals and are less likely to be scared. For toddlers (0-2 years old), large reptiles (especially crocodiles and giant pythons) might be frightening, so parents can adjust accordingly. During interactive experiences, the keepers will assess the child's height and bravery—no one is forced to participate.

**Q4: Do you need to pay extra to touch or hold the snakes?**

**A:** This is important! Currently (based on most visitors' experiences), touching and holding snakes is included in the admission ticket—no extra charge! Just ask a keeper when they're around, and they'll be happy to help. That said, it's a good idea to prepare a small tip (e.g., 20,000-50,000 Indonesian rupiah, about USD$1-2) for the hardworking keepers to thank them for their expert guidance and safety care.

**Q5: What's the best time to go? Are there set feeding shows?**

**A:** I highly recommend entering right when it opens at 9:00 AM! Three reasons:

- **Cooler weather**: Reptiles are cold-blooded and hide when it's too hot; they're more active in the morning.
- **Fewer crowds**: You can take photos at a leisurely pace without the crowds.
- **Avoid afternoon rain showers**: Bali often has brief heavy rain in the afternoon.
As for feeding shows, ask at the front desk when you enter for the exact schedule that day (usually on weekends or busy days, but not always on weekdays). Even if there's no show, just observing the animals is fascinating.

**Q6: Are there restaurants inside? Can I bring my own food?**

**A:** The Reptile Park has a simple café selling drinks, light snacks, instant noodles, etc., but the selection is limited. The Bird Park has more dining options, including Indonesian dishes, Western-style meals, and air-conditioned seating. You can also bring your own food and water—there are no strict restrictions. I'd suggest bringing a water bottle (it gets hot and you'll get thirsty) and having lunch at the Bird Park's restaurant for convenience.

**Q7: Can I take photos or videos?**

**A:** **Absolutely, and you're very welcome to!** There are no no-photo zones in the park (except for a few marked areas, such as certain venomous snake exhibits where flash might disturb the animals). However, here are a few tips:

- **Turn off the flash:** Strong flashes can startle reptiles and even harm their eyes.
- **Respect other visitors:** Don't block the display windows for too long just to get a photo.
- **During interactive experiences:** Ask the keepers to take photos for you—they're great at capturing the perfect angle!

**Q8: Is it easy to get there? Is it easy to get a ride back?**

**A:** The reptile park **isn't in the bustling city center**, but transportation isn't too difficult.

- **Best option: Private car hire.** About 600,000–800,000 IDR (roughly USD$19–26) per day. The driver will wait while you explore both parks, so you don't have to worry about a thing.
- **Second best: Grab / Gojek one-way ride.** It's usually easy to get a ride there, but **for the return trip, open the app 15–20 minutes in advance** to book, as there aren't many cars in the area and you may have to wait.
- **Not recommended: Taking a bus or hailing a taxi on the street.** There's no direct bus, and street taxis are scarce—they often quote inflated prices.

**Q9: Do I need to buy tickets online in advance?**

**A:** I'd recommend **buying them in advance on platforms like Klook, KKday, or Trip**! Here's why:

- **Cheaper:** Usually 10–20% less than the on-site price.
- **Skip the line:** On-site ticket purchases can involve waiting, while e-tickets let you scan a QR code and enter directly.
- **Avoid sellouts:** During peak season, on-site tickets may sell out (especially on holidays).
Of course, you can also pay in cash at the venue, but you'll need to spend a bit more time queuing.

**Q10: Are there other nearby attractions I can visit as well?**

**A:** Absolutely! The **Sukawati** area, where the Reptile Park and Bird Park are located, has plenty more to explore:

- **Bali Zoo**: About a 15-minute drive away, you can have breakfast with orangutans.
- **Sukawati Art Market**: Roughly 10 minutes by car, a great spot for souvenirs and bargaining.
- **Goa Gajah (Elephant Cave)**: Around 15 minutes away, a historic archaeological site.
- **Tegalalang Rice Terrace**: About 20 minutes by car, offering classic Balinese rice terrace views.
You can plan a day like 'morning at the Reptile Park + Bird Park → afternoon pick 1-2 nearby attractions'—it makes for a very fulfilling day!

## Travel Tips & Suggestions

1. **Attire & Protection**:
    - **Wear comfortable shoes**: Both parks require walking on stone paths and grass.
    - **Bring insect repellent**: Mosquitoes are active in tropical areas with lots of plants.
    - **Don't forget sunscreen**: While there's shade, many areas are exposed.
    - **Carry a light umbrella**: Bali often has brief afternoon showers, so it's better to be prepared.
2. **What to Bring**:
    - **Camera or phone**: The parks are incredibly photogenic, especially the Bird Park—bring a telephoto lens.
    - **Drinking water**: Vending machines and small shops are available, but bringing your own is more convenient.
    - **Some cash**: Handy for small purchases (drinks, ice cream, tips for guides).
3. **Mental Preparation**:
    - The animals at the Reptile Park won't greet you like cats or dogs. Be patient and observant, quietly appreciating their 'static beauty.'
    - During interactive experiences, **follow the keeper's instructions exactly**—avoid sudden movements or loud noises to prevent startling the animals.

Bali Reptile Park may not be as famous as Tanah Lot or Ubud Palace, but it's this 'low-key' nature that preserves more educational and exploratory fun. When you stand before a Komodo dragon or touch a cold python, you'll realize Bali's charm goes far beyond sun and sand. Here lies a more primal, authentic Indonesia.

On your next Bali trip, try adding this fantastical reptile world to your itinerary!
