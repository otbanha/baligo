Bali, this picturesque land, attracts travelers from all over the world. In our Facebook group, [Chihming Lai](https://www.facebook.com/profile.php?id=100008235455950), who is not a professional traveler or travel agency employee, makes a special trip to Bali every six months to relax, enjoy the sun, beer, beaches, and bikinis. For him, Bali's unique slow-paced atmosphere is the greatest charm of this land. Below are his personal travel experiences and practical tips that he is happy to share.

(The following content is adapted and reprinted with permission from [Chihming Lai](https://www.facebook.com/profile.php?id=100008235455950))

## **Pre-Trip Preparations:**

Before departure, he recommends **applying for an e-visa online**. When entering with a Taiwanese passport, you can use facial recognition by scanning your passport for quick clearance without queuing.

After collecting your luggage and before exiting the airport, you need to prepare your **electronic customs declaration**. It's crucial to complete this in advance as scanning it at customs can significantly speed up the process.

As for the **tourist tax**, he believes it's up to the individual to decide whether to pay it or not. Personally, he supports environmental activities like turtle release, despite concerns over fund usage. It's a small contribution to environmental protection, costing just USD$10, equivalent to a few beers.

## **Upon Arrival in Bali:**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_e13870a67da8a8539a2df155af5a63e5.jpg)

1. Don't overpack your itinerary - since you'll only be here briefly, it's better to slow down and enjoy the local pace of life. Wander aimlessly, sip a cool drink, and let your soul breathe.
2. Currently, Bali offers a more peaceful environment, and at least the beach pollution has improved significantly.
3. For those wondering which attractions to visit, his advice is: while the Monkey Forest and Ubud Palace are worth seeing, 5 minutes is enough - no need to spend too much time there. Instead, just across the street from the palace is Ubud Market, where wandering might be more interesting. As for rice terraces, they're visible along the roads, so visiting them can be optional. The journey itself is the scenery - focus on enjoying it and relaxing.

## **Accommodation and Itinerary Suggestions:**

1. He strongly advises villa guests not to pack their schedules too tightly, as it defeats the purpose of staying in a villa. Villas represent leisure - enjoy the pool and sunshine, sip some drinks, and relax. Only go out if you feel like it. There's no need to leave early in the morning and return late in the evening, let alone changing accommodations after just one or two nights - that's too exhausting. Staying in a villa means resting properly, appreciating the villa's scenery, and enjoying leisurely moments without overexerting yourself.
2. The length of your Bali trip should determine your itinerary - avoid rushing through attractions. Traffic here is often congested, roads are narrow and difficult to navigate, making it nearly impossible to visit all popular spots.
3. He always travels independently, staying in villas rather than hotels and not changing accommodations. The cost of a 10-day villa stay is roughly equivalent to a 5-day, 4-night hotel stay in Taiwan. Independent travel offers more flexibility without being tied to package tours.
4. Language barriers shouldn't be a major concern - a friendly smile is often the best communication tool. Additionally, smartphones can be great translation assistants.
