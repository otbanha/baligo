Bali, a land of stunning landscapes, draws travelers from around the world. In our Facebook group, [Chihming Lai](https://www.facebook.com/profile.php?id=100008235455950)—not a travel professional or agency employee—makes a special trip to Bali every six months to unwind, soaking up the sun, beer, sand, and bikinis. For him, Bali's unique slow-living vibe is the island's greatest charm. Below, he shares his firsthand travel experiences and practical tips.

(The following content is adapted and republished with permission from [Chihming Lai](https://www.facebook.com/profile.php?id=100008235455950))

## **Pre-Departure Preparations:**

Before you go, he recommends **applying for an e-Visa online**. With a Taiwanese passport, you can use facial recognition at immigration for fast clearance without waiting in line.

After collecting your luggage and before exiting the airport, you'll need to have your **electronic customs declaration** ready. Be sure to complete this in advance—just scan it at the checkpoint to save significant time. This step is crucial.

As for the **tourist tax**, he thinks it's up to each person whether to pay or not. He personally enjoys supporting eco-activities like sea turtle releases, even if the use of funds has been questioned—it's still a small contribution to the environment. At just USD$10, it's the price of a few beers.

## **After Arriving in Bali:**

![After Arriving in Bali:](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_e13870a67da8a8539a2df155af5a63e5.jpg)

1. Don’t pack your itinerary too tightly—after all, you’re just passing through. Instead, slow down, soak in the local slow-living vibe, wander aimlessly, sip a cool drink, and give your soul a breather.
2. Right now, Bali offers a relatively peaceful environment, and at least the beaches are much less polluted.
3. When asked about must-see spots, he suggests that while Monkey Forest and Ubud Palace are worth a visit, five minutes is enough—no need to linger. Across the road from the palace is the Ubud Market, which might be more fun to browse. As for the rice terraces, you’ll see them along the way, so you can decide whether to stop. In short, the journey itself is the scenery—just enjoy it and relax.

## **Accommodation & Itinerary Planning Tips:**

1. He strongly advises villa guests not to over-schedule their days—that would waste the villa’s purpose and cost. A villa is all about leisure: enjoy the pool and sunshine, sip a drink, and head out only when you feel like it. Why rush out early and return at dusk? Some guests even stay at a villa for just one or two nights before switching accommodations, which is too exhausting. When you book a villa, it’s to rest, admire the scenery, and savor downtime—no need to overdo it.
2. The length of your Bali trip should dictate your itinerary—don’t just skim the surface. Traffic here is often congested, and roads are narrow and tricky, making it nearly impossible to hit every popular spot.
3. He always travels independently, skipping hotels and staying in a single villa for the entire trip. A 10-day villa stay costs about the same as a 5-night hotel package in Taiwan. Independent travel offers more flexibility, without being tied to package tours.
4. Language barriers aren’t a big worry—a friendly smile is often the best communication tool. Plus, you can use your phone as a handy translator.
