Bali, this enchanting land, attracts travelers from all over the world. In our Facebook group, [Chihming Lai](https://www.facebook.com/profile.php?id=100008235455950), who is not a professional traveler or travel agency employee, makes a special trip to Bali every six months to relax, enjoy the sun, beer, beaches, and bikinis. For him, the unique slow-paced lifestyle of Bali is the greatest charm of this land. Below, he is happy to share his personal travel experiences and practical tips.

(The following content is adapted and republished with the consent of [Chihming Lai](https://www.facebook.com/profile.php?id=100008235455950))

## **Pre-Trip Preparations:**

Before departure, he recommends applying for an **e-visa** online. When entering with a Taiwanese passport, you can use facial recognition by scanning your passport for quick clearance without queuing.

After collecting your luggage and before exiting the airport, you need to prepare an **electronic customs declaration**. It is essential to complete this in advance, as scanning it upon arrival can significantly save time during customs clearance.

As for the **tourist tax**, he believes it's up to the individual to decide whether to pay it or not. Personally, he supports environmental activities like turtle release, despite concerns about fund utilization, as it contributes to environmental efforts. The tax is just USD$10, equivalent to a few beers.

## **Upon Arrival in Bali:**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_e13870a67da8a8539a2df155af5a63e5.jpg)

1. Don't pack your itinerary too tightly. Since you'll only be here briefly, it's better to slow down and soak in the local leisurely pace. Wander aimlessly, sip a cool drink, and let your mind breathe.
2. Currently, Bali offers a more tranquil environment, and at least the beach pollution has improved significantly.
3. For those wondering which attractions to visit, his advice is that while the Monkey Forest and Ubud Palace are worth a visit, five minutes is enough—no need to spend too much time there. Instead, just across the street from the palace is the Ubud Market, where wandering around might be more interesting. As for the rice terraces, they're visible along the way, so you can decide whether to visit them or not. In any case, the journey itself is the scenery, so focus on enjoying it and relaxing.

## **Accommodation and Itinerary Suggestions:**

1. He strongly advises villa guests not to over-schedule themselves, as it would defeat the purpose and expense of staying in a villa. Villas are meant for relaxation—enjoy the pool and sunshine, sip a drink, and only go out if you feel like it. There's no need to leave early in the morning and return late in the evening, let alone changing accommodations after just one or two nights. Staying in a villa is about resting, appreciating the villa's scenery, and enjoying leisurely moments without overexerting yourself.
2. The length of your Bali trip should determine your itinerary, and avoid rushing through it. Traffic here is often congested, and the roads are narrow and challenging, making it nearly impossible to cover all the popular attractions.
3. He always travels independently, avoiding hotels and staying in villas without changing locations. The cost of a 10-day villa stay is roughly equivalent to a 5-day, 4-night hotel stay in Taiwan. Independent travel offers more flexibility, freeing you from rigid package tours.
4. Language barriers shouldn't be a major concern—a friendly smile is often the best way to communicate. Additionally, your phone can be a handy translation tool.
