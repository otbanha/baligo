Are you considering traveling solo to Bali? Let me tell you first: **This is absolutely a great idea.**

Bali is one of the most suitable Southeast Asian destinations for solo travel in my opinion. It's safe, tourist-friendly, reasonably priced, and being 'alone' here is never awkward. Whether you want to quietly unwind or meet new friends, Bali can fulfill your needs.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775350592267-mixcollage-05-apr-2026-07-55-am-578.jpg)

Here are practical tips I've gathered from my own experiences for solo travelers:

***

## 1. Where to Stay? Choosing the Right Area is Crucial

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775351493354-mixcollage-05-apr-2026-08-11-am-1312.jpg)

Bali is vast, and different areas offer completely different vibes:

- **Ubud**: The top choice for solo travel. Yoga, meditation, healthy eating, rice field views—it's very comfortable to walk alone on the streets, filled with fellow solo travelers. Plenty of cafes and a thriving hostel culture.
- **Canggu**: A hub for digital nomads, featuring beaches, cafes, surfing, and nightlife. Suitable for those who enjoy energy and don't mind noise. The downside is very heavy traffic.
- **Seminyak**: A bit more upscale, with great restaurants, spas, and beach clubs, quieter than Canggu.
- **Nusa Dua**: A luxury resort area. If you have the budget and want to purely relax, it's an option, but it's more suited for couples or families—solo travel here might be a bit dull.
- **Jimbaran**: Famous for its sunset seafood barbecues.

**My suggestion**: If your trip is short (5–7 days), choose to stay in **Ubud + Canggu** for a few days each to experience two completely different sides of Bali.

## 2. How to Handle Transportation?

Traveling solo in Bali comes with its biggest headache: **Bali's transportation is terrible, and it's not very walker-friendly.**

https://youtu.be/ePutHL_Ob1Q

Here are a few solutions:

1. **Gojek / Grab**: Southeast Asia's version of Uber, affordable and convenient. Hailing a motorbike is the most cost-effective option (around USD$1–2 from Ubud center to nearby attractions). However, during peak hours (especially in Canggu), you might have to wait longer.
2. **Rent a motorbike**: If you have an international driver's license (and sufficient riding experience), this is the most liberating way to get around. It costs about USD$3–5 per day. **But be warned**: Bali's traffic is chaotic, and road conditions vary, so it's not recommended for beginners.
3. **Private car hire**: Around USD$40–60 per day (8–10 hours), ideal for longer trips (like Ubud's mountainous areas or Uluwatu). While pricey for solo travelers, you can split costs with hostel mates.

https://youtu.be/lIV-T2jYhVk

**My recommendation**: Use Gojek motorbikes for short distances, and hire a private car or join a local tour for longer trips.

## 3. Dining Alone Without Awkwardness

Bali's restaurants are very solo-traveler-friendly.

https://youtu.be/VuG8s-u_I0Q

- **Warung (local eateries)**: Sit at the counter and enjoy Nasi Goreng (fried rice) or Mie Goreng (fried noodles) for USD$2–3, both cheap and delicious.
- **Healthy vegetarian restaurants**: Found everywhere in Ubud, many offer counter seating or shared long tables.
- **Night markets / street stalls**: Perfect for grabbing a bite while walking around. Highly recommend satay and Babi Guling (roast suckling pig).

Just note: Some upscale restaurants or beach clubs may not welcome solo diners occupying large tables. It's best to ask beforehand or opt for counter seating.

## 4. Is It Safe? Honest Advice for Solo Travelers

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775351807418-3e530ee91368827f35ce3d2046220e06.jpg)

Bali is generally safe, but there are a few things to keep in mind:

- **Always wear a helmet when riding a scooter**: Police will stop you, and it's genuinely dangerous.
- **Beware of monkeys**: In places like Uluwatu and the Sacred Monkey Forest, monkeys may snatch glasses, phones, or food. Don't turn your back on them.
- **Keep valuables close**: Especially your passport, wallet, and phone. Use the hostel safe if available.
- **Don't accept drinks from strangers**: This applies everywhere.
- **Avoid walking alone in dark alleys late at night**: Especially in more remote areas of Canggu or Kuta.

**A small reminder**: Balinese people are generally friendly and warm, but those who are overly eager to chat or insist on taking you to a "friend's shop" usually have ulterior motives. Politely decline.

## Five: The Best Part of Solo Travel—Meeting New People

Traveling alone doesn't mean you have to stay alone.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1775352447434-54bd8eecd33bfc05d58f6767e3816f0a.jpg)

- **Stay in hostels**: Ubud and Canggu have many fantastic hostels with common areas, free breakfast, yoga classes, and coworking spaces... perfect for striking up conversations. Recommendations: Ubud Raya, Puri Garden, Tribal Bali.
- **Join day tours**: Like hiking Mount Batur for sunrise, snorkeling, or taking a Thai cooking class. Many solo travelers join these groups.
- **Yoga/meditation classes**: Ubud is Asia's yoga hub, and people naturally chat before and after classes.
- **Digital nomad cafes in Canggu**: Bring your laptop, sit at the bar or shared tables, and conversations will flow easily.

## Six: My Suggested Solo Travel Itinerary (6 Days, 5 Nights)

- **Day 1–3 Ubud**: Explore the palace, Sacred Monkey Forest, Tegallalang Rice Terraces, join a yoga class, watch a Legong dance performance, and enjoy vegan food.

https://youtu.be/JtxZHtijEkE

- **Day 4–6 Canggu**: Surf (hire an instructor if needed), watch the sunset at the beach, stroll Batu Bolong Beach, visit beach clubs (La Brisa is great), and soak up the café culture.

https://youtube.com/shorts/f0N6FE2dxys

- **A day trip**: From Ubud, hire a car to visit Jatiluwih Rice Terraces + Ulun Danu Beratan Temple, or from Canggu, head to Uluwatu's hidden beach for sunset + Kecak dance.

https://youtu.be/hQbBTktqEqY

## 7. Final Reminder: Slow Down

Many first-time solo travelers tend to pack their itineraries full. But in Bali, **the most beautiful moments are often the empty ones**—sitting at a cafe by the rice fields daydreaming, watching the waves at the beach doing nothing, chatting late into the night with friends you just met at the hostel.

The meaning of solo travel isn't about how many attractions you visit, but how comfortable you are with yourself.

Bali will gently remind you: being alone can feel complete.
