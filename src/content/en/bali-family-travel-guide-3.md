## **Visa**

I opted for the e-visa. Since traveling with seniors and kids tends to take more time, I preferred to handle everything possible in advance. Not just the visa—I also paid the tourist tax in Taiwan to minimize processing time upon arrival.

Bali’s customs process is now quite digital, but I missed that the departure declaration had also moved online. So I had to urgently fill it out on my phone on the spot, which took over ten minutes. Luckily, my family had already learned to relax, so this small hiccup didn’t dampen the first day’s mood—instead, it made everyone appreciate the flexibility and spontaneity of travel.

Though the trip had a few bumps, it really drove home how important it is to stay flexible and patient when traveling with family. I’ll continue sharing more details and insights from this Bali trip.

## **Flight Arrangements**

When it comes to flights during the Chinese New Year period, regardless of the destination, my decade-plus experience tells me the best strategy is to start monitoring prices closely a year before your intended departure date. Once you spot a good deal, don't hesitate or wait for travel fairs or airline promotions, as flights during this peak season only tend to get more expensive.

For this Bali trip, we originally planned to go to Australia, but after discussing with family in March, we decided to switch to Bali. While searching for flights during the Chinese New Year period, I unexpectedly found that EVA Air's round-trip business class tickets to Bali were only around NT$35,000 (USD$1,129), while economy class prices were already close to NT$20,000 (USD$645). In comparison, the business class fare seemed particularly worthwhile, especially considering the nearly five-hour flight to Bali—the added comfort came at a price that wasn't as steep as one might imagine.

## **Local Transportation**

Public transportation in Bali is not well-developed, so getting around mainly involves three options: hiring a private driver, taking taxis, or renting a car or scooter.

1. **Private Driver**: This is the most common and convenient option, especially for families with elderly members and children. You can tailor the itinerary to your schedule, adjust times and locations flexibly, and the driver often doubles as a guide, recommending local attractions and restaurants that only insiders know about.
2. **Taking Taxis**: For short trips, taxis are a good choice. It's best to use local ride-hailing apps like Grab or Gojek, which offer transparent pricing and convenience.
3. **Renting a Car or Scooter**: For travelers who prefer independence, renting a car or scooter is a viable option. However, be aware that traffic in Bali can be chaotic, and driving habits differ from those in Taiwan, so this is recommended only for experienced travelers.

This time we chose a private driver, which not only made the trip more relaxing but also allowed the family to rest well in the car, saving energy for the journey ahead. While Bali's transportation isn't as convenient as in big cities, with proper planning, the trip can still go smoothly and enjoyably.

### **Taxis and Traffic Conditions**

We visited during the rainy season. It's pretty much the same everywhere—when the weather's good, ordering food or hailing a taxi is smooth sailing, but once it rains, everything changes. So on days without a private driver, don't plan trips that are too far—30 minutes by car is the absolute limit. I suggest squeezing popular attractions into your private driver days; otherwise, on the way back, you might get overcharged or, if the weather turns bad, might not even find a ride.

This time we encountered an extreme situation of ordering food delivery in the rain: we placed an order at 11 AM, but by 1 PM no driver had accepted it. Eventually, Grab gave up and let us cancel the order! Luckily, we had stocked up on instant noodles, vegetables, and eggs at the supermarket the day before, so we didn't go hungry.

Traffic in Bali is indeed far from ideal, mainly due to the very haphazard road planning. Often, places that look close on the map require five times the travel time to reach by taking a long detour (sometimes I really just want to climb over the wall!).

### **Grab Ride-Hailing Experience**

Most Grab drivers are decent, but we did encounter one who asked us to cancel the order when he picked us up, so he wouldn't have to pay the platform fee. When we arrived at the destination, he jokingly asked, 'No tip?' In such situations, just stay calm—every place has its own culture and habits.

## **Accommodation Arrangements**

Since this trip was all about 'lazing around and relaxing,' I focused on booking villas. Browsing through Airbnb felt like a tour of luxury homes, with all kinds of extravagant villas dazzling me. It took about three days to snap out of those dreamy options and start seriously considering practical needs.

In Bali, the more luxurious the villa, the more remote its location tends to be, which may mean less convenience compared to city-center accommodations. So, based on my family's personalities and needs, I tried to strike a balance between 'luxury' and 'convenience.' The villas I booked this time were all in areas where Grab food delivery or taxis are easy to hail, ensuring we could conveniently handle meals and transportation when needed.

However, this accommodation experience also taught me a few lessons. Villa designs in Bali often place common living spaces—like living rooms, dining areas, and kitchens—outdoors or semi-outdoors to bring guests closer to nature. While this setup feels very resort-like, it also brings more mosquitoes, which was tough for someone like me who attracts them. Next time, I'll pay more attention and try to choose villas with air-conditioned, enclosed common spaces to enjoy a comfortable environment while avoiding mosquito troubles.

### **Booking Process**

The booking process this time went very smoothly. Bali's villa booking system already has quite a few small to medium-sized management teams in operation, with solid communication and service quality. If your English is up to par, you can communicate directly with hosts through Airbnb or other platforms, and you'll usually get prompt and detailed responses.

In terms of accommodation, this villa experience left our whole family very satisfied. Especially being able to enjoy a leisurely vacation in a private space, it's truly ideal for family trips with elderly parents and kids.

## Dining Experience

Bali’s water is quite hard, which was a struggle for a tea lover like me. Luckily, a friend’s sister who lives in Bali warned me in advance: even bottled water from the supermarket makes tea taste far worse than what I’m used to in Taiwan. She suggested bringing tea bags made from broken leaves or stems, as they brew more acceptably. Although I packed some nice red oolong leaves, after the first day of trying tea bags from Taiwan, I gave up entirely—I didn’t even use the travel gaiwan I brought.

### Local Food

When it came to eating, I tried local street stalls a couple of times—things like crispy roast pork rice and satay—but quickly stopped. The locals’ standards for ingredient quality didn’t quite match ours, so I mostly ended up dining at restaurants frequented by foreigners. These places tend to cater to international tastes, even when serving local dishes, making them a safer bet, especially for those who aren’t big on spicy food.

### Dairy & Desserts

My travel companion is a big fan of dairy products and was keen to find local fresh milk. It's important to note that Bali has no dairy industry, so there's no locally produced milk. If you're looking for good milk by Taiwanese standards, you'll just be wasting your money. Compared to twenty years ago when my first trip to Bali only offered powdered milk and UHT milk, there are now some products labeled "Fresh milk" in English that come close, but they're far from ideal for drinking straight—they're only passable in coffee or tea.

Similarly, Bali doesn't produce flour, and locals' taste for sweets isn't very Westernized, nor do they pay much attention to quality. So I wouldn't recommend spending time or money here searching for good Western-style pastries or bread. This is Bali, not Korea or Japan—adjust your expectations accordingly.

## **Transportation & Itinerary Planning**

As mentioned earlier, Bali's road design lacks comprehensive planning, resulting in traffic jams for over two-thirds of the day, almost around the clock. So if you have a packed itinerary, especially for spots more than an hour's drive from your accommodation, **it's highly recommended** to set off by 7 or 8 in the morning. Otherwise, the traffic alone can drain half your energy, leaving you exhausted for the rest of the day.

When planning your itinerary, try to group attractions in the same area together, and don't fantasize about covering over 40 kilometers in a single day! With Bali's narrow roads and the fact that Lunar New Year falls during the island's [third peak season](/en/blog/bali-peak-season-travel-tips/), it's common for a trip under 40 kilometers to take two hours. **Don't cram your schedule just because the distance looks short on the map**—you'll only end up stuck in traffic, frustrated, and missing out on the joy of travel.

### Family Travel Tips

For family trips, especially with elderly relatives and kids, I highly recommend a **'one day with a driver, one day off'** approach. This way, everyone gets enough time to rest and relax between outings, avoiding the fatigue from long commutes.

For example, on the day with a driver, you can visit farther attractions or popular spots like Ubud Market, the rice terraces, or Tanah Lot Temple. On the rest day, you can enjoy the private pool at your villa, get a spa treatment, or have a leisurely meal at a nearby restaurant. This pace keeps the elderly comfortable and gives kids plenty of time to play and recharge.

## **Massage Experience**

I didn't have high expectations for massages in Bali at first. In my memory, whether budget or luxury, the pressure tends to be too light for Taiwanese people—like a gentle stroke over the muscles. For those used to deep tissue work, it never feels quite right. Even when I chose the strongest pressure and repeatedly asked the therapist to go harder, the effect was still limited.

This time in Ubud, I tried the Deep Tissue Massage at Nata Spa. The pressure was indeed heavier than your typical Balinese massage, and it felt quite pleasant—I even dozed off for most of it. While it was much better than the 'gentle touch' I'd experienced before, it still left a bit to be desired. However, our driver mentioned that massages in Kuta and Seminyak are much better, while Ubud's are just average, so I wasn't too disappointed.

### A Surprising Discovery: Hot Bamboo SPA

![驚喜發現：Hot Bamboo SPA](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_c72ed5775df8b39abb816fd5c7abd11d.jpg)

After arriving in Canggu, I started searching for nearby massage spots and stumbled upon 'Hot Bamboo SPA.' After reading the reviews, I was immediately drawn to its uniqueness. It offers a massage using warm bamboo sticks, hence the name 'Hot Bamboo.' The reviews were overwhelmingly positive, especially about the pressure—claiming it's the most intense massage in Bali.

So, I decided to go for it! However, this SPA turned out to be unexpectedly popular and is one of the rare places that requires a deposit when booking, payable only in cash or via bank transfer to an Indonesian account. The massage fee also can't be paid by card, only cash. Luckily, with the help of my driver, I asked him to transfer the deposit first, and I managed to secure a reservation.

### Simple Yet Professional Choices

Compared to the overwhelming options at other massage places, Hot Bamboo SPA's menu is refreshingly simple, offering only three types of massages:

1. **Relaxing Bali Aga** (gentle relaxation)
2. **Hot Bamboo** (warm bamboo massage)
3. **Strong Barefoot** (intense foot massage)

The pressure ranges from light to heavy, and you can choose from 60, 90, or 120 minutes—straightforward and clear. The interior is clean and artsy, with bamboo and natural linen tones, creating a cozy and natural atmosphere.

### Strong Barefoot Massage Experience

Without hesitation, we chose the most intense 'Strong Barefoot' massage and booked a 2-hour session. The pressure was indeed spot-on—firm yet gentle. Especially when pressing on my back, I initially thought about asking the therapist to ease up, but within a minute, I felt completely relaxed and even started to doze off. I couldn't bear to ask them to lighten the pressure.

This massage experience completely changed my view of Balinese massages. Hot Bamboo SPA's professionalism and unique techniques truly offer a full-body and mind relaxation. If you enjoy a firmer massage, this SPA is absolutely worth a try!

## **Final Thoughts**

This Bali family trip, though challenging with elderly parents and kids in tow, brought us unforgettable memories and valuable lessons. From flights, accommodation, and transportation to food and massages, every detail deepened my understanding that travel isn't just about exploring the world—it's about creating beautiful moments with family.

Bali's charm lies in its diversity and inclusiveness. Whether it's luxurious villas, unique massage experiences, or nature-close landscapes, there's a way for everyone to unwind. While traffic and dietary habits may take some getting used to, with a bit of planning and flexibility, these small challenges only make the journey richer and more fun.

This trip taught me that traveling with family isn't about packing the itinerary full—it's about whether everyone feels comfortable and happy along the way. Bali's slow pace and warm hospitality provided the perfect space for relaxation. I hope this travelogue offers practical tips for future Bali travelers, and I look forward to creating more wonderful memories with my family on our next adventure!
