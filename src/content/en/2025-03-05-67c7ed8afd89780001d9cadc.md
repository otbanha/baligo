## Challenges and Solutions for Group Trips of 10+ People in Bali

Bali is one of the most popular group travel destinations in Asia, but planning a trip for 10 or more people involves navigating numerous challenges, from itinerary planning to managing unexpected situations. Below are the five common challenges when organizing group trips of 10+ people and how professional travel agencies can help resolve these issues to create a perfect journey.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_f185d21cca0ea1179fff3e7a15c7aad9.png)

## 5 Major Challenges for Group Trips of 10+ People

**1. Complex Itinerary Planning with Conflicting Needs**

With group members of varying ages and interests, balancing cultural experiences, adventure activities, family-friendly options, and relaxation can be tricky. For example, some members may want to wake up early for yoga classes, while others prefer sleeping in and enjoying breakfast leisurely. Some may be eager to explore local markets, while others would rather relax by the beach. Designing an itinerary that caters to diverse needs is a significant challenge.

**2. Difficulty in Resource Integration and Booking**

Popular Bali attractions like Ubud's rice terraces and Tanah Lot Temple attract large crowds during peak seasons. For groups of 10 or more, it’s essential to book transportation, guides, restaurants, and unique venues (such as private beach dinners or traditional dance performances) months in advance. Additionally, the quality of local services varies, and self-screening can be time-consuming and risky. Another critical point is that while online recommendations might lead you to a restaurant, it may not accommodate large groups or meet expectations without prior inspection.

**3. Budget Control and Hidden Costs**

When group size increases, while per-unit costs for transportation and accommodation may decrease, additional needs (such as special diets, medical support, insurance) and hidden expenses (such as delays) are often overlooked, leading to budget overruns. For example, adding a vegetarian member last minute may require menu adjustments at restaurants and even incur extra charges.

**4. Insufficient Crisis Management Capabilities**

From sudden weather changes affecting outdoor activities to health issues or visa delays, groups of 10 or more face more pronounced ripple effects from unexpected events, requiring immediate support from a team with local connections. For instance, if a member gets injured during rafting, quickly contacting local hospitals and adjusting subsequent itineraries will directly impact the overall experience.

**5. Primary Planner Falling Ill, Affecting Itinerary Progress**

Large group trips often rely on a primary planner for itinerary coordination and on-the-spot decision-making. If the primary planner falls ill or experiences sudden health issues in Bali, it can halt trip progress, leaving other members unsure how to proceed. Another example is when the planner cannot join last minute due to unforeseen circumstances, leaving inexperienced members struggling to handle changes or emergencies.
