## **Does Bali Have a Remote Worker Visa?**

Although Indonesian government officials have long been studying the possibility of a remote worker visa, they finally introduced the 'Second Home' visa last year.

While the 'Second Home' visa is valid for 5 or 10 years, its stringent requirements make it somewhat out of reach for most remote workers. The most exaggerated condition is that you must have at least 2 billion Indonesian Rupiah (approximately USD$130,000) in an Indonesian bank account under your name as proof of funds, which cannot be touched. This visa also does not allow you to legally work for Indonesian companies or provide commercial services locally.

## The "Golden Visa" for Investors

Just launched on September 3rd by the Indonesian government, the "Golden Visa" program aims to attract foreign investment and stimulate the local economy. This program requires a minimum investment of $2.5 million for a minimum duration of 5 years. Applicants can obtain residency rights ranging from 5 to 10 years. Individual investors who invest $2.5 million and establish a company will receive 5 years of residency, while increasing the investment amount to $5 million extends the residency period to 10 years.

For corporate applications, the minimum investment requirement is $25 million, with directors and designated personnel eligible for 5 years of residency. If the company doubles its investment, the residency period also extends to 10 years.

You can inquire in advance about the speed and reliability of the internet at hotels or guesthouses.

## **Practical Living Tips**

You've already arranged your work, accommodation, and visa. Now, let's talk about practical matters—how to manage daily life?

> **Transportation:** Renting a scooter: Renting a scooter in Bali is relatively cheap and straightforward (but remember, you must have an international driver's license). You'll definitely see many people on the road without helmets, but don't forget to wear yours. The police are cracking down on helmetless riding, but more importantly, Bali's roads can be dangerous, and wearing a helmet is a way to protect yourself.

> **Using Ride-Hailing Apps like Grab and Gojek:** These apps are convenient and easy to use for booking rides, motorcycle taxis, and ordering food delivery. However, due to pressure from local taxi companies, these services may not be available at certain island attractions or remote areas.

> **Taxi Services:** Some taxi drivers in Bali may refuse to use the meter or claim it's "broken," opting to negotiate prices verbally and inflate fares. If possible, using Blue Bird taxis is the most reliable option, as their drivers always use the meter.

> **Stay Connected:** Purchase a local SIM card for affordable internet plans and reliable coverage. Telkomsel and XL are Bali's two best mobile network providers. Additionally, it's worth spending a bit on a VPN service to use when connecting to public Wi-Fi networks, as some websites are inaccessible in Indonesia, and a VPN can bypass the country's internet censorship.

> **Health and Safety:** While Bali has many excellent medical facilities, you should still ensure you have adequate travel health insurance to cover you in case of emergencies requiring overseas treatment. It's also important to note down emergency contact numbers and reputable hospitals in Bali.

> **Learn the Language:** Although many Indonesians living in Bali speak English well, we recommend learning a bit of Indonesian to make it easier to make friends with locals, get around, and enjoy meals at local restaurants, which are often inexpensive and delicious. We mostly speak Indonesian in Bali!

> **Respect Local Culture and Laws:** There are too many stories of foreigners in Bali getting into trouble for disrespecting local culture or breaking local laws. When in someone else's land, please respect their cultural laws.
