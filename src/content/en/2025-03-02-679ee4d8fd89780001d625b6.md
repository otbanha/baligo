## **Visa Process**

I opted for an e-visa. Since I was traveling with seniors and kids, I knew the trip would require more time, so I preferred to complete all preparations in advance. Not only did I handle the visa, but I also paid the tourism tax in Taiwan to minimize the procedures upon arrival.

Bali’s customs process is now quite digitized, but I overlooked one detail — the exit declaration has also moved online. As a result, I had to quickly fill it out on my phone at the airport, which took about ten minutes. Fortunately, my family had already learned to stay relaxed, and this little hiccup didn’t affect the mood on our first day. Instead, it helped everyone embrace the flexibility and spontaneity of travel.

Although there were minor bumps along the way, this trip deeply reminded me of how important it is to stay flexible and patient when traveling with family. I’ll continue sharing more details and insights from this Bali trip.

## **Flight Arrangements**

During the Lunar New Year period, regardless of your destination, the best strategy based on my over ten years of experience is to start closely monitoring ticket prices a year before your intended departure date. Once you spot a good deal, don't hesitate or wait for travel fairs or airline promotions, as ticket prices during this period tend to only increase.

Originally, this trip was planned for Australia, but after discussing with my family in March, we decided to switch to Bali. While checking for Lunar New Year tickets, I unexpectedly found that EVA Air's round-trip business class tickets to Bali were only around USD$1,130, while economy class was already close to USD$645. Comparatively, the business class price seemed exceptionally reasonable, especially considering the nearly five-hour flight to Bali. The comfort upgrade didn't come with an unattainable price tag as one might expect.

However, sometimes shifting your perspective and slightly increasing your budget for business class can lead to pleasant surprises. ## **Local Transportation**

Public transportation in Bali is not well-developed, so there are mainly three ways to get around: hiring a private car, taking a taxi, or renting a car/motorcycle.

1. **Private Car Hire**: This is the most common and convenient option, especially suitable for families with elderly or children. Hiring a car allows you to flexibly adjust your itinerary according to your schedule, and the driver often doubles as a guide, recommending spots and restaurants known only to locals.
2. **Taking a Taxi**: For short distances, taking a taxi is also a good choice. It's advisable to use local ride-hailing apps like Grab or Gojek, which offer transparent pricing and convenience.
3. **Renting a Car/Motorcycle**: For travelers who enjoy independent travel, renting a car or motorcycle is a great option. However, it's important to note that Bali's traffic can be chaotic, and driving habits differ from those in Taiwan, so this option is recommended for experienced drivers.

This time, we opted for a private car hire, which not only made our itinerary more relaxed but also allowed my family to rest well in the car, conserving energy for the journey ahead. Although Bali's transportation isn't as convenient as in big cities, with proper planning, your trip can still be smooth and enjoyable.

### **Taxis and Traffic Conditions**

We visited during the rainy season, which is similar to the rainy season in most countries. When the weather is good, ordering food delivery or taxis is smooth, but once it rains, the situation changes completely. Therefore, on days when you don't have a private car, avoid planning trips that are too far—30 minutes of travel time is already pushing it. I suggest including popular attractions in your private car itinerary; otherwise, you might get overcharged when calling a taxi for the return trip, or worse, you might not even be able to get a ride in bad weather.

During our trip, we experienced an extreme situation when trying to order food delivery on a rainy day: we placed an order at 11 AM, but by 1 PM, no driver had accepted it. Eventually, the Grab platform gave up and asked us to cancel the order! Fortunately, we had bought enough instant noodles, vegetables, and eggs at the supermarket the day before, so we didn't go hungry.

The traffic situation in Bali is indeed less than ideal, mainly because the road planning is quite haphazard. Often, places that look close on the map require five times the time to reach because you have to take a long detour (sometimes you really feel like just climbing over a wall!).

### **Grab Ride Experience**

Most drivers we got through Grab were good, but we did encounter one driver who, upon picking us up, asked us to cancel the order so he wouldn't have to pay the platform fee. When we arrived at our destination, he jokingly asked, 'No tip?' In such situations, it's best to stay calm, as every place has its own culture and customs.

## **Accommodation Arrangements**

This trip was all about relaxation, so I focused on booking Villas. While browsing Airbnb, it felt like I was on a tour of luxurious mansions—there were so many stunning Villas to choose from. It took me about three days to snap out of the dreamy options and start seriously considering our actual needs.

### Latest Update (December 2025):

In Bali, the more luxurious Villas tend to be located in more remote areas, which can compromise convenience compared to staying in the city. Therefore, I tried to strike a balance between "luxury" and "convenience" based on my family’s preferences and needs. The Villas I booked were all in areas where Grab food delivery or taxis were easily accessible, ensuring we could conveniently handle meals and transportation when needed.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_16220458feb59e44fa83e1669a7981f0.jpg)

However, this accommodation experience also taught me a few lessons. Many Villas in Bali are designed with outdoor or semi-outdoor common spaces (like living rooms, dining areas, and kitchens) to bring guests closer to nature. While this design adds to the vacation vibe, it also comes with the downside of more mosquitoes—especially for someone like me who attracts them easily. Next time, I’ll pay more attention and try to book Villas with air-conditioned and enclosed common spaces, allowing us to enjoy comfort while avoiding mosquito troubles.

### **Booking Process**

The booking process went very smoothly this time. Bali's villa booking system already has quite a few small to medium-sized teams operating, and the communication and service are quite comprehensive. If your English is good, you can directly communicate with the hosts through Airbnb or other platforms, and usually, you'll get prompt and detailed responses.

In terms of accommodation arrangements, this villa stay experience left our entire family very satisfied, especially being able to enjoy the leisurely feeling of a vacation in a private space. It's really suitable for family trips with elders and children.

## **Dining Experience**

The water quality in Bali is quite hard, which was a bit of a pain for someone like me who loves tea. Fortunately, a friend's sister who lives in Bali reminded me that even the bottled water from supermarkets doesn't make tea as good as it does in Taiwan. She suggested bringing crushed tea leaves or tea bags made from tea stems, as they would make more acceptable tea. Although I brought some decent red oolong tea leaves, after brewing the tea bags I brought from Taiwan on the first day, I completely gave up and didn't even use the travel tea set I brought.

### Local Food

When it comes to food, I tried local street vendors a couple of times, like crispy pork rice and satay, but quickly gave up. The locals' standards for ingredient quality differ from our family's, so I mostly ended up choosing restaurants frequented by foreigners. These restaurants usually cater to foreign tastes, and even local dishes are modified to be safer and more friendly, especially for those who don't enjoy spicy food.

### Dairy Products and Desserts

One of my travel companions is a big fan of dairy products and was eager to find fresh local milk. It’s important to note that Bali doesn’t have a dairy industry, so there are no locally produced dairy products. If you’re expecting milk that meets Taiwanese standards, you’ll only end up wasting money. Compared to twenty years ago when the only options were powdered milk and long-life milk, there are now some products that come close to fresh milk, labeled "Fresh milk" in English. However, drinking it straight is far from ideal—it’s better used in coffee or tea.

Similarly, Bali doesn’t produce flour, and the local palate for sweets isn’t particularly Westernized. The quality isn’t a priority either, so I wouldn’t recommend spending time or money searching for good Western-style desserts or bread here. This is Bali, not Korea or Japan, so it’s best to adjust your expectations accordingly.

## **Transportation and Itinerary Planning**

As mentioned earlier, Bali's road design lacks comprehensive planning, resulting in traffic congestion for more than two-thirds of the day. Therefore, if you have plans for the day, especially for attractions more than an hour's drive from your accommodation, **it's highly recommended** to depart by 7 or 8 AM. Otherwise, just the traffic alone can drain half your energy, making the entire day's itinerary exhausting.

When planning your itinerary, try to group attractions in the same area together, avoiding the unrealistic idea of covering more than 40 kilometers in a single day! Because Bali's roads are narrow and it happens to be the Lunar New Year, which is Bali's [third busiest season of the year](/blog/2024-06-04-665ef28dfd89780001adfa98/), it's common for a journey of less than 40 kilometers to take two hours. Don't fill your schedule to the brim just because the distance looks short on the map; this will only leave you stuck in traffic, crying tears of frustration and completely losing the joy of travel.

### Family Travel Recommendations

For family trips, especially those involving seniors and children, I highly recommend adopting a "**one day of private car hire, one day of rest**" pattern. This not only allows everyone enough time to rest and relax between activities but also avoids the fatigue that comes with long hours of travel.

For example, on the day of private car hire, you can arrange visits to more distant attractions or popular activities, such as Ubud Market, rice terraces, or Tanah Lot Temple; on the rest day, you can choose to enjoy the private pool at your villa, indulge in a spa treatment, or leisurely dine at nearby restaurants. This rhythm not only ensures comfort for the seniors but also gives children ample time to expend energy and rest.

## **Massage Experience**

Originally, my expectations for massages in Bali were not high. In my impression, whether it's the common or luxurious version of massage, the pressure tends to be too light for Taiwanese people, akin to gently stroking the muscles. For Taiwanese accustomed to deep tissue massages, this level of pressure always feels insufficient. Even when choosing the strongest massage intensity and repeatedly urging the masseuse to apply more pressure, the effect remains limited.

During my time in Ubud, I first tried the Deep Tissue Massage at Nata Spa. The pressure was indeed stronger than typical Balinese massages, and the overall experience was quite comfortable—I even dozed off for most of the session. While it was much better than the previous 'light touch' massages I’ve had, it still felt a bit like scratching an itch through a boot. However, my private car guide reminded me that massages in Kuta and Seminyak are more impressive, while Ubud’s offerings are relatively average, so I wasn’t disappointed.

### A Pleasant Discovery: Hot Bamboo SPA

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_c72ed5775df8b39abb816fd5c7abd11d.jpg)

After arriving in Canggu, I started searching for nearby massage spots and stumbled upon 'Hot Bamboo SPA.' Reading the reviews, I was immediately drawn to its unique feature: a massage using heated bamboo, which inspired its name. The reviews were overwhelmingly positive, especially praising its intensity, claiming it to be the strongest massage in Bali.

So, I decided to give it a try! However, this SPA was surprisingly popular and one of the few places that required a deposit upon booking. Payment could only be made in cash or via bank transfer to an Indonesian account. Fortunately, with the help of my private driver, I managed to transfer the deposit and secure my reservation.

### Simple Yet Professional Choices

Compared to other massage places with extensive menus, Hot Bamboo SPA’s offerings were refreshingly simple, with only three options:

1. **Relaxing Bali Aga** (Gentle Relaxation)
2. **Hot Bamboo** (Heated Bamboo Massage)
3. **Strong Barefoot** (Intense Foot Massage)

The intensity ranged from light to strong, and you could choose between 60, 90, or 120-minute sessions—straightforward and clear. The interior design was clean and minimalist, with bamboo and natural linen tones creating a comfortable, natural ambiance.

### Strong Barefoot Massage Experience

Without hesitation, we opted for the strongest option, the 'Strong Barefoot' massage, and booked a 2-hour session. The pressure was indeed spot-on—firm yet gentle. While I initially considered asking the masseuse to ease up during the back massage, within a minute, I felt completely relaxed and even started drifting off, making it impossible to ask them to lighten the pressure.

This massage experience completely changed my perspective on Bali's spa scene. The professionalism and unique techniques at Hot Bamboo SPA truly provided a holistic sense of relaxation for body, mind, and soul. If you enjoy deep-tissue massages, this spa is definitely worth a try!

## **Conclusion**

This family trip to Bali, though challenging with elders and kids in tow, gifted us with unforgettable memories and valuable experiences. From flights and accommodations to transportation, dining, and massages, every aspect deepened my understanding that travel isn't just about exploring the world, but also about creating beautiful moments with family.

Bali's charm lies in its diversity and inclusivity. Whether it's luxurious villas, unique massage experiences, or natural landscapes, everyone can find their own way to relax. While adapting to traffic and local cuisine requires some effort, planning ahead and staying flexible can turn these small challenges into enriching aspects of the journey.

This trip taught me that traveling with family isn't about packing the itinerary full, but ensuring everyone feels comfortable and happy. Bali's slow pace and warm hospitality provided the perfect setting for relaxation. I hope this travelogue offers practical insights for future Bali travelers and look forward to creating more cherished memories with my family on our next adventure!
