## How Was That Reflection Photo Actually Taken?

Almost all Sky Gate photos online feature a perfect reflection—the sky, the gate frame, and Mount Agung mirrored on a calm water surface. Many assume there's a pond in front of the temple or that a specific angle captures a natural reflection.

**The truth: The reflection comes from a phone screen, not water.**

Here's how it works on-site: You stand in front of the gate, and the temple photographer (or you) places a phone/tablet horizontally on the ground or at a low angle, screen facing up. Another phone captures the shot from the front, simultaneously photographing you, the gate, and the reflection from the screen below.

Here are the key details:

- The larger the screen and the higher the brightness, the better the reflection effect (iPhone 15 Pro Max and Samsung S24 Ultra offer the most stable results)
- Strong sunlight can overpower the screen reflection; the best time is between 7:00–8:00 AM when the light angle is low
- The temple photographers know the best angles, and you can finish shooting within 30 minutes

**Recommendations for two types of visitors:**

**Want reflection photos** → Arrive before 7:00 AM, pay the photographer’s service fee (see next section), and finish in 30 minutes.

**Just want to see the temple without queuing** → Enter after 3:00 PM when the crowd disperses. Mount Agung is occasionally visible in the dry season afternoon. The cost is the same, but you won’t need to wait for a photographer.

## Location—Where is it in Bali? How far from where you’re staying?

The Lempuyang Temple complex is located in **Karangasem Regency** in eastern Bali, administratively part of the Abang District, near Tirta Gangga Water Palace and Amed Bay.

**Exact coordinates:** `-8.3920°S, 115.6297°E` (Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang, the location of the Gates of Heaven)

**Altitude layers:**

- Scenic parking lot: Approximately 350m
- Gates of Heaven (Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang): Approximately 600m
- Summit Temple (Pura Lempuyang Luhur): Approximately 1,175m

At an altitude of 600m, it is 4–6°C cooler than Kuta or Ubud city areas. Early mornings can be as cool as 18–22°C, so consider your attire (see the clothing section).

**Driving times from major tourist areas (peak season morning rush may add an additional 30 minutes):**

| Departure Point | Driving Time | Main Route |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Kuta | Approximately 2.5–3 hours | Bypass → Sanur → Gianyar → Klungkung |
| Seminyak | Approximately 3 hours | Same route as Kuta, plus 20 minutes north |
| Sanur | Approximately 2 hours | Most direct route along the coastline east |
| Ubud | Approximately 1.5–2 hours | Mountain road east through Klungkung |
| Amed | Approximately 45 minutes | Closest, the preferred base for eastern Bali visitors |

## Lempuyang is More Than One Temple – The Complete Structure of Six Temples (99% of Visitors Only Reach the Bottom Level)

The 'Gates of Heaven' is merely the **entrance at the lowest level** of the entire Lempuyang temple complex, where almost all tourists stop. The true pilgrimage route begins here, ascending along the ridge, passing through six temples, and finally reaching the summit temple Pura Lempuyang Luhur at an altitude of 1,175m—one of Bali's nine directional temples (Sad Kahyangan Jagat).

**Six Temples from Lowest to Highest:**

| # | Temple Name | Altitude | Walking Time from Previous Stop | Worth It? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang | ~600m | Approximately 20 minutes from parking lot | ✅ Must-visit, the Gates of Heaven are here |
| 2 | Pura Pasar Agung | ~800m | Approximately 45 minutes further up | ✅ Recommended, fewer people, ancient shrines, open views |
| 3 | Pura Puncak Bisbis | ~950m | Another 30 minutes | ⚪ Only if physically fit, steep stone path |
| 4 | Pura Lempuyang Madya | ~1,050m | Another 20 minutes | ⚪ Resting point halfway up |
| 5 | Pura Lempuyang Luhur | ~1,175m | Another 25 minutes | ✅ Summit temple, one of Bali's most important temples |
| Annex | Pura Telaga Mas | ~350m | Near parking lot | ⚪ Can be skipped by most visitors |

**Complete ascent requires:**

- One-way uphill hike takes about 2.5–3 hours (moderate fitness level)
- Bring: 1.5L water, sunscreen, lightweight rain jacket (sudden fog at summit)
- Footwear: Non-slip soles, stone steps, no hiking gear needed
- Sarong required throughout (mandatory at every temple along the way)

99% of visitors leave after taking reflection photos, but the higher you go, the quieter it gets. The second temple, Pura Pasar Agung, has almost no tourists, creating a completely different atmosphere from the base. If you have an extra 1.5 hours and decent stamina, continuing upward is highly rewarding.

## Opening hours and best visiting times

**General opening hours:** Daily 07:00–17:00 (some sources indicate entry possible as early as 06:30)

| Time slot | Best for | Mount Agung visibility | Crowd level |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 06:30–08:00 | Reflection photos, sunrise chasing | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (best in dry season) | Low → increasing |
| 08:00–10:00 | Peak arrival time for general tourists | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | High |
| 10:00–14:00 | Peak season rush | ⭐⭐⭐ | Highest |
| 14:00–17:00 | Temple-only visitors | ⭐⭐ (cloudy) | Decreasing |

**Peak Season (July–August, Chinese New Year) Queue Situation:**

- Waiting time for reflection shots: Over 1–2 hours
- Actual shooting time: Less than 5 minutes
- Recommended arrival time: 06:30—not 07:00, but 06:30

**Off-Season (February–April, October–November):**

- Waiting time reduced to under 30 minutes
- During rainy season (November–March), cloudy weather affects reflection photos, but the temple remains open, often saving 1–2 hours of queuing

**Rainy Season vs Dry Season:**

- Dry season (May–October) offers the highest chance of clear views of Mount Agung, with mornings in June–September almost always clear
- Rainy season mornings are usually cloudy, with occasional clear skies around 14:00–15:00, but conditions are unstable

**Local Insider Tip:** During peak season, Monday to Thursday between **09:30–11:00** has the shortest waiting times—early birds have left, and the next wave hasn’t arrived yet. However, Mount Agung is usually cloudy by then, making it unsuitable for reflection shots; visiting the temple alone is perfectly fine.

## Dress Code, Taboos, and On-site Notes

### Sarong Requirement

When entering any Lempuyang temple, **both men and women are required to wear a sarong** (kain / sarong). The temple provides them for free at the entrance, which you can borrow and return after use. Bringing your own is optional, but borrowing one is sufficient.

Upper body: Shoulders must be covered. Tank tops or sleeveless shirts are not allowed. The temple provides waist wraps, but this doesn’t mean tank tops are acceptable — temple staff may stop you and ask you to add a shawl. **Wearing a sleeved shirt is the simplest solution.**

### Temperature at 600m Altitude

Pura Penataran Agung (the Gate of Heaven itself) is located at around 600m, which is 4–6°C cooler than Kuta:

- Early morning 06:30–08:00: Around 18–22°C, cooler with mountain breezes
- Morning 09:00–12:00: Around 22–26°C, the temperature difference with lower areas narrows
- If you continue climbing to the summit temple at 1,175m: It may drop to 16–18°C, colder with mist

**Recommended attire:** A light jacket or thin long sleeves (even if it feels hot after photos, it’ll cool down during the hike). Avoid wearing just tank tops and shorts.

### Menstruation Prohibition in Temples

According to Balinese Hindu tradition, **women are not allowed to enter temples during menstruation**. Temple staff usually confirm this verbally at the ticket booth, and some temples post notices, but they won’t enforce verification. This is a basic courtesy to respect religious spaces, so please make your own judgment and decision.

### Restroom Situation

There are public restrooms near the parking area, costing 2,000–5,000 IDR. The restrooms inside the temple are farther away, so **take care of business before entering the temple**.

### Is It Suitable for Kids?

- **5 years and older, able to wear a sarong** → Can go, but be prepared for potential impatience during peak season waits
- **Toddlers (1–3 years old)** → Not recommended, as it’s a 20-minute walk from the parking lot to the temple entrance with stone steps
- **Climbing further up the temple** → Suitable for kids 8 and older with sufficient stamina, make sure to bring water

### Physical Requirements

Visiting only the Gates of Heaven (first temple): Low physical demand, 20–30 minutes of mostly flat walking. Climbing further up the temple: Basic hiking stamina recommended, 2.5–3 hours one way with continuous stone steps. Not advisable for those with severe knee issues or poor cardiovascular health.

## Reflection Photo Guide

### Paid Photographer vs DIY

**Hiring a temple photographer (recommended for beginners):**

- Cost: 150,000–350,000 IDR/30 minutes/group
- Process: Queue up → Wait for your number → Position yourself → Photographer handles phone angles and composition guidance
- Pros: Precise angles, saves time figuring things out, consistent photo quality
- Cons: Long waits during peak season, unclear pricing

**DIY Photography (For Experienced Travelers):**

1. Bring a secondary phone or tablet with a large screen (to use as a reflector)
2. Set up the composition on your main phone, and have a companion hold the secondary phone horizontally at a low angle in front
3. Set the screen brightness to maximum, and avoid direct sunlight (early morning side lighting works best)
4. Fine-tune the angle of the "reflector" phone until the reflection appears in the main camera's frame

### Composition Tips

- **Portrait (9:16)**: Center the subject, keep the door frame symmetrical, and place the reflection in the lower third of the frame
- **Landscape (3:2 / 4:3)**: Include more background, ensuring Mount Agung is fully framed
- Have the subject stand about 1 meter behind the center of the door frame, allowing the frame to "enclose" Mount Agung
- On cloudy days when Mount Agung is obscured, it’s not worth forcing the shot—the reflection photo’s essence is the volcano, and without it, the photo loses meaning

### Post-Processing

On-site reflection photos often appear dull and lack contrast. Simple editing steps:

- Brighten the shadows (the reflection area)
- Slightly increase blue saturation (to make the sky bluer)
- Crop so the reflection occupies 1/4–1/3 of the frame
- Use Lightroom Mobile or your phone’s built-in editing tools

## Things to Know Before You Go: The Disappointment Checklist

This passage isn't meant to discourage you, but to help you set the right expectations so you don't end up thinking 'Is that it?' after your visit.

**1. Wait 1-2 hours, shoot for 3 minutes**

During peak season at the Gates of Heaven, 90% of your time will be spent waiting in line for photographers, not admiring the temple. If you're short on time or have low tolerance for queues, seriously consider visiting during the off-season or arriving early on a weekday.

**2. The reflection is fake (you already know this)**

This isn't an issue for some people - beautiful photos have value in themselves. But if you're particular about 'authentic experiences', that 30-second 'So what?' feeling after taking the reflection shot might be more pronounced than expected.

**3. The gap between religious significance and tourism**

Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang is an important religious site in Bali, but the area around the Gates of Heaven - with its queue barriers, photographer numbering systems, and souvenir stalls - has become highly commercialized. For a true temple pilgrimage atmosphere, you'll need to hike up to the second temple or beyond.

**4. Mount Agung might not cooperate**

During the rainy season or on cloudy days, there's a high chance Mount Agung will be completely obscured. You might wait an hour only to get a completely white background. This is a matter of luck that no guide can guarantee.

**Who shouldn't visit:**

- If you're just following trends for social media but don't care about the outcome → Visiting [Tirta Gangga Water Palace](/blog/bali-tirta-gangga-empul/) is more worthwhile
- If waiting in line for over 30 minutes irritates you → Choose the off-season or skip this attraction altogether
- If you have no interest in religious culture and just want to snap a photo and leave → [Klook day tours](https://affiliate.klook.com/redirect?aid=116349&aff_adid=1277537&k_site=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.klook.com%2Fzh-TW%2Fsearch%2Fresult%2F%3Fquery%3DLempuyang%2520Temple) offer a more suitable schedule for you

**Who will definitely find it worthwhile:**

- If you want to take a photo that will surely make your Taiwanese friends ask "Where is this?" → The Gates of Heaven remains Bali's most photogenic spot
- If you're willing to continue up to the second and third temples → The authentic Balinese temple culture lies there
- If you're staying in Amed or the eastern region → It's close by and takes little time, easily done in half a day

## Frequently Asked Questions FAQ

**Q1: Is there an entrance fee for the Gates of Heaven?**

Yes, there is a "temple donation" which is mandatory, approximately 50,000–75,000 IDR per person. It's nominally a "voluntary donation," but you cannot enter without paying. Additionally, the photographer fee for the reflection photo is extra and not included in the entrance fee.

**Q2: Are the reflection photos real water reflections?**

No. The on-site reflection is achieved by placing a phone or tablet horizontally on the ground with the screen facing up, using the screen's reflection to create the effect. There is no pond in front of the temple, nor any natural water surface to produce a reflection. Knowing this mechanism, you can still choose to take the photo—it still looks great.

**Q3: Do I have to hire the temple photographer?**

To maintain order and efficiency on-site, each group/individual receives a **number tag** upon entering the temple. Numbers are called in order, and when it's your turn, you must hand your phone to the temple's official photographer for shooting. The photographer works very quickly (usually just a few seconds, 3-4 poses per person), and then it's the next group's turn.

**Q4: Can I take reflection photos with my own phone?**

It's not possible to achieve that effect on your own; you'll need assistance from the temple's photographers. There are two main practical reasons for this:

1. The photo spot is 'controlled'

To maintain order on-site, the **optimal shooting spot** directly in front of the Gate of Heaven (the position that perfectly aligns the temple architecture with Mount Agung in the background) is managed by temple staff.

- Regular visitors **cannot** casually walk over to that specific angle to take photos for friends.
- Everyone must queue in order, and when your number is called, you must hand your phone to the photographer seated at the spot, who will take the photo for you.

**Q5: How long does it take to get there from Ubud?**

The drive takes about 1.5–2 hours, depending on traffic and departure time. During peak season, it's recommended to depart from Ubud between 05:30–06:00, aiming to arrive at the temple entrance by 07:00–07:30. Including queuing, photo-taking, and breaks, the entire trip from departure to returning to Ubud takes approximately 5–6 hours.

**Q6: How long does it take to get there from Kuta?**

The drive takes about 2.5–3 hours, with a round trip totaling at least 5–6 hours of driving. If departing from Kuta, it's recommended to combine it with a [private car day tour](/blog/bali-private-car-hire-guide/), visiting Tirta Gangga, Amed, or Taman Ujung on the same day to make the long journey worthwhile.

**Q7: Is it suitable to bring elderly family members?**

Depending on the condition of elderly visitors. It’s about a 20-minute walk from the parking lot to Lempuyang Temple (the first temple), with some uphill stone paths. Those with mobility issues can take a shuttle to a closer point. The temple entrance itself has few steps and is accessible. However, climbing further up the temple is not recommended for those over 70 years old.

**Q8: Is it suitable for children under 5?**

Children under 5 will need to be carried along the stone paths, and during peak season, the long wait times can make it uncomfortable for them. If you must bring young children, it’s best to visit during weekday mornings in peak season or during the off-season to shorten the wait. Children aged 5 and above who can walk independently usually have no issues.

**Q9: Can I visit during the rainy season?**

Yes. The main issue during the rainy season (November–March) is the higher chance of Mount Agung being obscured by clouds, which may result in photos with a white mist background instead of the volcano. The temple itself is open year-round, and cultural experiences are unaffected. The rainy season sees fewer visitors and shorter wait times, making it more comfortable for those solely interested in visiting the temple.

**Q10: What other attractions can I visit on the same day?**

Lempuyang Temple is located in eastern Bali. On the same day, you can plan to visit 2–3 of these attractions (don’t try to do them all): [Tirta Gangga Water Palace](/blog/bali-tirta-gangga-empul/), [Taman Ujung Water Palace](https://youtu.be/zePMt3KSwTo), [Amed Snorkeling](/blog/amed-tulamben-diving-guide/), or Besakih Mother Temple (requires an additional hour of travel).
