That iconic Sky Gate reflection photo? It's created using a phone screen—not an actual water reflection. This isn't a critique; it's something you should know before you go, so you can decide whether to spend two hours queuing for the photographer or arrive at 7 AM to capture the Mount Agung view through the gate frame.

## How is that reflection photo actually taken?

Almost all Sky Gate photos online feature a perfect reflection—the sky, gate frame, and Mount Agung mirrored on a calm surface. Many assume there's a pond in front of the temple or that a specific angle captures a natural reflection.

**The truth: the reflection comes from a phone screen, not water.**

Here's how it works on-site: You stand in front of the gate frame, and the temple photographer (or you) places a phone/tablet horizontally on the ground at a low angle, screen facing up. Another phone captures the shot from the front, simultaneously photographing you, the gate frame, and the reflection on the screen below.

Key Details:

- The larger the screen and higher the brightness, the better the reflection effect (iPhone 15 Pro Max and Samsung S24 Ultra offer the most stable results)
- Strong sunlight can overpower the screen reflection; the best time is between 7:00–8:00 AM when the light angle is low
- The temple photographers know the best angles, allowing you to finish shooting within 30 minutes

Recommendations for Different Types of Visitors:

**For those who want reflection photos** → Arrive before 7:00 AM, pay the photographer's service fee (see the next section for costs), and finish in 30 minutes.

**For those who just want to see the temple without waiting** → Enter after 3:00 PM when the crowd disperses. Mount Agung may still be visible in the dry season afternoon. The cost is the same, but you won’t need to wait for a photographer.

## Location—Where in Bali? How Far from Where You’re Staying?

The Lempuyang Temple complex is located in **Karangasem Regency** in eastern Bali, administratively part of the Abang District, near Tirta Gangga Water Palace and Amed Bay.

**Exact Coordinates:** `-8.3920°S, 115.6297°E` (Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang, the location of the Gates of Heaven)

**Elevation Layers:**

- Scenic parking lot: approximately 350m
- Gates of Heaven (Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang): approximately 600m
- Summit Temple (Pura Lempuyang Luhur): approximately 1,175m

At 600m elevation, it's 4–6°C cooler than Kuta or Ubud city areas. Early mornings can be as low as 18–22°C, so consider your attire (see clothing section).

**Driving times from major tourist areas (peak season morning rush may add 30 minutes):**

## Lempuyang is More Than One Temple—Complete Structure of Six Temples (99% of Visitors Only Reach the Bottom Level)

The 'Gates of Heaven' is merely the **entrance at the lowest level** of the entire Lempuyang temple complex, where almost all tourists stop. The true pilgrimage begins here, ascending along the ridge through six temples, culminating at the summit temple Pura Lempuyang Luhur at 1,175m elevation—one of Bali's nine directional temples (Sad Kahyangan Jagat).

**Six Temples from Lowest to Highest:**

| # | Temple Name | Elevation | Walking Time from Previous Stop | Worth It? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang | ~600m | Approximately 20 minutes from parking lot | ✅ Must-visit, Gates of Heaven is here |
| 2 | Pura Pasar Agung | ~800m | Approximately 45 minutes further up | ✅ Recommended, fewer people, ancient shrines, open views |
| 3 | Pura Puncak Bisbis | ~950m | Another 30 minutes | ⚪ Only if physically fit, steep stone path |
| 4 | Pura Lempuyang Madya | ~1,050m | Another 20 minutes | ⚪ Mid-mountain rest point |
| 5 | Pura Lempuyang Luhur | ~1,175m | Another 25 minutes | ✅ Summit temple, one of Bali's most important temples |
| Additional | Pura Telaga Mas | ~350m | Near parking lot | ⚪ Can be skipped by most visitors |

**Complete Ascent Requires:**

- Uphill trek takes about 2.5–3 hours (moderate fitness level)
- Bring: 1.5L water, sunscreen, lightweight rain jacket (sudden fog at summit)
- Footwear: Non-slip soles, stone steps, no hiking gear needed
- Sarong required throughout (mandatory at every temple along the way)

99% of visitors leave after taking the reflection photos, but the higher you go, the quieter it gets. The second temple, Pura Pasar Agung, is almost tourist-free, offering a completely different atmosphere from the base. If you have an extra 1.5 hours and decent stamina, continuing upward is highly rewarding.

**General opening hours:** Daily 07:00–17:00 (some sources indicate entry possible as early as 06:30)

| Time slot | Best for | Mount Agung visibility | Crowd |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 06:30–08:00 | Reflection photos, sunrise chasing | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (best in dry season) | Low → increasing |
| 08:00–10:00 | Peak arrival time for general tourists | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | High |
| 10:00–14:00 | Peak season rush | ⭐⭐⭐ | Highest |
| 14:00–17:00 | Temple-only visitors | ⭐⭐ (cloudy) | Decreasing |

**Peak Season (July–August, Chinese New Year) Queue Situation:**

- Waiting time for reflection shots: 1–2 hours or more
- Actual shooting time: Less than 5 minutes
- Recommended arrival time: 06:30—not 07:00, but 06:30

**Off-Season (February–April, October–November):**

- Waiting time reduced to under 30 minutes
- During rainy season (November–March), cloudy weather affects reflection photos, but the temple remains open, often saving 1–2 hours of queuing

**Rainy Season vs Dry Season:**

- Dry season (May–October) offers the highest chance of clear views of Mount Agung, with mornings in June–September almost always clear
- Rainy season mornings are usually cloudy, with occasional clear skies in the afternoon around 14:00–15:00, but conditions are unstable

**Local Insider Tip:** During peak season, Monday to Thursday **09:30–11:00** has the shortest waiting time—early risers have left, and the next wave hasn’t arrived yet. However, Mount Agung is usually cloudy by then, making it unsuitable for reflection shots; visiting the temple itself is still fine.

## Dress Code, Taboos, and On-site Information

### Sarong Requirement

**Both men and women are required to wear a sarong** (kain/sarong) when entering any Lempuyang temple. The temple provides sarongs for free at the entrance, which you can borrow and return after use. Bringing your own is optional, but borrowing one is sufficient.

Upper body: Shoulders must be covered. Sleeveless tops or tank tops are not allowed. The temple provides waist wraps, but this doesn’t mean tank tops are acceptable—sometimes temple staff may ask you to add a shawl. **Wearing a shirt with sleeves is the simplest solution.**

### Temperature at 600m Altitude

Pura Penataran Agung (the Gate of Heaven itself) is located at around 600m, which is 4–6°C cooler than Kuta:

- Early morning 06:30–08:00: Around 18–22°C, cooler with mountain breeze
- Morning 09:00–12:00: Around 22–26°C, temperature difference with lowland narrows
- If you continue climbing to the summit temple at 1,175m: May drop to 16–18°C, colder with fog

**Recommended attire:** A light jacket or thin long-sleeve (even if it feels hot after photos, it’ll be cooler during the hike). Avoid wearing just tank tops and shorts.

### Menstruation Prohibition

According to Balinese Hindu tradition, **women are not allowed to enter temples during menstruation**. Temple staff usually confirm this verbally at the ticket booth, and some temples post notices, but there is no mandatory verification. This is a basic courtesy to respect religious spaces, so please make your own judgment and decision.

### Restroom Situation

There are public restrooms near the parking area, costing 2,000–5,000 IDR. The restrooms inside the temple are farther away, so **take care of business before entering the temple**.

### Is It Suitable for Children?

- **5 years and older, able to wear a sarong** → Can go, but be mentally prepared for potential impatience during peak season waits
- **Toddlers (1–3 years old)** → Not recommended, as it requires a 20-minute walk from the parking lot to the temple entrance plus stone steps
- **Continuing to climb higher temples** → Suitable for children aged 8 and above with sufficient stamina, be sure to bring water

### Physical Requirements

Visiting only the Gates of Heaven (first temple): Low physical demand, 20–30 minutes of mostly flat walking. Continuing to climb higher temples: Basic hiking stamina recommended, 2.5–3 hours one way with continuous stone steps. Not advisable for those with severe knee issues or poor cardiovascular health.

## Reflection Photo On-Site Guide

### Paid Photographer vs DIY

**Hiring a temple photographer (recommended for beginners):**

- Cost: 150,000–350,000 IDR/30 minutes/group
- Process: Queue up → Wait for your number → Position yourself → Photographer handles phone angles and composition guidance
- Pros: Precise angles, saves trial-and-error time, consistent photo quality
- Cons: Long waits during peak season, unclear pricing

**DIY Photography (For Experienced Travelers):**

1. Bring a secondary phone or tablet with a large screen (to use as a reflector)
2. Set up the composition on your main phone, and have a companion hold the secondary phone horizontally at a low angle in front
3. Set the screen brightness to maximum, and avoid operating under direct sunlight (side lighting in the morning works best)
4. Fine-tune the angle of the "reflector" phone until the reflection enters the frame of the main camera

### Composition Tips

- **Portrait (9:16)**: Center the subject, keep the door frame symmetrical on both sides, and place the reflection in the lower third of the frame
- **Landscape (3:2 / 4:3)**: Include more background, ensuring Mount Agung is fully framed
- Position the subject about 1 meter behind the center of the door frame, allowing the frame to "enclose" Mount Agung in the background
- On cloudy days when Mount Agung is obscured by clouds, it’s not worth forcing the shot—the essence of the reflection photo is the volcano, without it, the photo loses its meaning

### Post-Processing

On-site reflection photos often appear dull and lack contrast. Simple editing steps:

- Brighten the shadows (the reflection part)
- Slightly increase the saturation of blue (to make the sky bluer)
- Crop so the reflection occupies 1/4–1/3 of the frame
- Lightroom Mobile or built-in phone adjustments are sufficient

## Disappointment Checklist to Know Before You Go

This passage isn't meant to discourage you, but rather to help you go with the right expectations, so you don't end up feeling 'Is that it?'

**1. Queue for 1–2 hours, shoot for 3 minutes**

During peak season at the Gates of Heaven, 90% of your time will be spent waiting in line for the photographer, not admiring the temple. If your time is limited and your tolerance for queues is low, seriously consider visiting during the off-season or arriving early on a weekday.

**2. The reflection is fake (you already know this)**

For some, this isn't an issue—beautiful photos have value in themselves. But if you're particular about 'authentic experiences,' that 30-second 'So what?' feeling after taking the reflection shot might hit harder than expected.

**3. The gap between the temple's religious significance and its touristification**

Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang is an important religious site in Bali, but the area around the Gates of Heaven—with its queue barriers, photographer numbering system, and souvenir stalls—has become highly commercialized. For a genuine temple pilgrimage atmosphere, you'll need to head to the second temple or higher.

**4. Mount Agung might not cooperate**

During the rainy season or on cloudy days, there's a high chance Mount Agung will be completely obscured. You might queue for an hour only to find a blank white background. This is a matter of luck—no guide can guarantee otherwise.

**Who should reconsider visiting:**

- If you're just following trends for photos and don't care about the outcome → Visiting [Tirta Gangga Water Palace](/blog/bali-tirta-gangga-empul/) is more worthwhile
- If waiting in line for over 30 minutes irritates you → Choose the off-season or skip this attraction altogether
- If you have no interest in religious culture and just want to snap photos and leave → [Klook day tours](https://affiliate.klook.com/redirect?aid=116349&aff_adid=1277537&k_site=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.klook.com%2Fzh-TW%2Fsearch%2Fresult%2F%3Fquery%3DLempuyang%2520Temple) offer a more suitable schedule for you

**Who will definitely find it worthwhile:**

- If you want to take a photo that will surely make your Taiwanese friends ask "Where is this?" → The Gates of Heaven remains Bali's most photogenic spot
- If you're willing to continue up to the second and third temples → The authentic Balinese temple culture lies there
- If you're staying in Amed or the eastern region → It's close and time-efficient, can be done in half a day

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Is there an entrance fee for the Gates of Heaven?**

Yes, there is a "temple donation" which is mandatory, approximately 50,000–75,000 IDR per person. It's nominally a "voluntary donation," but you can't enter without paying. Additionally, the photographer fee for reflection photos is extra and not included in the entrance fee.

**Q2: Are the reflection photos real water reflections?**

No. The on-site reflection effect is achieved by placing a phone or tablet horizontally on the ground with the screen facing up, using the screen's reflection to create the effect. There is no pond in front of the temple, nor any natural water surface that could produce a reflection. Knowing this mechanism, you can still choose to take the photo—it still looks great.

**Q3: Do I have to hire the temple photographer?**

To maintain order and efficiency on-site, each group/individual receives a **number ticket** upon entering the temple. Numbers are called in sequence, and when it's your turn, you must hand your phone to the temple's official photographer. The photographer works very quickly (usually just a few seconds, 3-4 poses per person), and then it's the next group's turn.

**Q4: Can I take reflection photos with my own phone?**

You can't achieve that effect on your own - it requires assistance from the temple's official photographers. There are two main practical reasons for this:

1. The photography spot is 'controlled'

To maintain order, the **optimal shooting point** directly in front of the Gates of Heaven (the position that perfectly aligns the temple architecture with Mount Agung in the background) is controlled by temple staff.

- Regular visitors **cannot** casually walk over to stand at that specific angle to take photos for friends.
- Everyone must queue in order, and when your number is called, you must hand your phone to the photographer seated at the designated spot to take your photo.

**Q5: How long does it take to get there from Ubud?**

The drive takes about 1.5–2 hours, depending on traffic and departure time. During peak season, it's recommended to depart Ubud between 05:30–06:00, aiming to arrive at the temple entrance by 07:00–07:30. Including queuing, photography, and breaks, the complete trip from departure to return to Ubud takes about 5–6 hours.

**Q6: How long does it take to get there from Kuta?**

The drive takes about 2.5–3 hours, with a round trip totaling at least 5–6 hours of driving. If departing from Kuta, it's recommended to combine this with a [private car day tour](/blog/bali-private-car-hire-guide/), visiting Tirta Gangga, Amed, or Taman Ujung on the same day to make the long journey worthwhile.

**Q7: Is it suitable to bring elderly family members?**

Depending on the elderly's condition. It's about a 20-minute walk from the parking lot to the Gates of Heaven (the first temple), with some uphill stone paths. Those with mobility issues can take a shuttle to a closer point. The temple entrance itself has few steps and is accessible. However, continuing further up the temple is not recommended for those over 70 years old.

**Q8: Is it suitable for children under 5 years old?**

Children under 5 will need to be carried through the stone path sections, and with long wait times during peak season, it can be uncomfortable for them. If you must bring them, it's advisable to visit during weekday mornings in peak season or during the off-season to reduce waiting times. Children aged 5 and above who can walk independently usually have no issues.

**Q9: Can I visit during the rainy season?**

Yes, you can. The main issue during the rainy season (November–March) is the higher chance of Mount Agung being obscured by clouds, which means the reflection photos might have a white mist background instead of the volcano. The temple itself is open year-round, and cultural experiences at the temple are unaffected. The rainy season has fewer visitors and shorter wait times, making it more comfortable for those purely interested in visiting the temple.

**Q10: What other attractions can I visit on the same day?**

The Gates of Heaven is located in the east, and you can arrange to visit 2–3 of the following on the same day (do not choose all): [Tirta Gangga Water Palace](/blog/bali-tirta-gangga-empul/), [Taman Ujung Water Palace](https://youtu.be/zePMt3KSwTo), [Amed Snorkeling](/blog/amed-tulamben-diving-guide/), Besakih Mother Temple (requires an additional 1-hour drive). For a complete eastern Bali itinerary, see [Bali East Day Tour Guide](/blog/bali-east-tour-guide/).
