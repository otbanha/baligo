That famous Sky Gate reflection photo? It's taken using a phone screen—not an actual water reflection. This isn't a critique; it's something you should know before you go, so you can decide whether to spend two hours queuing for the photographer or arrive at 7 AM to capture the Mount Agung framed by the gate.

This article solves: the real cost breakdown (including hidden fees), the complete route through the six temples, the best time to visit, an honest comparison of three transportation options, and the question of 'who shouldn't bother coming.' Sort these out before your trip, and your Sky Gate experience will be smooth sailing.

## How Was That Reflection Photo Actually Taken?

Almost all Sky Gate photos online feature a perfect reflection—the sky, the gate, and Mount Agung mirrored on a calm surface. Many assume there's a pond in front of the temple or that a specific angle captures a natural reflection.

**The truth: The reflection comes from a phone screen, not water.**

Here's how it works on-site: You stand in front of the gate, and the temple photographer (or you) places a phone or tablet horizontally on the ground at a low angle, screen facing up. Another phone is used to shoot from the front, capturing you, the gate, and the reflection from the screen below.

Key Details:

- The larger the screen and higher the brightness, the better the reflection effect (iPhone 15 Pro Max and Samsung S24 Ultra yield the most stable results)
- Strong sunlight can overpower screen reflections; the best time is between 7:00–8:00 AM when the light angle is low
- Temple photographers know the best angles, allowing you to finish shooting within 30 minutes

**Recommendations for Different Types of Visitors:**

**Want reflection photos** → Arrive before 7:00 AM, pay the photographer's service fee (see next section), and finish in 30 minutes.

**Just want to see the temple without waiting** → Enter after 3:00 PM when the crowd disperses. Mount Agung may still be visible on clear afternoons during the dry season. The cost is the same, but you won’t need to wait for a photographer.

## Location—Where in Bali? How Far from Where You’re Staying?

The Lempuyang Temple complex is located in **Karangasem Regency** in eastern Bali, administratively part of the Abang District, near Tirta Gangga Water Palace and Amed Bay.

**Exact Coordinates:** `-8.3920°S, 115.6297°E` (Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang, location of the Gates of Heaven)

**Elevation Layers:**

- Scenic parking lot: Approximately 350m
- Gates of Heaven (Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang): Approximately 600m
- Summit Temple (Pura Lempuyang Luhur): Approximately 1,175m

At 600m elevation, it's 4–6°C cooler than Kuta or Ubud city areas. Early mornings can be as low as 18–22°C, so dress accordingly (see clothing section).

**Driving times from major tourist areas (peak season morning rush may add 30 minutes):**

## Lempuyang isn't just one temple—complete structure of six temples (99% of visitors only reach the lowest level)

The 'Gates of Heaven' is merely the **lowest entrance** of the entire Lempuyang temple complex, where almost all tourists stop. The true pilgrimage route begins here, ascending along the ridge through six temples, culminating at the summit temple Pura Lempuyang Luhur at 1,175m elevation—one of Bali's nine directional temples (Sad Kahyangan Jagat).

**Six temples from lowest to highest:**

| # | Temple Name | Elevation | Walking Time from Previous Stop | Worth It? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang | ~600m | Approx. 20 minutes from parking lot | ✅ Must-visit, Gates of Heaven located here |
| 2 | Pura Pasar Agung | ~800m | Approx. 45 minutes further up | ✅ Recommended, fewer people, ancient shrines, open views |
| 3 | Pura Puncak Bisbis | ~950m | Another 30 minutes | ⚪ Only if physically fit, steep stone path |
| 4 | Pura Lempuyang Madya | ~1,050m | Another 20 minutes | ⚪ Mid-mountain rest point |
| 5 | Pura Lempuyang Luhur | ~1,175m | Another 25 minutes | ✅ Final destination temple, one of Bali's most important |
| Additional | Pura Telaga Mas | ~350m | Near parking lot | ⚪ Can be skipped by most visitors |

**Full ascent requires:**

- Uphill hike takes about 2.5–3 hours (moderate fitness level)
- Bring: 1.5L water, sunscreen, lightweight rain jacket (sudden fog at summit)
- Footwear: Non-slip soles, stone steps, no hiking gear needed
- Sarong required throughout (mandatory at every temple along the way)

99% of visitors leave after taking reflection photos, but the higher you go, the quieter it gets. The second temple, Pura Pasar Agung, is almost tourist-free, offering a completely different atmosphere from the base. If you have an extra 1.5 hours and decent stamina, continuing upward is highly rewarding.

| 14:00–17:00 | Temple-only visitors | ⭐⭐ (cloudy) | Gradually decreasing |

**Peak Season (July–August, Chinese New Year) Queue Situation:**

- Waiting time for reflection photos: 1–2 hours or more
- Actual shooting time: Less than 5 minutes
- Recommended arrival time: 06:30—not 07:00, but 06:30

**Off-Season (February–April, October–November):**

- Waiting time reduced to less than 30 minutes
- During the rainy season (November–March), cloudy weather affects reflection photos, but the temple remains open, often saving 1–2 hours of queuing

**Rainy Season vs Dry Season:**

- Dry season (May–October) offers the highest chance of clear views of Mount Agung, with almost daily visibility from June to September in the morning
- Rainy season mornings are usually cloudy, with occasional clear skies around 14:00–15:00 in the afternoon, but conditions are unstable

**Local Insider Tip:** During peak season, the shortest waiting time is from **09:30–11:00** on Monday to Thursday—early birds have left, and the next wave hasn’t arrived yet. However, Mount Agung is usually cloudy by then, making it unsuitable for reflection photos; visiting the temple is still fine.

## Dress Code and Etiquette

### Sarong Requirement

When visiting any Lempuyang temple, **both men and women are required to wear a sarong** (kain/sarong). The temple provides them for free at the entrance, which you can borrow and return after use. Bringing your own is also fine, but borrowing one is sufficient.

Upper body: Shoulders must be covered. Sleeveless tops or tank tops are not allowed. The temple provides waist wraps, but this doesn't mean tank tops are acceptable—sometimes temple staff may stop you and ask you to add a shawl. **Wearing a shirt with sleeves is the simplest solution.**

### Temperature at 600m Altitude

Pura Penataran Agung (the Gate of Heaven itself) is located at about 600m, which is 4–6°C cooler than Kuta:

- Early morning 06:30–08:00: Around 18–22°C, cooler with mountain breezes
- Morning 09:00–12:00: Around 22–26°C, the temperature difference with lower areas diminishes
- If you continue climbing to the summit temple at 1,175m: It may drop to 16–18°C, colder with fog

**Recommended attire:** A light jacket or long-sleeve shirt (even if it feels warm after taking photos, it will cool down during the hike). Avoid wearing just tank tops and shorts.

### Menstruation Prohibition

According to Balinese Hindu tradition, **women are not allowed to enter temples during their menstrual period**. Temple staff usually confirm this verbally at the ticket booth, and some temples post notices, but they do not enforce verification. This is a basic courtesy to respect religious spaces, so please use your own judgment and make the decision accordingly.

### Restroom Situation

Public restrooms are available near the parking area, costing 2,000–5,000 IDR. Restrooms inside the temple are farther away, so **take care of business before entering the temple**.

### Is It Suitable for Kids?

- **5 years and older, able to wear a sarong** → Can go, but be prepared for potential impatience during peak season waits
- **Toddlers (1–3 years old)** → Not recommended, as it’s a 20-minute walk from the parking lot to the temple entrance plus stone steps
- **Climbing further up the temple** → Suitable for kids 8+ with sufficient stamina, make sure to bring water

### Physical Requirements

Just visiting the Gates of Heaven (first temple): Low physical demand, 20–30 minutes of mostly flat walking. Climbing further up the temple: Basic hiking stamina recommended, 2.5–3 hours one way with continuous stone steps. Not advisable for those with severe knee issues or poor cardiovascular health.

## Reflection Photo On-Site Guide

### Paid Photographer vs DIY

**Hiring a temple photographer (recommended for beginners):**

- Cost: 150,000–350,000 IDR/30 minutes/group
- Process: Wait in line → Get called → Stand in position → Photographer handles phone angle and composition guidance
- Pros: Precise angles, saves time figuring it out, consistent photo quality
- Cons: Long waits during peak season, unclear pricing

**DIY Photography (for experienced travelers):**

1. Bring a secondary smartphone or tablet with a large screen (to use as a reflector)
2. Set up the composition on your main phone, while a companion holds the secondary device horizontally at a low angle in front
3. Set the screen brightness to maximum, and avoid operating under direct sunlight (side lighting in the morning works best)
4. Fine-tune the angle of the "reflector" phone until the reflection enters the frame of the main camera

### Composition Tips

- **Portrait (9:16)**: Center the subject, keep the door frame symmetrical on both sides, and position the reflection in the lower third of the frame
- **Landscape (3:2 / 4:3)**: Include more background, ensuring Mount Agung is fully framed
- Have the subject stand about 1 meter behind the center of the door frame, allowing the frame to "enclose" Mount Agung
- On cloudy days when Mount Agung is obscured, it’s not worth forcing the shot—the reflection photo’s essence is the volcano, without it, the photo loses its meaning

### Post-Processing

On-site reflection photos often appear dull and lack contrast. Simple editing steps:

- Brighten the shadows (the reflection area)
- Slightly increase blue saturation (to make the sky bluer)
- Crop to ensure the reflection occupies 1/4–1/3 of the frame
- Lightroom Mobile or built-in phone adjustments will suffice

## Things to Know Before You Go: The Disappointment List

This passage isn't meant to discourage you, but to help you set the right expectations so you don't end up feeling 'Is that it?'

**1. Wait 1–2 hours, shoot for 3 minutes**

During peak season at the Gates of Heaven, 90% of your time will be spent waiting in line for photographers, not admiring the temple. If you're short on time or have low tolerance for queues, seriously consider visiting during the off-season or waking up early on weekdays.

**2. The reflection is fake (you already know this)**

This isn't an issue for some people — beautiful photos have value in themselves. But if you care deeply about 'authentic experiences,' that 30-second 'So what?' feeling after taking the reflection shot might hit harder than expected.

**3. The gap between religious significance and tourism**

Pura Penataran Agung Lempuyang is an important religious site in Bali, but the area around the Gates of Heaven — with its queue barriers, photographer numbering system, and souvenir stalls — has become highly commercialized. For a genuine temple pilgrimage atmosphere, you'll need to hike up to the second temple or beyond.

**4. Mount Agung might not cooperate**

During the rainy season or on cloudy days, there's a high chance Mount Agung will be completely obscured. You might wait an hour only to end up with a blank white background. This is a matter of luck, and no guide can guarantee otherwise.

**Who should skip this:**

- If you're just following trends for Instagram but don't care about the outcome → Visiting [Tirta Gangga Water Palace](/blog/bali-tirta-gangga-empul/) is more worthwhile
- If waiting in line for over 30 minutes irritates you → Choose the off-season or skip this attraction entirely
- If you have zero interest in religious culture and just want to snap a photo and leave → [Klook day tours](https://affiliate.klook.com/redirect?aid=116349&aff_adid=1277537&k_site=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.klook.com%2Fzh-TW%2Fsearch%2Fresult%2F%3Fquery%3DLempuyang%2520Temple) offer better time management for you

**Who will definitely find it worthwhile:**

- If you want to take a photo that will undoubtedly make your Taiwanese friends ask "Where is this?" → The Gates of Heaven remains Bali's most photogenic spot
- If you're willing to continue up to the second and third temples → The authentic Balinese temple culture awaits there
- If you're staying in Amed or the eastern region → It's close, takes little time, and can be done in half a day

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Is there an entrance fee for the Gates of Heaven?**

Yes, there's a "temple donation" which is mandatory, costing approximately 50,000–75,000 IDR per person. It's nominally a "voluntary donation," but you can't enter without paying. Additionally, the photographer fee for reflection photos is extra and not included in the entrance fee.

**Q2: Are the reflection photos actual water reflections?**

No. The on-site reflection is created by placing a phone or tablet horizontally on the ground, screen facing up, and using the screen's reflection to capture the effect. There's no pond in front of the temple, nor any natural water surface to create a reflection. Knowing this mechanism, you can still choose to take the photo—it remains visually stunning.

**Q3: Do I have to hire the temple photographer?**

To maintain order and efficiency on-site, each group/individual receives a **number ticket** upon entering the temple. Numbers are called in order, and when it's your turn, you must hand your phone to the temple's official photographer. The photographer works very quickly (usually just a few seconds per person, with 3-4 poses), and then it's the next group's turn.

**Q4: Can I take reflection photos myself?**

Unfortunately, you can't achieve that effect on your own — it requires assistance from the temple's official photographers. There are two main practical reasons for this:

1. The photo spot is 'controlled'

To maintain order, the **optimal shooting spot** directly in front of the Gates of Heaven (the position that perfectly aligns the temple architecture with Mount Agung in the background) is managed by temple staff.

- Regular visitors **cannot** freely walk over to stand at that specific angle to take photos for friends.
- Everyone must queue in order, and when your number is called, you must hand your phone to the photographer seated at the designated spot, who will take the photo for you.

**Q5: How long does it take to get there from Ubud?**

The drive takes about 1.5–2 hours, depending on traffic and departure time. During peak season, it's recommended to leave Ubud between 05:30–06:00, aiming to arrive at the temple entrance by 07:00–07:30. Including queuing, photo-taking, and breaks, the entire trip from departure to returning to Ubud takes approximately 5–6 hours.

**Q6: How long does it take to get there from Kuta?**

The drive takes about 2.5–3 hours, with a round trip totaling at least 5–6 hours of driving. If departing from Kuta, it's recommended to combine this with a [private car day tour](/blog/bali-private-car-hire-guide/), visiting Tirta Gangga, Amed, or Taman Ujung on the same day to make the long journey worthwhile.

**Q7: Is it suitable to bring elderly family members?**

Depending on the physical condition of elderly visitors, it takes about 20 minutes to walk from the parking lot to the Gates of Heaven (the first temple), with some uphill stone paths. Those with mobility issues can take a shuttle to a closer point. The temple entrance itself has few steps and is accessible. However, continuing to climb further up the temple is not recommended for those over 70 years old.

**Q8: Is it suitable for children under 5?**

Children under 5 will need to be carried through the stone path sections, and during peak season, the long waiting times can make children uncomfortable. If you must bring young children, it’s recommended to visit during weekday mornings in peak season or during the off-season to shorten the wait. Children aged 5 and above who can walk independently usually have no issues.

**Q9: Can I visit during the rainy season?**

Yes. The main issue during the rainy season (November–March) is the higher chance of Mount Agung being obscured by clouds, meaning the reflection photos may have a white mist background instead of the volcano. The temple itself is open year-round, and cultural experiences at the temple are unaffected. The rainy season has fewer visitors and shorter waiting times, making it more comfortable for those purely interested in visiting the temple.

**Q10: What other attractions can be visited on the same day?**

The Gates of Heaven is located in the east, and you can arrange to visit 2–3 of the following on the same day (do not try to visit all): [Tirta Gangga Water Palace](/blog/bali-tirta-gangga-empul/), [Taman Ujung Water Palace](https://youtu.be/zePMt3KSwTo), [Amed Snorkeling](/blog/amed-tulamben-diving-guide/), and Besakih Mother Temple (requires an additional 1-hour drive).
