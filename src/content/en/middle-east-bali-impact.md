Have you noticed that airfare prices seem to have quietly crept up recently?

It's not your imagination. In 2026, escalating geopolitical tensions in the Middle East continue to ripple through global energy markets. As jet fuel costs rise, the most immediate chain reaction is higher airfares—a warning sign that Bali, heavily reliant on tourism, cannot ignore.

### Rising Jet Fuel Prices Hit Bali First

Over 60% of Bali's GDP comes from tourism, making it deeply dependent on the aviation market. This year, the island expects to welcome over 7.5 million international visitors, but the shadow of an energy crisis looms.

Concrete actions are already underway: KLM Royal Dutch Airlines has announced the cancellation of hundreds of flights starting May 2026, including routes to Indonesia. This is just the beginning, as the industry fears that if other major airlines follow suit, international tourist numbers will take a significant hit.

### It’s Not Just International Flights—Domestic Airfares Are Soaring Too

What’s even more troubling is that Bali’s tourism industry doesn’t just rely on foreign visitors—local Indonesian travelers make up a significant portion. But domestic airfares are no longer what they used to be—

Flying from Bali to Jakarta used to cost around 1.5 million IDR (approximately USD$10), but now prices have skyrocketed to 4.5 million, even 5 million IDR. This threefold increase poses a significant burden for most Indonesians.

This means Bali's previous strategy of 'shifting focus to attract domestic tourists' during tough times might not work this time—as domestic flights are equally affected, consumers will naturally reconsider their travel plans.

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/images/2026-04/1776853335462-airplane.jpeg)

### What do tourism industry players think about this?

A representative from the Indonesian Hotel and Restaurant Association (PHRI) directly pointed out the core issue: the tourism industry heavily relies on air transportation, and when ticket prices rise, people tend to back out. If energy supply continues to be disrupted and fuel costs keep climbing, Bali might face a 'tourism crisis,' impacting hotel occupancy rates, employees' jobs, and even leading to some businesses shutting down.

This sounds serious, but it's not entirely pessimistic.

### Bali still has its advantages

Frankly, Bali's appeal remains steady amidst this turbulence. According to travel consultants, independent travelers and group tours from Southeast Asian countries haven't shown significant declines, the Indian market continues to grow, and while Japanese tourists have seen slight fluctuations, the situation is expected to stabilize as direct flights resume.

Compared to other popular Asian destinations, Bali's position in travelers' minds hasn't been shaken by geopolitical tensions. Many have already booked flights and hotels and are mentally prepared to absorb some of the rising costs.

### What should you pay attention to before traveling?

If you're planning a trip to Bali, my advice is:

**Book your flights early**. The risk of flight changes is higher than before, so if your schedule is fixed, it's much safer to book now rather than wait until the last minute.

**Choose accommodations with free cancellation**. In case of major changes in the situation, maintaining flexibility is crucial.

**Stay updated on airline developments**. Especially for European routes (like KLM) and transit flights through the Middle East, as they might be the first to be affected.

But Bali has this magical allure that makes people want to go, even knowing that flight prices might rise and travel costs might increase. This is probably why Bali's tourism industry, despite facing numerous challenges, keeps bouncing back time and time again.
