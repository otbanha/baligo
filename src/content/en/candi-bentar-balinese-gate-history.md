## The Origin and History of Candi Bentar

Have you ever noticed those unique traditional entrance structures without roofs while traveling in Bali or Java? These structures, known as Candi Bentar, are important symbols of local culture and history. Candi Bentar, meaning 'split temple gate,' is a distinctive traditional architectural form primarily used in temples, palaces, and other significant sites, serving as the entrance from the outer courtyard to the inner sanctum. These structures consist of two symmetrical buildings without a connecting roof, only linked at the base by steps. Candi Bentar is a common traditional architectural feature in Bali, originating from ancient Indonesian religious and cultural traditions, reflecting Bali's rich historical and artistic heritage. Although Hinduism has greatly influenced Bali's religion and architectural style, the specific form and design of Candi Bentar are products of Balinese local culture.

## The Religious and Cultural Significance of Candi Bentar

### Its Role in Balinese Religion

In Balinese religious life, Candi Bentar plays a crucial role. It marks the boundary between the mundane world and the sacred realm, serving as the essential passageway before entering temples or palaces.

The design of Candi Bentar is a symbolic separation, representing the transition from the secular world to the sacred realm. They typically consist of two symmetrical tower-like structures, intended to create a sense of transition as one passes through the gate, as if moving from one world to another.

## The Role of Candi Bentar in Modern Bali

### Modern Applications and Variations

Despite being a traditional structure, Candi Bentar is still widely used in modern Bali and has undergone some contemporary adaptations. For instance, you can spot Candi Bentar at Bali's airport, as well as in various hotels and resorts.

### Cultural Preservation and Development

The people of Bali place great importance on preserving their cultural heritage. As a cultural symbol of Bali, Candi Bentar has been well-maintained and restored, continuing to play a role in new architectural designs.

## Why "Gate of Good and Evil" is a Mistranslation

The Chinese translation "Gate of Good and Evil" is incorrect!!

### Linguistic Explanation

Many people refer to it as the "Gate of Good and Evil," but from a linguistic perspective, this translation is inaccurate. It imposes a moral judgment on Candi Bentar, suggesting that those who do wrong will face consequences, whereas the original meaning does not include this layer of interpretation.

Additionally, according to Balinese Hindu beliefs, ghosts and evil spirits can only move in straight lines and cannot turn or bypass obstacles. The design of Candi Bentar typically consists of two symmetrical, separate tower-like structures with a space in between. This design prevents ghosts and evil spirits from passing through, forcing them to stop at the gate.

### Details in Architectural Design

In the design of Candi Bentar, certain details often serve a protective function against evil spirits. For example:

1. **Reliefs and Carvings**: The reliefs and carvings on the tower-like structures typically depict Hindu deities, mythological stories, and protective symbols. These carvings are believed to possess the power to ward off evil.
2. **Gate Structure**: Candi Bentar is not a completely enclosed gate but has certain gaps. These gaps symbolize the transition into a sacred space and also form an invisible barrier that prevents ghosts and evil spirits from entering.

To more accurately convey the meaning of Candi Bentar, one might consider using terms like '**Yin-Yang Gate**' or '**Sacred Gate**.' This preserves its sacred connotations while avoiding the misconceptions that can arise from adding moral judgments.

## Conclusion

Although Candi Bentar originated in ancient times, its design and aesthetic principles continue to be applied in modern architecture. Many contemporary architects incorporate this traditional element into new designs, creating works that blend historical and modern sensibilities.

## **Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)**

**1. What is the historical background of Candi Bentar?** Candi Bentar dates back to the Majapahit era, initially used for religious and royal ceremonies, and gradually became a part of the local architectural style.

**2. Why does Candi Bentar lack a roof?** The absence of a roof in Candi Bentar symbolizes a passage between heaven and earth, emphasizing the independence and connectivity of the structures on either side.

**3. Where can Candi Bentar be seen in Bali?** Candi Bentar can be found at many significant temples in Bali, such as Tirta Empul Temple in Ubud, Lempuyang Temple (the Gate of Heaven), Besakih Temple, and Tanah Lot Temple.

**4. What is the difference between Candi Bentar and Paduraksa?** Candi Bentar lacks a roof and is used as an entrance from the outer area to the inner sanctum of a temple, while Paduraksa has a roof and is typically used for entrances within the temple.

5. **How do you correctly pronounce Candi Bentar?**

The correct pronunciation of Candi Bentar is 'chan-dee Ben-tar'.

6. Can I visit Candi Bentar?

Yes, many non-commercialized Candi Bentar structures are located in public temples and palaces, allowing visitors to explore and admire their architectural beauty.
