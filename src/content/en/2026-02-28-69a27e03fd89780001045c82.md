Many people have privately messaged me asking, 'Will I get scammed on my first trip to Bali?' Honestly, Bali is generally very safe, and the locals are warm and friendly. But wherever there are tourist areas, there are bound to be 'tricks'. To ensure your trip is filled with only beautiful scenery and no traps, be sure to stay alert to these common scams in 2026.

# ⚠️ Those 'Tourist Traps' You Should Walk Away From

## **The Sleight of Hand at Magic Money Changers**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_9f72c79c18a0688540bdd57dd45f5fad.png)

Small shops with outrageously high exchange rates often use 'quick counting' to slip bills away from your sight. Remember, abnormally high exchange rates are almost always a scam. It's best to only go to reputable, established shops, and after exchanging money, always count it slowly at the counter yourself. Don't rush to put it away just because you feel awkward.

## **The Taxi Meter Mystery**

Some drivers will claim that the meter is broken or ask you to cancel your app order and pay in cash instead. If you encounter this situation, there's no need to argue—just move on to the next taxi. Stick to using **_Grab_**, **_Gojek_**, or the official **Blue Bird** to ensure transparent pricing.

## **Fake Visa Websites on Search Engines**

When searching for e-visas, the top results are often beautifully designed but unofficial agencies that charge high service fees or even provide incorrect documents. Always verify the official Indonesian domain `.go.id` to avoid unnecessary expenses.

## **Disputes Over "New Scratches" When Returning Vehicles**

While renting scooters is convenient, disputes over pre-existing damage when returning the vehicle can be troublesome. Always record a full video walkaround of the scooter upon pickup, including all details and mileage, and never leave your passport with the rental shop.

## **Fake Staff at Temple/Attraction Entrances**

At attraction entrances, people often claim special ceremonies require donations or guides for entry. Most temples clearly display ticket prices and rules at official ticket counters. If approached by aggressive solicitors, head directly to the official ticket booth.

## **New Internet Risks with Mobile Hotspots**

Recently, there have been instances of strangers asking to borrow mobile hotspots under the pretext of having no internet access. This not only poses a risk of data leakage but also leaves your records exposed if the other party engages in illegal activities. Politely refusing to share your hotspot or connect to unknown Wi-Fi networks is a basic step in protecting your personal privacy.
