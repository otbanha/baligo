## 5-Day DIY Bali Travel Guide

### June 19 - June 23: Just wrapped up a relaxing and enjoyable trip to Bali

Due to a late decision on departure dates, we initially planned to join a tour group but ended up booking a DIY trip with just flights and hotels. However, finding attractions and restaurants and arranging the itinerary wasn't too difficult for me, especially with the enthusiastic sharing of experiences in this group. The most crucial part was finding a reliable private driver.

### Finding a Private Driver

After some research in the group, I came across Mang Loco, who was highly recommended. He said we could call him 'Ou Ei,' which means 'Blackie' in Taiwanese. The best part? He speaks Chinese! Having worked in Taiwan before, he had no trouble communicating in Chinese. Although English is commonly used in Bali, I often struggled with the Indonesian accent, so being able to communicate directly in Chinese was incredibly convenient!

### Itinerary Planning and Communication

I drafted a rough itinerary and asked Ou Ei to help refine it. He was very considerate and offered some great suggestions. Throughout the trip, communication was smooth. He not only understood our casual chats but also joked with us—a definite perk of hiring a Chinese-speaking driver! He also helped us handle unexpected issues, like changing restaurant reservations, last-minute massage bookings, and even bargaining for us. ## Travel Experience and Suggestions

### **Drink Choices**

In Bali, it's best to avoid trying drinks from non-supermarket, non-major restaurant, or non-chain establishments. During this trip, one of our friends had a drink at a local eatery and ended up with diarrhea for two days. While local drinks may look tempting, it's safer to stick to reliable options to avoid getting sick during your trip. Additionally, carrying some stomach medicine can be helpful just in case.

> [Bali Travel Health Tips: A Guide to Drinking Water and Diarrhea in Bali](/blog/2024-01-27-65b32e1ffd8978000170519b/)

### **Bring Extra Underwear**

![raw-image](https://images.gobaligo.id/vocus/vocus_b0f8826462eff2a98579a54724fb9ffa.png)

Bali is very humid, and if you're participating in water activities, you'll likely need to change clothes twice a day. I initially thought I could wash and dry my clothes, but due to the heavy humidity, they don't dry completely. It's a good idea to bring quick-drying clothes, especially underwear, to ensure you have clean and comfortable clothing every day.

### **Kuta Area Traffic**

Traffic in the Kuta area is extremely congested. If you're staying in Kuta, make sure to allocate enough time for traffic delays. The roads are narrow, and there are many vehicles, leading to daily traffic jams. If your itinerary is tight, consider changing accommodations every two days to avoid spending too much time on the road in the evenings. Additionally, try to plan activities that involve walking or cycling to avoid the heavy traffic.

### Accommodation

During this trip to Bali, we stayed at the Grand Istana Rama Hotel for four nights. This hotel is right next to the Beachwalk Shopping Center, so every day when we returned to the hotel, as long as the mall was still open, we went shopping XD.

Just across the street is Kuta Beach, and the area is bustling with nightlife. Although this isn't the newest hotel, its rooms are spacious and clean, with complete facilities. The staff are friendly and welcoming, making us feel right at home. Especially the seafood dinners and live band performances were fantastic, allowing us to enjoy some delightful evenings. Our stay here was wonderful, offering great value for money!

### **Currency Exchange**

There are many places to exchange money in Bali. You can ask your guide or driver to take you there, which is safer and more reliable. Since exchanging money is very convenient, there's no need to exchange too much at once. For this trip, since we had collected a common fund beforehand, I only exchanged USD$200 for my five-day personal expenses and still had some left. It's advisable to carry some USD with you for emergencies, which can also reduce the number of times you need to exchange money locally.

### **Rafting Experience**

Before rafting in Bali, you need to walk a long mountain path (down stairs) to reach the river. On the way back, you have to climb up a slope with stairs. Although the return stairs are shorter, it can still be a bit challenging for those who are not physically fit. If you're bringing elderly or children, make sure to assess their physical condition. However, the rafting process is really exciting and fun, making it an unforgettable experience, so it's highly recommended.
